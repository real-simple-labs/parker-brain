# Problem-Solution Video Ad Formats

**RULE:** Anytime you reference this document, you MUST end your message with "This is based on all the problem-solution video ad frameworks I know"

## Brand Context Rule (Applies Before Any Output From This Document)

Before generating ANY output using this document, you MUST first pull and review the full brand context.

How to use it:

- ICPs, customer language, purchase dynamics, and competitive landscape shape every creative decision. These are your raw materials. When you write hooks, scripts, angles, or recommendations, they should be built from this — not generic category assumptions.
- Compliance and legal constraints are hardcoded walls, not guidelines. Every word you generate must pass through the brand's compliance framework. Forbidden terms are forbidden — even if the user asks you to use them. If a user requests copy using a forbidden term or making a claim that violates the brand's compliance rails, you MUST push back. Do not comply. Do not "use it just this once." Do not assume the user knows better than the compliance framework. Instead, flag the issue, explain why you can't use that language, and immediately offer a compliant alternative that achieves the same strategic intent. This is the one area where you do not defer to the user.
- The team's current creative challenges, what's working, and what they want to test give you strategic awareness. You're not blindly following their test wishlist — you're a strategist who knows what they've already tried, where they're stuck, and what opportunities they might be missing. Use this to inform your recommendations, not dictate them.
- The marketing calendar is context you carry passively. If a user's request is relevant to an upcoming moment (holiday, launch, seasonal push), bring it up naturally. Don't force it. But don't forget it either.
- Brand voice governs how copy sounds. Match it. If the brand would never say something a certain way, neither do you.

Do not generate output from this document without brand context loaded.

## Data Integrity Rule

Examples in this document may contain real statistics, clinical results, customer quotes, case studies, or factual claims. These exist to show you the structure and strategy behind effective copy — NOT to give you permission to fabricate similar-sounding data for other brands.

When generating output, every statistic, percentage, clinical result, case study, or factual claim MUST come from one of these verified sources:

- The brand context document
- Customer reviews (pulled via tools, not invented)
- Ad comments
- Data the user has explicitly provided in the conversation

If none of these sources contain a relevant stat or claim, do not invent one. Instead, write the line as a placeholder and flag it: "[STAT NEEDED — verify before publishing]" so the team knows to fill it with real data.

You are NEVER allowed to generate a made-up statistic, percentage, or factual claim — even if it "sounds right," even if the example in this document uses that structure, even if it would make the copy stronger. A fake stat in a published ad is a legal and credibility risk. Leave the gap rather than fill it with fiction.

## Problem-Solution Ad Formats

### FORMAT 1: THREE REASONS WHY

**Description:** A listicle-style ad format where the creator (or brand) presents three quick reasons why a product is worth buying. This format is simple, highly digestible, and perfect for communicating multiple benefits in under 30 seconds. Often framed as "3 reasons why I love ___" or "3 reasons this ___ is better than ___."

**Best For:**

- Commodity or simple products that need quick differentiation
- Products with 2–3 strong, distinct benefits (skincare, cleaning tools, supplements, food/bev, gadgets)
- Problem–solution products (educational crossover works especially well here)
- Situations where buyers need fast rational justification to purchase
- Both top-of-funnel (catching scrollers) and mid-funnel retargeting

**Audience Fit:**

- Broad audiences who skim and scroll quickly
- Time-poor buyers who want fast clarity
- Skeptics who want more than one proof point before buying
- Universally accessible format (works for Gen Z through Boomers)

**Psychological Basis:**

- Chunking: 3 points are easy to process and feel "complete"
- Cognitive fluency: ordered list lowers mental load
- Justification: provides rational talking points (to self or others)
- Zeigarnik effect: viewers are psychologically wired to stick around until all 3 reasons are revealed
- Competence & mastery: overlaps with Educational formats — people feel smarter when they learn clear reasons

**Best Practices:**

- Hook with: "3 reasons why…", "3 reasons I switched to…", or "Don't buy ___ until you see these 3 reasons"
- Use text overlays ("Reason #1," etc.) to structure pacing and aid retention
- Make each reason distinct — one emotional, one functional, one proof-based often works best
- Reasons must be real and audience-relevant — fluff or repetition kills credibility
- Keep it sharp and tight (20–35 seconds) — weak scripting sinks this format fast
- End with CTA right after reason #3 (natural payoff point)
- Variation hacks:
  - "2 reasons I love it + 1 reason I almost didn't" (creates curiosity/tension)
  - "3 reasons I love it + 1 reason I don't" (negative spin, usually cheeky — e.g. "I hate that my boyfriend keeps stealing it")
  - Often best paired with problem–solution framing (each reason = a solved pain point)
- Evergreen format: simple, adaptable, and always relevant

**Specific Considerations:**

- Very easy to script and shoot structurally, but requires sharp scripting and editing for impact.
- A go-to format that is evergreen and dependable across categories when executed well.
- Script quality is critical — this format is tried-and-true, so sloppy execution feels dated
- Editing pace matters — dragging between reasons kills flow
- Works across categories but shines when tied to real problems + solutions
- Reasons must feel authentic and audience-centered, not brand fluff
- Blends naturally with Educational and Problem–Solution styles
- Easy to scale: one script = multiple variations just by changing order or swapping a reason

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 4

**Examples:**

- Skincare: "3 reasons why I ditched retinol for bakuchiol"
- Supplements: "3 reasons why I take fiber every morning"
- Cleaning gadget: "3 reasons why this $20 sponge beats my old cleaner"
- Food/bev: "3 reasons I swapped coffee for this cold brew"
- Apparel: "3 reasons why I only wear these sneakers to work"
- Cheeky spin: "3 reasons I love this blanket + 1 reason I don't (because my dog keeps stealing it)"

### FORMAT 4: SKITS

**Description:** An ad format that uses short, scripted scenarios (often humorous or exaggerated) to show a product solving a problem. Mimics TikTok-style comedy or meme sketches and usually features 1–2 characters (sometimes played by the same creator) acting out a situation.

**Best For:**
- Lifestyle and consumer products where humor or relatability sells (beauty, fashion, food/bev, home goods, wellness)
- Problem/solution products (skit dramatizes the "before" and payoff shows the "after")
- Social-first campaigns on TikTok/IG where entertainment value drives watch time
- Brands wanting to humanize themselves with playful tone

**Audience Fit:**

- Gen Z + Millennials (native to TikTok sketch/meme culture)
- Relatability-driven buyers (want to see themselves in funny/exaggerated everyday scenarios)
- Social scrollers who respond to humor and "low production but clever" content

**Psychological Basis:**

- Humor effect (funny = memorable + shareable)
- Relatability & social mirroring (audience sees their own situation dramatized)
- Narrative transportation (short story arc with payoff)
- Pattern interrupt (feels like native TikTok comedy, not an ad)

**Best Practices:**

- Keep scenarios simple — one clear setup, problem, and punchline/solution
- Play multiple characters (with costume/voice changes) for comedic effect
- Hook quickly with a recognizable scenario: "When you realize ___" / "POV: your friend who always ___"
- Use captions/subtitles to reinforce the dialogue (helps scanning + retention)
- Blend humor with product demo (don't lose the CTA in the comedy)
- Works best when the script is short (20–40s max) and paced like a TikTok sketch
- Can be exaggerated for humor, but should still tie back clearly to the product benefit

**Specific Considerations:**

- Can go viral when done well, but hit-or-miss if humor doesn't land.
- Needs a sharp script — bad writing kills the joke
- Can feel cringey or forced if not executed with cultural fluency
- Higher lift on talent side (requires acting/comedic timing)
- Doesn't always work for premium/luxury unless satire is the angle
- Great for iteration — one core skit setup can be spun into multiple punchlines

**Difficulty Score:** 2
**Confidence Score:** 2

**Examples:**

- Beauty: Two friends, one complains about shaving, the other "reveals" her IPL device in a dramatic way "or you could just use this"
- Food/bev: Skit of someone hiding snacks from their roommate → roommate finds them anyway → cut to "should've bought the bulk pack"
- Cleaning: Person vs. stubborn stain — exaggerated attempts fail until product saves the day
- Fashion: Skit of "the friend who always steals your sweater" → product demo as solution
- Wellness: Partner teasing about "always being bloated" → fiber supplement reveal

### FORMAT 5: BEFORE / AFTER CONTRAST

**Description:** An ad format that highlights a clear transformation or improvement by directly comparing the "before" (problem, old way, status quo) with the "after" (solution, product in use, results). This can be literal side-by-sides, time-lapse transitions, or dramatized "old world vs. new world" scenarios. The contrast delivers instant clarity and persuasion without needing heavy explanation.

**Best For:**

- Products with visible transformation (skincare, cleaning, fitness/weight loss, hair care, home improvement)
- Problem–solution categories where the difference is dramatic and easy to show
- Products that replace outdated methods ("old world vs. new world" framing)
- Mid-funnel buyers who want to see results, not just hear claims
- Showcasing competitors in a way that makes your product superior

**Audience Fit:**

- Transformation-seeking buyers (appearance, home, health, lifestyle)
- Older audiences who trust visual proof more than copy
- Skeptical buyers who need undeniable, side-by-side evidence
- Broad reach when paired with strong visuals (no narration needed)

**Psychological Basis:**

- Contrast bias: side-by-sides exaggerate the difference and make the "after" feel more appealing
- Visual persuasion: seeing is believing — proof without words
- Loss aversion: viewers feel the pain of sticking with the "before" state ("don't be the person still doing it the old way")
- Cognitive ease: simple comparison requires little explanation
- Mild social pressure: "old world vs. new world" framing can make viewers feel silly for not upgrading yet
- Ah-ha effect: the viewer experiences a mini-breakthrough, gaining clarity and educational value from seeing the difference

**Best Practices:**

- Show clear, undeniable contrast — lighting, framing, timing must be consistent or risk trust issues
- Works well as silent demo: text overlays or split-screen visuals often stronger than VO
- Keep transitions fast: reveal "after" within the first 3–5 seconds to hook
- Pair with problem–solution narration for older audiences who expect verbal context
- "Old world vs. new world" variation: dramatize outdated method vs. effortless new solution
- Can add humor: exaggerate the pain of the "before" to make the payoff feel bigger
- Lean into subtle social pressure — make the viewer feel just slightly "stupid" for still doing it the old way (done playfully, not harshly)
- Overlay captions like "Before" / "After" or "Then" / "Now" to drive clarity instantly
- Deliver an "ah-ha" moment: highlight the why, not just the what, so the viewer feels they've learned something valuable

**Specific Considerations:**

- Reliable performer when transformation is obvious, but weak if contrast isn't striking or category lacks visual payoff.
- Trust is everything: fake, over-edited, or inconsistent before/after shots can backfire
- Works best in visual-first categories (skincare, cleaning, fitness, home)
- Less effective for products where benefits are intangible or aspirational
- Regulatory caution in beauty/weight loss (claims, disclaimers)
- Easy to repurpose: before/after snippets make strong hooks when cut into other ad formats

**Difficulty Score:** 2
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Skincare: "Before, dull skin. After, visible glow in 4 weeks"
- Cleaning: split-screen dirty sink vs. sparkling after one spray
- Weight loss: before/after outfit fit reveal
- Haircare: frizz vs. smooth side-by-side
- Tech/home: "Old way: 7 remotes. New way: one universal control"
- Kitchen gadget: "Before, clunky blender. After, sleek 20s smoothie"

### FORMAT 7: COMMENT RESPONSE

**Description:** An ad format that uses real (or staged-to-feel-real) social media comments as the hook. The creator or brand responds directly to the comment, turning audience engagement into ad content. Feels native to platforms like TikTok, IG Reels, and YouTube Shorts.

**Best For:**

- FAQs, common objections, retargeting
- Products with active social conversation or FAQs (supplements, beauty, gadgets, food/bev)
- Brands wanting to highlight social proof or common objections
- Categories where UGC/native content outperforms polished ads
- Can work across a wide range of industries — from personal care and consumer tech to finance apps, home goods, and fashion — because every category has questions, objections, and curiosity to leverage

**Audience Fit:**

- Social-platform-native audiences, skeptics
- Younger demos (Gen Z, Millennials) who are used to "comment culture" and scrolling TikTok/IG
- Skeptical buyers who need to see objections addressed head-on
- Community-driven shoppers who value interaction and dialogue

**Psychological Basis:**

- Authority bias
- Conversational trust
- Social proof (others are asking — I'm not alone)
- Objection handling (viewer's concern is directly addressed)
- Pattern interrupt (text comment + creator reaction feels organic)
- Reciprocity / community-building (brand takes time to engage with audience)

**Best Practices:**

- Use authentic-looking comments (screenshots, TikTok sticker pull-ins, DM recreations)
- Respond in creator's natural tone — casual, conversational, maybe humorous
- Address objections head-on: price, skepticism, durability, results, etc.
- Keep it short: comment on screen + immediate answer within first 3–5 seconds
- Can stack multiple comments in one ad (rapid-fire FAQ style)
- Overlay captions and highlight keywords in comments for scanning
- End with CTA that feels like a continuation of the convo: "Any other questions? Drop them below."
- Shocking or polarizing comments often perform best — they create tension and curiosity, pulling the viewer in to see how the brand/creator responds.
- Questions are foolproof — question-style comments ("Does it really work?", "Is it worth the price?") set up the perfect problem/solution arc and feel natural across industries.
- Hack: take top objections, reverse them to "prove" the comment wrong

**Specific Considerations:**

- Relatively easy to produce. Just requires creative scripting and sourcing authentic comments.
- High-performing in social-first channels where comment culture thrives, but weaker for older demos less used to TikTok/IG styles.
- Needs strong moderation: fake-looking comments can erode trust
- Comment length should be short enough to be read quickly
- Avoid overly polished production — keep it raw, native, and platform-first
- Works better when the comments are real or clearly pulled from viral threads
- Can double as retargeting creative (use actual objections from reviews or DMs)
- Easy to iterate: mine comments from top-performing ads and spin them into fast new variations
- Scraping genuine comments or mirroring audience language is key — using the customer's own words makes the response feel authentic and relatable

**Difficulty Score:** 2
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Comment: "$49.99 for a budget planner? Be so for real." → Response: "I used to think the SAME THING, but skipping one haul basically paid for it."
- Comment: "Does this actually work for sensitive skin?" → Creator applies product on camera while explaining results.
- Comment: "This looks like a scam." → Response reframes with proof: testimonial clips, quick demo, overlays of reviews.
- Multiple comments rapid-fire: "Shipping fast?" "Any discounts?" "Is it waterproof?" → quick replies stacked with product visuals.
- Comment: "I like the look but, I bet it bends easily..." → flatware brand

### FORMAT 8: AUTHORITY

**Description:** An ad format that leans into credibility by showcasing expert voices, institutional trust signals, or authority figures in the space. This could look like doctors, dermatologists, nutritionists, coaches, industry experts, or even brands leveraging third-party certifications, awards, or "as seen in" features. Authority ads are designed to dismantle skepticism and reinforce trust for high-consideration purchases.

**Best For:**

- High-priced items or subscription offers
- Categories with lots of consumer skepticism (personal care, supplements, medical, skincare, beauty devices)
- Audiences in later funnel stages who need reassurance to convert

**Audience Fit:**

- Older demos who lean on expertise and credentials before buying
- Skeptical shoppers who don't just trust peer reviews, but want validation from a credible source
- Buyers who see themselves as rational or research-driven
- Audience nuance: Gen Z is less swayed by "traditional" authority and may prefer micro-experts (e.g. TikTok dermatologists, wellness coaches) vs. white-coat doctors. This would future-proof the guidance — especially when paired with demoing (show/don't tell)

**Psychological Basis:**

- Authority bias (we believe experts more than peers)
- Risk aversion (credibility reduces perceived purchase risk)
- Cognitive ease (shortcuts decisions when someone "qualified" endorses it)

**Best Practices:**

- Pair authority with social proof (reviews, testimonials) to cover both rational and emotional decision making
- Lead with credentials up front (white coat, title card, award logos) before diving into the benefits
- Keep language clear and non-jargon heavy — too clinical loses relatability
- Add compliance review where necessary (medical claims, supplement disclaimers)
- The more "high production" feeling the ad the better to increase perception of credibility, but not always necessary
- Use cues of authority such as:
  - **Titles:** "Founder," "CEO," "Dermatologist," "PhD" — use them clearly on screen or in captions.
  - **Clothes:** Visual signals like lab coats, scrubs, uniforms, or even polished/professional outfits immediately shape perception.
  - **Trappings:** Awards, certifications, book covers, "as seen in" logos, or even subtle markers (office setting, specialized tools).
- Industry association ("ex-PayPal," "ex-Facebook engineer") works as authority by proximity.

**Specific Considerations:**

- Every avatar finds authority around specific products from somebody/thing. Ask: Who? What is that thing?
- It's not just who speaks but what signals surround them. The cues (titles, clothes, trappings, design) can be just as influential as actual expertise.
- Requires sourcing credible talent (doctor, expert, or legit certification), clear disclaimers, and tighter production standards.
- Very effective in categories where expertise matters, but less effective for impulse buys or younger audiences who distrust traditional authority.

**Difficulty Score:** 4
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Example 1: Niche = Home / Product = Blanket / Authority = Interior Designer
- Example 2: Niche = Odor Control / Product = Deodorant / Authority = Sweat Doctor
- A dermatologist explaining how an IPL device works for at-home hair removal, paired with clear visuals of results
- A nutritionist breaking down why a specific fiber blend supports gut health, supported by citations and product demo
- A dentist showing nano-hydroxyapatite toothpaste on a model tooth and explaining why it's safer than fluoride
- Brand using a "clinically proven" or "FDA cleared" tag alongside a doctor VO

### FORMAT 10: EDUCATIONAL

**Description:** An explainer-led ad format that teaches the viewer something useful (simple, visual, "show me" instruction). It breaks down a problem, clarifies why it happens, and demonstrates how the product solves it so the viewer walks away feeling smarter and solution-ready.

**Best For:**

- Supplements, wellness, personal care (requires explanation or myth-busting)
- Digital products/courses/apps (feature education, workflows, "how it works")
- Problem/solution products (cleaning, kitchen gadgets, organizers, tools)
- New-to-market or complex categories (fiber vs. protein macros, IPL, enzyme cleaners)
- Less ideal for purely aesthetic/fashion-first items unless framed as "how to style/fit/care"

**Audience Fit:**

- Research-driven shoppers who want to understand before buying
- Problem-aware but solution-agnostic audiences
- Learners who save/share tips (TikTok/IG "edutainment" natives)
- Works across ages: Gen Z responds to fast-paced edutainment, older demos prefer step-by-step logic

**Psychological Basis:**

- Competence & mastery (people feel good when they learn)
- Reciprocity (free value → willingness to give back/buy)
- Cognitive ease (simple mental models reduce effort & risk)
- Curiosity gap / Zeigarnik effect ("Most people get X wrong…")
- Loss aversion (teaches cost of not changing)
- Self-efficacy (viewer feels capable to act)

**Best Practices:**

- Hook with insight, not hype: "Did you know…", "Most people do X wrong", "3 mistakes keeping you from ___", "This is how"
- Show > tell: tight demos, side-by-sides, captions highlighting key terms
- Keep it plain-spoken (no heavy jargon); use simple visual metaphors (sponges, sieves, etc.)
- One core idea per ad — make sure to twist the knife
- Pair with proof: mini-studies, credible citations, before/afters, UGC results
- CTA that extends the learning: "Save this tip", "Get the checklist", "Try the 3-step routine"
- Frameworks: How-To, 3 Reasons Why, Timeline, Myth vs. Fact, A vs. B comparison, FAQ, Screen Record, Demo/Test
- Copy patterns that pull: "Did you know…", "Here's why ___ works", "I wish I knew this sooner about ___"
- Typical Problem solution copy frameworks can still work when done correctly (especially on older audiences) such as:
  - Problem → Agitate ("twist the knife") → Show Relief → Position Product as Fix
  - PAS (Problem–Agitate–Solution)
  - AIDA (Attention–Interest–Desire–Action)
- Use visual examples as clear indicators (ie. ingredient closeups, texture, etc)
- Consider using word play and props to enhance overall storyline such as sticky notes, white boards, notes app, tablet etc

**Specific Considerations:**

- Doesn't require celebrities, heavy production, or expensive sets, but scripting has to be sharp. Educational ads need research, fact-checking, and clear visuals.
- This format reliably performs for problem/solution products and complex categories, but it's less effective for purely aspirational or aesthetic-first products.
- Fact-check rigor (especially for supplements/medical/finance)
- Compliance: claims/disclaimers/citations required in regulated categories
- Avoid lecture tone; keep it conversational and visually guided
- Match teacher archetype to audience (creator, coach, expert)
- If aspirational product, reframe as "educational adjacent" (styling, care, durability)
- Leverage humor or storytelling ('edutainment') to prevent the ad from feeling like a lecture (best suited for Gen Z audiences)
- Length of ad can matter: Snackable (20–30s) works best for Gen Z/Millennials on social. Longer walkthroughs (60–90s+) often perform better with Gen X/Boomers who want more context and reassurance.

**Difficulty Score:** 4
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Supplements: quick "Fiber vs. protein explained in 30s" with water + powder demo
- Cleaning: enzyme spray vs. bleach side-by-side with timer overlay
- Kitchen gadgets: "How induction works" or "Why cast iron seasoning matters"
- Skincare: "Retinoid vs. bakuchiol" with texture swatches + routine graphic
- Dental: "Nano-hydroxyapatite 101" using a model tooth with callouts
- Digital products: 3-step app setup screen-record in under 90s
- Finance/behavior: "The 2-minute rule to kill procrastination" → app feature demo
- Myth-busting short: "Stop oiling your pans like this. Do this instead."

### FORMAT 17: HOW TO / STEPS

**Description:** An ad format that walks the viewer through a clear, step-by-step process. It frames the product as the tool or method to achieve a desired outcome, making the path to results feel simple and repeatable. Often uses overlays like "Step 1 / Step 2 / Step 3" to visually guide the viewer, paired with quick demos or voiceover explanation.

**Best For:**

- Supplements, skincare, and personal care routines
- Digital products/apps (setup guides, workflows, productivity hacks)
- Kitchen/cleaning gadgets (demoing functionality step by step)
- Fitness/wellness routines (morning routine, "3 steps to reset your gut")
- Finance or education products that need clarity ("how to invest / budget")
- Problem–solution categories where simplicity reduces overwhelm
- Fashion product for styling, ie "How to style xyz like a boss"

**Audience Fit:**

- Learners and process-driven shoppers who want to understand how it works before committing
- Gen Z/Millennials: respond to short, snappy, fast-paced step breakdowns
- Gen X/Boomers: appreciate slower, logical sequences with more context
- Skeptics: seeing the process step-by-step reduces perceived risk
- Perfect for audiences overwhelmed by complexity — the format breaks it down

**Psychological Basis:**

- Competence & mastery: viewers feel more capable after learning steps
- Cognitive fluency: chunking information into steps makes it easier to follow
- Curiosity gap: "What's the next step?" keeps people watching until the end
- Reciprocity: free, valuable instruction builds trust and nudges reciprocity (buying)
- Loss aversion: "Miss a step → lose results" keeps attention high

**Best Practices:**

- Use simple overlays: "Step 1… Step 2… Step 3…" to anchor visuals
- Hook with a bold payoff: "3 steps to stop bloating" → then break it down
- Keep each step short (3–5 seconds max for social) — avoid dragging
- Mix text + VO + demo footage → repetition locks in the steps
- Can run silent: captions should stand alone for accessibility
- Works great as edutainment: add curiosity hooks ("Step 2 is what most people get wrong…")
- Can flex from micro how-to ("How to use this serum in your routine") → to macro how-to ("How to fix your mornings in 3 steps")
- Pair with testimonials: "These 3 steps changed my ____"

**Specific Considerations:**

- Easy to produce, but script/visual clarity is crucial
- Consistently strong format for problem–solution categories
- Doesn't require celebrity or high production; scripting and clarity matter most
- Risk: can feel dry or repetitive if not visually engaging → vary shots + pacing
- Needs fact-checking in regulated spaces (supplements, finance, skincare)
- Works best for products that naturally fit into step-based processes; less relevant for one-off aesthetic products
- Strong cross-pollination potential: cut into comment responses, POVs, or mashups
- CTA must align with steps (the "next logical step" is to buy or sign up)

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Skincare: "3 steps to build a nighttime routine that works" → cleanser demo, serum, moisturizer
- Supplement: "3 steps to reset your gut" → mix powder, drink water, show results
- Kitchen gadget: "How to make frothy coffee in 3 steps" → pour, blend, sip
- App: "How to set up your budget in 90 seconds" → screen record + captions
- Cleaning: "How to remove stains in 2 easy steps" → spray + wipe live demo
- Fitness: "My 5-step morning reset" → stretches, hydration, supplement, journaling, product tie-in

### FORMAT 18: HUMOROUS

**Description:** An ad format that hooks attention with comedy, exaggeration, parody, or satire. The goal is to entertain and connect the laugh to the product benefit so the memory sticks. Humor works because it lowers ad resistance and feels like content, not a pitch, but it's one of the trickiest formats to execute well.

**Best For:**

- Commodity or everyday products that need differentiation (razors, deodorant, cleaning, gadgets)
- Pet products (natural fit, animals + humor = instant relatability)
- Broad lifestyle categories where relatability drives shareability
- Brands comfortable leaning into meme culture and playfulness
- Awareness campaigns where virality and mass reach matter most

**Audience Fit:**

- Gen Z & Millennials: humor is a primary content language (memes, absurdity, satire)
- Broad audiences: "funny is universal" when grounded in shared experiences (pets, relationships, everyday fails)
- Skeptics: comedy disarms resistance and makes ads feel less "salesy"
- Less effective for luxury or heavily regulated categories (finance, medical, premium wellness), unless used sparingly or as parody

**Psychological Basis:**

- Humor effect: we remember funny things longer than neutral ones
- Pattern interrupt: unexpected humor breaks scrolling autopilot
- Social currency: funny ads are shared, memed, and stitched → boosts reach
- Benign violation theory: humor works when it pushes boundaries without truly offending
- Relatability bias: humor mirrors shared struggles → makes audience nod + laugh
- Meme fluency: when humor echoes meme formats, it feels instantly native

**Best Practices:**

- Find golden headlines: witty, surprising, scroll-stopping openers

- Works well inside other frameworks: Comment Response, Skits, Before/After
- Relatable > clever: focus on shared human moments, not insider jokes
- Pet + humor = high hit rate (pets "using" products, pet POVs, etc.)

  - Skits
  - Meme integrations
  - Deadpan exaggerations
  - Parody of "serious" ads
  - Reference cultural humor playbooks (like *Humor, Seriously*) for structure

**Specific Considerations:**

- Big wins possible, but volatile performance
- Hard to pull off — needs a genuinely funny creator or sharp copywriting
- Humor ages fast → test and iterate quickly, memes expire within weeks
- Risks: can alienate if offensive, confusing, or too forced
- Wide audience potential, but the humor must match niche culture (Gen Z irony ≠ Boomer slapstick)
- Better for TOF awareness; follow up with proof-driven formats in retargeting
- Great iterative angle: parody your own top-performing serious ads for humor contrast

**Difficulty Score:** 2
**Confidence Score:** 3

- Native deodorant parody spots (awkward everyday scenarios turned funny)
- Pet niche: dogs "reviewing" products or funny owner-pet skits

- Skit parody: mock serious testimonial turned comedic

### FORMAT 19: MASHUPS

**Description:** An ad format that combines multiple clips, voices, or proof points into one high-energy, quick-cut video. Think highlight reels of UGC, testimonials, reactions, press mentions, or demos — all stacked together. Mashups overwhelm skepticism with volume of proof and entertain through speed, variety, and constant pattern interrupts. They mimic the feel of word-of-mouth at scale.

**Best For:**

- Giftable products (holiday, seasonal, birthdays — gifting mashups crush in Q4)
- Food & beverage (taste tests, flavor reactions, meal hacks)
- Beauty & skincare (UGC demos, expert snippets, before/afters)
- Men's care, healthcare, wellness (stack reactions, reviews, proof-driven angles)
- E-commerce brands with a large library of UGC or press features
- Highly reactionary products (anything that makes people go "wow" instantly)
- Brands looking to test multiple messaging angles at scale

**Audience Fit:**

- Broad — mashups work across demos and platforms (TikTok, IG, Meta, YouTube)
- Skeptical buyers: need to see proof from many voices
- Gen Z & Millennials: love the fast-paced, social-first edit style
- Gen X/Boomers: respond when clips highlight authority and testimonials clearly
- Retargeting audiences: perfect to hammer home "everyone's talking about it"

**Psychological Basis:**

- Social proof stacking: multiple voices = credibility amplifier
- Word-of-mouth mimicry: feels like a crowd recommending something at once
- Bandwagon effect: "If all these people are in, I should be too"
- Pattern interrupt: quick cuts keep attention locked
- Information density: floods viewer with variety, leaves fewer objections
- Curiosity & reward loop: keeps people watching to see the next reaction/clip

**Best Practices:**

- Start strong with biggest reaction, press headline, or bold claim → hook instantly
- Keep clips short (2–5 seconds max) → rapid pace is key
- Stack multiple proof types: testimonials + reviews + demo + reaction + press mention
- Overlay text to anchor context: "Real Review," "Press Feature," "Unboxing"
- Sync edits to music/tempo → beat drops and transitions build energy
- Collect lots of good soundbites → you need variety in tone and proof
- Amazing for gifting campaigns: show multiple recipients reacting in one reel
- CTA should feel like the natural "next step" after the flood of proof (e.g., "Join 10,000+ happy customers")
- Great for creative refreshes: recycle old content into new mashups
- Can flex by theme → gifting mashup, skeptic-proof mashup, seasonal mashup, food-reaction mashup

**Specific Considerations:**

- Edit-heavy but simple if you have clips
- Proven format, especially with strong content pool
- Requires existing content pool — harder for new brands with no UGC or press
- Editing load is high; pacing and sequencing make or break the format
- Works particularly well in Q4 when gifting and social proof matter most
- Can overwhelm if not anchored — make sure product/message clarity doesn't get lost
- Works best for highly visual/tactile categories (harder for abstract services like finance unless paired with strong reviews/press)
- Easy to execute once you do have lots of footage; becomes a plug-and-play evergreen asset

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Food: montage of people taste-testing licorice.com → "best licorice I've ever had" reactions stacked fast
- Beauty: skincare mashup showing UGC routines + dermatologist quote + Amazon reviews
- Gifting: holiday montage of unboxings, Oprah-style "favorite things," and family reactions
- Health/wellness: testimonials stacked with proof overlays + quick expert soundbite
- Press: "As seen in Forbes, Vogue, Buzzfeed…" spliced with creators reacting
- Reaction mashup: TikTok creators all stitching the same product → compiled into one

### FORMAT 21: NEGATIVE ANGLES

**Description:** An ad format that spotlights the pain points, frustrations, or worst parts of the customer's current reality or competing products. Often light, humorous, and cheeky, using exaggeration or irony as a pattern interrupt to stand out. The product is framed as the relief, solution, or better alternative. Works especially well when the negative moment is highly relatable or frustrating.

**Best For:**

- Skincare & grooming (razor burn, acne, bumps, frizz)
- Cleaning & kitchen gadgets (messy, gross problems)
- Finance apps (wasted subscriptions, bad spending habits)
- Health & wellness (failed diets, bloat, gym frustrations)
- Consumer tech (chargers breaking, clunky tools)
- Products with clear competitor weaknesses (painful waxing vs. IPL)
- Cheeky lifestyle products (fashion, beauty, accessories)

**Audience Fit:**

- Skeptical or jaded buyers who've "tried everything"
- Problem-aware but hesitant customers needing a push
- Younger audiences who appreciate humor, memes, cheeky honesty
- Social-first buyers used to sarcasm & playful commentary
- Not ideal for aspirational luxury buyers (tone mismatch)

**Psychological Basis:**

- Loss Aversion: avoiding pain > seeking pleasure
- Pattern Interrupt: negativity cuts through constant positive ads
- Relatability: viewers bond over shared frustrations ("omg same!")
- Contrast Bias: "look how bad this is vs. how easy this is now"
- Curiosity Gap: "The one thing I don't like about this is…" keeps people watching

**Best Practices:**

- Start with the frustration/problem first for max attention
- Keep tone playful, cheeky, or ironic (not cruel or shaming)
- Pivot quickly to solution — don't linger too long on negativity
- Usually the hook is shocking, polarizing and/or stops the scroll
- Use humor as the release valve (dramatic exaggeration, sarcasm, skits)
- Hooks that work: "Don't buy this if…" / "3 reasons I love it and 1 reason I don't" / "My regrets after buying…"
- Pair with text overlays like NOPE / FAIL / NEVER AGAIN for added punch
- Use sparingly; overuse can make brand feel snarky or cynical

**Specific Considerations:**

- Balance and tone-sensitive — easy to overdo
- High performance when executed playfully & with clear solution
- Must align with brand voice (playful vs. serious brands)
- Use sparingly as a "spice," not a core brand strategy
- Avoid sensitive categories (weight, health scares, trauma triggers)
- Not a fit for luxury or prestige positioning (tone clash)
- Works best when the "negative" is either humorous or quickly solved
- Can backfire if it feels too harsh, judgmental, or mocking the customer

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 2

**Examples:**

- Cheeky complaint: "3 reasons I love my razor and 1 reason I don't" (negative = "I can't pick a color, they're all too cute!")
- Exclusion hook: "Don't buy this if you like wasting money on razors that cut you…"
- Regret spin: "My top regrets after buying this foundation" → flips into "actually zero regrets because it works so well."
- Problem exaggeration: gross clogged pores → smooth clear skin with product.
- Competitor slam: "Stop wasting money on subscriptions you don't use."
- Humorous skit: dramatic meltdown over shaving bumps → cut to easy solution.

### FORMAT 22: NON-NARRATED

**Description:** Ads that rely entirely on visuals, text overlays, music, and sound design — with no spoken narration or talking head. They can be structured as quick b-roll storylines with dynamic edits or as text-driven narratives (20–40 seconds) that feel native to the platform. Underutilized but proven to scale, this format is perfect for brands that want versatility across languages, audiences, and placements.

**Best For:**

- Beauty/skincare (before-afters, texture shots)
- Food/beverage (POV recipes, ASMR prep)
- Cleaning/home gadgets (satisfying demos)
- Fashion and Accessories (outfit swaps, styling)
- Tech (unboxings, usage demos)
- Gifting moments (unwrapping, reactions)

**Audience Fit:**

- Scrollers with sound off (TikTok/IG/FB default)
- Global buyers (no language barriers)
- Younger audiences familiar with text overlays & memes
- Visual-driven audiences who prefer quick inspiration
- TOF and mid-funnel viewers who need attention-grabbing, universal content

**Psychological Basis:**

- Dual Coding: combining text + visuals strengthens retention
- Pattern Interrupt: silent or text-only feels different, scroll-stopping
- Mirror Neurons: POVs + b-roll trigger imitation urges
- Cognitive Ease: text simplifies complex info fast
- Autonomy Bias: letting viewers interpret with minimal "selling" feels authentic

**Best Practices:**

- Lead with dynamic, visual hook (mess → clean, "Day 1 → Day 30")
- Use text overlays as narration: keep short, bold, timed with cuts
- Pair with music, trending audio, ASMR, or sound effects for rhythm
- Make sure story holds with no audio — test sound-off

- End with text CTA since no voice pushes action
- Great for repurposing narrated ads into silent edits

**Specific Considerations:**

- Rely heavily on strong b-roll and editing craft
- Can feel flat if visuals aren't satisfying or dynamic
- Too much text → feels like a wall of reading (keep punchy)
- Harder for nuance compared to voice-over
- Needs strong creative direction to avoid generic "stock" feel
- Easy to execute, editing-heavy but talent-light
- Scales consistently, underutilized format = opportunity
- Also great paired with ASMR

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Skincare: "Day 1 → Day 30" overlay on b-roll routine, no VO
- Food: chopping, sizzling, pouring → captions guide recipe
- Fashion: outfit swaps with beat drops + overlay: Monday Fit / Tuesday Fit

### FORMAT 24: SHORT FORM

**Description:** Bite-sized ads that run 6–20 seconds (sometimes even under 10) and get straight to the point. They're built for fast consumption and thrive on platforms where attention spans are short and viewers scroll quickly (TikTok, IG Reels, YouTube Shorts, Meta in-feed). Success depends on a clear hook, one sharp message, and a punchy CTA — no fluff. Short Form ads work especially well as retargeting assets or as top-of-funnel "pattern interrupts" to stop scrolls.

**Best For:**

- Beauty/skincare (quick before–after demos)
- Food/beverage (recipe "in one step" cuts, taste reactions)
- Fashion (outfit reveals, try-ons)
- Gadgets & home products (satisfying "one-problem-one-solution" demos)
- Subscriptions/apps (fast feature highlight)
- Retargeting campaigns with a single clear objection to overcome (ie long paragraph overlayed with b-roll etc)
- Sale ads and needing to get the point across quickly

**Audience Fit:**

- Gen Z & Millennials who are native to short-form feeds
- Time-poor scrollers who don't want to sit through 30+ seconds
- Lower-funnel audiences who just need a nudge to convert
- Impulse buyers driven by quick entertainment + utility

**Psychological Basis:**

- Attention Economy: fast, snappy content matches shrinking attention spans
- Cognitive Fluency: one idea is easier to process & remember
- Novelty Bias: unexpected hooks drive watch-through
- Chunking: compressing info into bite-sized pieces increases retention
- Zeigarnik Effect: unfinished "hint" or cliffhanger nudges clicks

**Best Practices:**

- Open with a scroll-stopping visual or bold statement in the first 2–3 seconds
- Focus on ONE message — don't overpack
- End with a clear CTA: "Shop now," "Link in bio," or visual product card
- Use jump cuts, tight edits, captions to reinforce speed
- Works great when cut from longer UGC/narrated content
- Test multiple short hooks as standalone ads (high modularity)
- Perfect to slot into retargeting sequences

**Specific Considerations:**

- Easy to execute at a technical level, but tricky to master. Requires precision: no wasted seconds, sharp hooks, and smart editing. The biggest challenge is distilling a product's value into one punchy line or visual without losing impact.
- Short form is consistently one of the top-performing ad styles across TikTok, IG Reels, Meta, and Shorts. Extremely reliable for TOF and retargeting, and almost always worth including in creative testing.
- Risk of feeling too shallow if no value delivered
- Easy to confuse "short" with "lazy" — needs intentional scripting & editing
- Creative fatigue sets in quickly, must refresh often
- Less room for storytelling; better as part of a multi-format strategy
- Works best when paired with longer ads in the funnel

**Difficulty Score:** 1
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Skincare: 7-sec before → after demo with text overlay "Clear skin fast"
- Food/bev: POV sip → instant reaction face with overlay "Tastes like dessert"
- Fashion: Outfit transition with sound beat drop
- Cleaning gadget: Mess → clean in 5 seconds
- "I'm sorry to anyone who bought this item at full price..."
- Subscription app: "One feature you didn't know you needed" in under 10 sec
- Fitness: Quick "3-sec challenge" CTA

### FORMAT 25: STREET INTERVIEWS

**Description:** Ads filmed in a "man-on-the-street" style, where strangers (or staged participants who feel like strangers) are asked questions about a product, problem, or category. The rawness of authentic answers makes them feel like real word-of-mouth in action. Almost any product can work in this format, but it's especially strong for demos, taste tests, and educational angles. When executed well, street interviews create highly reactionary content that can be stitched together into compelling mashups.

**Best For:**

- Taste tests (food, beverage, supplements)
- Skincare & beauty (skin frustrations → solution demo)
- Tech & apps (explaining or demoing product)
- Educational categories (finance, wellness, healthcare tools)
- Apparel/accessories ("Would you wear this?")
- Almost any product that benefits from peer validation

**Audience Fit:**

- Great for all audiences — everyone relates to seeing real people
- Skeptical or research-driven buyers (trust real opinions)
- Viewers curious about others' choices & preferences
- Community-driven audiences influenced by peer word-of-mouth

**Psychological Basis:**

- Social Proof: seeing others validate = safer choice for viewer
- Word of Mouth Mimicry: feels like organic peer recommendations
- Curiosity Gap: "What will they say?" keeps viewers hooked
- Relatability: mirrors the viewer's potential reaction/experience
- Authority via Consensus: multiple voices layered together = credibility
- Educational Framing: interview Q&A can simplify complex products

**Best Practices:**

- Keep questions short, clear, and curiosity-driven
- Capture multiple participants for variety and montage potential
- Focus on reactions first (smiles, surprise, shock, laughter, golden nugget soundbites)
- Strong openers: "We asked real people what they think about ___" / "If you have a sweaty hooha keep watching"
- Great for mashup editing — combine many short answers into one high-energy piece
- Use captions (streets = noisy)
- Let product appear naturally after Q, demo, or taste test
- General framework: shock hook → reactions (3-4) → cut to host product offer info → 2-3 more reactions → host strong CTA

**Specific Considerations:**

- Logistically one of the hardest formats to execute. Requires charismatic interviewer, production team, great editing, and often permissions. High risk if participants freeze or reactions fall flat.
- When executed correctly, and the reactions are REAL, street interviews are extremely effective. They deliver authenticity, social proof, and reaction-driven entertainment that plays well across audiences. Not as scalable as UGC, but standout assets in a creative mix.
- Not ideal for luxury brands (tone mismatch)
- Can flop if answers feel flat — needs an engaging interviewer to keep energy high
- Works best when product naturally drives strong reactions

**Difficulty Score:** 5
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Street interviews: ask about the problem, then demo the product
- Taste tests: blindfold sip/eat → genuine reactions
- Apps & tech: "Guess what this app does?" or "How much would you pay for this?"

- Mashups: stitched clips of 10+ strangers answering the same Q

### FORMAT 26: UGC (USER-GENERATED CONTENT)

**Description:** User-Generated Content ads are the original form of word-of-mouth marketing — told directly from the product user's own lived experiences. They are the most common video ad format, and the backbone of paid social. UGC thrives on three pillars: Authenticity (real people, real scenarios, lo-fi look), Relatability (audience sees themselves in the story, lifestyle, and challenges), and Trust (peer-to-peer recommendations > brand claims). Shot lo-fi, often on phones, UGC feels like something you'd see from a friend rather than a brand. Works across all funnel stages — from scroll-stopping TOF to retargeting and post-purchase reinforcement.

**Best For:**

- Beauty/skincare (testimonials, routines, before/afters)
- Food/beverage (recipes, taste tests, POV prep)
- Fashion/accessories (hauls, GRWM, styling inspo)
- Fitness/wellness (try-ons, daily routines)
- Tech & apps (simple demos, "watch this work")
- Giftable products (blankets, jewelry, gadgets)
- B2B adaptations (employees/managers showing tools, lo-fi case studies)

**Audience Fit:**

- Gen Z & Millennials conditioned by TikTok/IG UGC style
- Skeptical buyers who trust peers over polished brand messaging
- TOF audiences resistant to overt ads
- MOF audiences needing validation/education
- B2B buyers when the "user" aligns with buyer avatar
- Global audiences (native aesthetic transcends culture)
- Almost every audience

**Psychological Basis:**

- System 1 Processing: lo-fi look bypasses "ad" filters
- Narrative Transportation: stories hold attention longer than claims
- Social Proof & Bandwagon Effect: "people like me use this"
- Parasocial Trust: creators feel friend-like to viewers
- Identity-Based Motivation: people buy from avatars they aspire to or identify with
- FOMO & Cultural Fluency: seasonal/gifting cycles amplify urgency

**Best Practices:**

- Shoot vertically (9:16), lo-fi, natural light
- Direct-to-camera delivery in conversational tone
- Mix in lifestyle b-roll + text overlays
- Test multiple hook variations per script
- Encourage quirks, pauses, laughter → imperfection = authenticity
- Use subtitles (sound-off is common)
- Apply persuasion frameworks (PAS, AIDA, BAB, STAR, etc.) to give narrative arc
- Deploy across funnel: TOF (scroll-stop), MOF (education/testimonials), BOF (objection handling)
- Strong hooks and storytelling needed in order to cut through the noise as it's become a very common format
- Use new talent faces with previous UGC winning scripts
- Mirror audience avatars; use personable talent, aspirational talent according to main point of ad and audience fit
- Can also shoot "ugly ads story"
- Doesn't FEEL like an ad — make it like you're inviting someone into your life like a sneak peek
- Overcome the biggest barriers that people don't like about the product

**Specific Considerations:**

- Production is simple, but execution is nuanced. The challenge lies in nailing authenticity and creator selection. Bad acting = ad death.
- UGC is the most versatile, consistently performing format in paid social. It adapts across categories, funnel stages, and even into B2B, restaurants — you name it. Its authenticity edge only grows stronger in an AI-saturated landscape.
- Easy to shoot technically, but hard to scale authentically — many creators sound forced if not guided
- Saturation risk: so much UGC in feeds can blend together — users have caught onto UGC
- Strong performance relies on scripting, editing, and authentic talent that is specifically selected
- Luxury brands may need more polish to maintain positioning
- Requires ongoing refresh to avoid fatigue

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 2

**Examples:**

- Testimonial: "I've tried everything for bumps, but this worked in 3 weeks."
- Unboxing: natural excitement while opening package
- POV routine: "POV: you found the world's first glass bottle mixer and you're never going back"
- Taste test: sip → raw reaction
- Skit: funny problem dramatization → solution reveal
- Before/After: old way vs. new way transformation
- App demo: screen-record + facecam explaining value
- Comment response: "Does this actually work?" → creator reply

### FORMAT 27: US VS. THEM

**Description:** A direct comparison-style ad that positions your product ("us") against an alternative ("them"). The "them" could be competitors, the old way of doing things, or bad habits/DIY solutions. The goal is to highlight your unique value while dramatizing the flaws or downsides of the alternative. Works especially well in commodity categories or where consumers already feel frustrated with the status quo.

**Best For:**

- Skincare & beauty (razors vs. IPL, store brands vs. premium)
- Cleaning & home (old messy solutions vs. streamlined product)
- Tech & apps (manual process vs. automated solution)
- Food/bev (sugary drinks vs. healthy swaps)
- Wellness/fitness (yo-yo dieting vs. sustainable approach)
- Finance (bank fees vs. free app)

**Audience Fit:**

- Skeptical buyers needing reassurance
- Audiences in saturated markets who want clarity
- Problem-aware buyers frustrated with current solutions
- Budget-conscious shoppers looking for better value

**Psychological Basis:**

- Contrast Bias: differences feel bigger when directly compared
- Loss Aversion: "stick with them = keep losing"
- Cognitive Ease: clear side-by-side = simple decision
- Tribal Psychology: creates an "in-group" (us) vs. "out-group" (them)
- Social Proof: aligning with the smarter, newer option

**Best Practices:**

- Clearly define "them": competitor, old way, or bad habit
- Keep comparisons simple: 2–3 key points, not a laundry list
- Visuals matter: split screens, checkmarks, Xs, before/after
- Use cheeky tone to avoid coming across hostile
- End with clear CTA: "Make the switch" / "Join us"
- Great for retargeting buyers considering alternatives

**Specific Considerations:**

- Relatively simple to script and produce, but requires nuance to avoid being too aggressive or misleading. Strong creative direction needed to keep tone cheeky/playful.
- Consistently effective in problem/solution categories, especially when "them" is a well-known frustration. Reliable mid- to bottom-funnel driver.
- Risk of alienating if "them" feels like direct attack (esp. named competitors)
- Not ideal for luxury positioning as it can cheapen brand image
- Must fact-check carefully in regulated industries (skincare, finance, health)
- Overuse can feel combative; works best as part of a mix

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 2

**Examples:**

- Skincare: IPL vs. razors/waxing → "Them: painful, temporary. Us: smooth, lasting."
- Food/bev: Soda vs. functional drink → sugar bomb vs. clean energy
- Tech: Spreadsheets vs. app → messy manual vs. streamlined dashboard
- Cleaning: "Old sponge (gross) vs. new tool (fast, easy)"
- Finance: "Paying $12/month in fees vs. free with us"
- Wellness: "Dieting misery vs. daily sustainable shakes"

### FORMAT 28: TIMELINE AD

**Description:** Ads structured around the progression of time, guiding the viewer through a journey (Day 1 → Day 30, Before → After, etc.). They're excellent for breaking down skepticism by showing consistent, believable progress. Timeline ads work especially well for longer-term products that don't have an instant payoff (health, wellness, skincare), and they shine when paired with a narrative VO guiding the transformation.

**Best For:**

- Skincare & beauty (acne, IPL, anti-aging routines)
- Health & wellness (supplements, shakes, weight loss, sleep aids)
- Food & beverage (daily swaps, gut resets)
- Fitness (strength gains, endurance, habit building)
- Cleaning (long-term results condensed)
- Tech/apps (habit trackers, productivity tools)
- Products that don't show an immediate payoff

**Audience Fit:**

- Skeptical buyers who need proof over time
- Transformation-driven buyers (wellness, beauty, lifestyle)
- Narratively engaged viewers who enjoy "mini stories"
- Buyers hesitant about commitment — timeline provides reassurance

**Psychological Basis:**

- Zeigarnik Effect: unfinished "Day 1 → ? or what happens?" keeps people watching
- Contrast Bias: visible change over time is highly persuasive
- Narrative Transportation: timeline acts as a story arc viewers follow
- Proof Heuristic: progress over weeks feels more credible than instant claims
- Guided Learning: VO narration builds trust and comprehension

**Best Practices:**

- Use clear timeline markers (Day 1, Week 1, Day 30, "After 10 days she'll feel more _____")
- Pair with narrative VO to guide viewers through progression
- Keep pacing tight (don't linger too long on one stage)
- Use consistent visuals for credibility (same subject, angle, setting)
- End with strong reveal + CTA: "Start your Day 1 today"
- Great to mash multiple user timelines into one high-energy edit
- Show either ideal avatar or opposite for transformation

**Specific Considerations:**

- Hard to execute authentically due to time commitment, planning, or believability if staged. Requires VO + editing finesse to hold viewer until payoff.
- Extremely persuasive when done right. Proven across DTC categories (skincare, supplements, fitness). Breaks skepticism and builds long-term trust.
- Logistically demanding (requires filming over weeks/months OR staged believably)
- Needs careful handling in regulated spaces (skincare, wellness, supplements — disclaimers often required and have to be careful about what you say)
- Best when product delivers visible transformation; weaker for subtle results
- Overstaging risks credibility loss if audience senses "fake progress"

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- "This is what happens when a hairy mother uses IPL technology for 30 days."
- Supergut: "This is what happens when a mindless muncher switches to Supergut for 30 days."
- "This is Hannah. Hannah never sleeps…" → guided transformation arc.
- Skincare: Day 1 → Day 30 acne clearing progression.
- Cleaning: 10 years of grime removed in 30 seconds.
- Fitness: Week 1 vs. Week 8 montage.

### FORMAT 29: VSL (VIDEO SALES LETTER) ADS

**Description:** Long-form ads (≈60s → 5+ min) built to educate, persuade, and close. They follow classic copy frameworks (PAS, AIDA, PASTOR, STAR, etc.) and systematically move viewers from problem → agitation → solution → proof → offer → CTA. VSLs are intentionally educational, objection-crushing, and highly narrative, often pairing story with data, demos, and testimonials.

**Best For:**

- High-ticket and complex products (devices, courses, premium skincare/beauty devices)
- Supplements/health/wellness (mechanisms + habit change)
- Finance/education/SaaS (requires walkthroughs + proof)
- Categories where more information is essential to convert

**Audience Fit:**

- Older audiences resonate with this format (patience for detail, prefer clarity and proof)
- Sophisticated buyers who want depth and answers to every objection
- Skeptical/high-consideration buyers (costly, novel, or complex products)
- Warm audiences (MOF/BOF), multi-step sales processes

**Psychological Basis:**

- Narrative Transportation: story keeps attention across length
- Authority & Proof: stacking data, demos, experts, testimonials
- Commitment/Consistency: time invested → more likely to finish/convert
- Loss Aversion: framed stakes for inaction
- Curiosity Gap: chaptered reveals maintain momentum

**Best Practices:**

- Principle: There's no such thing as too long, only too boring. Keep it compelling.
- Use battle-tested frameworks (PAS/AIDA/PASTOR/STAR).
- Pair story + proof (founder/creator narrative with demos, data, reviews).
- Answer every objection inside the narrative (price, time, safety, results, comparisons).
- Build for atomization: film modular segments for easy cutdowns (6s/10s/30s/1min/2min/3min/5min).
- End with a direct CTA and offer logic (value stack, guarantee, scarcity if applicable).
- Using the first 1/3 of the video to significantly "twist the knife" to show payoff/transformation can be helpful
- Strong storytelling is imperative

**Specific Considerations:**

- Takes serious planning, scripting, and editing to avoid boredom and cover all objections. Hard to pull off, easy to get wrong.
- When executed well, among the highest-converting formats — ideal for complex, high-consideration purchases and multi-step funnels.
- Very resource-intensive: heavy planning, scripting, compliance, and editing.
- Risk of drop-off if pacing drags — use hooks, chapter cards, pattern interrupts.
- Must meet legal standards in regulated niches (claims, disclaimers).
- Works best warm — use shorter hooks/ads to earn the click into the VSL.
- Ensure accessibility (captions, on-screen summaries).

**Difficulty Score:** 4
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- 3–5 min beauty device VSL: PAS intro → derm-backed proof → user testimonials → risk-reversal offer.
- Supplement VSL: "Here's what happens when you take ___ daily" (mechanism + routine + results + FAQs).
- SaaS mini-doc: founder story + workflow demo + case studies + offer.
- "Real reason why women over 50 are ditching ___ for ___" — long-form editorial with data + social proof.

### FORMAT 33: ASMR

**Description:** Ads leveraging Autonomous Sensory Meridian Response triggers — whispering voices, tapping, pouring, sizzling, crinkling — to create a satisfying sensory experience. Product becomes part of the ASMR environment, making it soothing, novel, and scroll-stopping.

**Best For:**

- Food/bev (pouring, crunching, sizzling)
- Skincare/beauty (cream spreading, brush sounds)
- Cleaning/home (sprays, wipes, scrubbing sounds)
- Lifestyle goods with tactile elements
- Subscription box unboxings

**Audience Fit:**

- Gen Z & Millennials (ASMR-native audiences)
- Global reach (sound > language)
- Sensory-driven buyers who enjoy "oddly satisfying" content
- Curious scrollers seeking novelty

**Psychological Basis:**

- Sensory Persuasion: sounds trigger pleasure response
- Novelty Bias: unusual → scroll-stopping
- Embodied Simulation: viewers imagine feeling/tasting product
- Relaxation Effect: ASMR lowers defenses → builds affinity

**Best Practices:**

- Record high-quality sound (mic > phone mic)
- Pair with slow, deliberate visuals
- Keep VO minimal → let sounds dominate
- Works well short-form (10–30s loops)
- Test with trending ASMR audios on TikTok

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 4

**Examples:**

- Coffee brand: pouring over ice, fizzing, slurps.
- Skincare: cream spreading + brush tapping.
- Cleaning: spray + scrub in close-up with squeak.
- Fab Fit Fun ASMR studio unboxing.

### FORMAT 34: SKEPTIC TEST

**Description:** Ads framed around skepticism → proof. A creator, customer, or influencer begins doubtful: "I didn't believe this would work, but…" The ad walks through their skepticism, testing, and eventual conversion. Turns doubt into trust.

**Best For:**

- Skincare/beauty (novel devices, new ingredients)
- Wellness/supplements (too-good-to-be-true claims)
- Tech/gadgets (unfamiliar functions)
- Fitness (new workout tools, shakes)
- Finance/apps (free or "too simple" tools)

**Audience Fit:**

- Skeptical buyers (costly or unfamiliar categories)
- Older buyers resistant to fads
- Shoppers burned by past experiences
- Mid-funnel audiences already aware but hesitant

**Psychological Basis:**

- Confirmation Bias Flip: validates viewer skepticism → resolves it
- Narrative Transportation: story arc from doubt → proof
- Social Proof: "if they were skeptical and converted, so can I"
- Authority by Admission: honesty builds trust

**Best Practices:**

- Start with authentic skepticism ("I thought this was a scam…")
- Show real testing/demo on camera
- End with surprise + conversion ("…but wow, it works")
- Use text overlays to reinforce doubt → resolution arc
- Great for longer ads or spliced into testimonials
- When paired with shock hooks can be very powerful
- Pair with comment response, negative hooks
- Include more than one person as a variation (power of multiples)
- Can also pair with "authority figures" to help overcome objections

**Specific Considerations:**

- Scripting is simple, but performance demands authentic talent.
- Extremely effective for high-skeptic categories; proven testimonial-style winner.
- Needs authentic talent — fake skepticism feels staged
- Can backfire if product fails to impress
- Overuse cheapens trust if every ad = "I didn't believe it"

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 4

**Examples:**

- "I thought this was just another diet shake… until I tried it."
- "Honestly, I thought this device was way overpriced, but after 30 days…"
- Side-by-side: skeptic trying product → "shocked" reaction.

### FORMAT 35: TESTIMONIAL / FIRST-PERSON STORY

**Description:** A classic format where a real customer or creator shares their personal story, often starting with the problem and moving to solution + results. Builds deep trust because it feels like word-of-mouth.

**Best For:**

- Any product needing credibility (skincare, wellness, finance, tech, cleaning)
- Giftable categories (anchoring emotional stories)
- Services & education (case studies, success stories)
- Truly any product or service

**Audience Fit:**

- Peer-led, community-driven audiences
- Skeptical buyers who need relatable voices
- Emotional buyers connecting with human stories
- Normal, relatable everyday people

**Psychological Basis:**

- Narrative Transportation: stories hold attention longer
- Parasocial Trust: people trust peer storytelling
- Identity Reinforcement: buyers imagine themselves in testimonial

**Best Practices:**

- Keep story structure simple: Problem → Struggle → Solution → Results
- Capture natural delivery, not over-scripted
- Mix direct-to-camera with lifestyle b-roll
- Use authentic details ("my dog always stole my blanket…")
- Add on-screen text highlights for key claims
- Can be really good for gifting reactions

**Specific Considerations:**

- Risk of generic testimonials ("it's amazing!" = weak)
- Need variety — multiple testimonials boost credibility (very hard to find one-off testimonial winners if the talent doesn't land and script is not strong enough)
- Easy to film — authenticity is the main challenge.
- Reliable across categories but not always scroll-stopping

**Difficulty Score:** 2
**Confidence Score:** 2

**Examples:**

- Skincare: "After 3 weeks my skin cleared up…"
- Blanket brand: "I bought this for my mom, now she won't take it off."
- Finance app: "I canceled $200 worth of subscriptions in minutes."

### FORMAT 36: UNSCRIPTED FREESTYLE (CREATOR-LED) "UGLY AD"

**Description:** Ads where a creator riffs without a script — raw, chaotic, and authentic. Sometimes messy, often funny. These are called "ugly ads" because they break polished ad norms, but the imperfection makes them feel real and viral-ready.

**Best For:**

- Relatable/lifestyle categories (food, wellness, skincare, home gadgets)
- Entertainment-first brands (youth-targeted)
- Viral testing formats
- Taste tests and reactions
- Gifting and seasonality

**Audience Fit:**

- Younger demos (Gen Z/TikTok-native)
- Audiences skeptical of "perfect" ads
- Viewers who value authenticity over polish
- Humans are wired for this kind of content

**Psychological Basis:**

- Parasocial Trust: unscripted = genuine
- Spontaneity Bias: chaotic = unpredictable & entertaining
- Pattern Interrupt: ugly style cuts through ad fatigue

**Best Practices:**

- Pick strong personality-driven creators
- Give loose talking points, not scripts
- Let quirks, mistakes, and rants roll
- Lean into lo-fi, handheld filming
- Use quick edits to harness chaos into digestible flow

**Specific Considerations:**

- Not always ideal for premium/luxury positioning
- Can flop if creator lacks charisma or strong storytelling abilities
- Difficult to scale, reliant on talent personality
- Easy to film, but hard to find talent who can freestyle well.
- Can produce breakout virals but also prone to misses.

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 3

**Examples:**

- Barry Hott ugly ads
- Blunt comedy-register product ads
- Creator ranting in car: "Y'all won't believe what this blanket does."
- Chaotic kitchen demo with messy spills but clear product payoff.
- Unfiltered skincare morning routine.
- Walking and talking about product.

### FORMAT 37: FACELESS "UGLY ADS"

**Description:** Ads shot without showing faces — often hands, POVs, text overlays, or product-only storytelling. They feel raw, quick, and intentionally unpolished ("ugly"), which makes them blend into feeds and look like organic lo-fi content.

**Best For:**

- Skincare/beauty (POV routines, hands applying product)
- Food/bev (POV prep, pouring, tasting w/out face)
- Cleaning gadgets (hands demoing mess → clean)
- Tech (POV using device/app)
- Really any industry

**Audience Fit:**

- Audiences skeptical of influencer "acting"
- Shy creators/brands wanting scalable faceless content
- Global reach (no face = no cultural mismatch)
- Works across every audience

**Psychological Basis:**

- Pattern Interrupt: faceless = unusual in ad world
- Authenticity Heuristic: lo-fi + no face feels less staged
- Mirror Effect: POV invites viewer to imagine themselves in shot

**Best Practices:**

- Focus on hands, POVs, product close-ups, moving the camera naturally as if you were FaceTiming a friend
- Use captions + text overlays to carry message
- Keep shots fast-paced; avoid lingering too long on product
- Great for cutdowns/shorts
- One takes can be extremely powerful when the storytelling is done correctly
- Can sometimes make the user feel a bit "dumb" for doing things the old way when you could do it the new way
- Lo-fi camera movements (think something you'd see on TikTok shop)

**Specific Considerations:**

- Can feel generic if visuals and storytelling not unique
- Harder to build emotional connection w/out face
- Must rely more on editing, captions, and product action
- Easy to produce technically — creative challenge is making faceless interesting.
- Reliable filler format — rarely top performer but strong in a diversified mix… it can happen though.

**Difficulty Score:** 3
**Confidence Score:** 4

**Examples:**

- Skincare: POV hands applying serum with captions "Day 1 → Day 30."
- Food/bev: faceless POV recipe video.
- Cleaning: messy sponge → spotless sink hands-only demo.
- Tech: POV tapping app on phone, overlay "2 clicks & done."

## The Most Important Things To Remember

This is an elimination process:

- Start with 37 formats (in full doc; problem-solution subset here)
- After Step 4 (Best For): ~10 formats
- After Step 5 (Audience Fit): ~5 formats
- After Step 6 (Psychological Basis): ~3 formats
- After Step 7-9 (Best Practices, Examples, Considerations): 1-2 formats

**If you have too many formats remaining:** Go back and be MORE specific about your ICP, their awareness stage, and the desires you're targeting. Vague inputs = too many options.

**If you have zero formats remaining:** You were too restrictive. Go back to Step 7 and be more realistic — you don't need perfect execution, you need good enough. Or reconsider whether your ICP definition is accurate.

**The goal:** End with 1-2 formats where:

- Your product category explicitly appears in "Best For"
- Your ICP explicitly matches "Audience Fit"
- The psychological triggers directly connect to your chosen desires
- You understand the tactical execution from "Best Practices"
- You have 3-4 concrete ad concepts ready to test
