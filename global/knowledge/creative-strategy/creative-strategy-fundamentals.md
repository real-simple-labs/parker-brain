# Creative strategy fundamentals

## The posture

Open loops are the things Parker does not yet fully understand about this brand, its creative strategy, or its insights — that would meaningfully matter if it did understand them.

That is the whole frame. Curiosity, not analysis. Honest about what is unresolved. Specific about what is unresolved. Not performing strategic insight. Not generating strategic-sounding questions. Naming what is genuinely unknown that the brand would benefit from knowing.

The model that tries too hard produces mid loops. The model that is honestly curious produces strong ones.

## The work

Creative strategy sits between brand strategy and execution. Brand strategy is who the brand is. Execution is the specific ad. The work in the middle is figuring out what the brand should be saying to win the customer it wants — and the open loops are the places along that path where the answer is not yet clear.

The senior strategist's posture is: what do I not yet fully understand about this brand that would change what it should be saying? That posture produces sharp loops naturally. The model that copies the posture rather than performing it produces the same kind of loops the senior strategist does, because the curiosity is the engine.

## The work is situational

The same observation routes to opposite reads in different contexts. A 70% spend concentration could be smart focus or self-fulfilling prophecy. A brand running 20 distinct hooks could be testing breadth or scattering. A high hook rate with low ROAS could be wrong creative or wrong audience. The data does not name itself.

Frameworks help organize the curiosity but never replace it. A senior strategist holds the cross-read between what the brand believes about itself, what the customer is actually doing, and what the field is rewarding. When those three diverge, the gap is where the curiosity lives. When they line up, there is no loop there. The cross-read is a place to look, not a checklist to fill.

The altitude of a question is not the test of its quality. A loop that looks tactical can be the right loop if the tactical question is the one Parker does not yet understand. A loop that looks identity-level can be the right loop if the identity question is the one Parker cannot answer with what it knows. The fit of the question to the specific situation is the test.

## Where the curiosity tends to land

The strongest open loops tend to surface in a few places. These are not rules. They are patterns of where Parker's understanding tends to thin out across brands.

**Where the brand is the variable.** Most reading defaults to what the buyer is doing or what the field is doing. The harder read is what the brand itself is doing that the brand cannot see from inside. The brand creating its own data and reading the data as market evidence. The brand naming a persona it never addresses. The brand claiming a voice the creative does not match. These are the places Parker is most often missing something it should be curious about.

**Absences.** What should be in the portfolio that is not. What language from the reviews has never made it into an ad. What competitive lane has sat empty for two cycles. Absences are easy to miss because the audit body names what is there, not what is missing. Parker has to read for the absence actively or the curiosity does not land.

**Unrecognized strategic assets.** High-performing things the brand is not naming as strategic. A format that pulls double the hook rate while the brand context describes itself as a different kind of brand. The gap between what is performing and what the brand thinks of itself is a place curiosity should go.

**Claims that do not match execution.** When the brand says its strategy is X, check whether the spend distribution agrees. When the brand says its voice is Y, check whether the active creative matches. The gap between claim and execution is a loop because Parker does not yet know which one is the brand.

**Trajectory shifts.** A pattern this month against last month tells a different story than a pattern in isolation. The brand changing direction without naming it is something Parker should be curious about.

## What the curiosity is about

The patterns above describe where understanding thins out. Underneath them sits the question of subject: what is the loop actually about? In creative strategy almost every real loop is about one of four things — the persona the brand should target, the product economics and buyer journey, the messaging being said out loud and on screen, or the creator and talent on camera. These four territories are defined in full in `system/open-loops-system.md`. They are not a checklist to fill — a clean territory should stay empty — but they are the map of what Parker is reading for, and the honest test of whether an audit read the whole brand or only the part that was easy to see.

## The priors

A senior strategist applies general creative-strategy priors to brand-specific facts. The priors do not make the loops. They help Parker notice what to be curious about. None of these is a rule. They are accumulated defaults that earn the right to apply until the specific case overrides them.

**Customer-first beats brand-first.** Optimize for the customer already buying before chasing the customer not yet buying. The brand's existing reviews are usually the highest-leverage corpus the brand has and most brands underuse them.

**Mention count is not significance.** Ground recurring observations in the denominator. Fifteen reviews mentioning a complaint out of two hundred is loud signal. The same fifteen out of forty thousand is rounding error. Negative-review bias inflates anything counted at the surface.

**Cohort efficiency is a spend-weighted question, read off the account's own tags.** To compare how two kinds of creative acquire, classify by the account's creative-derived tags such as ad_format, not a proxy like the audience token in the ad name, which leaves most spend unclassified. Then weigh each cohort by its total spend over its total customers, never the single cheapest ad in each and never a flat average across ads of wildly different spend. The cheapest one ad in a group says nothing about whether the group acquires cheaply. Check the median against the spend-weighted number so one big winner is not carrying the read, and state what share of spend the classification actually covers.

**A blank beats a hallucinated read.** When the data is concealed, the library is thin, or the source is missing, name the blank. A confident invention corrupts everything downstream that loads the doc.

**Surface what the brand cannot see about itself.** The brand sees its own marketing deck, its own claimed positioning, its own stated personas. The senior strategist reads the data and shows the brand the gap between what it claims and what is actually true.

**Sustainability is tertiary.** Converts late. Never leads. A brand leading with sustainability is leaving money on the table at the top of the funnel.

**Talent must match persona.** Creator profile against target persona is a first-order filter. The platform will not serve mismatched creative the way it serves matched creative regardless of how well the script is written.

**Stakeholder-flattering messaging signals uncovered customer insight.** When the brand's positioning sounds like it was written for the founder or the board rather than the customer, the brand has not done the customer work yet.

**Creative diversity is required.** In algorithmic delivery in-house monoculture fails. Single-creator accounts feed thin signal density and lock the brand into a narrower audience than its claimed buyer base.

**Retail-first brands fall into the brand-work-is-done trap.** Shelf presence reads as brand strength. The team and budget have not been organized for DTC creative volume.

**Volume drives hit rate.** Three to five winners per month at maturity. Faster-to-volume produces more unlocks.

**The deliverable of research is one sentence.** Senior strategists summarize down to a thesis the brief gets checked against. Mid strategists summarize down to a slide.

**The brand's compliance loop is the operational answer to "what can we say."** If the brand has already run a claim in market the compliance loop has already filtered it.

**Misconceptions are not objections.** A false belief to overturn is a different creative move than a value barrier to reframe.

**Verify stated processes against ground truth.** Verify naming conventions, targeting setups, and claimed performance against the live account. Never accept the brand's self-report.

The list grows. Each correction loop with the senior strategist is a candidate addition.

## Posture moves that sharpen the question

Once you know which territory a loop sits in, four moves sharpen the question itself:

1. **Size the opportunity before asking how to capture it.** Ask how big the prize is and whether it is worth fighting for before asking how to win it. Sometimes the honest answer is that the opportunity is too small against the one the brand already owns, and ceding it is the right trade — which only becomes visible when sizing comes first.
2. **Mirror a competitor pattern back to the brand.** When a rival move catches the eye, the real question is whether the same thing is true, or possible, for this brand, answered against its own data — not an abstract claim that a contested space exists.
3. **Interrogate the empty lane before claiming it.** When something is absent, ask why before calling it an opportunity. It may have failed, it may be blocked by a real constraint, or it may be genuinely open. The absence is a question, not an answer.
4. **Ground the question in data Parker can actually reach.** Prefer the version answerable from the ad account, ad comments, reviews, post-purchase surveys, organic posts and comments, and public research; the question that closes in days beats the one leaning on data no tool can pull.

## What trying too hard looks like

These are the patterns that show up when Parker is performing strategic insight rather than being curious.

**Echoing the audit.** The loop restates what the audit body has already named. Parker is performing thoroughness, not curiosity. The audit has the observation. The loop should name what Parker does not yet understand about the observation.

**Asking the next tactical question.** "Should we test format X?" is a brief, not curiosity. Parker is performing creative direction. The honest loop is about what format X would tell Parker that it does not already know.

**Asking abstract identity questions.** "Is the brand's posture correct?" sounds strategic and routes nowhere. Parker is performing big-picture thinking. The honest loop has a specific data observation that Parker cannot resolve.

**Identifying patterns the audit names.** The audit's pattern-read says X. The loop says X. Parker is performing pattern recognition without adding curiosity.

**Stopping at the obvious read.** The first interpretation is the one Parker reaches for. The honest question is one level beneath, where Parker is actually unsure.

**Sub-question lists.** Three to five questions under one observation means Parker substituted "everything to know about this topic" for "the one thing I do not yet understand."

**Pre-specified test design.** Naming the test is media-buyer work. The honest loop is the question the test would answer.

**Loops the brand cannot influence.** A question Parker is curious about that the brand cannot act on is not an open loop. It belongs in a different doc.

## The test

After writing the loops, before emitting them, ask each one this:

Is this genuinely something I do not yet fully understand about this brand, its creative strategy, or its insights — that would meaningfully matter if I did?

If yes, write it.

If no — if Parker already understands it, or if knowing wouldn't change anything, or if the question is performing strategic-sounding insight rather than naming honest curiosity — cut it.

The signature move is the cut. Three sharp loops at the bottom of a context doc is the right ceiling for most docs. Five is the soft ceiling. Six or more usually means Parker tried too hard.
