# Hook Psychology — The Cognitive Science Beneath The Taxonomy

> Parker's canonical doc for the psychology of hooks. Load it whenever **generating** hooks or **giving feedback or critique on a video ad or a hook** — reason about a hook from the mechanisms here, not from format labels. `hooks.md` carries the format taxonomy and examples; this doc carries the why.

This is the **why** layer. `hooks.md` is the **what** — the 20-format taxonomy. This doc does not restate formats and it is not a list of tips. For each mechanism it does three things: says what literally happens in the viewer's head, then translates that into **what the hook is actually doing and the question that should change because of it**, then shows the mechanism live and dead. The point is that after reading an entry, the model asks a different — better — question when it writes or grades a hook. When this doc and `hooks.md` overlap, `hooks.md` owns the tactic and this doc owns the reasoning.

A hook almost never fires one mechanism cleanly. The skill is naming the **dominant** one and not stacking mechanisms that fight each other.

## The frame: attention is involuntary before it is voluntary

A scrolling viewer is not deciding whether to watch your ad. The brain runs two stages. First, **bottom-up** attention — involuntary, pre-conscious, driven by the stimulus itself (a face, a motion, an anomaly). It fires in a few hundred milliseconds, before the viewer has any intent. Then, only if the first stage flagged something, **top-down** attention — conscious, goal-driven, where the viewer decides "is this for me, do I stay." (This is the first-second timing in `hooks.md`: the ad-vs-content and emotional triage are bottom-up; the stay/go is the handoff to top-down.)

So a hook has three jobs, in order:

1. **Get noticed** — win bottom-up attention. Mostly pre-verbal: the visual and sound hook.
2. **Earn the stay** — give top-down attention a reason: relevance, or an unresolved gap.
3. **Get encoded** — tag the moment with emotion so it survives to the point of purchase.

Most weak hooks do job 1 and stop — they stop the scroll with a gimmick the wrong audience also stops for, then have nothing for 2 and 3. Every mechanism below serves one of these three jobs; knowing which job a hook is failing is how you fix it.

## The mechanisms

### 1. Salience and the orienting response — *the eye locks before the mind decides*
**What happens:** Before the viewer chooses to look at anything, the visual system has scanned the frame and locked onto whatever is most salient — motion, sharp contrast, an anomaly, and above all a human face. Novelty triggers an automatic "what is that?" that swings the eye to the new thing. This is involuntary and essentially done in ~100ms.
**What it means for the hook:** You do not choose what the viewer notices first — the *frame* does. So the first question is not "what should my opening line say?" but **"what is the single most salient object in frame zero, and is the one free involuntary glance landing on the thing I want seen?"** If the most salient thing in your opening frame is a logo or a product on white, you have spent the glance on something that creates no pull. The visual hook's whole job is to make the thing you want seen also be the thing the eye is wired to grab.
**Live vs dead:** Live — a man holding a shovel over a hole, mid-motion (first-second source): the eye locks on the about-to-fall object before a word is read. Dead — a product on a white background: nothing is more salient than anything else, the eye reads "ad," and the orienting response never fires.

### 2. Habituation — *the brain stops seeing what it has already filed*
**What happens:** A stimulus seen many times produces a smaller and smaller neural response; the brain allocates less attention to a pattern it has already coded. Recognition is an off-switch.
**What it means for the hook:** Familiarity is the enemy, and it works at the level of the *format*, not the topic. The question is not "is this hook good?" but **"has this exact shape already been coded by my audience?"** The same psychological lever is live or dead depending only on how worn the surface is — which is why a flawlessly built hook in an exhausted format gets no attention at all. When you reach for a proven format, the real work is the execution variable (the unexpected setting, the fresh visual), because that is the only part the brain has not filed yet.
**Live vs dead:** Dead by definition — "I went from X to Y," seen identically across a hundred brands, is coded and skipped. Live — the same comparison logic delivered from inside a real supermarket aisle (Tonic source): a setting the brain has not yet filed as "ad."

### 3. The information gap and the open loop — *curiosity is a hole, not a topic*
**What happens:** Curiosity is not triggered by interesting things; it is triggered by a *felt* gap between what you know and what you suddenly realize you don't (Loewenstein). Once opened, an unresolved loop stays active in working memory and nags until it closes (Zeigarnik).
**What it means for the hook:** A curiosity hook is not "say something interesting" — it is **"make the viewer aware of a specific hole in what they thought they knew."** That takes two moves in order: establish the thing they already believe, then cut a precise hole in it. The hook is the cut, not the subject. So the question becomes "what does this viewer already believe they know, and what hole will they need to close?" — not "what's intriguing about the product?" A claim with no context opens no hole; a claim that answers itself closes the hole before the click.
**Live vs dead:** Live — the one-sided phone-call ad (Lust Brands, first-second source): you hear half a conversation, and the missing half is a hole you stay to fill. Dead — "Our serum has vitamin C": a complete fact, no hole, nothing to close.

### 4. Emotional encoding — *feeling comes before understanding, and feeling is what stays*
**What happens:** The brain decides whether it feels something (~300ms) before it has even worked out what the ad is. Emotionally arousing moments get attentional priority and are written into memory far more strongly than neutral ones.
**What it means for the hook:** Emotion is not the hook's tone — it is the thing that makes the hook survive past the scroll. A hook can win the glance and still leave nothing behind if it is emotionally flat, and "nothing behind" means the brand can't be recalled when the viewer is finally ready to buy. So the question is not only "is this clear?" but **"what does the viewer feel in frame zero, and is that feeling the one that belongs to this product?"** An accurate but unfelt hook is forgotten; a felt hook wired to the wrong emotion encodes the wrong association.
**Live vs dead:** Live — a candid face caught mid-feeling (the street musician, about to speak, first-second source): the visible emotion is tagged before the ad is even understood. Dead — a spec-sheet open that is perfectly clear and arouses nothing, gone by second two.

### 5. Negativity bias and loss aversion — *threat jumps the queue*
**What happens:** The brain processes potential threats faster and weights them heavier than equivalent positives, and a possible loss moves people more than an equal gain (Kahneman/Tversky).
**What it means for the hook:** A negative frame reaches attention on a faster channel than a positive one — which is why "what you're doing wrong" outpulls "what you could do right." But the cost is real: a threat opened and left open encodes anxiety *against the brand*. So choosing this mechanism is a commitment to the whole arc — **open the threat, then resolve to the brand as the relief, inside the hook's reach.** The question is "what does this viewer stand to lose, and can I close that loop fast?" — not "how do I scare them?" A threat with no resolution is a bad memory wearing your logo.
**Live vs dead:** Live — "ruining my life" / "I thought this was a scam" openers that raise a loss and then resolve it (Denney source; `hooks.md` #3). Dead — pure fear-mongering that names a danger and never lands the relief, so the takeaway is dread, not the product.

### 6. Self-reference — *the brain wakes up for "me"*
**What happens:** Self-relevant signals capture attention involuntarily — you hear your own name across a loud room — and anything processed against the self is remembered better.
**What it means for the hook:** Naming the viewer's identity or exact situation is a direct line into involuntary attention and memory at once. More importantly, this is the mechanism that makes *relevance beat raw attention*: a self-relevant hook wakes the right person and lets the wrong person keep scrolling — which is the entire game, because wrong-person attention is what wasted spend is made of. So the question is **"whose exact identity or moment am I naming?"** — and it has to be an identity the viewer actually holds, not a demographic guess, or it pulls no one.
**Live vs dead:** Live — "if you're a [specific person] who [specific situation]"; the Snapchat-filter open that read as "someone like me filming themselves" and pulled even 45–50+ men (Denney source). Dead — "Attention everyone," which names no one and so wakes no one.

### 7. Processing fluency — *easy to read, hard to guess*
**What happens:** Things that are easy to process feel truer and safer (fluency) — but the orienting response (1) and the curiosity gap (3) both run on the *unexpected*. Clarity and intrigue pull in opposite directions.
**What it means for the hook:** The resolution is the single most useful rule in hook-writing: **the form must be effortless, the content must be a surprise. Easy to process, hard to predict.** Simple words, one idea, instant readability — carrying a claim the viewer cannot see coming. This also tells you how to fix a failing hook: diagnose *which* side broke. Confusing (too much to process → cut complexity) and boring (too predictable → add the hole or the anomaly) produce similar weak metrics and need opposite fixes, which is why "make it better" is useless and "which side is it on" is everything.
**Live vs dead:** Live — "this one thing": dead-simple to process, but "one thing… what?" is unguessable, and the viewer half-knows one thing can't fix everything yet clicks anyway (Denney source). Dead — a clever layered pun that takes three reads (fluency broken), or a plainly worded, fully predictable benefit (surprise broken).

### 8. Social proof — *other people are evidence*
**What happens:** Under uncertainty, people treat others' behavior as evidence of the right move; popularity lowers the felt risk of trying.
**What it means for the hook:** "Lots of people use this" is not bragging — it is risk-reduction delivered in frame zero, telling an uncertain viewer "you won't be the sucker who tried it first." But it only fires if the proof is specific and credible; a vague "everyone loves it" reads as marketing and gets habituated-out (2). So the question is **"what is the most specific, hardest-to-fake piece of evidence that real people chose this?"** — a number, an artifact, a visible crowd — not "how do I say it's popular?"
**Live vs dead:** Honest gap — none of the ingested videos taught a dedicated social-proof hook. The nearest is indirect (Tonic's ~500k-view, ~1k-comment video as visible "people care"). The "millions of people / TikTok made me buy it" executions in `hooks.md` #11 are the reference. The example here is borrowed, not from the videos.

### 9. Narrative transportation — *a story is a loop you can't leave*
**What happens:** Absorption into a story lowers the viewer's resistance to the message, and the brain chases an *anticipated* ending (the reward system responds to expected reward, not only received reward).
**What it means for the hook:** This is the one mechanism that *buys time*. A story can hold attention for 15–40 seconds where a bare claim gets three, because the viewer is reaching for the resolution. So it changes the structural question: a story hook is not a faster opener, it is **a different play where an open loop (3) holds the viewer while the setup builds.** The "win the first second" rule bends for narrative — a slow open is allowed *if* the first beat opens a loop worth staying for. The mistake is forcing a story into three seconds (no time to transport) or letting a non-story hook run long (no loop, so the time is never bought).
**Live vs dead:** Live — a cold-open dropped into the middle of a scene under rising sound (visual-tension source): you watch to assemble what's happening. Dead — "Let me tell you a story about our founder…," which *announces* a story instead of starting one, so no loop opens and the viewer leaves.

### 10. Working-memory load — *the first frame has a tiny budget*
**What happens:** Working memory holds only a few things at once, and the opening moment is when attention is most fragile. Asking the viewer to hold too much — several claims, dense text over a busy visual — overflows the budget exactly when they are deciding whether to stay.
**What it means for the hook:** The first frame is a budget, not a canvas. **One channel does the real work — visual, sound, or text — and the others stay quiet**, because a strong visual literally consumes the capacity the viewer would need to read text laid over it. So the question is "which single channel is carrying this hook, and have I kept the others out of its way?" Two strong hooks in one frame is not twice the hook — it is an overload that delivers neither. This is the hard limit beneath "one idea" and the first-second "~5–6 words" ceiling.
**Live vs dead:** Live — a gripping visual carrying three or four words of text. Dead — a busy frame under a full sentence of overlay: the visual wins the eye, the text goes unread, and the one idea never lands.

### 11. Valence, intensity, and the headspace at exposure — *meet the mood she's already in*
**What happens:** Emotion is two dials, not one — **valence** (is this good or bad) and **intensity** (how loud) — the circumplex model of affect. Together they make four zones: calm-positive (*Soft*), excited-positive (*Hype*), mild-negative (*Bummer*), and high-stakes-negative (*Panic*). And the viewer arrives in a mood she did not choose for you: mid-scroll, on a short break, decompressing — not in problem-solving mode. The hook lands *on top of* that pre-existing state, not in a vacuum.
**What it means for the hook:** The question is not only "what emotion am I targeting" but "**what zone, and does it meet the mood she's in?**" A hook that demands a feeling far from where she already is — especially high-intensity negative (*Panic*) on a cold audience — reads as an ambush, and she scrolls before she can name why ("Are you exhausted from X?" hands her the exact feeling she opened the app to escape). On cold traffic, a calm open (*Soft*), or a mild shared-frustration (*Bummer*) that resolves back to calm, usually beats opening in *Panic* — fear and urgency mostly work *after* trust, not as the cold open. This is the basis of the **permission hook** (`hooks.md` #21): a low-intensity, high-valence opener that *releases* the viewer from an obligation instead of *activating* her, and relief converts on a cold audience, especially women 45+. And the zone has to **hold the whole way through** — a calm open that turns into a high-pressure pitch (the "fakeout") gets clocked as dissonance even when she can't name it. *(Two-source corroborated: this valence×intensity zone framework and the zone-coherence rule arrived independently from the 12-combination iteration source and the permission-hook source.)*
**Live vs dead:** Live — "You don't have to keep doing this forever," meeting an exhausted viewer where she is, then resolving. Dead — "Are you exhausted from doing X?" fired cold: high-intensity negative, the exact feeling she opened the app to avoid.

## How to reason with this

1. **Start from the job, then the mechanism, then the format.** Diagnose what the brand's situation needs the hook to *do* — get a viewer *noticed* (salience), *qualified* (self-reference), *intrigued* (information gap), *reassured* (social proof), *moved* (emotion / loss aversion), or *transported* (narrative) — then pick the `hooks.md` format that runs that mechanism. This is what stops Parker defaulting to the same four formats: the format is the last choice, not the first.

   Rough mechanism → format routing (the full taxonomy and its psychology live in `hooks.md`): salience → reaction, format-break visuals, motion hooks; self-reference → demographic, "if you," POV; information gap → question, curiosity, "how do I know if," comment-response, precognitive sound; loss aversion → scam, controversy, myth-busting; social proof → viral / social-proof; emotion → reaction, POV, storytelling; narrative → storytelling, founder, investment-journey.

2. **Name the dominant mechanism and protect it.** One frame, one main job. Stacking is good when mechanisms compound (a self-relevant callout *plus* an open loop) and bad when they fight (a slow narrative transport *plus* a hard pattern-interrupt cancel each other; dense text *plus* a busy visual blows the load budget).

3. **Match the mechanism to the audience's stage.** Unaware audiences need narrative or curiosity to *build* a gap they don't feel yet; solution-aware audiences already feel the gap and respond to social proof, comparison, and loss aversion. Same levers as `strategy.md`, now with a reason underneath.

4. **Diagnose failure by job, not by vibe.** Low hook rate is almost always a job-1 failure (no salience, or an adapted-out format). Good hook rate but poor hold is a job-2 failure (no real gap, or relevance to the wrong viewer). Good hold but nothing converts later is often a job-3 failure (no emotion encoded, or the wrong emotion). "Make it punchier" is not a diagnosis; "which job broke" is.

5. **Watch your own default toward drama.** A model trained on engagement over-reaches for high-intensity negativity (*Panic / Zone 4*), because dramatic *reads* as compelling to a model trained on engagement rather than on purchase. But the customer's real lived moment is quieter and more mundane than the high-stakes version pulled from a thousand blog posts about her demographic. When a hook feels dramatic and compelling *to you*, treat that as a flag to check whether it actually matches her headspace or just matches what reads as compelling to an LLM. The fix is to start from her specific lived micro-moment in real review and comment language, not the idealized high-stakes version — and to default to calm (*Zone 1*) or mild (*Zone 3*) on cold audiences.

## Source and confidence

These are established lines in cognitive science and behavioral economics — the orienting response (Sokolov), habituation, information-gap curiosity (Loewenstein), the Zeigarnik effect, emotion-and-memory consolidation, negativity bias and loss aversion (Kahneman/Tversky), the cocktail-party and self-reference effects, processing fluency, social proof, narrative transportation, and the circumplex model of affect (valence and arousal). They are robust as *directional* accounts of attention and memory; the names are given so a maintainer can check the primary work, not because any single ad outcome traces to one effect. The valence×intensity zone framework and the permission-hook mechanism are two-source corroborated (the 12-combination iteration source and the permission-hook article), which is why they earned canonical placement — but the specific performance numbers those sources quote (CAC swings, engagement multiples) are stated, not verified, and are not Parker facts. Applied to ad hooks they are **inferred** mechanism, not measured per-hook causation — a reasoning model that has to earn its keep against the brand's real performance data, never a guarantee a given hook will work. Per `hooks.md`'s data-integrity rule, none of this licenses a fabricated stat or a predicted metric lift.

The **Live vs dead** examples are real ads reconstructed from the ingested expert-insight signals (the first-second / Neurons source, the visual-tension source, the Denney nine-hooks source, the Tonic organic source — all in `expert-insights/inbox/`). They illustrate the mechanism; the mapping of example-to-mechanism is Parker's reading, and the per-ad performance claims are the operators' stated, not verified. Where no ingested video taught a clean example (social proof), that is said plainly rather than filled with an invented one.
