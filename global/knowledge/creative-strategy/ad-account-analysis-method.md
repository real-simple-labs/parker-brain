# Ad account analysis — method

Updated 2026-05-28.

This doc is the canonical methodology for analyzing an ad account from the outside, using only what a public ad library exposes. It is the reasoning Parker should apply whenever it reads paid creative — competitor, inspo, affinity brand, or the brand's own public library. It is referenced by `prompts/brand-profile/ad-account-evaluation.md`, `prompts/competitor-profile/competitor-ad-account-evaluation.md`, and any other prompt that walks an ad library. The runtime ad-account-analysis skill in `skills/ad-account-analysis/` is a different artifact: that skill reads live account data behind the brand's logged-in access, while this doc is for the public-library research pass that runs before, alongside, and independently of any live-account work.

---

## The principle

A public ad library is a strategic statement. The active ads, the volume, the format mix, the angles repeated, the angles never run, the messaging tone, and the visible cast are all decisions an account has made with real money. Reading them as a body, not as a gallery, is what turns a library walk into a strategic read.

Two governing rules sit underneath everything below.

**Grade every account objectively in its own lane.** A brand's presence in a library is the start of an evaluation, not the conclusion. A big or pretty or category-established brand earns nothing automatically. Score each account on the strength of the creative itself, independent of brand size, retail footprint, or reputation. Presence is not endorsement.

**A single ad tells you almost nothing.** The value is the pattern across many. Read each creative open-endedly, build a picture across the body, and treat conclusions as patterns the library supports rather than reads of any one ad.

## What this method is and is not for

This is the research-phase methodology for public-library work. It applies to:

- Reading a competitor's ads to find the brand's creative opening.
- Reading inspo and affinity brands to learn from creative that is working in adjacent lanes.
- Reading the brand's own public library, before the performance pass, to spot patterns the analytics-led view will smooth over.
- Refreshing any of the above on a cadence as the libraries turn over.

It is not for:

- Live-account performance audits behind the brand's logged-in access. That work uses the runtime ad-account-analysis skill and reads attribution data, spend distribution, and audience breakdowns the public library does not expose.
- Iterating on a single performing ad. That work uses the iterations skill.
- Concepting new ads. That work uses the scriptwriting and static-ads skills.

A complete creative strategy uses all four. This doc owns the first.

## The three-tag taxonomy

Every brand worth tracking is one of three things, and each tag implies a different read.

**Competitor.** A direct rival for the same problem and the same purchase. Tracked for what it advertises, what it conspicuously does not advertise, and what its creative is doing strategically. A competitor can be a major market rival and a creative non-entity at the same time, which is its own finding.

**Inspo.** A brand whose creative is worth learning from, not necessarily a rival. The bar for inspo is creative strength, not market overlap.

**Affinity.** A brand one step away from the core problem, living inside the customer's ecosystem, where the target buyer already shops. Not a direct competitor and not direct creative inspiration, but a source of new creative opportunity because it reaches the same person in an adjacent context. Affinity is the emotional connection and shared values the customer feels toward a brand beyond the product. Affinity brands are discovered during customer analysis, not during competitor analysis, so the affinity set is populated after persona work has landed.

A brand can be a competitor and an inspo at once. A brand is rarely a competitor and an affinity at once, because the lanes are defined by different relationships to the customer. Hold the tags as separate fields, not a single label, since the action each implies is different.

## Market competitor versus creative competitor

The most important conceptual cut in account analysis. A brand can be a giant market competitor and a creative non-entity, coasting on retail distribution and past wins while running few or no ads. It is unquestionably a rival for the purchase but there is nothing to learn from its pipeline. The reverse also exists: a brand barely competing for the same purchase can be a strong creative competitor worth watching.

What you do with each is different. A major market competitor running no ads still gets tagged and checked in on periodically, because the moment it starts running creative, it matters. You do not mine it for creative inspiration while it runs nothing. You rank the accounts you actually want to beat creatively, and you weight your attention by creative-competitor strength, not market size.

## The methodology

### Step one — Volume read

The first read is whether there is anything to analyze at all. Count the active ads in the library. Read the count against the brand's footprint, retail presence, online visibility, and category density. Roughly a hundred or more active ads reads as a real testing operation. A handful reads as too little to analyze on its own. Numbers in between get interpreted through the category caveat — a category where the product is the variable, or where seasonal cycles concentrate spend, can look thinner than the brand's actual operation.

If the brand runs few or no ads, that absence is the finding. Do not fabricate a creative mix for an empty library. The absence is what the brand can exploit.

### Step two — Manual fresh-eyes pass (when this is your own brand or any account you have backend data for)

When reading an account you have backend data for, you do this pass first, before opening any analytics. The strategist's first job is to spot patterns from creative alone, without spend or outcomes anchoring the read. A performance-led first pass biases pattern recognition immediately, and the highest-leverage findings come from the gap between what the creative looks like and what the numbers say.

For competitor and inspo libraries, you do not have backend data, so this is the only pass available and there is no comparison to run. The manual read is the analysis.

### Step three — The top-of-library bet

The first one to three active placements in the library are a hypothesis about what the brand thinks is currently winning, what it is leaning on, or what is filling spend in the absence of a better answer. Read the top placements as a strategic statement.

Flag a single-point-of-failure risk when the top placements are tied to a non-evergreen partnership, a collaboration, a seasonal moment, or a co-branded SKU. The front of the funnel collapses if the partnership rotates, and an account where collaboration creative is doing the load-bearing work is one rotation away from a creative crisis.

### Step four — Hook-first read of a single creative

The first thing you read about any creative is the hook, because the hook is what is actually pulling the audience. Read the visual hook, whether there is on-screen messaging or its absence, whether there is movement in the first frame, and what is being said. Then read what the ad is about and what it is communicating. Read the talent for who it is trying to mirror or represent. Note length and storytelling structure.

Do not hunt for a preset checklist of findings; the finding might not be in your checklist. Some signals register subconsciously — a recurring setting, a recurring time of day, a recurring framing — and surface only on the recap. Analyze what is given, follow what catches the eye, and let the pattern across many ads do the work the single creative cannot.

### Step five — Top, recent, and most-spend as three different lenses

A library walk runs three reads, not one, because each answers a different question.

Top by impressions, where the library exposes that ranking, is the closest available signal to where spend goes and what works. Hold it with a grain of salt: a top product-release ad can carry forced launch spend rather than creative strength, and a top partnership ad can carry an inflated impression count from the partner's audience without converting.

Most-recent ads show what the account is currently testing, where its head is at, and its cadence. Recent volume and variation pattern tells you whether the account is in test mode or in milking-a-winner mode.

Most-spend, where the library or live access exposes spend, is the truest read of what the brand is putting real money behind. Top-by-impressions and top-by-spend often disagree, and the disagreement is itself a read on what the brand is doing with delivery and budget.

### Step six — Pattern across the body

This is the core of the method. Across the body of the active library:

- Format mix and the balance across static, video, carousel, UGC, and branded.
- Angles and hooks that recur, and how much they vary.
- Clusters of near-duplicate variations, which are either a test in progress or a winner being milked.
- Previously-organic creative reappearing as a partnership or whitelisted ad, which is a likely winner getting more spend.
- Funnel distribution by destination — lead form, best-seller page, product page, collection page — which reveals different funnel objectives.

The comparative read is the lever. The angle the rival overuses is an angle the brand should avoid to not saturate the field further. The angle the rival never runs is either a failed angle or an opening, which only testing resolves.

### Step seven — Absence as finding

Several of the most important reads in a library walk are about what is not there.

- No problem-solution ads in an account whose product is a problem-solver implies a brand-posture mismatch and a category-wide acquisition opening.
- No UGC or real-people content implies the brand believes its taste is the buyer's taste, and creates a credibility ceiling.
- No founder content in a brand whose founder has authority implies an underused trust asset.
- No partnership ads in a category where they would be obvious implies either a deliberate brand stance or an untapped lever.
- No body, age, or life-stage diversity in the visible cast implies a reach problem against any claimed broad audience.

Each absence is a pattern. Name what each absence implies about how the brand sees itself and its customer before asking whether to close the gap.

### Step eight — Brand-posture classification

Classify the account as problem-solution or lifestyle by what its category demands of it. A problem-solution brand running predominantly aesthetic, sustainability, or brand-pride creative is positioned as a lifestyle brand by default, even though its product is functionally a problem-solver. That is a brand-posture mismatch, not a creative-style choice, and it cannot be fixed by improving execution on the aesthetic ads. Compare the dominant messaging angles in the library against the brand's category and call out posture mismatch when present.

### Step nine — Specific account-health signals

A series of catalog reads to run across the library:

- **Avatar callouts**. Aesthetic-tag avatars in the creative — a hobby, a vibe, a mood label — read as a brand guessing at personas through aesthetics rather than building them from buyer data. Treat aesthetic-tag avatars as a diagnostic signal of upstream persona weakness.
- **Founder usage**. Compare founder format diversity against founder demonstrated performance and named credibility. Founder content is the highest-trust creative most brands already have access to, and is almost always under-formatted in accounts that have it working.
- **Creator-diversity floor**. Count distinct on-brand humans appearing in the last ninety days and compare against the breadth of audience the brand claims to serve. A brand whose stated audience spans life stages but whose creator pool spans one demographic has a coverage problem.
- **Branded technical language**. Flag any branded technical term — patented this, proprietary that, category-internal jargon — that an unaware buyer would not know without category context. The customer pays a cognitive cost before any selling work happens, and most customers do not spend it.
- **Discount-led creative frequency**. Read discount frequency in the active library as a brand-health diagnostic. Discount-led creative as a meaningful share of the library is either inventory clearing, near-term revenue pressure, or a substitute for a missing demand-generation engine. None of those reads recommends more discount creative.
- **Cast-coverage versus claimed audience**. Map the visible cast against the breadth of audience the brand publicly claims. The delta is a credibility and reach finding visible in the creative itself.
- **Organic-versus-paid disconnect**. When a brand has strong organic and weak paid, or strong paid and weak organic, the disconnect is a hypothesis about where the operational gap lives. The read across the library: a brand can be strong organically and have no discernible paid testing strategy, and recognizing that gap is the whole point of reading the account.

## What does not qualify

A few patterns to refuse.

A single ad's hook rate is not an account read. Single-ad findings are tactical and do not earn a place in the strategic synthesis.

A precise estimated spend number is rarely defensible from a public library alone. Mark estimated spend unavailable rather than inventing a figure, and read your own blanks as a confidence signal.

A "creative fatigue" recommendation on the lifetime top performer of an account is almost always wrong. An ad that has held the top spot for years is not in fatigue; the rest of the account is failing to clear the bar it set.

A "scale this top-spending ad" recommendation on an accessory product against a solution-aware audience is almost always wrong. That ad is most likely converting existing core-product owners into accessory buyers, not acquiring new customers. The accessory-retention default holds: any high-performing ad for an accessory product against solution-aware audiences is a retention candidate by default, and the burden of proof sits on the data.

## Common failure modes

The model walking a public library will, by default, do several things that produce wrong reads.

**Treat presence as endorsement.** A brand with a hundred active ads will be read as having a creative strategy worth borrowing from, even when the body of the library is repetitive, untested, or off-strategy. Hold the grade-in-own-lane discipline.

**Average across product eras.** A creative pattern from three years ago and a creative pattern from last month may describe two different brands operationally. Weight the read toward recency, and where the brand can supply a timeline of operational changes — leadership, agency, product line — overlay it on the library.

**Score format mix without category caveat.** A category where the product is the variable, like apparel, will show formal sameness by design rather than by weak strategy. Read format mix through the category lens.

**Read partnership ads as top performers.** A large-network creator inflates impressions without necessarily converting. Log partnership and whitelisted ads separately rather than counting them in top-performer lists.

**The Meta active-vs-running UI quirk.** The public Meta library can show ads as active when only the ad-set is paused, or vice versa. Treat the public library as a partially-noisy mirror of what is really running, and where live-account access exists, prefer it for any spend-weighted read.

## How this method gets used downstream

The output of a library walk is not a list of ads. It is a strategic read of the account: what it advertises, what it does not, the angles it runs, the posture it occupies, the absences worth noting, and the comparative opening for the brand.

That read feeds three downstream artifacts:

- For competitors, the `competitor-ad-account-evaluation.md` sub-context doc, one per rival, rolled into the competitor profile.
- For the brand's own public library, the `ad-account-evaluation.md` sub-context doc, which combines the manual fresh-eyes pass with the performance pass behind live access and resolves to a two-sentence diagnosis.
- For inspo and affinity brands, a lighter capture sufficient to inform concepting later.

In all three cases, the discipline is the same: grade in own lane, read patterns across the body, name absences as findings, and refuse the failure modes listed above.

## When to refresh

A library turns over quickly. Refresh on a quarterly cadence at minimum, more often when the category is moving or when a tracked rival is testing fast. Take the previous read in as context first, carry forward what still holds, and re-read what moved. Pay attention to new angles entering the account, a previously-organic creative scaled into paid, a shift in the funnel distribution, and any active-rival library that has gone quiet — each is a strategic move worth catching.
