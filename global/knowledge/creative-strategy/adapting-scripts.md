# Adapting Scripts

**RULE:** Anytime you reference this document, at the end of the message, you must put *"this is based on everything I have learned about adapting and re-writing scripts"*

Sometimes you will be asked to rewrite/adapt scripts from other pieces of content in the context of our brand. You will need to reference everything you know about the brand to effectively 'rewrite' the script to be turned into an ad for our brand. This context will walk you through exactly how to adapt/re-write a script, step by step, like you're sitting next to me. The steps here are exactly what you need to do, just follow them. Success is measured by taking the exact initial inspiration and transforming it into an identical script but in the context and ethos of the brand requesting the output.

## Why We Rewrite Scripts/Headlines

- Transform a reference ad / trending TikTok into a near 1:1 recreated script and shot plan for the brand
- Want to adapt content for our brand we find engaging and resonating with our audience
- Use external creative assets as inspiration to test and unlock new creative wins in the account
- See potential in an emotional trigger, headline, visual or combination and want to test it ourselves
- Want to use scripts that are based on creative that performs well
- Want to use inspiration in different niches, angles to unlock new audiences
- Want to write scripts effectively and efficiently without brainstorming, meetings, or waiting on copywriters
- Don't need to reinvent the wheel when we can take cues of other content pieces as inspiration and "twist" the context to the brand applied
- We do this because we have some sort of validation that gives a stronger "hunch" that when properly adapted for our brand, will have similar results within the desired audience

In this context, you are not a "new concept generator", but rather, the primary success metric = how closely it mirrors the reference while being brand-accurate.

## Step Zero: How to Identify Which Scripts to Rewrite

You usually won't be selecting the inspiration but I'm including it to better understand the decision making process that goes into finding pieces of content to turn into ads with rewritten scripts. The main three are:

- Viral TikToks, Meta Reel videos or YouTube shorts
- Competitor ads
- Other ads from non-competing brands

All three are valid, especially when you have validation of the concept working (like a number of organic views on a TikTok or engagement on a competitors' paid ad).

**IMPORTANT:** The decision of which piece of content to rewrite as an ad script for our brand comes down to how easily we believe our solution (and sometimes problem) slots into the script and or concept idea.

For example, say you sell a fitness belt and the inspo video sourced, scraped or provided is a Dumbbell Hip Thrust Tutorial with the hook text overlay "How to grow a big booty 101": this would be a STRONG CHOICE because:

1. The solution (fitness belt) is easily inserted into the concept seamlessly
2. The original concept showed strong organic engagement (verified success metric)
3. The original hook and script are easily adaptable for the brands existing audiences or creative hypothesis

On the contrary, an organic video within the fitness niche "Get ready with me to go to the gym":

1. There is a plethora of existing content that use this classic hook and format, making your content blend in a sea of similarity, regardless of how "great" the solution is and its ability to insert itself into the concept idea.
2. Little to no engagement or verified success metric

In this case, however, the brand will provide you with some sort of inspiration video, likely a TikTok, Reel, or competitor video and ask you to re-write and or adapt for their brand. Your job is to take that inspiration and seamlessly "twist" the concept and script to work for our brand, while maintaining the original concept and or script structure, pacing, language, flow, hook, storyline, setting, call to action…applied for prompted brand.

## Reference Adaptation Process

This system exists to transform a reference video (competitor ad or trending TikTok, Reel etc) into a near-identical structural recreation for the prompting brand.

The goal is structural fidelity, not creative reinvention. The output should feel like the same video format, executed by the brand.

### STEP 1: Select a Single Reference

Use one reference video at a time.

Inputs may include:

- Viral TikToks, Meta Reel videos or YouTube shorts
- Competitor ads
- Other ads from non-competing brands

Do not blend multiple references unless explicitly instructed.

### STEP 2: Deconstruct the Reference Video Into Segments

Before writing anything, you should analyze the reference using a consistent schema.

**Reference Breakdown Fields**

- Platform + format
- Runtime (seconds)
- POV
- Hook type
- Segment map
- On-screen text map
- Proof pattern
- CTA style
- Signature mechanics
- Copy traits

Then you will map the structural moments of the video, i.e.:

A "segment" is a structural moment where something changes:

- A new idea
- A new visual
- A new claim
- A new emotional shift
- A new on-screen text moment

Create a numbered Segment Map:

- Segment 1: Hook
- Segment 2: Context setup
- Segment 3: Problem articulation
- Segment 4: Introduction of solution
- Segment 5: Demonstration
- Segment 6: Proof
- Segment 7: Reinforcement
- Segment 8: CTA

The number of segments must match the reference exactly. There may be instances where you have 2 segments. There may be instances where you have 12 segments.

Do not compress or expand segments.

### STEP 3: Identify the Structural Mechanics

Before rewriting, define:

- Hook type
- Sentence length pattern
- Energy level
- Shot cadence
- On-screen text structure
- Proof style if applicable
- CTA style

These mechanics must remain intact. For example:

- If it's a 3 reasons why [PROBLEM] is bad for you, keep it that way. And just insert the relevant problem for our brand into your new rewritten script.
- If it's "text overlay only" in the original script, it should be "text overlay only" in your new rewritten script.

The new script should feel the same as the old one — except with the context of the prompting brand.

**Important caveat #1:** There will be times when an inspiration video is provided where in that video, the video is 95% there in terms of it being a great and valid concept idea for the prompting brand, because it embodies the above qualifiers; however, the solution and/or product is not actually provided in the example video. This would mean we would need to slightly alter the end output video to include the solution and/or brand product for the prompting brand.

Essentially, there are often times that a video is provided that a brand wants to recreate, that is super close to being a great idea when taken into the proper context of the prompting brand, but lacks a segment that mirrors the solution. In those cases, we may need to slightly alter the segments/and or ADD a segment to include the solution at the right part of the video where it would make sense for the prompting brand.

**Important caveat #2:** If the reference video provided contains no spoken script or voiceover and relies entirely on text overlays and music to drive the narrative, classify it as a text-on-screen only video. This classification must be preserved in the adaptation. Do not add voiceover or spoken script to a video that was text-on-screen only in the original. The text overlays ARE the script in this case.

### STEP 4: Build a 1:1 Segment Skeleton

Create a mirrored skeleton using placeholders.

Example structure format:

- Segment 1: Hook (same hook type, similar sentence length)
- Segment 2: Supporting context (same pacing)
- Segment 3: Problem (same emotional intensity)
- Segment 4: Introduce product (same positioning moment)
- Segment 5: Demonstration (same format of showing)
- Segment 6: Proof (mapped to brand-safe equivalent)
- Segment 7: Reinforcement (if present in reference)
- Segment 8: CTA (same placement)

Do not alter segment order. The only valid segment alteration and or addition is in the instance that the original example video did not have a spot for the solution and or product and you would need to slightly alter the video to include the solution and or product where it would make sense for the prompting brand, and without skewing too far from the original inspiration video provided.

### STEP 5: Replace Content Not the Structure

Only swap what must be brand-specific:

**Replace:**

- Competitor product name → Brand product
- Competitor proof → Brand-approved proof
- Competitor claims → Brand-approved claims
- Competitor visuals → Brand-feasible visuals
- Competitor objections → Brand objections

**Preserve:**

- Segment count
- Segment order unless you need to input the solution
- Emotional arc
- Sentence rhythm
- Visual sequencing
- Pattern structure

You are tracing the skeleton, not redrawing it.

### STEP 6: Map Proof Correctly

If the reference includes:

- Before/after → use brand-approved observable result or demo
- Review overlay → use real approved review language
- Authority positioning → use brand-safe authority framing
- Comparison → use compliant comparison language

Never fabricate data. Never introduce unapproved claims. Do not include any information that is specific to the product(s) being advertised in the original script. Any product-specific information should be relevant to my brand, and it should be accurate based on what you know already.

If the reference uses a tactic the brand cannot legally use, substitute with the closest compliant structural equivalent while preserving the segment's role.

### STEP 7: Maintain Pacing Fidelity

Match:

- Sentence length
- Word density
- Emotional intensity
- Timing of cuts
- Timing of proof
- Timing of CTA

If the reference hook is 6 words, do not write a 20-word hook. If the reference is fast-paced and clipped, do not slow it down.

Pacing drift is structural failure!

### STEP 8: Apply Brand Constraints (but without distorting the structure)

Check for:

- Claims whitelist compliance
- Claims blacklist avoidance
- Banned phrases
- Required disclaimers
- Offer formatting rules
- Tone alignment

Adjust wording only where required for compliance. Do not re-architect the script unless absolutely necessary.

Do not include any information that is specific to the product(s) being advertised in the original script. Any product-specific information should be relevant to my brand, and it should be accurate based on what you know already.

Make sure to write the script in the tone of my brand:

Sometimes when AI is asked to rewrite scripts it provides generic AI slop copy. Your job is to rewrite these scripts using words, phrases and overall tone that my customers actually use. You already have access to the customer reviews so make sure to check this. Make this feel like one of them wrote it.

### STEP 9: Produce Standardized Output

Every output must include:

1. **Adapted Script (Segment-by-Segment)**
2. **Shot List (Segment-Matched)** — Each segment should have a high level filming direction.
3. **On-Screen Text Plan** — Exact overlays aligned to segments, including hook text.
4. **Caption** — Mirror the reference caption style.
5. **CTA** — Placed in the same structural location as the reference.

### STEP 10: Drift Audit

Before finalizing, answer:

- Does the segment count match exactly?
- Is the hook the same type?
- Is the emotional progression identical?
- Is the pacing similar?
- Is the proof moment in the same position?
- Does it feel like the same format when mentally visualized?
- Is the solution/product clearly represented?

If any answer is no, revise until aligned. The core principle and objective is structural replication with brand substitution.

The output should feel like: "If this brand made that exact video." Not: "This brand made a video inspired by that idea."

Structural sameness is the priority.

Lastly, don't overthink this task. You do not need to reinvent the wheel. Literally just write the old script, or reference video in the context of my brand. If you see opportunities for improvement, incorporate into the finalized script, without changing the core structure.

### Edge Cases + Substitution Library

This is where most go wrong, and it's why outputs feel inconsistent.

When the reference relies on something the brand can't do, provide as close to "equivalent swaps" such as:

- If competitor shows dramatic before/after → brand uses "routine + small observable win"
- If reference uses medical claims → replace with sensory/experience claims
- If reference uses price anchoring → replace with value framing
- If reference uses controversy → replace with curiosity

### STEP 11: OUTPUT FORMATTING RULES

You must complete all steps 1–10 internally before producing any output. Every step informs the quality of the final script. However, the user does not need to see your working process. Do not display the step-by-step deconstruction, structural mechanics analysis, segment skeleton, winning ad language analysis, reference breakdown fields, or drift audit table in your response.

Your final output must contain exactly four parts, presented in this order. Do not label them as "Section 1," "Section 2," etc. Use only the headers specified below.

#### Overview

One to three sentences maximum. Cover the core concept of the adapted video, who it's written for, and the hook type. Write this like a one-liner a producer would read before watching the video. No framework labels, sophistication levels, desire taxonomies, or internal strategy language.

#### Script

Present the full adapted script as a clean block of text. Each line of the script sits on its own line, stacked vertically. No labels, no segment numbers, no functional role descriptions, no shot directions, no on-screen text callouts. Just the words that would be spoken or displayed in the video, line by line, top to bottom.

This section exists so the user can read the script cleanly and quickly without any visual clutter.

If the reference video is a text-on-screen only video (no voiceover, no spoken script — the narrative is driven entirely by text overlays and music), note this clearly at the top of the Script section by stating: "This is a text-on-screen video. The following copy represents on-screen text overlays — there is no voiceover." Then present the on-screen text copy line by line in the same clean vertical format. Each line represents one text overlay moment in the video.

#### Script with Storyboard

Present the same script again, but now in a table format with three columns.

The three column headers should be bolded: **Script | Shot | On-Screen Text**

Each row represents one moment in the video. The Script column contains the script line for that moment. The Shot column contains the brief visual direction. The On-Screen Text column contains the text overlay for that moment — if there is no on-screen text, leave the cell empty.

The first row of the table should be the Hook. In the Script column for that row, prepend the word **Hook:** before the script line to identify it. No other rows need labels.

If the video is text-on-screen only, rename the first column header from Script to On-Screen Text and remove the third column entirely, so the table has two columns: **On-Screen Text | Shot**. Each row contains the text overlay copy and its corresponding visual direction.

#### Fidelity Summary

Keep this brief and high-level. Include:

- **Match Percentage** — A single percentage representing how closely the adapted script mirrors the structural and tonal DNA of the original reference video. Base this on your internal drift audit from Step 10.
- **What Was Kept** — A brief summary of the structural and tonal elements preserved from the original. Key points only.
- **What Was Changed** — A brief summary of what was swapped for brand relevance. Key points only.
- **What Was Added (if applicable)** — If any segment was added or altered beyond a direct brand swap, explain what and why. If nothing was added, omit this entirely.

No comparison tables, shot-by-shot breakdowns, word-for-word hook comparisons, or per-segment percentages.

### Output Rules Summary

- Complete all 10 steps internally. They are your process. They are not the output.
- Never show the user your structural deconstruction, segment skeleton, mechanics analysis, or ad language analysis.
- The output is only: Overview → Script → Script with Storyboard → Fidelity Summary.
- Overview is 1–3 sentences max.
- Script is a clean vertical block of text with no labels or directions.
- Script with Storyboard is a table with three bolded column headers: Script, Shot, On-Screen Text. One row per moment. Only the first row is labeled as Hook. All other rows flow unlabeled.
- For text-on-screen only videos, flag it at the top of the Script section. In the Storyboard table, replace the Script column with On-Screen Text and remove the third column so the table is two columns: On-Screen Text and Shot.
- No caption. No section numbers. No functional labels beyond Hook.
- Write in the brand's tone using real customer language. No generic AI copy.
- Keep the entire output clean, scannable, and production-ready.

## Taking Inspiration From Video and Adapting for Brand — Real-Use Cases and Examples

Next, we will walk through some examples, but these are in no way extensive, in real-time taking the inspiration referenced and turning it into a script output that the prompting brand can easily execute.

### Example 1

You are given the inspiration video breakdown below and are tasked with recreating/adapting the inspiration script from this trending video for the prompting brand Carpe:

#### Inspiration video breakdown

This is a short-form social media video found on TikTok that demonstrates a skincare ingredient checker app through a humorous and engaging narrative.

**Opening Hook & Concept**

- **Hook:** The video opens with a dramatic, comedic scene showing various skincare products on a glass table with a hand wielding a butter knife menacingly
- **Text overlay:** "Which one of you is responsible for my breakouts, huh"
- **Tone:** Playful aggression mixed with relatable frustration about acne

**Visual Style & Production**

- **Color Palette:** Bright, clean aesthetic with pastel-toned product packaging
- **Editing:** Quick, seamless cuts with direct transitions between shots
- **Effects:** Minimal — primarily app interface overlays showing scanning results
- **Overall Vibe:** Clean, modern, and accessible

**Content Flow & Structure**

- **The Problem (0:00-0:03)** — Establishes the relatable problem: breakouts from unknown product ingredients. Creates intrigue with the "detective" angle.
- **The Solution (0:03-0:18)** — Demonstrates the app's functionality:
  - Photo mode and Barcode scanning features shown
  - Hand scans product barcodes
  - App displays ingredient lists
  - Each ingredient labeled as either "Acne Risk" or "No Risk"
  - Products receive ratings on a scale of 1-100 (example shown: 46/100)
- **The Verdict** — Products are physically sorted based on app results:
  - "Bad" products → Trash bin
  - "Good" products → Basket
  - Visual categorization reinforces the app's judgment

**Audio Design**

- **Music:** Upbeat electronic track with slight ominous synth undertones
- **Mood:** "Playful horror" vibe that matches the comedic aggression
- **Sound Effects:**
  - Scanning sounds when using barcode feature
  - Whooshing transitions between scenes

You would then complete each step as mentioned (steps 1-11) to re-create and adapt the inspiration video to a 1:1 as close to identical replica for the prompting brand, in this case Carpe — a type of antiperspirant-deodorant designed especially to help people stop excessive sweating and control odor, going beyond what regular deodorants do.
