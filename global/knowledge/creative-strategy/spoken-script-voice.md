# Spoken-Script Voice

## Why this document exists

Our scripts sound written. Real winning scripts sound spoken. The gap is the whole problem.

A written script is built for the eye. Balanced clauses, parallel lists, clean grammar, every sentence the same temperature. A spoken script is built for the mouth. It starts in the middle, drops words, repeats itself, breaks grammar, and changes speed constantly. When a human reads a written-sounding line out loud it dies. When a human reads a spoken-sounding line out loud it lands, because that is how the human was going to say it anyway.

This document is the doctrine. It does two jobs. First, it shows how winning scripts actually sound, pattern by pattern, with real quoted lines. Second, it gives the method for studying any brand's own account so adaptation lands in that brand's register and not in generic ad-voice. Read it before writing any script. Audit every script against it before output.

The studied corpus is the authority. Nothing below is theory.

## Part 1: How winning scripts actually sound

### Pattern 1: Sentences are short, and the length jumps around

Real scripts do not hold a steady sentence length. They snap between two-word fragments and long run-ons, and the jumping is the rhythm. The page-written instinct is to make every sentence a complete, medium-length thought. The mouth does the opposite.

- "Number one. Your current denim is giving you that saggy diaper-butt look. Number two. Your nuts are pinched and screaming for help." (TPJ, *IF YOU'RE A GUY 40+*). A one-word beat, then a long one, then a one-word beat again
- "It's not a quick fix, but it's worth the time." (Nutrafol, *menopause changes*). A short line dropped right after two longer ones
- "In jeans. This was Truett Hanes." (TPJ, *marathon podcast*). The two-word fragment "In jeans." is the whole move

The measure: read the script aloud and watch where you breathe. If every breath comes at the same interval, the rhythm is written. Winning scripts make you breathe at uneven intervals.

### Pattern 2: Contractions and elisions everywhere: the mouth never spells it out

Every winning script contracts. "You are" is always "you're." "It is" is always "it's." And past the textbook contractions, real scripts elide the way speech actually slurs. Dropping the back half of words, swallowing "going to" into "gonna," "them" into "'em."

- "I like 'em, they're comfortable, they look great, and I can do anything in 'em." (TPJ, *DDP*). "'em" twice, never "them"
- "So I'm not selling s**t, I believe in 'em." (TPJ, *DDP*)
- "you're going to want to try this" (Nutrafol, *menopause baby hair*). Said fast, the "going to" barely lands
- "they're they're freaking perfect" (organic petite-jeans creator (TPJ niche)). The doubled "they're" is a real stammer, left in

A script with zero contractions reads as a press release. A script that also elides reads as a person.

### Pattern 3: Fragments are sentences

Real speech is full of fragments that a grammar checker would flag. They are not errors. They are emphasis. A fragment lands harder than a complete sentence because it strips out everything but the hit.

- "Stiff. Tight in the thighs. Digging into your waist by noon." (TPJ, *Slim Fit personification*). The brand's own ad copy, spoken cadence
- "Sexy. Unrestricted." (TPJ, *Pilates*)
- "I'm extension-free. It's so incredibly healthy." (Nutrafol, *Christine Andrew postpartum*)
- "Yikes... yellow pee?" (Liquid I.V., *THIRSTY*)

### Pattern 4: Real speakers start in the middle and leave the mess in

Written scripts open with a clean topic sentence. Spoken scripts open mid-thought, with a filler, a false start, or a stammer. And the mess is what reads as real.

- "So I want to show you. I have all this hair growth here." (Nutrafol, *Erin Busbee menopause*). Opens on "So," the way people actually start talking
- "Okay. If you've had any hair thinning..." (Nutrafol, *Erin Busbee*). "Okay." as a standalone beat
- "Um these are also the same style" (organic petite-jeans creator). The "Um" is left in the transcript and it works
- "Because once you know your real size, you never go back to guessing who... whew!" (TPJ, *Slim Fit*). An actual flub ("guessing who... whew!") that survived into the winning cut

The discipline that feels wrong but is right: leave the "so," the "okay," the "um," the small stumble. Polishing them out is what makes a script sound like an AI wrote it.

### Pattern 5: Direct address: "you" and "your," constantly, about the body

Winning scripts talk *to* one person about *their* specific situation, and they name body parts and private problems out loud. The copy is not about the product. It is about the viewer's body.

- "Your nuts are pinched and screaming for help." (TPJ, *IF YOU'RE A GUY 40+*)
- "You squeezed into me at 9:00 AM and by noon you'd lost circulation in both legs." (TPJ, *Slim Fit*)
- "Do your man's jeans look like this?? Or this? Or they cause him to do this?" (TPJ, *PointingOut*). Three "this"-es, all aimed at the viewer's own man
- "If you're losing hair fast, listen up. I'm a dermatologist who lost 50% of my hair." (organic derm (Nutrafol niche))

### Pattern 6: Repetition as emphasis: say it twice, say it three times

Written copy fears repetition and reaches for a synonym. Spoken copy repeats the exact word, because repetition is how the voice underlines. The studied scripts repeat openers, repeat structures, repeat the punch.

- "Hi, I'm Slim Fit jeans... Hi, I'm the size up jeans... Hi, I'm your third return this month... Hi, I'm the Perfect Jean." (TPJ, *Slim Fit*). The entire ad is one repeated frame
- "he wears them all the time now. Like all the time." (TPJ, *marathon podcast*). Repeat for emphasis, not for new information
- "I'm ordering them right now! I'm ordering them right now!" (TPJ, *DDP*)
- "nothing digs in, nothing gaps, and nothing needs a belt" (TPJ, *Slim Fit*). Anaphora the mouth can ride

### Pattern 7: Specificity: numbers, brand names, places, exact body facts

Winning scripts are concrete to the point of over-sharing. They name the real number, the real city, the real competitor, the real measurement. Vague copy reads as marketing; specific copy reads as someone who actually knows.

- "He ran the New York City marathon in under 3 hours." (TPJ, *marathon podcast*). The city and the time, not "a big race fast"
- "waist sizes 26 to 52 inches, lengths 26 to 38 inches, and six different fits" (TPJ, *IF YOU'RE A GUY 40+*). Exact ranges, repeated across nearly every TPJ script
- "For reference I'm 4'11" and 103 pounds." (organic petite creator). Exact height and weight, which is what makes the recommendation trustworthy
- "can interfere with important lab assays like thyroid and cardiac troponin, which I as a cardiologist use to determine if someone's having a heart attack" (Wellbel cardiologist podcast). Troponin, named, is the entire WOW of the ad
- "870 milligrams of electrolytes, zero sugar" (Liquid I.V., *THIRSTY*)

### Pattern 8: Transitions are spoken, not signposted: and they vary

The written instinct is to bridge every beat with the same connective tissue: "but here's the thing," "the best part?," "here's why." Real scripts transition by just moving. A hard cut to the next thought, a "so," a "and," a question, or simply the next fragment. They almost never use the same bridge twice.

- "Fret not good sir. The Perfect Jean are here to save your boys and your butt." (TPJ, *IF YOU'RE A GUY 40+*). The transition is a character voice ("good sir"), not a formula
- "And why is that you ask?" (TPJ, *What looks cooler*). A question as the bridge
- "It's not what you think." (Mott & Bow, *over-50*). A four-word pivot that resets the whole ad
- "So how do we get the water inside the cell?" (organic Celtic-salt educator (LIV niche)). The next beat is teed up by a real question, answered immediately

Across the entire corpus, "but here's the thing" appears zero times. That phrase is an AI tell, not a human transition.

### Pattern 9: CTAs sound like a friend's recommendation, not an ad close

The studied CTAs are conversational, low-pressure, and often framed as personal advice or risk reversal. They rarely say "Buy now." They say "give it a shot," "nothing to lose," "thank me later," "go check them out."

- "With free shipping, exchanges, and returns, there's really nothing to lose." (TPJ, *PointingOut*)
- "I promise you once you try these on, you won't want to take them off. Go check them out at theperfectjean.nyc." (TPJ, *BiggerGuy*)
- "Be consistent. Thank me later, and you're going to say, 'Oh my gosh, my hair!'" (Nutrafol, *Christine Andrew*)
- "go pick these up... so I hope this helps." (organic petite creator). The softest possible close, and it is the highest-engagement organic unit in the set

### Pattern 10: Humor and self-awareness break the ad frame

The comedy-register winners earn attention by being willing to be silly, crude, or self-mocking in a way no AI default reaches for. The joke is specific and a little unhinged.

- "Hi, I'm Slim Fit jeans and I was designed for a man who doesn't have thighs and doesn't eat lunch." (TPJ, *Slim Fit*)
- "People think wrestling wrecked my body. It was the jeans. The stiff f***ing jeans." (TPJ, *DDP*)
- "Ridiculously soft, because cardboard denim is dead." (TPJ, *Meet the jeans*). "cardboard denim is dead" is a writer having fun
- "Still hot. Still wise. Still...shedding?" (Wellbel menopause body copy). The ellipsis-and-turn is the whole joke

## Part 2: The AI-tells list, shown by contrast

Each tell below is paired with a real line from the corpus that does the same job the human way. The left side is the AI default. The right side is what a winning script actually said. Train against the right.

### Tell 1: The em-dash cadence
AI reaches for the em-dash to stack a dramatic clause onto a sentence, like this, over and over, giving every line the same theatrical breath.
- WRONG (AI): "These jeans move with you — from morning to night — without ever holding you back."
- RIGHT (TPJ, *Meet the jeans*): "These are jeans that move with you. From your 9:00 AM to your night out." Two sentences, a period, no dash.

### Tell 2: The triplet ("X, Y, and Z" balanced for rhythm)
AI loves the balanced rule-of-three with a final "and". Three adjectives or phrases of matching weight. Real scripts do use triplets, but they are jagged, repeated-word, or broken, not smooth.
- WRONG (AI): "It's soft, comfortable, and durable."
- RIGHT (TPJ, *Slim Fit*): "nothing digs in, nothing gaps, and nothing needs a belt to hold it together". The triplet repeats "nothing" three times instead of balancing three pretty synonyms. The repetition is the human move.

### Tell 3: "It's not just X, it's Y"
The signature AI construction. It appears almost never in the studied corpus, and where a real script makes a similar turn, it does it in plain speech.
- WRONG (AI): "It's not just a pair of jeans, it's a whole new way to feel comfortable."
- RIGHT (TPJ, *Meet the jeans*): "Meet the jeans that fit perfectly. And replace everything else in your closet." The elevation happens through a hard cut to a new sentence, not the "not just/it's" frame.

### Tell 4: Over-parallel structure
AI builds every beat on the same grammatical skeleton, so the whole script marches in lockstep. Real scripts vary the skeleton constantly and only repeat structure deliberately, as a bit.
- WRONG (AI): "Designed to move. Built to last. Made to fit." Three identical participial fragments, no reason.
- RIGHT (TPJ, *Slim Fit*): The script repeats "Hi, I'm ___" four times. But on purpose, as the whole concept. Then breaks the pattern completely for the product reveal. Parallel structure as a *device*, then abandoned, not as a default.

### Tell 5: Perfectly grammatical sentences
AI writes clean, complete, comma-spliced-correctly sentences. Real speech is full of fragments, run-ons, and dropped subjects.
- WRONG (AI): "If you have experienced thinning hair due to menopause, you may want to consider trying this product."
- RIGHT (Nutrafol, *Erin Busbee*): "Okay. If you've had any hair thinning as a result of perimenopause or menopause, you're going to want to try this." Starts on "Okay," contracts twice, and the cadence is a person talking, not a sentence diagrammed.

### Tell 6: Adjective stacking
AI piles modifiers to sound premium: "luxuriously soft, incredibly durable, beautifully designed." Real scripts pick one vivid word or one concrete image and move on.
- WRONG (AI): "Our premium, ultra-soft, expertly-crafted denim."
- RIGHT (TPJ, *Meet the jeans*): "Ridiculously soft, because cardboard denim is dead." One adjective ("ridiculously soft"), then a concrete image that does the rest of the work.

### Tell 7: The smooth signposted transition
AI bridges with "but here's the thing," "the best part?," "here's why it works." The corpus uses these zero times.
- WRONG (AI): "But here's the thing — the fit is what makes the difference."
- RIGHT (Mott & Bow, *over-50*): "It's not what you think." And (TPJ, *What looks cooler*): "And why is that you ask?" Transitions that are character or question, never the formula.

### Tell 8: The absence of mess
The deepest tell. AI removes every "so," "um," "okay," false start, and stammer, leaving a surface too clean to be human. The winning scripts leave the mess in.
- WRONG (AI): "I want to show you my hair growth. If you've experienced hair thinning from menopause, you should try this."
- RIGHT (Nutrafol, *Erin Busbee*): "So I want to show you. I have all this hair growth here. See the baby hair? Yeah, super exciting. Okay." The "So," the standalone "Okay," the little "Yeah, super exciting" aside. The mess is the realness.

### Tell 9: Marketing abstractions instead of body parts and named things
AI describes benefits in the abstract: "enhanced comfort," "optimal fit," "supports your wellness journey." Real scripts name the nut, the butt, the troponin, the city, the exact size.
- WRONG (AI): "Experience all-day comfort and a flattering fit."
- RIGHT (TPJ, *IF YOU'RE A GUY 40+*): "Your nuts are pinched and screaming for help." The body part, named, crudely, specifically.

### Tell 10: The pristine CTA
AI closes with "Don't wait. Order yours today and experience the difference!" Real CTAs are a recommendation or a shrug toward low risk.
- WRONG (AI): "Don't wait — transform your wardrobe today!"
- RIGHT (TPJ, *BiggerGuy*): "with free shipping, exchanges and returns, there's really nothing to lose. I promise you once you try these on, you won't want to take them off." A promise and a risk reversal, in a friend's voice.

## Part 3: The spoken-register rules

These are operational. Apply them while writing and while auditing.

### Write for the mouth, not the page
Every line has to survive being said out loud by a real person on camera. If a line can only be read, it fails. The test is not "does this look good". It is "would a human say this exact string of words."

### Make the read-aloud test operational
Read the full script out loud, at performance speed, before output. Three specific checks:
1. **Breath check.** Mark where you naturally pause for breath. If the pauses are evenly spaced, the sentence-length variety is too flat. Break some sentences, run others together.
2. **Stumble check.** Find any line you stumble on while reading. A line you trip over is a line the talent will trip over. Either it is too long, too grammatical, or too written. Rewrite it the way you would actually say it.
3. **Cringe check.** Find any line that makes you feel like you are reading an ad. That line has an AI tell in it. Usually an em-dash cadence, a triplet, or a signposted transition. Cut it to plain speech.

### Keep the mess
Do not polish out the "so," the "okay," the "um," the repeated word, the small false start. They are the realness. The instinct to clean them is the instinct that produces AI-sounding scripts. This does not mean inventing fake stumbles. It means not deleting the natural ones, and writing openers that start mid-thought the way speech does.

### Vary the speed on purpose
Mix two-word fragments with long run-ons in the same beat. The jumping is the rhythm. A script where every sentence is the same length is written, not spoken.

### Name the concrete thing
Numbers, brand names, cities, body parts, exact sizes, real competitors. Specificity is credibility. Reach for the named thing over the abstract benefit every time.

### Transition by moving, not by signposting
Cut to the next thought. Use a question, a "so," an "and," or a character aside. Never "but here's the thing." Never use the same bridge twice in one script.

### Close like a friend
The CTA is a recommendation or a risk-reversal, in the body's voice. "Give it a shot." "Nothing to lose." "Thank me later." Not "Buy now."

## Part 4: Per-register notes

A brand's voice lives in one of these registers, sometimes two. Match the register before matching anything else.

### Comedy register: exemplified by The Perfect Jean
The voice is crude, specific, self-aware, and willing to be silly. It names body parts bluntly ("nuts," "diaper butt"), personifies objects ("Hi, I'm Slim Fit jeans"), uses celebrity chaos (the DDP "BANG!" skit), and writes lines a writer clearly enjoyed ("cardboard denim is dead"). Pacing is fast, fragments are frequent, and the product specs are rattled off in a rapid list ("waist sizes 26 to 50, lengths 26 to 36"). The humor is never generic. It is one weird, exact joke per beat. When adapting comedy-register, the failure mode is writing *clean* jokes; real ones are messy and a little unhinged.

### Confession / testimony register: exemplified by Nutrafol
The voice is intimate, vulnerable, and starts mid-thought. It opens on "So" or "Okay," shows rather than claims ("See the baby hair?"), and frames the product inside a personal journey ("Before starting my hair growth journey... now I feel more like myself again"). It admits the catch ("It's not a quick fix, but it's worth the time") and closes soft ("thank me later"). The proof is the speaker's own body and the small physical detail: "there would be so much hair on my hands... now there is like maybe one or two hairs." When adapting confession-register, the failure mode is sounding scripted; the realness comes from the asides, the hedges, and the specific physical observation.

### Authority register: exemplified by the Wellbel cardiologist, the LMNT physicians, the Nutrafol grooming writer
The voice is credentialed, plain-spoken, and lets the expert use insider language as proof. It states the credential up front ("Longtime men's grooming writer here," "I'm a dermatologist who lost 50% of my hair," lower-third "Wellbel Founder"), then teaches by withholding the payoff ("biotin... can interfere with important lab assays like thyroid and cardiac troponin"). It uses one technical term as the WOW, not a wall of them. It often opens on a real question ("why is adding electrolytes to our hydration so important?") and answers in teaching cadence. When adapting authority-register, the failure mode is stacking too much jargon, which charges the cold viewer a cognitive cost. One named technical fact lands; five lose the room.

### Organic-creator register: exemplified by the top TikToks in each niche
This is the purest spoken voice in the whole corpus, because no agency touched it. It is maximally messy and maximally specific: "they're they're freaking perfect," "for reference I'm 4'11" and 103 pounds," "um these are also the same style." It addresses the viewer as a friend ("Girls!! Go grab these," "stop scrolling, 'cause I just found"), over-shares exact specs, and closes with the softest possible nudge ("so I hope this helps"). Organic creators are the register to study when a brand's own scripts are not available or are too polished. They are how the audience actually talks about the category. When adapting, do not sand off the doubled words or the exact numbers; those are the signal.

## Part 5: The brand script-voice profile method

Before adapting any script for a brand, build that brand's voice fingerprint from its own account. This is what makes adaptation land in the brand's register instead of in generic ad-voice. The operational steps live in the process file `skills/scriptwriting/processes/build-brand-voice-profile.md`. The doctrine is here.

### What to pull
Pull the brand's own winning video scripts first. Top spenders and top hook-rate units from the brand's ad account, with full transcripts. Ten to fifteen scripts is enough to see the fingerprint. If the brand's own account is thin or too polished to read a voice from, pull the captured competitor and inspo scripts already in the brand's vault next, then the niche's top organic creators. The brand's own account is always the first source; organic is the purest spoken register when you need to hear how the audience actually talks.

### How many, and read them out loud
Read every pulled script out loud, at performance speed. The fingerprint is audible, not visible. You are listening for what recurs across the brand's winners. The moves that show up again and again are the brand's signature, not one-offs.

### What to note: the five fingerprint dimensions
1. **Pacing.** How short are the sentences, how much does the length jump, how fast does the brand cut between thoughts. TPJ jumps hard and cuts fast; Nutrafol confession runs slower and more even.
2. **Diction.** The brand's actual words. Does it say "nuts" or "fit issues"? "grows like a weed" or "improved growth"? Pull the exact recurring phrases. TPJ repeats "nothing to lose," "no tailor required," "theperfectjean.nyc"; Nutrafol repeats "hair growth journey," "less shedding," "thank me later."
3. **Signature moves.** The structural tics the brand reuses. TPJ personifies objects and rattles spec lists. Nutrafol shows the body and admits the catch. Note the move so the adaptation can carry it.
4. **Direct-address habit.** Who the brand talks to and how. TPJ talks to "you" about your body, crudely. Nutrafol talks to "you" as a peer sharing a tip. Match the address.
5. **CTA shape.** Exactly how the brand closes. Copy the brand's real closing cadence. Its risk-reversal phrasing, its sign-off, its URL read.

### Banned-by-absence words
The most useful output. List the words and constructions that *never appear* in the brand's winners, because their absence is as much the voice as their presence. If TPJ never says "elevate" or "premium experience," those words are banned-by-absence for TPJ even though no rule forbids them. Using them would break the register. Build this list from what the corpus does not contain, and treat it as a hard constraint on the adaptation.

### The output
A short voice fingerprint: pacing read, diction list with exact recurring phrases, signature moves, direct-address habit, CTA shape, and the banned-by-absence list. This profile is loaded as the brand baseline for every adaptation, so every generated line is checked against how the brand actually sounds, not against generic ad-voice.

## The one-line test for everything above

Read the line out loud. If a real person would say it that way to a friend, keep it. If it sounds like an ad, it has a tell. Find it and cut it to plain speech.


## Two more tells: rhythm and cleanliness

**The stacked-declarative drumbeat.** Short parallel statements are punchy alone and robotic in sequence. When consecutive lines share the same shape, the script stops sounding like a person and starts sounding like copy: a human builds a rhythm and then breaks it with a fragment, a sentence that runs long and corrects itself midway, or a stray aside. The check: if three consecutive lines share a structure, rewrite the middle one into a different shape.

**The missing disfluencies.** Real speakers hesitate, restart, and reach for words: before a number they are recalling, before an honest admission, after a joke that landed on themselves. A talking-head script a person could deliver perfectly on the first take is too clean to be believed. Write the hesitation into the line at the spot where the thought genuinely turns, sparingly and never as decoration. The studied corpus shows where these live; copy their placement, not their frequency.

**The ellipsis is not a hesitation.** Writing a gap as "…" is the page showing silence, and people rarely go silent mid-thought. They fill the gap with a sound: a "so," an "I mean," a "you know," a "honestly," a repeated word. Every "…" written as a pause gets replaced with the filler a real person would actually make there. The em-dash is the same move in a different coat, a written pause standing in for a spoken one, and it stays out too. The point is not to remove the pause; it is to voice it the way a mouth does.

## Written tics that sneak into dialogue

**The rhetorical fragment.** Constructions like a question asked and answered by the same speaker mid-story are writing, not talk. A person telling a story connects the clauses: "and the first thing he said to me was." The fragment-question form belongs only in an opener built deliberately as a question hook, never inside the body of someone's story.

**Acting notes.** Parenthetical emotion directions on dialogue are the writer doing the actor's job, and they are where generated scripts hide feelings the words failed to carry. If the line needs a note to land, rewrite the line until it lands alone. Physical direction belongs in the visual column and only when it traces to an observed shot.

**The page test, inverted.** These are spoken scripts. The draft is not done when it reads well. It is done when saying it out loud feels like something a person would actually say to someone they know, hesitations and all. Read it to an empty room before delivering it. If your mouth stumbles or your ear flags a line as copy, the line is wrong.

## The hundred-script rule

A handful of scripts is an impression, not a profile. The voice profile is built on volume: pull fifty to one hundred of the brand's real scripts when the account has them, and read enough of them end to end that the brand's actual imperfections become familiar. The goal is not to admire the winners. It is to notice that the real scripts are not that perfect: where they stumble, what they repeat, which words they reach for under pressure, how they actually get out of a video. A draft is checked by laying it next to three real transcripts and asking whether a stranger could tell which one was generated. If the draft is the cleanest of the four, it fails.
