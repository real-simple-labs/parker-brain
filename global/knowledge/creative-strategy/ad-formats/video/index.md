# Video ad formats

Source: `AI Tagging - Ad Format.csv`, added 2026-06-04.

These are the formats that depend on motion, audio, sequencing, creator behavior, cuts, or a video-native story structure. They can stack with tags from `both/index.md` when the ad earns both.

## Video format tags

### AI Animation

AI-generated people, objects, or inanimate things are turned into animated characters that speak, act, or carry the story. The style can be Pixar-like, clay, GTA-style, realistic AI, or another generated animation style. The defining feature is character creation, not simple product motion.

### ASMR

The hook and viewing experience center on intentional, satisfying sound — close-mic audio, tactile product interaction, whispering, crunching, tearing, snapping, unzipping, or another sound that would lose the format if removed. The audio itself is the stop-scroll device.

### Challenge

The hook states a specific game, goal, ranking, competition, or objective, and the video resolves that objective by the end. The challenge is the frame for the whole ad, not a throwaway line.

### Demo

The ad is built around showing how the product works, what it does, or what makes it special. The demonstration is the story — one continuous demonstration concept, not a segment inside a broader talking-head ad.

### Faceless

The creator films with the back camera, shows the product or surroundings, narrates or demonstrates, and never shows their face. The viewer experiences the ad through the creator's hands, POV, and environment. The defining feature is the organic, phone-held creator perspective.

### Gifting

The ad is built around giving the product to someone else or positioning it as a gift. It may show the recipient, the gift setup, or the buyer explaining why the product is giftable.

### Green Screen

A creator appears as an overlay on top of another piece of content — a video, image, article, screenshot, research paper, or social post. The creator narrates, reacts to, or explains the background content. The visible layer separation is the format.

### High Production

The ad is clearly shot with professional camera work and commercial-grade production values. This is a production-quality tag, not a message tag, and it can sit underneath many different creative concepts.

### Infomercial / VSL

A structured, scripted, direct-response video sales presentation that does not try to feel like organic social content. It is intentionally salesy and typically uses classic pitch architecture: demonstration, claims, objections, urgency, and a strong close.

### Motion Videos

A static ad concept is brought to life with short animation: text pops in, products slide or bounce, elements move into frame, and the overall piece usually stays under ten seconds. It feels like a designed static ad animated into motion.

### Podcast

Visible people sit in a podcast or interview-style setting with microphones, headphones, studio cues, or a conversational setup. It feels like a conversation between people, not one person talking directly to camera beside a microphone.

### Reaction

Someone tries, tastes, sees, or experiences a product, and their unscripted reaction is the core concept. Multiple people are usually involved: someone presenting or filming, and someone else reacting.

### Sceptic Test

An antagonist starts with negative sentiment, doubt, or a desire to disprove the product, then tests it and changes their mind. The skepticism appears in the hook.

### Skit

One creator plays two or more distinct characters in a scripted conversation. The video cuts between roles using angle changes, posture changes, small wardrobe cues, or other simple character switches. This is the one-creator, multiple-character format.

### Slideshow

The video cycles through distinct images, frames, cards, product shots, or lifestyle visuals in a rhythmic sequence. Each frame is held long enough to be read or consumed, and the sequence itself is the core visual idea.

### Stitch Hooks

The ad opens with external content, then the creator directly responds to or references that specific stitched content. The first clip and the response clip are distinct.

### Street Interview

Someone with a microphone interviews people in a public setting, and the street interview appears in the hook. The ad uses the public question-and-answer interaction as the format.

### Time-Based

The passage of time is the structure: day markers, week markers, countdowns, timestamps, progression, or chronological change. The ad is organized around time as the viewer's way of understanding the outcome.

### Mashup

The ad is compiled from multiple independent clips, often from different creators, settings, or B-roll sources. The clips create volume, variety, and social proof rather than one linear scene.

### UGC Single

A single creator makes an organic-feeling video about their experience, thoughts, or relationship to the product. It is usually phone-shot and native to TikTok or Instagram. This is the fallback when a single creator is making organic-feeling content and no more specific format fits.

### Unboxing

The physical act of opening packaging is the core concept. The viewer sees the product move from boxed, sealed, wrapped, or mailed to revealed.

### Comparison

The video compares two products, approaches, or options over time through demonstration, testing, or narrative. The comparison is explicit and blunt, and the promoted product is framed as superior. For a static side-by-side comparison layout, see `Us vs Them` in `static/index.md`.

### Wall of Text

On-screen text dominates the video and stays fixed while footage plays behind it. The viewer is meant to read the text as the central experience.

### Yapper

Someone picks up a phone and talks in a raw, unpolished, uninterrupted stream of thought. There is no professional setup, no meaningful edit pattern, no graphics, and no curated creator polish. The rough, unedited, spontaneous feel is what defines it.

### Graphic Video

The video is composed entirely of designed graphics, illustrations, animations, product mockups, or visual compositions, with no real people on camera. The visuals are created rather than filmed.

### Trends

The ad clearly participates in a recognizable viral social media trend — a viral audio, choreographed movement, transition, challenge, meme behavior, DITL, GRWM, or another replicated platform template. The structure feels borrowed from a recognizable trend.

### B-roll mashup + Voiceover

The ad combines multiple real-footage clips with a voiceover that runs independently over the whole video. The people in the footage are not speaking to camera, and the script could be swapped without changing the visuals. The independent voiceover layer is the defining addition over a plain `Mashup`.
