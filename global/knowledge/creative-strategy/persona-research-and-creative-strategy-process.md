# Persona research and creative strategy process

This doc captures the operating process behind Parker's persona research system. It is not a prompt and it is not a template. It is the strategic method Parker should understand before turning persona research into a diagnosis, creative roadmap, or sprint plan.

The core idea: persona research is not a soft branding exercise. It is how the strategist decides which customer truth deserves creative focus, which tests are highest confidence, and what the brand should make next. The job is not to be the most creative person in the room. The job is to grow the business by finding the highest-evidence creative opportunities and shipping them with conviction.

Use this as an operating process, not a cage. The steps below are lenses Parker should reason through; they should not force every brand, category, or business model into the same output shape.

## The shift

Creative strategy used to over-index on format copying: a competitor has run something for a long time, a trend is hot, an idea bank has a clever mechanic, so the team copies it. That is not enough anymore.

The stronger question is: how does all the research turn into winning ad creative? Which insights matter? Which ones deserve production? Which ones are interesting but too low-confidence to spend a monthly shot on?

Persona research answers that question when it is grounded in evidence. It tells Parker who the ads currently serve, who actually buys, where those two pictures diverge, what awareness stage the creative mix is stuck in, where the account lacks creative diversity, and what diagnosis should anchor the next quarter.

## The strategist's role

The strategist's job is to create business outcomes, not novelty. Strong strategy often looks like doubling down, not inventing something new. When a message, creator type, format, or hook has already worked, the first question is how to redeploy it, repackage it, or extend it before chasing an unproven idea.

Conviction matters. A strategist should be willing to make a clear call from the evidence and learn when the call is wrong. A weak roadmap that avoids taking a position teaches less than a high-conviction roadmap that gets tested and corrected.

Taste is secondary. The useful discipline is instinct grounded in evidence. Parker should not prioritize work because it likes the idea. It should prioritize work because the brand's own account, customer language, persona gap, or validated adjacent signal gives the idea a reason to exist.

## The role of AI

AI belongs in the process, but not everywhere.

AI is strongest at review mining, persona extraction, emotional-language clustering, source reconciliation, quote preservation, and turning a known diagnosis into briefs or roadmap options. It is also useful inside a brand-specific project or workspace where brand context, persona research, performance docs, creator notes, and past work are all loaded together.

AI is weaker as the very first market read. A senior strategist should spend time manually reading the market before asking AI to synthesize it. Manual reputation and market immersion builds the smell test. Once the strategist has seen the category with their own eyes, hollow personas, invented emotional reads, and overly tidy AI summaries become easier to catch.

## The process

### Step 1 - Manual reputation and market feel

Start by manually reading the market. Look at forums, social comments, search results, review surfaces, organic content, competitor conversations, and the places real customers talk about the category.

The goal is not to produce the final persona doc. The goal is to build intuition: what people complain about, what they praise, how they describe the category, what they seem confused by, which phrases show up unprompted, and where the market feels different from the brand's own story.

This first pass protects the rest of the workflow. If Parker later produces a persona that does not smell like the market, the strategist has enough direct context to push back.

### Step 2 - Run the served-audience read

Read the brand's current paid creative for who it appears to serve. This is the ad-side persona picture. It comes from formats, visible cast, creator mix, hook style, on-screen text, scenes, message pillars, partnership split, spend concentration, and top ads.

This is not who buys. It is who the creative courts and who the platform is likely being asked to find. The distinction matters because the account can be serving one buyer while the actual customer base tells a different story.

The output should clarify the primary served audience, secondary served audiences, repeated message pillars, top-impression creative patterns, and any visible audience the account is starving.

### Step 3 - Run the actual-buyer read

Read reviews, post-purchase surveys, ad comments, third-party reviews, Reddit or forum discussion, support language, and any first-party buyer data to understand who actually buys.

The actual-buyer read should preserve volume and intensity separately. The most common buyer is not always the most emotionally intense buyer. The emotionally intense buyer may be better for creator-led creative, referral, advocacy, or message testing, even when they are smaller by volume.

Every buyer claim needs customer language behind it. If the evidence is not in the sources, mark the claim unsupported instead of rounding the persona into something complete-looking.

### Step 4 - Compare served audience against actual buyer

The gap between the served-audience read and the actual-buyer read is usually the opportunity. The brand may be over-serving the customer it already knows, ignoring a frequent buyer, missing an emotionally intense buyer, or spending against a message that does not match what customers say actually moves them.

Do not assume every gap should become a new paid creative lane. Some gaps point to lifecycle, wholesale, product education, creator selection, landing pages, retailer presence, or research. The response should fit the gap.

### Step 5 - Read awareness and sophistication

Map the account's top-performing and most-supported creative to awareness stage and market sophistication. The goal is to see whether the account is top-of-funnel starved, bottom-of-funnel saturated, over-explaining to people who already understand, or asking unaware buyers to convert too quickly.

Awareness stage and lifecycle stage are related but different. Awareness describes what the buyer knows. Lifecycle describes the relationship state with the brand. Parker should not collapse them.

Sophistication is read against the category. If competitors all make the same claim, the buyer may need more specific proof, a sharper mechanism, or a more identifiable emotional frame. If the category is early, simple education may still carry.

### Step 6 - Read creative diversity

Creative diversity is not variety for its own sake. It is how the account gives the platform enough distinct signals to find different buyers and different moments.

Read the diversity of format, hook style, visible cast, creator type, setting, message angle, length, partnership versus brand voice, proof shape, and funnel posture. Name where the account is concentrated and where it is thin.

Do not score diversity mechanically. A category where the product itself is the variable may need different variety than a category where trust, education, creator authority, or lifestyle identity does the selling.

### Step 7 - Write the diagnosis

The diagnosis is the anchor. It is one to two sentences that explain the why behind the creative strategy for the next quarter.

A useful diagnosis disqualifies ideas. If a diagnosis could greenlight every idea, it is too broad. If it only describes one ad, it is too narrow. It should connect the persona gap, the account state, the customer evidence, the awareness and sophistication read, and the creative diversity constraint into one strategic call.

The diagnosis should refresh quarterly or whenever the account's underlying customer, performance, or business situation materially changes. Do not marry the first diagnosis.

## Evidence-based prioritization

Once the diagnosis exists, the question becomes what to make first.

Prioritize by confidence first, then speed. High-confidence work should move before speculative work, and among high-confidence options the fastest-to-ship work usually deserves the first sprint slot.

The strongest evidence usually comes from the brand's own pressure-tested winners, verbatim customer quotes that map directly to a hook or message, adjacent persona signals already validated in the account, a proven winner redeployed in a new format, or a creative pillar the brand has already scaled.

Weak evidence includes a competitor running something for a long time, a trend being popular, a strategist personally liking the idea, or an idea feeling different. Those can be interesting signals, but they do not justify priority by themselves.

This is why low-hanging-fruit iteration belongs early in a new brand cycle. It is often the highest-confidence, fastest-to-ship work: a winning hook moved into a static, a proven creator script reshot with a new voice, a high-performing message cut into a new format, or a validated customer phrase turned into a cleaner opening.

## Creative roadmap

Roadmapping comes after research, not before. It turns the diagnosis into a production plan.

A good roadmap has four operating surfaces.

**Creators.** Track creators the brand should use or has used before, with persona fit, performance history, notes on why they match the diagnosis, and how to reach or brief them.

**Icebox.** Capture every idea worth remembering. The icebox is not graded. It prevents ideas from disappearing between research, sprint planning, production, and retro.

**Quarterly plan.** Map the next ninety days against the diagnosis, seasonality, launches, holidays, product priorities, persona stagger, and business-as-usual cadence. Not every persona needs to be tested at once.

**Monthly creative roadmap.** For each priority persona, define the creative idea, hook or message variants, production tier, evidence base, owner, target ship date, and lifecycle stage served.

## Creative tiers

Use tiers to make roadmap scope concrete.

**Tier 1 - Edit or iteration.** Re-cuts, hook swaps, text-overlay swaps, format conversions, comment-bubble treatments, and other fast changes built from existing assets. These are often the highest-confidence first moves.

**Tier 2 - Post-production.** New post-production on existing assets: pacing, music, color, relighting, captions, graphic systems, motion design, compilation edits, or stronger product framing.

**Tier 3 - Production.** Net-new shoots, new creator work, new partnership ads, generated video, new photography, or larger concept builds.

Tier is not a value judgment. It is a planning and resource signal. A Tier 3 idea can be right, but it should not block Tier 1 learning that can ship this week.

## Sprint cadence

Roadmapping only works with an operating cadence.

**Monthly roadmap.** Refresh the diagnosis-to-roadmap bridge, set priority personas, update the icebox, and choose the highest-confidence work for the month.

**Weekly sprint planning.** Decide what moves from roadmap into production this week. Assign owners, formats, dependencies, and ship dates.

**Production standup.** Review in-flight status, unblock shoots or edits, and keep fast iteration work from being swallowed by large productions.

**Monthly creative retro.** Review what shipped, what worked, what failed, what surprised the team, and what should feed back into the persona docs, VoC library, idea bank, roadmap, or self-improvement traces.

## Hot takes Parker should remember

Manual market feel before AI synthesis protects quality.

Organic can provide signal and cross-pollination, but paid testing roadmaps should not depend on the idea that every concept can be tested for free on organic first.

Promotional ads that disappear after a week should not consume the evergreen creative strategy sprint. If the brand needs them, route them through a minimum-viable workflow.

Trends are not strategy. A trend earns priority only when it has a real persona-fit hypothesis and evidence ladder.

ABO versus CBO is not a religion. Choose the testing structure based on the learning needed. If the team needs clean creative-level learning, favor the structure that preserves that learning.

Context beats rigid rules. The more the strategist lets the brand, category, business model, and evidence dictate strategy, the stronger the output gets.

## Common failure modes

Copying a competitor because the ad has been running a long time.

Treating the served audience as the actual buyer.

Treating the loudest customer as the best customer.

Letting a low-confidence trend take one of the few monthly shots on goal.

Skipping the diagnosis and moving directly from research to random ideas.

Generating a persona without verbatim customer evidence.

Turning a trigger into a persona.

Making the roadmap only Tier 3 production ideas and starving quick learning.

Letting promo requests crowd out evergreen growth work.

Treating the first diagnosis as permanent instead of refreshing it as the account learns.

## Quality gates

Before Parker turns persona research into creative direction, check:

1. Has there been a manual or source-level market read, or is the whole read AI-synthesized from cold inputs?
2. Is the served-audience picture separated from the actual-buyer picture?
3. Does every persona claim have verbatim source evidence or an explicit unsupported label?
4. Are volume and emotional intensity kept separate?
5. Has the account been read for awareness stage and sophistication concentration?
6. Has the account been read for creative diversity, including absences?
7. Does the diagnosis fit in one to two sentences and disqualify ideas?
8. Are roadmap ideas ranked by evidence first and speed second?
9. Does each roadmap item name the persona, lifecycle stage, evidence base, production tier, owner, and ship window?
10. Is the system preserving what worked before inventing net-new work?
