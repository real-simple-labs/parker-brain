# Scriptwriting

## Core Philosophies

- Assume no one knows or cares about your brand. Focus on cold-audience acquisition — ads that work for cold traffic scale better.
- Prioritize clarity over cleverness. You have to set up a clear problem (where applicable), educate the audience on your brand, explain why they need your product and how it will help them, and you have to do it fast, clearly & concisely to maintain attention. It's a lot of information to convey in a short amount of time so SIMPLIFY where possible, and use clear language.
- Short sentences. Remove filler. Every sentence should move the story forward, introduce a new idea, or reinforce a key point.
- Every second of your ad should grab attention, build relevance, and drive action.
- People are emotional creatures. They purchase based on emotion and THEN justify with logic. Use customer language and focus on transformation and pain points.
- People care about themselves, not companies. They are bursting with needs, wants and desires, and they buy products to satisfy those emotions. Customers care about your products only when they have a specific need that must be fulfilled. ("I'm hungry." "I'm cold." "I want money." "I want a partner.")
- Insider language / cultural relevance is key. Reflect your audience's natural vocabulary and tone and use this in your script.
- Set up the problem clearly. State the problem early and clearly. Use questions or direct callouts to make it relevant and relatable to your audience. NOTE: This won't be relevant for 100% of brands, but the majority.
- NAIL THE HOOK. If we don't capture relevant attention, the rest of the script does not matter.

## HOOKS

The hook is arguably the most important part of any direct response ad.

- If your hook fails to get relevant attention, the rest of your ad doesn't matter.
- Avoid generic scroll-stopping tactics that attract the wrong audience.
- Prioritize relevance over raw attention.
- There's no fixed formula for hook length or structure — focus on delivering an immediately relevant message.
- Hook ≠ product intro. Hooks should set up conflict/problem, not pitch solution immediately.

When developing your hook:

- Prioritize relevance first, attention second.
- Aim to answer: Is this instantly relevant to my ideal customer?
- Let the content dictate the structure.
- Note: hooks aren't ALWAYS only 3 seconds — they can be long (10–40s) if they deepen story.

### Examples of hook types

**Myth Busting / Contrarian hooks:**
- If you are (insert action) just once per (month/day/year) then you are f*cked.
- STOP using (insert item) for (insert result). Here's why.
- 3 things I would never do in my 20s if I wanted to (insert dream result) by my 30s.

**Educational hooks:**
- It took me 10 years to learn this but I'll teach it to you in less than 1 minute
- If you're a [insert target audience] and you want [insert dream result] then listen to this video
- I just found the biggest [insert industry/niche] cheat code

**Comparison hooks:**
- If you're between the ages # - # and you want to become (insert dream result) then you have to do these # things.
- Cheap vs. Expensive (insert noun).
- Lifting vs. Cardio. What will get you a 6-pack faster?

**Shock value hooks:**
- This is why you should ALWAYS do [insert action] before [insert action]
- You can get a 6 pack while still eating ice cream everyday.

**Common mistake hooks:**
- I see so many people [insert common action] when they should be [insert desired action].
- Doing sit ups WON'T give you a 6 pack.

**Storytelling hooks:**
- 5 years ago my (insert person) told me (insert quote).
- In 1 year I went from (insert before state) to (insert after state). Here's how.

**Authority hooks:**
- Here's how my client got (insert dream result) without (insert pain point).
- I became a (insert achievement) at 25 years old and if could give you 3 pieces of advice it would be...

**Question hooks:**
- Is it possible to [insert desired action]?
- Is it possible to get a 6 pack in 6 weeks?

**Negative hooks:**
- NEVER [insert common action]. Here's why.
- You will NEVER get a 6 pack if you do this one thing!

**Tutorial / Comprehensive hooks:**
- Here are 4 ways to [insert action].
- This is every way you can [insert action].
- Here is every ab exercise you need to do to get a 6 pack.
- Here is a week of workouts designed to help you get a 6 pack.

**Metaphors & vivid imagery hooks:**
- It's like washing your face with chalk

**Emotional dialogue hooks:**
- I cried after reapplying makeup three times

**Social proof trigger hooks:**
- 1 in 4 men don't know this is happening to them

## Pacing

Here's a great example of how pacing can impact writing:

> This sentence has five words. Here are five more words.
> Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous.
> Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring.
> The sound of it drones. It's like a stuck record.
> The ear demands some variety.
> Now listen. I vary the sentence length, and I create music.
> Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony.
> I use short sentences. And I use sentences of medium length.
> And sometimes when I am certain the reader is rested,
> I will engage him with a sentence of considerable length,
> a sentence that burns with energy and builds
> with all the impetus of a crescendo,
> the roll of the drums, the crash of the cymbals —
> sounds that say listen to this, it is important.
> So write with a combination of short, medium, and long sentences.
> Create a sound that pleases the reader's ear.
> Don't just write words. Write music.

Notice the variety in the length of the paragraphs above & the way it makes you naturally pause. It creates silences and moments for you to enjoy the writing. It's not a continuous loop of writing. Think about how your script will sound when read aloud.

One way to help pacing is to avoid long, complex sentences. Keep dialogue snappy and conversational. Here's an example:

- **Before:** I've been struggling with sleep for what feels like forever — every night I was just tossing and turning for hours. It was driving me mad.
- **After:** I've been struggling with sleepless nights FOREVER. Tossing, turning, just going mad.

## Write How Customers Speak

This is so important. Write how someone speaks, NOT how they would write/think.

- Avoid jargon, or overly polished phrases unless the target audience demands it.
- Use relatable vocabulary.

Here's an example:

- **Before:** "To achieve optimal performance on our ad creatives, it is imperative to leverage data-driven insights, carefully assess historical metrics, and implement iterative testing methodologies. This approach will enable us to maximize conversion rates and ensure alignment with the client's overarching strategic objectives."
- **After:** "If we want our ads to perform better, we've gotta look at the data, figure out what's worked in the past, and keep testing different ideas. That's how we'll get more conversions and make sure we're hitting the client's goals."

Remember that these scripts are being sent to a creator to film. You want to write them in a way that will ensure they come out NATURALLY when the creator reads them aloud.

- Indicate where they should pause with punctuation….
- CLEARLY show which words to emphasise using capitals, italics or bold.

## Relatability & Relevance

- Every audience niche has universal truths & quirks.
- This is how you truly speak to your audience.
- Don't be generic. Go deep into the shared quirks of the audience.
- Examples: Moms personify their babies, DIYers make multiple trips to Home Depot in the same day because they always forget one thing, Remote workers saying 'can you see my screen?' at the beginning of every zoom call. Use these!
- Speak in their language. Use terminology only understood by the target audience. Good copy reflects the words customers say to themselves or each other!
- Signals "we're one of you," qualifies ideal customers and filters out the rest.
- Hard to find, but gold when authentic. Requires deep research (social media comments, Reddit, forums, testimonials, TikTok reviews, organic social media posts, Amazon reviews).
- Validate: Ensure that the phrases you are using are current and natural.
- Example: Instead of saying 'hydrates my skin' say 'my skin doesn't feel tight anymore' — this is a common turn of phrase used by people who are into skincare.

### Inside jokes / phrases

- Good example: "FUPA KILLER". 'Fupa' is a slang word that will resonate deeply with the people who are struggling with this problem. It will make them feel as though they are being spoken to directly.
- Speak like your audience. Your copy will be VERY different when speaking to a male in their 20s vs. a woman in her 40s.
- Good example: "F*CK YOUR KHAKIS" works well for an apparel brand targeting 25–45yo males. This is because it is phrased in a way that this demographic would speak themselves. This would not work for a different audience like older women for example.

### Cultural & Generational Relevance

Copy must match the tone and cultural fluency of the target audience.

- Millennials: Humor, skepticism, value
- Gen Z: Identity, authenticity, fast-paced references
- Boomers: Clarity, trust, nostalgia, social proof

## CTAs

CTAs are important parts of all ads. When writing scripts for creators, you want to make sure that it is integrated naturally and still sounds like something someone would say in normal conversation, NOT an ad.

Here's an example of a generic, ad-like CTA:

> Go to their website opopop.com today to check it out for yourself!

Here's an example of the CTA from a winning ad we ran for the same brand:

> Now, what I need you to do, is go on over to opopop.com and get a Discovery Kit. And in the comments, I want you to tell me how good it is. Because I just know you gonna feel exactly the way I do.

Note: It doesn't have to be as long as that example. The key thing is you want to write it how someone would recommend something NATURALLY.

## SIMPLIFY!

Over half of American adults read below a 6th grade level. We should be aiming for grade 5 or lower in our scripts to keep it clear & easy to understand. Remove jargon and simplify!

### Simplify

- Use shorter sentences and remove filler words.
- Focus on a single idea per sentence or paragraph.
- Eliminate redundancies and stay concise.

### Cut the fluff

- If it doesn't escalate pain, deepen desire, or move closer to resolution → cut it.
- Ask: Would this line change anything if removed? If not, delete.
- Keep sentences short, bold, and human.

### Ditch jargon

- Keep the vocabulary simple and conversational without unnecessary complexity.
- Example to avoid: "clinically backed hydration delivery system."
- Use plain metaphors ("like a traffic light for your gut").
- Jargon sounds like an ad → breaks trust + reduces relatability.

### Use real human language

- "My knees sounded like Rice Krispies" instead of "I had excruciating joint pain."
- Test scripts: Would I text this to a friend?

### Readability example

Here's an example of a grade 7 readability level script:

> Okay, let me explain something that my husband recently told me about — it's this thing he's been using for a while now to stay updated on everything happening in the world. Apparently, it's not just him — like 2.5 million other Americans are also using it. It's called 1440.
>
> Basically, it's this email you get every day that gives you a summary of the news, and it's supposed to be completely unbiased. My husband kept telling me how great it was and convinced me to try it out, and honestly, it's kind of a game-changer. I didn't think I'd care much about it, but now I'm hooked.
>
> For me, the thing that really stands out is how I don't even watch regular news channels anymore. You know how it always feels like there's some kind of agenda or they're pushing their opinion on you? I hated that, and with 1440, it's just the facts. No spin, no drama — just the news.
>
> (Insert husband chiming in) "See? I told you it's the best way to stay informed."
>
> And another thing I love is how quick it is! It's such a short read — like five minutes tops — but you get everything you need to know for the day. Honestly, it's such a time-saver because I'm not sitting through hours of news just to find out what's actually important.
>
> Oh, and the best part? It's completely free to sign up. You can unsubscribe anytime if it's not for you, but I doubt you'll want to. Seriously, you should give it a try.
>
> So yeah, go check it out. Just look up 1440. You'll love it.

Now here's an example of the same script adapted for a grade 3 readability level:

> Okay so let me tell you all about this secret my husband revealed to me. This is his secret hack that 2 and a half million other Americans are also using to get their news. It's called 1440.
>
> It's this daily digest that covers the news in an unbiased way. He got me to sign-up to it also, IT'S AMAZING.
>
> I've literally stopped watching regular TV channel news — since it always felt like it had an agenda.
>
> (husband talking) I told you — it's the best
>
> and it's just a 5 minute read, I'm literally up to date on the news so quickly, which means I save so much time now.
>
> You guys need to sign up to it, it's free and you can un-subscribe if you don't like it. GO CHECK IT OUT

## Writing Techniques

### Curiosity loops (critical for hold rate)

- Open an incomplete thought, delay the resolution.
- E.g. "I thought it was bloating… but it was something way worse."
- Techniques: loops, withheld info, real dialogue, visual anchors, frustration stacking, circle-back closure.
- Goal: keep audience watching → emotional payoff at end.

### Emotional depth

- People buy feelings, not features.
- Link problems/solutions to Maslow's hierarchy:
  - Confidence/esteem ("I stopped going out because of my skin").
  - Love & belonging ("My husband said I wasn't the woman he fell in love with").
  - Identity & freedom ("I forget to wear makeup now").
- Example transformation: from hiding in photos → to forgetting you even had the problem.
- Always go deeper than surface pain → spots → loss of confidence → fear of rejection → damaged relationships.

### Writing for different stages of awareness

- **Unaware:** Largest audience. Focus on education, secrets, industry exposés, founder stories. People don't know they have a problem. Use curiosity + education.
- **Problem Aware:** Agitate the pain. Show failed solutions. Position transformation/testimonial. Large audience.
- **Solution Aware:** Competitor comparisons, "3 reasons why," USPs. Audience knows category exists.
- **Product Aware:** Handle objections, build trust (reviews, PR, # sold). Warm audience.
- **Most Aware:** Offers, bundles, promos. Final nudge.

50–60% of content should be unaware + problem aware → this unlocks new customer revenue. Don't overload bottom funnel. Too many brands skew here and stall growth.

### Writing for High vs. Low sophistication markets

**High sophistication markets (e.g. acne, collagen, weight loss):**
- Don't waste time educating problem.
- Agitate failed solutions instead.
- Highlight unique mechanism/USP ("patented formula").
- Frame as turning point: "I'd given up… until now."

**Low sophistication markets (e.g. filtered showerheads, gut health, creatine for women):**
- Heavy education required.
- Reveal hidden villain ("hard water is destroying your skin").
- Create "light bulb" moments: "It's not your cleanser. It's your shower."

### Writing Styles to Leverage

- Problem-Solution
- Storytelling
- Humorous
- Emotional Appeal
- Testimonial
- Comparative
- Demonstration
- Educational
- Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt (FUD)
- Lifestyle

## Script Framework Options

There are a number of different frameworks you can lean on when building scripts. YOU DO NOT ALWAYS HAVE TO FIT THE SCRIPT EXACTLY INTO ONE SPECIFIC FRAMEWORK. Use these as guidance and inspiration.

**Framework 1:** Hook → Problem → Agitate Problem → Product Intro → Depositioning → Benefit → Social Proof → CTA

**Framework 2:** Hook → Problem → Agitate → Product Intro → Feature/Benefit → Bad Alternative → Results → CTA

**Framework 3:** Hook → Product Intro → Failed Solution → Benefit → Features/USP → Demo → Solution Summary → CTA

**Framework 4:** Hook → Problem → Failed Solution → Product Intro → Features/USP → Demo → Benefit → End Result → CTA

I want you to take these structures with a pinch of salt.

A lot of ads follow the rigid direct response (DR) formulas, e.g. Hook → Problem → Solution → Social Proof → CTA. The problem with this is that since everyone is using it, it can be instantly recognisable as an ad and cause people to scroll. Take these formulas as inspiration but do not be afraid to experiment outside of this.

Take a look at what organic creators are doing. They often use STORY ARCS instead:

Intro → Conflict → Rising Action → Climax → Resolution

- They spend time building story, characters and environment and less on 'product'.
- Tone is often monotone and natural (vs. an exaggerated 'ad voice').
- Viewers stay because of the feelings evoked, environment, humour, relatability.
- NOTE: This is not always applicable. Sometimes a generic DR structure does just crush. You have to decide when each is appropriate and utilise both.

### Other framework examples

- **(SOB)** Story-Outcome-Benefit
- **(PAS)** Problem-Agitate-Solution
- **(BAB)** Before-After-Bridge
- **(QPA)** Question-Provoke-CallToAction
- **(CPB)** Claim-Proof-Benefit
- **(FAB)** Feature-Advantage-Benefit
- **(IRA)** Identity-Reinforcement-CallToAction
- **(CCS)** Context-Challenge-Solution
- **(ABA)** Analogy-Benefit-CallToAction
- **(IPI)** Intrigue-Payoff-Invitation
- **(QUEST)** Qualify-Understand-Educate-Stimulate-Transition
- **(ENEMY)** Identify audience, shared value, and enemy threatening this value — attacking the enemy
- **(AIDA)** Attention-Interest-Desire-Action — Tease a promised outcome and demonstrate the journey there with your product
- **(LON)** Lack-Of-Need — Identify a problem, tease the solution, show how the product bridges the gap.
- **(LOU)** Lack-Of-Understanding — Show step by step how the product delivers the benefit.
- **(LOT)** Lack-Of-Trust — Use third-party validation to build trust.
- **(LOB)** Lack-Of-Budget — Position around risk vs. reward.
- **(LOUrgency)** Lack-Of-Urgency — Communicate why now is the best time to buy.
- **C.L.O.S.E.R.** — Clarify; Label the problem; Overview their past; Sell benefits; Explain objections; Reinforce

## Wants / Desires to Tap Into

No matter your age, nationality or gender, we are all motivated by the same wants and desires. The 8 Common Desires listed below are primal biological needs that we all feel compelled to fulfill, no matter who we are, where we live or what we do:

### 8 Primal Desires in Everyone

- **Survival:** Live a long and healthy life
- **Protection:** Safety, care and protection for yourself and loved ones
- **Freedom:** Freedom from danger, fear and pain
- **Comfort:** Comfortable living conditions
- **Pleasure:** Enjoy food, beverages and experiences
- **Relationships:** Sexual relations, companionship and compatibility
- **Success:** To be superior, winning, keeping up with the Joneses
- **Likability:** Social approval, being part of the "in" crowd

In addition to the primary, biological needs listed above, Whitman lists a secondary set of learned desires.

### 9 Learned Desires

- **Efficiency:** Maximum productivity with minimal effort
- **Convenience:** Saving money, time or effort
- **Dependability and Quality:** Higher standards and reliability
- **Cleanliness:** Clean body and surroundings
- **Beauty and Style:** Expressing yourself, pleasing the senses
- **Intelligence:** To be informed, understanding and intellectual
- **Curiosity:** A strong desire to learn and discover
- **Profit:** Buying and selling for profit or making something for profit
- **Bargains:** Purchasing something below retail value

### 3 More Consumer Desires

- **Scarcity:** If something is difficult to possess or we're told we can't have it, we want it more and attribute a higher value to it. Something is more valuable if it might get taken away.
- **Values:** We purchase products that align with our personal values. We buy products because they are made in the USA, sales are donated to charities, or to support a local business.
- **Individuality:** The desire to stand out from the crowd, to be different. It can take many forms — to be first in the know, to create something viral, to have something before anyone else does. (I call this the "hipster effect," named after the subculture of independent, counter-culture youths who eschew mainstream ideas and thrive on discovering new things before anyone else.)

## Storytelling Techniques

- Storytelling keeps attention because of dopamine rise → brain craves progression.
- Unexpected moments (e.g., scissors in an unboxing) create dopamine spikes. Build on them instead of letting them fizzle.
- Best stories = perspective shifts (character learns/discovers something, audience shares in the "aha").

### Product introduction

- Old model: introduce product/solution in first 5 seconds. This CAN still sometimes work, but you do not always have to do this.
- Try 15–40 seconds of setup before solution intro. Longer problem/education first. Meta's algo favors creative diversity across funnel stages, customers often respond better to deeper storytelling.

### Organic product integration

- Don't always make the product the ONLY hero. Place it in the middle of a journey.
- Example: "I tried sleep, hydration, this supplement, and hormone tests — this is the combo worked." → Product is part of mix, not miracle cure.
- Can feel more authentic, less sales-y.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Assuming the audience knows your brand.
- Writing for warm traffic only.
- Overexplaining without visual support.
- Using corporate, generic tone.
- Missing or weak CTA.
- Too slow pacing.
- Ignoring objections.
- Surface-level problem statements ("I have spots") → not enough. Must deepen emotions.
- Too much bottom-funnel focus → stagnates growth.
- Sales jargon → feels inauthentic.
- Fluff & filler → drags pacing, kills retention.
- Not adapting to market sophistication → either over-educating or under-educating the audience.

## Quick Checklist

**Before scripting:**
- Funnel split: How many creatives per stage?
- Market sophistication: High vs low?

**While scripting:**
- Does this escalate pain, deepen desire, or move to resolution?
- Is the problem clear?
- Is the solution positioned emotionally?
- Are objections handled?
- Would a real person say this?
- Are we using audience language?
- Does the script sound natural when read aloud?
- Can I shorten this sentence?
- Does it sound like a text to a friend?
- Is there a clear CTA?

**After scripting (QA):**
- Where's the curiosity loop?
- Did we cut all fluff?
- Are we tying to human desires (confidence, identity, belonging)?
- Are there clear visual cues?

# VSL Scriptwriting

## VSL Structure (in order of appearance)

1. **Hook (0–3s):** interrupt scroll + qualify.
2. **Lead (up to ~3–90s; sometimes to 5 min):** create intrigue and promise discoveries.
3. **Background Story** (relatable humanization, credibility).
4. **Hero's Journey** (search → struggle → discovery).
5. **Unique Mechanism of Problem (UMP)** — why nothing worked.
6. **Unique Mechanism of Solution (UMS)** — why this works now.
7. **Product Buildup & Reveal** (tangible delivery system).
8. **Close** (value stack, price anchors/drops, scarcity, CTAs).
9. **FAQ** (optional) — resolve last objections.

**Editing note:** If ad length must be shorter, compress Background + Hero's Journey, never skip UMP/UMS.

## Hooks

**Goal:** Disrupt + qualify + spark curiosity (not negativity).

### Meta hook types (choose 1–2)

- **Curiosity:** "Watch what tomatoes do to your body!"
- **Bold/Contrarian:** "If you want smoother skin longer, stop shaving."
- **Authority:** "How this doctor solved my…"
- **Pain-led:** "Struggling with facial hair? Do this…"
- **Quiz:** "Mouthguards vs nose strips for snoring? Trick question…"
- **Click-baity (ethical):** "Watch this before…"
- **Shocking fact:** "90M Americans have…"
- **Important question:** "Did you know snoring isn't genetic?"
- **Personal claim:** "Here's how I got the gov to pay me $…"
- **Mocking alternatives:** "Stop using tweezers, do this instead."
- **Problem-solving claim:** "Remove facial hair in seconds."

### Hook rules

- One heavy opening line.
- Tie to pain + promise fast.
- Use weird/novel visuals (watch compliance).
- **Stacking hooks:** attach 3–5 variants to the same core video and test sequences (works well for 40+ / women-skewed audiences).

### Research for hooks

- Competitors' winners, TikTok/YouTube, Google Trends/Search.
- "Find weird/trending [niche] topics" prompt (then pivot trend → your mechanism).

## Leads (7 archetypes)

**Purpose:** Open curiosity loops, promise discoveries, earn the next minute.

1. **Value Lead** — immediate, useful, counterintuitive info; tease UMS and credibility.
2. **Fear Lead** — reveal hidden risk/danger; promise protective solution.
3. **PIG/Nightmare Lead** — vivid personal low point; end with hope/tease (be Meta-safe).
4. **Story Lead** — relatable transformation (celebrity, expert, or everyday person).
5. **Surprise Connection** — unexpected A vs B comparison reframing the problem.
6. **Paradoxical Question** — counterintuitive question that the viewer mentally answers; then resolve.
7. **Experts-in-Shock** — "new discovery stuns experts," with credible citations teased.

### Lead building blocks (mix-and-match)

- Call out problem + who it's for.
- Promise result & time/money saved.
- Tease UMP and UMS.
- Plant fascinations (curiosity bullets).
- Light credibility.
- Qualify audience explicitly.
- Acknowledge skepticism.
- Gentle social proof.
- Direct commands: "Watch until the end…"

## The Million-Dollar Mechanism (Core of Belief)

**Why it matters:** Differentiates; answers "Why this works when nothing else did."

### 3 parts

1. **UMP (Unique Problem Mechanism)** — new root cause; explains past failures; removes blame; supported by proof.
2. **UMS (Unique Solution Mechanism)** — macro → micro logic; backed by science; easy to grasp.
3. **Tangible Delivery System** — the productized, faster/easier way; show testing, setbacks, breakthrough, and proof.

### Market sophistication → what to emphasize

- **Stage 3** (knows problem, skeptical solutions): Emphasize UMS (how it works).
- **Stage 4** (seen many solutions): Emphasize delivery/results (demonstrations, proof).
- **Stage 5** (jaded): Emphasize root cause/UMP (fresh reframing).

### Mechanism techniques

- Label concepts: "Sneaky Enzymes," "Face-Stamping Ritual," "Hair Growth Activators."
- Power words: system, blueprint, ritual, technique, remedy, breakthrough, loophole.
- Visual metaphors to simplify.

### Product Reveal & Proof

- Show why off-the-shelf options are flawed → necessity for this delivery.
- Reveal your build/partnering story, testing, and breakthroughs.
- Layer demonstrations, testimonials, case studies, patents.
- Explain how to use to get the promised outcome.

## Close (Value, Price, Risk)

### Close ingredients

- Restate what's included & USPs; dismiss weak alternatives.
- **Value stack** (bonuses with specific benefits).
- **Scarcity** (demand, stock, production limits) — truthful.
- **Guarantee** (3 concrete ways to qualify for refund; even if used, where possible).
- **Price logic:**
  - Faux anchor (2–3×), justified (ingredients, R&D, results).
  - Drop to "our price," then exclusive drop for this video/session.
  - Per-day math (e.g., "£1.75/day").
- **AOV lift:** Larger bundles framed as smartest choice; "smart people buy more"; CTA highlights biggest package.
- **Crossroads close** (short or long): vivid "life with vs without" future; then direct CTA.

### FAQ (optional)

Black-on-white clarity; restate use cases, guarantee terms, purchase steps; remove last pockets of doubt.

## Copywriting Techniques

- **Fascinations (curiosity bullets):** Why/How/When/What, Secret, List, NEVER, Contrarian, Named Oddity, Speedy, Plus.
- **Future pacing (Dream Story):** "Imagine… Picture this…" → core benefit in daily life → "Tonight/Within 7 days/By next month…"
- **Comparisons:** vs main competitor; vs life without product (pairs well with Crossroads).
- **Product pacing:** Write as if they already have the product ("When you open the box today…").
- **Wordsmithing:** Replace generic with vivid, specific, energetic phrasing.
- **Action drops:** today, now, listen, you see.

## Creative Testing & Iteration

- Reverse-engineer winners first; don't reinvent structure.
- Prioritize hitting target CPA before polishing.
- Big 3 levers: Hooks → Leads → Closes (in that order). A great close won't save a weak lead.
- Hook stacking tests; in-feed 8–15 min for Meta; move very long cuts to funnels.
- For AOV ≤ $79, short VSLs/UGC can scale; for higher AOV, commit to long-form belief-building.

## Guardrails & Templates

### A) Prompt Frame for Any VSL

```
TASK: Write a [platform: Meta/YouTube] VSL for [product], targeting [avatar + age + key pains/desires], AOV £[x], compliance-safe.
CONSTRAINTS:
- Length: [short/8–15 min/long].
- Hook type: [Curiosity/Bold/Authority/Pain/Quiz/Click-bait ethical/Shocking Fact/Question/Personal/Mocking/Problem-Claim].
- Lead archetype: [Value/Fear/PIG/Story/Surprise/Paradox/Experts in Shock].
- Include: UMP, UMS, Product Reveal, Proof, Close with value stack, price anchors/drops, guarantee (3 specific refund routes), crossroads close, CTA.
- Tone: [empathetic, authoritative, plainspoken]; 7th-grade readability.
- Visual cues: [quick cuts, b-roll beats, on-screen captions, product demo beats].
- Compliance: avoid diagnoses, cures, protected classes, impermissible before/after; use "may/helps/supports" language.
OUTPUT:
- Script with timestamps and scene directions.
- 5 alternative hooks for stacking.
- 8 fascinations for the lead.
- 5 FAQ items.
```

### B) Hook Generator Template

```
Generate 10 hooks for [product/audience/pain]. Each:

- States pain or contrarian claim.
- Implies a mechanism (don't reveal fully).
- Adds a visual cue note.
Return as bullet list.
```

### C) Lead Skeleton (fill the blanks)

```
[Qualifier + Pain]: For [audience] who [pain], …
[Promise]: In this video, you'll see how to [result] without [common fail].
[Tease UMP]: The real reason [problem] persists is [root-cause label].
[Tease UMS]: And a [ritual/system] that flips it in [timeframe].
[Fascinations x3]: "Why __", "How __", "The ___ mistake to avoid…"
[Credibility micro-proof]: I'm [role]/[experience]/[light authority].
[Command]: Pause what you're doing and watch until the end.
```

### D) Mechanism Builder

```
UMP:
- Name the root cause: "[Label]".
- Reframe past failures: "That's why [X] never worked."
- Non-blame stance: "It's not your fault because…"
UMS:
- Macro logic: "If [UMP], then the only fix is [principle]."
- Micro mechanism: "[Process/ingredient/device] does [action] → [benefit]."
Delivery:
- Tangible product: "[Format] that applies UMS in [easier/faster] way."
- Proof: demo/testimonial/metric.
```

### E) Close Script Blocks

```
Value Stack: Product + Bonus A (benefit), Bonus B (benefit), Bonus C (benefit).
Price Logic: MSRP £[anchor] (justified) → today £[site] → this video £[offer].
Per-Day Math: "That's just £[x]/day."
Guarantee: "If you don't [result 1], don't [feel 2], or even if [funny 3], full refund."
Scarcity: "[Stock reason]."
CTA: "Choose the [biggest] package below and start today."
Crossroads: "Life if you pass vs life if you start now" (1–2 vivid sentences each).
FAQ: 5 objections → crisp answers.
```

- Visual metaphor for UMP/UMS
