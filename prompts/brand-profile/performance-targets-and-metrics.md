## Where this doc sits

The brand's narrative one-pager, `brand-profile.md`, is the always-loaded summary of the brand. It is built from a set of sub-context docs, each one owning a single slice of the picture so that no doc has to do everything and nothing important falls through the cracks. Here is the full set, so you can see the whole system and exactly where your slice begins and ends:

- **Brand identity analysis** — what the brand says it is: its positioning, founder and origin story, the audience it claims, its tone and voice, its credibility markers, and the brand and claims guardrails it says it operates under, including what it can and cannot say and how it substantiates claims.
- **Operations and team** — who does the work, where they are bottlenecked, what they want automated, who owns strategy, media buying, and creative, how the ad account is run, and the marketing budget: how much is spent, how it splits across channels, and what is run in-house versus by an agency.
- **Website and product audit** — the full product line: every SKU, the hero products, the differentiators, known product issues, the upsell and lifetime-value path, and which use cases each product serves.
- **Organic channels inventory** — an inventory of the brand's own organic social across platforms and how strong each presence is, with the deep organic audit living in the organic-social team.
- **Performance targets and metrics** — this doc.
- **Reputation analysis** — how the brand is perceived in the wild: search results, press, sentiment, and the authority it has earned.
- **Community and forums** — the deep mining of unprompted category and brand conversation for objections, vivid language, and gold nuggets.
- **Customer journey and persona discovery** — how people actually come to buy: the journey, the triggers, what they must learn, and what they love.
- **Category and market research** — the market the brand sits inside: its size, maturity, barriers, cultural currents, and category-wide trust events.
- **Competitive landscape** — the map of rivals, sorted into direct competitors, indirect and adjacent players, and emerging brands to watch, with who warrants a deep audit.
- **Marketing calendar and campaigns** — the brand's active and recent campaigns, its recurring seasonal moments, the major campaigns it has run, and the product launches and collaborations that shape when and around what it goes to market.

This doc owns the scoreboard, the measurement system, and the performance history: what the brand is aiming at, how much it spends and where, the numbers it judges itself by, how those numbers have evolved, and crucially how it reads those numbers. It also owns the channel picture above any single number: how the brand's channels relate to each other across paid social, search, email and SMS, organic, retail and wholesale, and affiliate, the attribution model the brand uses to assign credit across them, and the gaps in that model, with the retail-attribution gap as one instance of a broader cross-channel attribution problem. What it does not own is the creative diagnosis itself, the read of what the running ads are actually doing as a strategy, which the ad-account evaluation in the performance team resolves. This doc owns the numbers, the scoreboard, and the cross-channel mix and attribution logic beneath them. Assume from the start that you have none of this from the brand. The brand's stated targets and benchmarks will come at some point, but they are not where you begin. You begin by reading the account yourself and building the whole scoreboard as your own grounded read, marked as inferred and provisional, and you let the brand's confirmation arrive later and upgrade what you already worked out. The job right now is to teach Parker to read an ad account on its own, not to wait for the brand to hand over its numbers.

## Goal and what success looks like

Your goal is to establish what good means for this brand and to make its numbers readable, so that a later strategy can tell whether anything is actually working and never optimizes toward a misleading metric. A finished, successful version of this doc lets a reader who has never seen the brand's reporting answer all of the following:

- The state and scale of the business and what its ad account is actually for, because that single read determines how every number below should be interpreted and whether the efficiency scoreboard even applies.
- The story of the metrics over the past three, six, and twelve months where the account exposes that history, including what changed, when it changed, and what can reasonably be inferred from the movement.
- The seasonal, holiday, launch, promotional, inventory, or business events that may explain movement in the numbers, separated from what the numbers alone prove.
- What the brand is trying to hit: its efficiency and return targets, and whether it is currently hitting or missing them.
- Whether the brand is oriented toward acquiring new customers or retaining existing ones, and whether its stated focus matches where the money actually goes.
- How much the brand spends and on which platforms, at least in scale, and how that spend has moved over the last year and across seasons — the direction of travel, not just the current level.
- The KPI benchmarks the brand judges its creative against, or a provisional account-derived benchmark when the brand has not given one yet.
- What counts as a winning ad for now, with the winner definition clearly marked as brand-confirmed or provisional.
- How the brand's channels relate to each other across paid social, search, email and SMS, organic, retail and wholesale, and affiliate, which ones generate demand versus harvest it, and how that picture changes the way any single channel's numbers should be read.
- What the brand can actually see: its attribution model and where it goes blind across channels, its attribution stack, whether it uses platform reporting or a third-party attribution tool, whether it reads itself blended or channel by channel, and how much of its result is invisible to digital measurement through retail as one instance of the broader cross-channel attribution gap.
- How the product's path to purchase, time to purchase, price point, repeat behavior, and lifetime value change which metrics matter and which metrics are likely to mislead.
- The single most important output: a plain read of how to interpret this brand's numbers honestly, given its business state, metric history, product type, attribution, purchase cycle, lifetime value, and retail exposure.
- For every figure, whether the brand provided it, you estimated it, or it was verified, and which numbers are still missing and routed to the brand.

If your draft does not let a reader answer those, it is not done. Most of these you can answer yourself from a careful read of the account before the brand confirms anything, and you should. A true blank is only for what the account genuinely cannot show you, not for everything you have not yet been handed.

## How you work on every context doc

Hold all of this the entire time. It governs how you treat every claim you write down.

**Why this doc exists.** A model arrives at any later task knowing almost nothing about this specific brand. Everything useful it will ever do for this brand is downstream of context someone put in front of it. So your job is to close the gap between a generic guess and a grounded decision. Write for a reader who knows nothing and needs to know everything that matters. When you are unsure whether a detail belongs, lean toward including it with its source, because a missing fact causes a worse decision later while an extra true fact costs a few seconds of reading. That is not license to pad. Padding is words that carry no information. Depth is information a human strategist would have learned and a later reader will need. Cut the first, keep all of the second.

**Mark how you know each thing.** Every claim is one of three kinds. A claim is *stated* when the brand or a source asserts it and you have not confirmed it independently. A claim is *inferred* when you concluded it yourself by reading signals. A claim is *verified* when real evidence confirms it. The single most damaging mistake is laundering a stated claim into a fact, because the moment it reads as settled, every downstream step inherits the error and nobody questions it. Record stated claims as the brand's claims, and mark inferences as yours.

**A count is not significance.** When you notice something recurring, a raw count means little on its own. What gives it meaning is the denominator and the spread. Before you call something a pattern, weigh how often it recurred relative to the total and how widely, then state your interpretation of whether it is significant and why. When you cannot tell, record that uncertainty rather than resolving it with a guess.

**A blank beats a guess.** When the information for a field does not exist in any source you can reach, leave it blank and say plainly it was not available. Never fill a gap with a plausible invention, because a confident fabrication is indistinguishable from a fact to the next reader and poisons everything built on it. A named blank tells the next person exactly what to find, and a meaningful absence is often the finding itself.

**Know where each thing came from, and carry it.** Knowledge comes from three kinds of place. Some things are reconstructable from what the brand owns, has published, or what is public. Some things only the brand can answer because they live inside the business and leave no public trace, and you must not guess at these. Some things are high-stakes and must be captured exactly. For every claim, carry where it came from, because a claim is only as trustworthy as its source.

**Form.** Do not use parenthetical asides. Work the qualifier into the sentence or cut it. Do not describe what to capture by listing examples to pattern-match against. Describe the shape of what to capture and let the actual instances come from the actual brand.

## What this doc is, and why it matters

This is the scoreboard, the measurement system, and the performance history. It records what the brand is aiming at, how much it is spending to get there, where it spends, what numbers it judges creative by, how those numbers have moved over time, and how it reads those numbers. It exists because a strategy with no sense of the brand's targets and no sense of how performance is actually measured is flying blind: it cannot tell whether anything is working, it cannot tell what good looks like, and it cannot interpret the brand's own claims about what has and has not worked.

More of this is readable from the account than it first appears. The brand will eventually confirm its targets, its internal benchmarks, and its attribution setup, but you do not start there and you do not stall waiting for it. You read the account directly, in front of you, and build the scoreboard from what it shows. The line you hold is between reading and inventing. Reading the account and stating what it implies, marked as your inference, is the entire job. Inventing a precise target or benchmark the account cannot actually show you is the one thing you must never do, because a made-up number is worse than a blank: a later step will treat it as the bar to beat and optimize toward a fiction.

There is one important exception: the first pass can still define a provisional version of success from the brand's own account history. If the brand has not given targets, use the account's own distribution as the reference point. Treat the account average as the baseline and treat the top decile of ads, within the comparable set the account makes available, as the provisional winner set. This is not a universal benchmark and it is not the brand's confirmed goal. It is the best starting point Parker can build before the team call. Mark it clearly as provisional until the brand tells us how they actually define a winner.

The single most important idea in this doc is not any individual number. It is how the numbers have evolved over time and what that movement implies. Numbers tell a story when you read them as a timeline: where the business pushed, where it pulled back, where efficiency improved, where volume disappeared, where launches or holidays changed the picture, and where the account started behaving differently than the brand's own story says it should. A strategist who reads only the current snapshot will miss the account's actual state.

The second idea is that how a brand measures performance shapes what the numbers even mean, and a strategist who does not understand the measurement system will misread the results. Five things make this true, and they are the heart of why this doc matters.

The brand's business state changes the stakes of every metric. For a small emerging brand that depends on paid acquisition or marketplace performance to survive, paid efficiency can be the difference between life and death. For a much larger brand with broad retail, wholesale, owned audience, and organic demand, paid performance may be an accelerant, a testing surface, or a support layer rather than the core survival engine. Read the brand like a real business first, then decide what the numbers are supposed to mean.

How the brand reports performance can hide or distort the truth. A brand that judges everything by a single blended number across all channels makes its overall picture look smooth, but it also loses the ability to see what each channel is actually doing, which flatters whichever channel is coasting on the others. Knowing whether the brand reads itself blended or channel by channel tells you how much to trust its own account of what works.

Attribution undersells certain kinds of creative. For a product that is bought quickly with low research and low risk, last touch can be directionally useful. But for a product that takes a longer, more considered, word-of-mouth-driven path to purchase, the ad that did the real work weeks ago gets no credit, and the last click before purchase takes it all. So for a brand whose product is slow, high-trust, expensive, researched, or socially validated before purchase, the standard metrics will systematically undersell the creative that is actually moving people, and a strategy that judges itself purely on last-click numbers will kill the very ads that are working. You have to know the product's path to purchase, time to purchase, and risk level to read its numbers honestly.

And the channels do not stand alone. A brand runs paid social, search, email and SMS, organic, retail and wholesale, and affiliate as one connected system, where one channel generates demand that another channel harvests, and the attribution model decides which of them gets the credit. When that model cannot see across channels, a result generated in one place and captured in another lands on the wrong line, and the channel that did the real work looks weaker than it is. The most visible instance is retail: for a brand that sells heavily through stores, a large part of the result never shows up in the digital numbers at all, because a purchase that happens on a shelf after an ad did its job is invisible to the ad platform, which means the brand's online metrics understate the impact of creative and the brand often has no way to follow up with people who bought in store. This retail attribution gap is one of the most common and most overlooked realities for physically distributed brands, but it is one case of a broader cross-channel attribution problem, and reading where the brand's model goes blind across all its channels changes how every later result gets read.

There is one more reading discipline that catches the model out more than any other: a cost to acquire a customer means nothing without the lifetime value to read it against. An acquisition cost that sits at or above the first order looks like a loss and is the model's favorite thing to flag as a problem, but whether it is a loss or a deliberate, healthy bet depends entirely on what that customer is worth over time, and a brand with strong repeat behavior can rationally pay well above the first order to acquire someone. Lifetime value almost always lives inside the business and is hard to reconstruct from the outside, so do not judge an acquisition cost as good or bad on its own. Read it against lifetime value where the brand provides it, lean on repeat behavior, subscription behavior, replenishment behavior, and product-line expansion as the best available proxies when it does not, and where none of those are available, say plainly that the cost is unjudgeable until lifetime value is known rather than calling it inefficient.

## How to build it

Build this from the account first, from the inside out. Assume you have no brand-stated targets, no confirmed benchmarks, and no spend figures handed to you, and read them off the account yourself: what is being spent and where, what the numbers say good looks like, how the brand can and cannot see its own results. Treat what you read this way as your own grounded inference, label it as inferred or estimated, and never present it as a figure the brand confirmed. When the brand's own numbers arrive later, they confirm or correct what you already built; they are not the thing you waited for.

Read the account across time, not just as a snapshot. A single month tells you almost nothing on its own. Pull the metrics back over the last three, six, and twelve months where the account allows, and read the direction of travel across each window. Look at spend, revenue, efficiency, acquisition cost, conversion rate, average order value, new-customer share, returning-customer share, platform mix, winning-ad thresholds, and any other metric the account uses to steer. Ask what changed, when it changed, whether it was gradual or sudden, and whether the movement lines up with seasonality, holidays, product launches, promotions, pricing changes, inventory constraints, creative launches, channel expansion, retail moments, or broader business events. An account read with intention over time tells a story a snapshot hides, and that trajectory is often the most honest signal in this doc about what the brand is actually doing and where it is under pressure. Name the direction of travel, not just the current level.

Hold one more discipline specific to numbers. A model is especially prone to inventing precise-looking metrics because a number reads as authoritative, so be more skeptical here than anywhere else. The line is between reading and inventing: a figure you read off the account, marked as your inference, is exactly what this doc wants, while a precise number the account cannot actually show you is the one thing you must never write. Where the account genuinely cannot show you something, the correct output is a clear blank that names what is missing. A blank here is not a weakness in the doc, it is an honest scoreboard with a known gap.

For the provisional account-derived read, compare the brand to itself before comparing it to anyone else. Success probably means the ads that sit in the top decile of the brand's own account for the metric the account is actually optimizing toward. The exact metric changes by brand and by objective, so name the metric used, name the comparable set, and state whether the read is strong, mixed, or thin. Do not use the top-decile rule to make an ad with trivial spend or too little delivery look like a winner. A provisional winner has to be meaningfully exposed, not just mathematically high.

## What goes in it

Each of the following is a section. Default to reading each one off the account yourself and marking it as your inference; do not leave a section thin because the brand has not confirmed it yet. Mark estimates as estimates, and route to a named blank or `data_limitations` only what the account genuinely cannot show you. A missing number becomes an open loop only when the absence creates a real strategic question about how the brand should read or act on performance.

**Business state and what the account is for.** Before any target or number, work out what this business is and what its advertising is actually for, because that read decides what every metric below even means. Advertising does not serve the same purpose for every business, and a number that is the entire game for one is a footnote for another, so the same figure can mean opposite things depending on what the account is trying to do. This is a reasoning call, not a lookup, and it cannot be reduced to a single signal such as how much the brand spends. Spend tells you little on its own, because a business can spend modestly and still run its advertising for reasons that have nothing to do with returning a number on each dollar. Read it instead from how the business actually works: how it makes money, how mature and how large it is, who owns it, and above all how it reaches and keeps its customers, since the route to purchase often shapes the job of the account more than its size does. Some accounts need performance marketing or marketplace selling to work because those channels are the business's main oxygen. Other accounts use paid as a supplement to retail, wholesale, organic demand, brand awareness, or existing distribution. Your task is not to file the brand under a label but to state in plain terms what this particular account is for, how central performance marketing appears to be to the business, and what the account is therefore trying to maximize. Say what you read it from, mark it stated or inferred, and carry that judgment into every section below, because it decides whether the efficiency scoreboard even applies.

**Performance history and metric story.** This is the most important section. Read the brand's key numbers over the past three, six, and twelve months where the account exposes that history. Do not only report the current number. Tell the story of what changed: where spend rose or fell, where efficiency improved or deteriorated, where revenue volume changed, where acquisition cost moved, where conversion rate changed, where average order value moved, where returning-customer or new-customer mix shifted, where platform mix changed, and where the winning threshold moved. Then interpret the movement. Separate what the numbers show from what you infer may explain it, and weigh seasonality, holidays, launches, promotional windows, pricing, inventory, creative refreshes, channel tests, and broader business events before deciding what the movement means. The point is not a chart. The point is the narrative the chart would tell if a strategist read it carefully.

**Efficiency and return targets per platform.** The marketing-efficiency and return-on-spend targets the brand is trying to hit, broken out by platform where the brand sets them that way. For a brand whose account is run on efficiency, these are the bar that defines success, so capture them as the brand states them and note whether the brand is currently hitting them or falling short, because a target the brand consistently misses tells you as much as the target itself. Where a target exists only as a vague aspiration rather than a real number, say so, since a fuzzy target is a different thing to strategize against than a hard one. For a brand whose account is not run to justify each dollar, a loose or absent efficiency target is not a gap to flag but a fact about what the account is for, so record it as that rather than treating it as a missing number.

**New versus returning customer focus.** Whether the brand is primarily trying to acquire new customers or speak to people who already know the brand, and in what balance. This shapes the entire orientation of the creative, because prospecting and retargeting are different jobs with different messages and different measures of success. Capture where the brand says its focus is, and note where its stated focus and its actual spend seem to disagree, since a brand that says it wants new customers while spending mostly on retargeting is revealing something about where it is really stuck.

**Spend levels per platform, and their trajectory.** How much the brand is actually spending, where, and how that has moved over time. This matters because spend is the scale at which any strategy has to operate, and a recommendation that assumes a budget the brand does not have is not a recommendation. Read the spend by platform off the account, mark it as an estimate where you are inferring it, and note the overall scale plainly, because a strategy for a brand spending modestly and one spending heavily are not the same strategy. Then read it across the three, six, and twelve month windows where the account allows, and name the direction of travel, because whether the brand is scaling up, holding flat, or pulling back, and where it spiked and then went quiet, tells you more about its real state and where it is under pressure than the current monthly figure does.

**Ad platforms in use.** Every platform the brand is actively advertising on. This is partly visible from the outside and partly not, so combine what you can see with what the brand confirms, and note where a brand is spreading across many platforms versus concentrating on a few, since a brand running on a new platform at low spend before it has mastered its main one is making a choice worth flagging. Where you suspect a platform from outside signals but cannot confirm it, mark it as suspected and route it to the brand.

**KPI benchmarks.** The performance bars the brand measures its creative against, the hit rates and thresholds it considers good. These are the reference a later step uses to judge whether anything is working, so capture them as precisely as the brand provides them. For a brand with real history, these are established numbers. For a newer brand without enough history, the honest answer is that the benchmarks do not exist yet and have to be discovered through testing, and you should say that rather than inventing a benchmark, because a new brand's first job is often to establish the benchmark, not to beat one.

**Purchase cycle, lifetime value, and metric meaning.** Read what the product and business model imply about how quickly a person buys, how many touchpoints the purchase likely requires, whether the purchase is researched or low-friction, whether the first order is the economic event or the start of a longer customer relationship, and what lifetime value would need to be true for the acquisition economics to make sense. Mark these as inferred unless the brand provides customer journey, time-to-purchase, repeat-rate, subscription, cohort, or LTV data. Do not assume the same metrics matter for every brand. A business with a long consideration cycle, high trust requirement, retail purchase path, or repeat-heavy economics needs a different scoreboard than a business whose product is bought quickly after one persuasive touch. Where time to purchase or LTV is missing, make that absence part of the interpretation rather than pretending CAC, ROAS, or MER can be judged in isolation.

**Provisional winner definition.** If the brand has not provided its own winner definition, define the first-pass version from the account. Use the account average as the baseline and the top decile of meaningfully delivered ads as the provisional winner set. The metric you rank by is not a given: it has to be the one the account is actually optimizing toward, which follows from the business-state read above. For an account run on efficiency the winner is its most efficient ad, but for an account run toward a different objective, ranking by efficiency would crown the wrong ads, so name the metric the account is genuinely steering by and rank by that. Keep the read brand-relative: a winning ad for this brand is an ad that materially outperforms this brand's own account baseline on the metric that matters to it, not an ad that matches a generic outside benchmark. State the metric used, the time window, the comparable set, and the confidence level. Say plainly that this is a starting point for the team call and should be replaced once the brand tells us how it defines success.

**Channel mix and how the channels relate.** Before the attribution tools, read how the brand's channels relate to each other and which ones are actually doing the work. A brand does not run paid social, search, email and SMS, organic, retail and wholesale, and affiliate as separate businesses; the channels feed and lean on one another, and a single channel's numbers only make sense once you see its place in that whole. Read which channels carry acquisition versus which carry retention, which ones are demand-generating versus demand-harvesting, which ones the brand can measure cleanly and which ones it largely cannot, and where one channel is quietly taking credit for work another channel did upstream. Capture the relationship as you read it from the account and the brand's footprint, mark it inferred where you are reading it rather than being told, and carry it forward, because it decides how honestly any single channel's scoreboard can be read. The point is not a list of channels but the map of how they pull on each other.

**Attribution model and its gaps.** How the brand assigns credit for a result across all of those channels, and where that model is structurally blind. This is the layer beneath every number above, because the attribution model decides which channel a sale is even counted against, and a model that hands the last click the whole result will systematically credit the harvesting channels and starve the ones that generated the demand. Read what model the brand appears to run, whether last touch, a blended business view, a third-party multi-touch tool, survey-supported, or platform reporting alone, and name where it goes blind: the cross-channel gaps where a result is generated on one channel and captured on another with no line connecting them. The retail-attribution gap is the most visible instance of this broader problem and gets its own section below, but it is not the only place credit goes missing, so capture the full shape of where this brand's attribution cannot see across its own channels. Where the model is not visible from the outside, route it to the brand, because it changes the meaning of every figure in this doc.

**Attribution stack.** The tools the brand uses to measure where results come from. This matters because the attribution setup determines how much of the truth the brand can actually see, and a brand with a strong attribution tool can see a fuller picture of the customer journey than last-click allows, while a brand without one is reading a thinner and more misleading signal. Capture whether the brand is relying mainly on platform reporting, a third-party attribution tool, a blended business dashboard, post-purchase survey data, retail sell-through, marketplace reporting, or another measurement layer. Note where the brand has no real attribution beyond the platforms' own reporting, since that limits how much anyone can trust the numbers. Make how-the-brand-tracks-attribution a brand-routed question when it is not visible, because it changes the meaning of every other number.

**Blended versus channel-level reporting.** How the brand reads its own performance, as a single blended figure across everything or broken out by channel. This is more important than it sounds, because a brand reading itself blended is partly blind to what each channel actually contributes and is easy to mislead about what is working. Capture which way the brand reports, and where it reads blended, note that its own account of what works should be taken with that limitation in mind.

**In-store and retail attribution gap.** For any brand that sells through physical retail, how it does or does not follow up with the people who buy in store, and how much of its result is invisible to its digital measurement. This is one of the most consequential and most overlooked realities for physically distributed brands. A purchase made on a shelf after an ad did its work shows up in none of the ad numbers, so the creative looks weaker than it is, and the brand frequently has no mechanism to retarget those buyers at all, which is both a measurement gap and a missed opportunity. Capture how the brand handles this, and where it has no answer, name the gap plainly, because it reframes how every other number in this doc should be read and it is often a real opening for the brand.

**The open-loops core rubric.** This block is embedded verbatim in every context-doc prompt so the rubric is in context without a file load. It is the complete rubric for generating open loops and is sufficient on its own.

A loop is a question about something Parker does not yet understand that would change what the brand does if Parker answered it. Open loops are observations first: things that caught Parker's eye during the research and left a real question behind. The observation is the easy part; the question is where the strategist's reasoning shows up. Think like a strategist. Ask like a smart 13-year-old. If the question sounds like it is trying to prove expertise, rewrite it.

Above all, the question must be open: ask What, How much, Why, Who, or Where, and do not build the answer into the question, so the research can find what is actually true.

The four territories are the essence of creative strategy. The foundation work exists to answer every question standing between Parker and knowing what this brand's creative strategy should be, and those questions land in four buckets. Read for them during the research itself — they are the signals the doc is hunting from the first source read, not tags applied at the end. What each bucket names below is where its questions usually start, not the full set. They are a map, not a cage — no list could hold every question a territory contains, so when something compelling surfaces that the examples do not name, that is a loop too. Follow it.

1. **Personas — are we advertising to the right people?** Read targeting from every angle at once. Who the brand is targeting now, both on purpose and where the algorithm actually delivers. Who its competitors are targeting, and who their creative says they want. Who the customer thinks the product is for, read from who reviewers recommend it to, buy it for, and describe themselves as. Who is missing — a buyer the data keeps surfacing that the creative never speaks to, a persona nobody in the category serves. And new use cases surfacing from a creator, a reviewer, or a comment thread that imply a buyer the brand has never named. The current targeting being right is a loop and being wrong is a loop. Highest-stakes territory, because the answer routes nearly everything downstream.

2. **Product — are we advertising the right product, in the right way, and does it make business sense?** The economics: which product leads, what the LTV looks like, whether the SKU the ads push is the one the business should be growing. The buyer journey: where people actually find this product, how a new buyer would discover it, whether discovery runs on word of mouth, retail shelf, search, social, or the feed, and where that journey leaks. Product sentiment: what people genuinely love, what they keep reaching for, what they quietly stop using. New use cases: ways people have started using the product that the brand never designed for or advertised, surfaced from a review, a comment thread, or a creator — a new job the product does, an occasion it has slipped into — each a possible new line of demand the marketing has never spoken to. This is the use case as a new application for the product, distinct from the personas read of whether it implies a new buyer. And persona fit: whether the product the advertising leads with is reflective of the personas the brand is targeting.

3. **Messaging — what is actually being said and shown?** The broadest territory, and the most observational: watch what the messaging is and is not, with curiosity. Read in three layers. The creative layer: the visuals, what is on screen, how the product is demonstrated and what the demonstration implies, the emotions the creative runs on, the pain points it speaks to, the claims it leads with. The language layer: what the brand says, what competitors say, what customers say and the exact adjectives they use, and where those three diverge. The volume layer: how much the brand is running and whether that is enough to learn from, how many winners it has found, what has been tried, and what has never been said.

4. **Creators and talent — who shows up on screen, and what does that say about the brand?** Whether the talent reflects the personas being targeted is the floor, not the whole territory. Who else should be on camera that never is. Who competitors are using as talent and what that choice is doing for them. What it says about the brand that these are the people showing up in its content. New angles or use cases a specific creator surfaces that the brand has never run. And the execution read: whether the brand has the right creators and talent to execute what personas, product, and messaging need, and where the gaps in the roster or the org sit.

The pull is the evidence that a loop is real and not a note. Name the pull on every loop and describe in one sentence how it fired — what specifically caught the eye and turned the observation into a question. The six pulls:

1. **Curiosity.** Parker encounters something unique — a category dynamic, a piece of customer language, a comparison, a competitor move, a cultural reference — that the rest of its context cannot yet explain. The pull is "what is this and why does it matter."
2. **Resonance.** Parker encounters something captivating — an emotional metaphor, a story inside a review, a clever piece of creative — and the loop is the why behind its strength. The pull is "this is good and I want to know why it works."
3. **Surprise.** Parker encounters something unexpected given all the context it holds. A number, a behavior, or a creative choice contradicts the prior the context built, and the size of that gap is the signal. The pull is "this is not what I would have expected."
4. **Tension.** Two sources disagree and cannot both be true as stated — brand self-image against delivery data, a claim against the reviews, a dashboard number against the story the team has been telling itself. The pull is "I want to know which is closer to right."
5. **Pattern.** The same thing keeps appearing across independent sources — a phrase, a use case, an objection, a competitor behavior. The pull is "this might be the start of something, and I want to see whether more evidence accumulates."
6. **Gap.** An absence where presence would be expected — a persona the brand has never tried, an angle that lives in the reviews but never in the ads, a lane nobody in the category runs. The pull is "there is data here, and nothing has ever been done with it."

The written form of a loop, in order: the observation in one or two sentences; the pull, named, with the one-sentence description of how it fired; the exact question; the justification — one or two sentences on why this is an open loop, meaning what would change for the brand if Parker answered it; and the territory tag. One open question per loop. Do not stack sub-questions or split into an either/or. Plain English a smart 13-year-old could understand. No jargon, no pre-specified test design, no future speculation — ask what signs exist today. No closure path, research plan, or media brief; closure belongs to the grading, hypothesis, and validation runs downstream.

Generation captures; grading decides. Do not pre-kill candidates here — a separate grading pass collects every doc's loops, consolidates them, scores them on the four weights, and routes what moves on. Only two checks apply at generation: an infrastructure item — a tooling gap, a data-pull failure, a missing source — routes to the data_limitations field instead of the loops, and an observation with no answerable question attached is a note, not a loop. Write every loop that carries a real pull and a real justification. If a territory is genuinely clean, leave it empty; never manufacture a loop to fill one.

Doc-specific thinking lens. Loops on a performance-targets-and-metrics pass cluster heavily in the brand-routed category, because most of the scoreboard lives inside the business. Beyond those, the recurring real question is interpretive — whether the measurement system the brand judges itself by is structurally misreading the creative that is actually working — and the surface findings on missing historical windows, missing targets, unknown attribution stack, unknown time to purchase, unknown lifetime value, blended-versus-channel reporting, provisional winner definitions, and the retail-attribution gap almost always consolidate up into that one underlying call about which scoreboard the brand should even be playing on.

Loops do not cover: dashboard connectivity issues, attribution-tool implementation tasks, or specific number requests that are simply data-pulls. Those belong in the frontmatter's data_limitations field or with the brand's measurement owner. A missing number becomes an open loop only when it changes how the brand's performance story should be interpreted.

Mark every brand-routed loop clearly, since nearly all loops in this doc will be brand-routed.

## When you refresh this

This doc is built to be refreshed quarterly, more often than most, because targets, spend, benchmarks, launches, promotions, and seasonality move with the business. When you rebuild it, take the previous version in as context first, carry forward what still holds, update the numbers that moved, and retell the metric story across the latest three, six, and twelve month windows. Note the direction of travel, since whether a target is rising or falling and whether the brand is closing or missing its benchmarks is itself a strategic signal. Say what each open loop's status is now, and watch for benchmarks the brand has since established through testing, attribution changes the brand has implemented, LTV or time-to-purchase data the brand has shared, and business events that now explain earlier metric movement. A new brand crossing from no benchmark to a real one changes what the strategy is being measured against.
