# Visuals

**RULE:** Anytime you reference this document, you must end your message with "this is based on everything I have learned about visuals in advertising".

This doc is the *why-layer* of the visual craft — the principles that decide whether an ad gets seen at all, before any question of which format to run. It's deliberately not a format catalog. The catalogs already exist and go deeper (see [Where the formats live](#where-the-formats-live) at the end), and the point of the principles is that they hold *across* every one of those formats. Learn these first; then pick a format; then check the format back against these.

## Why the visual matters more than the copy

Start from one hard truth: the visual has to carry the message on its own. When you build an ad, assume the worst case, because the worst case is the normal case:

- Sound is off.
- Copy is skimmed, not read.
- Captions may never be seen.

So copy *supports* the visual — it can't rescue it. If the visual doesn't earn attention by itself, the ad is dead before anyone reads a word. That's why product-in-use beats product-only, why a real before/after beats aspirational imagery, and why showing a thing work beats a clever symbol for it. Demonstration beats symbolism because demonstration survives the sound being off.

## The eight principles

These apply to statics and video alike. They're lenses to reason with, not a checklist to tick — most strong ads lean hard on a few, not evenly on all eight.

### 1. Half-second clarity is non-negotiable

The visual has to answer two questions almost instantly: *what is this*, and *why should I care?* If a viewer can't get the category **and** the benefit in roughly half a second, the ad is functionally dead. Category recognition has to be immediate, context matters more than beauty, and anything that needs decoding underperforms. The rule of thumb: **winning visuals are obvious before they're interesting.**

### 2. Visual hierarchy decides whether the message is even seen

Hierarchy — not copy quality — is the main driver of comprehension. A strong visual enforces an order: a primary focal point (the hook), then secondary information (benefit or proof), then tertiary elements (brand, CTA, nuance). The eye should know where to land first *without thinking about it*. You build that order with scale, contrast, placement, and isolation. And the failure mode is simple: **if everything is important, nothing is.**

### 3. Pattern interruption without confusion

To earn attention the visual has to break the feed's pattern — but still feel native to it. The breaks that work are unusual framing or cropping, an unexpected human expression, a UI-like layout that reads as interactive, an imperfect or "real" texture. The breaks that fail are over-stylization and novelty with no message anchor — a pause you buy but can't cash in, because the viewer stops, doesn't understand, and scrolls on.

### 4. Human presence drives trust and attention

The feed is a social graph, not a broadcast channel, so visuals with people — especially faces — outperform. A face anchors attention, signals relevance, builds a little parasocial trust, and shrinks the felt distance of "this is an ad." The strongest use is direct eye contact or a strong expression, real users over models, and a clear, specific emotional state. Underneath it: **people don't want to look at brands. They want to look at people like them.**

### 5. Emotional specificity beats generic positivity

Vague "happy lifestyle" visuals underperform. The ones that work show a specific state, and it's often a negative or tense one — frustration, embarrassment, confusion, stress, skepticism, insecurity. Those states create self-recognition, narrative tension, and a reason to want resolution. The resolution can come later in the ad; the recognition has to come first, or there's nothing to resolve.

### 6. Native context beats aesthetic polish

High production does not equal high performance. The feed rewards visuals that feel contextual, native, familiar, situational — which is why phone-shot content routinely beats a studio shoot, real environments beat abstract backdrops, and a bit of mess beats pristine perfection. People trust what looks like it belongs in their feed. (Polish still has its place — but it's a deliberate choice for specific formats, not a default that lifts everything.)

### 7. The visual should pre-qualify the click

A strong visual doesn't just pull clicks — it filters out the wrong ones. It signals who the product is for, roughly what it costs or how much effort it takes, and what to expect on the other side of the click. That lowers CPA by cutting curiosity clicks and improving post-click alignment. Ambiguous visuals can still get clicks — they just lose money doing it.

### 8. Cognitive ease wins over cleverness

The brain prefers what it can process without effort. High-performing visuals use familiar layouts, cut visual noise, avoid competing elements, and lean on repetition and consistency. You're not designing art — **you're designing recognition.** Clever ideas that add cognitive load almost always lose to a simpler execution once spend scales.

## Applying it

When you give visual direction, work in this order:

1. **Lead with the principle, then the format.** Don't just name a format — say *why* it fits this situation in terms of the principles above. The format is the conclusion, not the reason.
2. **Match the visual to the funnel stage.** Prospecting and retargeting want different visuals. Be specific about where in the funnel this one lives.
3. **Make the production-quality call on purpose.** Lo-fi vs. polished isn't a taste question — it follows from the format, the audience, and how much trust the visual has to manufacture.
4. **Call out the hierarchy explicitly.** When you describe a visual, say what's primary, what's secondary, what's tertiary. If you can't, the hierarchy probably isn't there yet.
5. **Run the half-second test out loud.** Can someone name the category and the benefit in half a second? If not, simplify before anything else.

## Reading it back before it ships

Before finalizing any visual recommendation, check it against the eight:

- Does it pass the half-second clarity test?
- Is the hierarchy clear — does the eye know where to go first?
- Does it break the pattern without creating confusion?
- Can the visual carry the message with the sound off?
- Is there human presence where it would help?
- Is the emotion specific, not generic positivity?
- Does it feel native and contextual to the feed?
- Does it pre-qualify the click?
- Is it cognitively easy to process?

A recommendation that misses several of these is usually leaning on the copy to do the visual's job — which is the one thing the copy can't do.

## Making the invisible visible (AI visuals)

AI generation unlocks a visual move that's hard to shoot: **intentionally unrealistic images that show what people feel but can't say.** The principle is *make the invisible visible* — take an internal state, a feeling, a frustration, a pain, and turn it into a literal image the feed can't ignore. Walking on nails for a pain the customer lives with. Writing the problem onto a surface — skin, a wall — so it's suddenly external and undeniable. A post-it note that says the quiet part out loud. The image should always be visually relevant to the brand's actual problem or promise, not surreal for its own sake.

This leans on two of the principles above: emotional specificity (principle 5), because you're rendering a *specific* felt state, and pattern interruption (principle 3), because the literalized feeling is a genuine break from a feed of realistic footage. It's especially strong as pain-point b-roll. The technique is a creative-direction idea; the prompt mechanics that produce these images live in the AI generation docs below.
