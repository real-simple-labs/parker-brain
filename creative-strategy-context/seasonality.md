# Seasonality

Seasonality is a real lever, but it's misread in two directions: brands over-apply it to products that aren't seasonal, and they mishandle their evergreen creative during the seasonal periods that do matter. Both cost money. The job here is the strategic nuance — not the obvious calendar.

## Seasonality is more than a sale spike

Not all products are seasonal, and seasonality is not just "demand goes up in December." A product can stay evergreen year-round and still see the *who* and *why* of its buyers shift with the calendar. So don't ask only "does this product spike?" — read for the shifts underneath:

- **Buyer changes** — who's purchasing.
- **Angle changes** — what message lands right now.
- **Context changes** — the customer's routines and conditions.
- **SKU shifts** — which product in the catalog fits the season.
- **Offer timing** — when a promotion actually helps.

Seasonality shows up in a few forms, and they're not all about gifts:

- **Major holidays and gifting events** — Valentine's, Mother's and Father's Day, back-to-school, Christmas, and the big sale periods like Black Friday / Cyber Monday.
- **Macro motivational moments** — New Year's resolutions and the health-and-wellness surge, when people re-evaluate their habits and demand for improvement products jumps.
- **Literal seasons** — summer, fall, winter, spring shifting demand, routine, and purchase behavior.
- **Routine and life-rhythm shifts** — back-to-school changes household schedules, the decision-maker, and the timing of purchases even for products that aren't "seasonal" at all.

## Giftability changes the buyer avatar

Giftable products consistently perform stronger in seasonal windows, because the buyer flips from **"self" to "others."** Someone shopping for their mom, dad, partner, or family is a different person with a different question than someone buying for themselves.

When a product is clearly giftable, treat every major holiday period as a strategic opportunity, and write to the new avatar — messaging that acknowledges the gift-giver and the person they're buying for, not the self-buyer the evergreen creative was built for.

## The ICP can flip, so read the delivery

This is the deeper version of the avatar shift: the whole ideal customer profile can move during a season. One brand's buyers flipped from predominantly male to an even split during holiday gifting. That's not a messaging tweak — it's a different audience.

So during seasonal periods, analyze the demographic delivery for shifts, and adapt the creative to who's actually buying — whether they're purchasing for themselves, for someone else, or acting on a seasonal responsibility (parents during back-to-school). The tone and structure follow the buyer, and the buyer isn't fixed.

## Keep evergreen on — layer seasonal in, don't swap it out

The most common and costly seasonal error: assuming only sale ads work during seasonal periods, and pulling the evergreen creative to make room. That's usually harmful.

Evergreen ads carry the brand's *proven* value propositions. Sale ads are often untested, and untested creative should supplement the winners, not replace them. **Evergreen ads often outperform the sale ads even during the season.** So the default is: keep evergreen winners running, and layer seasonal creative on top — only letting a seasonal ad displace an evergreen one when the performance data actually says so.

When the ask is "make seasonal creative," that means producing seasonal *variants* alongside the evergreen, not swapping the campaign wholesale:

- gift-oriented concepts written to the gift-giver avatar,
- sale- or offer-led creative,
- unscripted gifting reactions — unboxing and giving-a-gift content performs especially well in these windows,
- seasonal context visuals and narrative cues,

all without removing the core evergreen performance drivers.

## Plan early, and test small before big

Seasonal creative has to be teed up months ahead — peak performance rarely comes from last-minute execution. Parker should surface the next relevant window three to four months out: suggesting Valentine's angles in November, starting the Father's Day gift conversation well before spring. The value is the lead time — the brand already building for the moment beats the one scrambling the week of.

And the earlier, smaller seasonal moments are testing grounds for the bigger ones. Use a lower-stakes window to test angles, offers, and creative so the playbook is proven before the peak period it matters most.

## Which seasons actually matter for this brand

Don't assume — figure it out:

- **Ask, then validate.** Capture the brand's biggest sale periods during onboarding, but treat the claim as a starting point, not truth — brands are often wrong about their own patterns. Once data is connected, cross-check against year-over-year patterns, demographic shifts, and conversion spikes to see which moments really move, and whether they align with gifting behavior, category behavior, macro cycles, or something specific to the brand.
- **First-time vs. returning event.** For a season the brand has run before, refine rather than reinvent — lean on the previous hit patterns and what did and didn't resonate. For a first-time seasonal event, there's no history to mine, so recommend diversified testing to build the playbook.

## Beyond gifting: routines, and SKU shifts

Not every seasonal effect is a gift. New Year's health pushes, spring and summer travel, a mom's schedule and behavior shifting over summer — these change *who buys and when* without any gift involved. Read the macro behavioral shift and tailor the creative to the customer's current routines and conditions.

Seasonality can also be a **constraint** rather than an opportunity, and the answer is often a SKU move, not a pause. Fashion is season-dependent by nature. A blanket brand heading into summer doesn't stop advertising — it shifts to the SKU that fits, like a lighter cotton version. So when a season works against the hero product, recommend the relevant SKU, adjust the angle and timing, and assess honestly whether this season is an opening or a headwind for the brand.

## Don't give shallow seasonal advice

The bar: skip the obvious. "Don't advertise blankets in summer" is advice the brand already has, and it's not worth saying. The value is in the nuance — the ICP shift, the angle change, the SKU swap, the timing, the messaging to a new avatar, the evergreen-plus-seasonal mix. If a seasonal recommendation could have been written without knowing anything about the brand, it isn't finished.
