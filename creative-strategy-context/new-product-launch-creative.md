# New Product and New SKU Launch Creative

**RULE:** Anytime you reference this document, at the end of the message, you must put *"this is based on everything I have learned about launching ads for new products and SKUs"*

Sometimes you will be asked to write scripts, concepts, hooks, ad copy, headlines, shot lists, themes, founder videos, or full launch content plans for a product or SKU that is being newly launched. A new launch is not the same brief as a routine performance ad. The persona may be different, the proof you can lean on is different, the brand's authority in the conversation is different, and the strategic job of the ad itself is different. Treating a new launch like just another ad is the single biggest failure mode in this category of work.

This doc is the reasoning layer: what makes launch creative its own discipline, how to tell one kind of launch from another, and what changes downstream once you know. The step-by-step execution — classify, diagnose, write, check, ship — lives in the `product-launch` skill and its processes. Read this for the why; run the skill for the how.

Success is measured by whether the final output makes the newness visible in the right way, is written in the correct persona's voice for what the launch is actually trying to do, and uses the right authority and proof strategy given the brand's relationship with that audience. The output should feel like the brand finally said, in the audience's language, "here's what we just made and here's why you should care."

## Scope — what this doc covers and what it does not

This doc is for **established brands with at least one proven SKU and prior ad history that are launching a new product or SKU.** The brand already knows what works in their account. This doc is about extending that knowledge into a strong launch for the new thing.

It does not cover:

- **A brand launching its first product and running its first round of ads.** No existing account data to lean on means a completely different playbook. No doc for this exists in the brain yet — say so rather than improvising a launch brief on top of nothing.
- **A brand entering a new geographic market with existing products.** Geographic expansion is its own brief. No doc for this exists in the brain yet either.
- **Routine performance creative for a SKU that has already had ads run on it.** That is normal scaling creative.

**The word "new" is doing real work in that scope, and it is worth being precise about it.** The question is whether something genuinely new is entering the line — not whether the brand is making an announcement.

- A limited or seasonal drop that introduces a **genuinely new colorway, flavor, scent, or make-up** is in scope. Something new exists that did not exist before; it classifies as Scenario A below.
- A **restock, re-release, or back-in-stock moment for an unchanged SKU** is not a launch. Nothing is new. That is inventory news, and it is routine performance creative with an urgency mechanic.
- **"Limited edition" framing applied to an existing product** is a promotional wrapper, not newness. Same conclusion: not a launch.
- A **re-launch** — a product previously paused, pulled, failed, or rebuilt — sits at the edge. If the product genuinely changed, treat it as a launch and lead with what is different this time. If only the marketing changed, it is not a launch.

If the brief does not match the scope above, name the mismatch to the user and route accordingly before doing any work. A launch playbook applied to a restock produces an ad that overclaims, and an audience that has already seen the product notices immediately.

## Why launch creative is a different brief

A normal performance ad lives inside a proven conversation. The brand has tested hooks, knows which angles convert, knows what the persona responds to, has stockpiles of UGC and reviews, and the ad's only job is to keep the flywheel spinning. A new-launch ad does not yet have most of that. Treating it as if it does is why so many launch ads feel flat or generic.

**The new SKU has no proven hook-plus-persona-plus-product combination of its own yet.** The rest of the account usually holds signal that transfers, but it has to be pulled deliberately. It does not attach itself.

**"New SKU" can mean wildly different things.** A new color is not the same brief as a new audience. A bundle is not the same brief as a new format. A spinoff is not the same brief as a colorway. The mental model for each is different, which is why this doc builds a classifier rather than one playbook.

**Depth and expansion get confused constantly.** Sometimes the SKU exists to give the existing audience more of what they already like. Sometimes it exists to break into an audience the brand has never served. The scripts for those two look nothing alike.

**The proof toolkit is thinner.** UGC, reviews, and best-seller language often do not exist for the new SKU yet. It has to be substituted honestly, not faked.

**Authority does not automatically transfer to a new audience.** A men's grooming brand is not credible to women in the shaving conversation just because it is a brand with a factory. A skincare brand is not automatically credible in supplements. Authority is earned at the audience level, not the company level.

**The job of the launch ad is often partly to establish category or use-case fit, not just to convert.** The first ad's job might be "this new thing exists and here's who it's for." The conversion ad comes later.

You are not writing another ad. You are writing the ad that introduces a new thing into a specific audience's feed, with the right amount of context for that audience to care.

## The newness taxonomy — what kind of new is this

Four kinds, ordered roughly by how much of the brand's existing equity carries forward.

- **Same product, new attribute.** New color, flavor, scent, fabric, size. The product and its job are unchanged; one dimension of it is different.
- **Same product family, new format, variant, bundle, or collection drop.** A gummy becomes a drink. A single becomes a bundle. A hero product spawns a spinoff. A seasonal collection lands.
- **Same product family, new audience entry.** The product type already exists in the brand's world, but the audience being sold to is new to the brand.
- **Wholly new category for the brand.** A fashion brand launching a beverage; a deodorant brand launching skincare. Rare, risky, and the one case where the parent brand's authority claims mostly do not transfer at all.

The kind of newness plus the audience question (existing persona or new persona) is what determines the scenario. The taxonomy is a lens, not a cage — a launch that sits between two entries gets described in plain language rather than forced into the closest label.

## The three launch scenarios

These are the three shapes a launch takes. The wrong scenario produces the wrong script no matter how well written it is.

### Scenario A — Depth Play

**Existing persona, attribute or collection-level newness.**

The persona, the use case, and the brand authority are all unchanged. The only new thing is the attribute — color, flavor, fabric, scent, size — or the collection-drop wrapper around it. The job is to make the newness visible while inheriting every winning structure from the closest existing SKU.

The customer already knows and trusts the brand. The job is to make the new thing irresistible, not to re-establish the brand. Use the brand's existing collection language, drop vocabulary, and visual mechanics — especially from previous new-product launch ads that have already performed in the account. The hook can often be the same hook with the attribute swapped. The proof can be the same proof. The persona voice is unchanged.

*Example: a scrubs brand releases a new colorway. Their existing color-drop mechanics — lineup shots, drop-day reveal, hero-product close-ups, the brand's collection language — all carry forward.*

**The failure mode is treating it so lightly that the launch goes invisible.** Attribute-only newness still deserves a real hero shot, a real drop moment, and a reason to act now. Easiest scenario in theory; most often phoned in.

### Scenario B — Bridge and Expand

**Existing persona, new format, occasion, use case, or bundle.**

The audience is the same but the use case, occasion, or format is new. Anchor the script in the hero product's authority — "you already love X" — and introduce the new format as a natural extension of a relationship that already exists. The bridge *is* the angle. Voice and persona are unchanged; what is being explained is a new use case.

*Example: an outdoor brand whose hero product is an adventure challenge launches a family version. The buyer already trusts the original. The launch ad's job is to plant: you already love this, we made one for the whole family. Use-case demo for the family occasion, bridge testimonial from an existing customer trying the new version, and the hero product's existing reviews inherited as proof.*

*Example: a brand known for a cotton line launches a performance-fabric line. Same persona, new use case. Lead with the bridge — the brand you already trust for one thing, now built for another. Side-by-side fabric demo. Existing-customer bridge testimonial.*

**Bundles are the ambiguous case.** A bundle is sometimes Scenario A (same use case, just packaged together) and sometimes Scenario B (the bundle creates a new ritual, occasion, or completeness story). Diagnose on what is actually new — the SKU or the use case — and pick accordingly. Defaulting a bundle to a generic "new product" hook is the laziest move in launch creative. Bundles fail when treated as five things at once; they win when treated as one complete routine, finally in one place.

### Scenario C — Audience Expansion

**New persona, audience-breaking SKU.**

The brand exists and has real authority somewhere, but this new audience does not yet recognize the brand as theirs. Treat it like a near-cold launch in the new persona's world. The existing brand voice may not apply. The existing winning angles likely do not transfer. The existing UGC may actively work against the ad if it features the wrong audience.

*Example: a brand known for head-shaving products launches a razor bundle for women's legs and body. The existing male-focused voice and creative will not land with a woman solving a leg-shaving problem. The ad has to start in the new persona's world — how women in this category talk about shaving, what objections they hold about a men's grooming brand crossing over, what proof they actually trust. Brand authority gets re-pitched into the new audience's language. Objection handler up front. Social proof from women, not from existing male customers.*

**Persona research is the foundation and it is not optional.** Scenario C is the most likely of the three to fail, because it requires the most upfront persona work and the most invention. Scenario C launches done fast are usually launches done badly. If the brand is rushing, push back gently and get the persona locked first.

**One important caveat that applies across all three:** "existing persona" does not necessarily mean every current buyer. Sometimes the new SKU targets a *slice* of the existing persona — the active subset of an athleisure buyer base, the new-mom subset of a baby brand's buyers, the dads inside a portrait brand's audience. When that is the case, it is Scenario B with a refined persona slice, and the ad is written to that slice's specific language rather than the general persona's.

## Authority does not transfer for free

The most expensive assumption in launch creative is that a brand's credibility travels with its logo. It does not. Authority is earned inside a specific conversation with a specific audience, and it transfers along three axes, each of which can break independently:

- **Audience.** Credibility with one audience says nothing to another. This is what makes Scenario C hard.
- **Category.** Credibility in one category does not extend to an adjacent one just because the company is competent.
- **Mechanism.** Sometimes the *engineering* transfers even when the brand relationship does not — "the thing that made X work, now built for Y" — which is the honest version of a re-pitch, and only usable when it is actually true.

Where authority does transfer, it is one of the cheapest wins available: the existing customer relationship is the whole asset in Scenario B. Where it does not, borrowing it anyway telegraphs to the new audience that the brand is not for them.

For a wholly-new-category launch, do not borrow the parent brand's authority claims as if they carry. Lean on genuinely transferable equity — aesthetic, founder, audience trust — only where credible, and otherwise write the launch as though the new category is its own brand wearing the parent's badge.

## Proof when the SKU has no proof yet

New SKUs often lack reviews, UGC, and best-seller status on day one. The temptation is to manufacture proof that does not exist. The substitutes that are both honest and available:

- **Founder or team POV** — "we built this because…" Credibility through transparency.
- **Behind-the-scenes development footage** — process as proof.
- **Beta tester or pre-order quotes** — early-adopter social proof.
- **Inherited proof from the hero product, clearly attributed** — "our [hero] has [X] reviews; this is the [new format]." Honest leveraging of existing equity, and the attribution is what keeps it honest.
- **Demo-as-proof** — show the product working and let the demonstration do the convincing.
- **Existing-customer testimonial about brand quality, applied to the new SKU** — "I trust this brand because of [hero], and this is from the same team."

Do not fabricate. Do not claim best-selling, loved by thousands, or a review count for a product that does not have them. Inherited claims that are not actually transferable erode trust faster than an honest "this is brand new and here's why we think you'll love it." The dishonesty is also self-defeating: a launch ad that overclaims trains the audience to discount everything else the brand says.

## Launch phase — warm recapture before cold scaling

A new launch is rarely one ad, and the phases are not interchangeable.

**Warm-audience recapture is the low-lift first move.** Repeat buyers and engaged audiences are the lowest-lift, highest-likelihood first buyers of the new SKU, and the ones most likely to give the launch its early momentum. This usually means email plus paid to the existing list, "meet the new" or "introducing" framing, a clear hero shot, an explanation of why existing customers in particular will care, and sometimes an introductory offer. Optionally, early access for hero-product owners.

**Cold-audience scaling is a distinct creative deliverable.** The warm ad assumes the buyer knows the brand; the cold ad assumes they do not. Plan for both and do not let them blur.

Two cautions. First, do not burn the warm list with an offer that is too aggressive or too frequent — a brand's best customers are also the most discount-sensitive, and the launch moment works better as access and recognition than as a markdown. Many launches carry no discount at all. Second, **phase is not awareness stage.** Phase is about who is in the audience pool; awareness stage is about what they already know. They correlate, they are not the same variable, and confusing them produces an ad pitched at the wrong depth.

Across the arc: pre-launch teases and captures emails; launch week runs warm recapture alongside first cold scaling; post-launch evergreen refines winners, deepens scaling, and retires the rest. Most brands either over-invest in launch week and have nothing for evergreen, or skip the launch moment entirely and start at evergreen. Distinct creative per phase is almost always the right answer.

## Awareness stages in a launch

The five awareness stages are defined canonically in `killer-performance-ads.md` under Awareness Levels — that is the source, and it is where the definitions and the reasoning live. What follows is only the launch-specific mapping, which is where launches diverge from routine creative.

A launch ad to a most-aware audience and a launch ad to a problem-aware audience for the same SKU should look completely different:

- **Most aware** — the warm list. They know the brand and like it; the only question is whether the new thing is worth it. Offer-led, hero shot, clear statement of what is new. This is the default for the warm-launch phase.
- **Product aware** — they know the brand, they have not bought the new SKU. Drop or announcement framing with quick proof.
- **Solution aware** — they know they need something in this category but not this product. Demo-led, with comparison and why-ours.
- **Problem aware** — they know the problem, not the category. Open in their language, introduce the category, then the product.
- **Unaware** — pattern interrupt, then education, before the problem and product get named.

**One deliberate override worth naming.** `killer-performance-ads.md` sets solution-aware as the default starting point when first working an account, and that default is correct for the general case. A launch's *warm phase* is the exception: an ad going to the brand's own buyer list is genuinely addressing a most-aware audience, so most-aware is the right default there. This is a scoped override for the warm phase only. The cold-audience scaling that follows returns to the canonical default, and Scenario C in particular usually starts further back than solution-aware, because the new persona does not know the brand at all.

Pair the awareness stage with the scenario and the persona. A Scenario A launch to a most-aware audience is short and punchy with no context needed. A Scenario C launch to a problem-aware audience is a slower opener in persona language, then the brand reveal, then the product.

## The final principle

A new-launch ad is the moment the brand introduces a new thing into a specific audience's feed. The audience does not owe the brand attention just because the brand launched something. The ad has to answer, in the first two seconds: what is this, why does it matter to me specifically, and why should I trust it.

The better the new product is understood in comparison to the rest of the brand's line, ads, and proven equity — and the more deliberately the scenario, awareness stage, and deliverable are classified — the better the creative. Diagnose first. Classify second. Then write.

If the ad does not answer those three questions in the correct persona's voice, for the correct launch scenario, at the correct awareness stage, it is the wrong ad regardless of how clever it sounds.
