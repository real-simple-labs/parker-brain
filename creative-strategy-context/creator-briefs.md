# Creator briefs

A creator brief is the direction handed to the person who'll actually film the ad. The whole craft of it is one tension: how much to lock down versus how much to leave open. Get that call right and the creator does their best work; get it wrong and you either kill the spark with over-scripting or get something off-brand from too little guidance.

This is the brief-*writing* layer. The upstream reasoning — what an idea, a concept, and a brief each are, and the rule that unscripted moments need open-ended briefs — lives in `ideation-and-brainstorming.md`; read it first. This doc is about how to actually shape the brief once you know what the concept is.

## Freedom vs. direction is a multi-variable call

There is no one-size-fits-all brief. How much freedom to give is a decision you make per situation, weighing the creator, the concept, and the format. Two poles to place a brief between:

- **Freestyle brief** — talking points, a reference, a direction, and room for the creator to bring their own spin. Right when the power of the ad is the creator's natural, unscripted reaction.
- **Director's brief** — heavily scripted, closer to a shot-by-shot. Right when the concept depends on precise beats, claims, or timing.

The move that decides most briefs:

- **Authorities do better with more freedom.** A credible expert or a strong personality performs worse on a tight script and better with room to talk in their own voice. The more the ad rides on who the person is, the looser the brief should be.

## What makes a brief land

- **Clear references help massively.** A reference video does more than a paragraph of description — it shows the tone of voice, the setup, the pacing, the energy you're after. Lead with references wherever you can.
- **Give a story structure.** Even a freestyle brief should hand the creator a shape to build on — the arc the content should follow — rather than leaving them to invent structure from scratch. Direction on *structure* and freedom on *delivery* is often the right split.
- **Speak the creator's language.** Briefs work better when they use the shared vocabulary creators already film to. Parker should carry and use a running list of these terms, for example:
  - **POV** — first-person camera, as if the viewer is living the moment.
  - **Handheld selfie** — creator holding the camera at arm's length, talking to it.
  - **Direct-to-camera** — creator speaking straight into the lens.
  - **B-roll** — supplementary footage cut over the talking (the product, the hands, the setting).
  - (This vocabulary grows — add terms as briefs use them, and pair each with a plain description so a creator reads it the same way you meant it.)
