# Emotional Delivery and Timing — the layer beneath hooks

Most creative strategy is built to *generate* emotion: the better hook, the sharper story, the truer customer language. This doc is about what happens to that emotion *after* it is generated — whether it lands, sticks, and moves someone, or dissipates, creates resistance, or arrives at the wrong moment. Two diagnostics govern that layer: the **emotional state the viewer is already in** when the creative reaches her, and **where she is in her internal decision** (TEEP). Good creative that ignores this layer keeps hitting a ceiling no amount of hook/offer/targeting testing breaks. This is the why; `hook-psychology.md` is the mechanism layer it builds on, and the skills (`hooks`, `scriptwriting`, `iterations`) and `brief-creation.md` are where it gets executed.

## The emotional landing state — emotion lands *into* a state, not a vacuum

The viewer arrives in an emotional state she did not choose for you, and that state actively shapes how much of your message she can take in:

- **High-arousal states** (excitement, urgency, fear) **narrow** attention onto whatever is in front of her — useful for driving an immediate action, but they *suppress* the reflective processing that builds memory, identification, and meaning.
- **Low-arousal states** (calm, fatigue, mild disappointment) **expand** attention and engage self-referential processing (the default-mode network) — the state where someone takes information and makes it part of their identity.

So the *same asset, experienced in two different states, is neurologically two different pieces of creative.* The question is not only which emotion your creative creates, but **what emotional environment it lands in, and whether the two are in a productive relationship.** The distance between the state your ad assumes and the state she's actually in is where your CPA lives.

For the two-dial model underneath this — **valence × intensity** and the four zones (Soft / Hype / Bummer / Panic), plus the headspace-at-exposure rule — see `hook-psychology.md` #11; it is not repeated here. The one addition worth stating: the quadrant most DTC brands underinvest in is **low-intensity positive (Zone 1)** — empathetic, reflective, emotionally honest creative that acknowledges difficulty without amplifying it. It is uncomfortable to make, so it's rare, and it tends to outperform **mid-funnel** precisely because it creates the identification high-intensity creative can't.

## TEEP — where she is in her internal decision

**Trigger → Exploration → Evaluation → Purchase.** This is distinct from funnel stage and awareness level. Awareness/funnel describe what someone has *done* (what they're already aware of). TEEP describes what cognitive and emotional work they are *doing right now* — and that internal state determines what kind of message they are actually capable of receiving.

- **Trigger** — the moment a need becomes conscious. She isn't shopping; she's making sense of something she's experiencing in real life. Creative that **mirrors her internal state back to her with precision** outperforms creative that introduces a solution before the problem has been fully felt.
- **Exploration** — active looking: comparing, categorizing, building a mental map of what exists. The cognitive work is *differentiation*. What lands is **brand feeling, POV, and the emotional texture of what it's like to be a customer** — not features or offers.
- **Evaluation** — managing risk and resolving the one objection sitting between her and a decision. She is *not* looking for more information. This is where most conversion creative goes wrong: **applying pressure or over-explaining your worth here makes her decide you're not the one.** Name the specific hesitation instead.
- **Purchase** — the decision is made or nearly made. The creative now needs to **subtract, not add**: remove anything subjective, give a clear path to what she wants, and deliver without overhyping a decision she's already making.

The point: the same message, written the same way, lands differently depending on the phase. Most creative is written for one phase and deployed across all four — doing the right thing in the wrong moment for most of the people who see it. Match the message to the phase.

## The funnel as an emotional arc

A messaging sequence (broad → specific, brand → offer, awareness → conversion) is a *logical* structure. It is not an emotional arc. Movement happens through emotional transitions — stranger → curious → interested → evaluating → decided — and each transition requires something emotional to happen. Design the creative at each stage around facilitating **one specific shift**: what state does she arrive in, what does she need to hear to take the next step, and what tone / pacing / intensity / valence / format / creator creates that movement.

## How Parker should use this

- **Audit for valence + intensity, and for the landing state.** Map the brand's last ~90 days of creative on the valence×intensity grid by the state each asset *actually produces in a cold viewer*, not what it was briefed to do — clustering in one or two zones usually explains funnel gaps that targeting and offer can't. Then, separately, name the state the audience is most likely *in* when each asset reaches them. Where the state created and the state arrived-in are far apart, that's a delivery problem better creative won't fix.
- **Feed TEEP into the brief.** Name the phase and the emotional shift the asset must create (see `brief-creation.md`). This is the single highest-leverage intervention in the article and lives there.
- **Treat pacing as a cognitive variable, not an aesthetic one.** Fast cuts hold high arousal and narrow attention (good for "receive and act on a clear message"); slow pacing opens the reflective processing that *feeling something* requires. Match pace to whether you want action or identification.
- **Separate context from intent when setting intensity.** Cold/warm/hot differ in how much context *and* how much intent they carry; both decide which intensity lands. Ask: does she have the context to receive this message at this intensity, and does her intent match the pacing and frame?
- **Retarget around the internal conversation, not the behavior.** "Visited the PDP, didn't buy" is an action; the customer is *managing* something — a specific objection, a competing priority, a trust gap. That something lives in reviews, support tickets, DMs, and post-purchase surveys. Creative that names the hesitation converts differently than creative that adds pressure or restates benefits. This is a research question before it's a creative one.

## Source and confidence

The mechanism is established psychology — arousal's effect on attentional breadth and on memory/reflective processing, the default-mode network and self-referential encoding, and the circumplex model of affect (valence and arousal). TEEP is a practitioner model, corroborated across sources (the 12-combination "TEE funnel lens" and the emotional-delivery article) and consistent with established decision-process and elaboration-likelihood thinking. Applied to ads it is **inferred** mechanism, not per-asset causation — a reasoning lens that earns its keep against the brand's real performance data. The specific performance figures the source articles quote (CAC swings, engagement multiples) are stated, not verified, and are not Parker facts.
