# Creative strategy by brand size

What a brand should be doing with its creative changes a lot with how much it spends. A brand doing $10k a month and a brand doing $1m a month have different problems, so the same advice can be right for one and wrong for the other. The spend bands below are rough guides, not hard cutoffs — read the brand's real constraints (spend, testing capacity, how proven the message is) rather than treating a dollar figure as a rule. The round-sizing math that operationalizes this lives in `ideation-and-brainstorming.md`.

## ~$10k/month — get shots on goal

At this size the job is to get ideas on the board and find out what works. The brand usually doesn't yet know *what* angles land, so the whole game is discovery.

- **Go for the go-tos.** Check the playbooks and run the proven format defaults — this isn't the moment for exotic swings.
- **Test messaging cheaply.** Statics or short-form video are the efficient way to identify which personas and which angles actually convert. You're looking for the winners, not polishing them yet.
- **Then upgrade the winners.** Once an angle proves out, turn the best performers into proper video ads. Find it cheap, then invest behind it.
- **It's performance-driven.** The read is more directly about what's working and what isn't — getting shots on goal so something goes in.

## ~$50k/month — volume, founders, and new audiences

Now the brand has some proven angles and the problems shift to scaling and reaching beyond the people already inclined to buy.

- **The founder story becomes usable.** At this size a startup founder is still relatable, so founder ads start to work — *make more of them*. (At very large brands the founder is too disconnected to feel relatable, so this window matters.)
- **Volume matters more.** Consistent creative output becomes a real lever, not an occasional push.
- **Reaching new audiences becomes critical.** Don't just run statics that sweep up existing demand — those harvest people already likely to buy. The question to keep asking is *which ads bring in new audiences and which don't.* Cost per 1,000 accounts reached (CPMR) is a useful read on which ads are actually hitting fresh eyes versus recycling the same audience (see `ad-account-analysis.md`).
- **Check what you think you know about the customer.** A lot of brands at this stage are sure they know who their customer is and are actually wrong. This is exactly why indexing customer reviews, ad comments, and the like matters — the real customer is often not the assumed one.

## ~$1m/month — big swings and new personas

Big brands often want "big creative swings" — creativity for its own sake. Some of that is vanity, but at this volume there are genuinely valid reasons to swing hard.

- **Unlocking new personas is the huge unlock.** For a brand already spending a lot, opening a new audience is where the next leg of growth is — for example, an established denim brand starting to advertise to older audiences (see `advertising-to-older-audiences.md` for that specific play). This is the swing most worth making.
- **Controversial hooks work better here.** They tend to land for big brands and for brands carrying a lot of negative reviews — exposing a category leader, deinfluencing angles, "is this well-known product even worth it?" You're playing on the awareness the brand already has; the size is what makes the controversy land.
- **Net-new over iterative.** At this scale the appetite is for net-new concepts rather than iterating existing ones, and the volume supports pushing for wilder creative swings.
