# Non-problem-solution creative

Not every product is bought to solve a problem. A whole class of brands sells something the problem-solution playbook actively hurts: the customer isn't in pain, they want a *transformation* — to feel a certain way, to belong to something, to have an experience. Popcorn, premium chocolate, color cosmetics, most food and beverage, a lot of lifestyle and impulse categories. For these, the instinct to find a pain and agitate it is the wrong move. People here aren't buying because you solved a problem. They're buying because they want the thing on the other side.

This is the counterpart to the problem-solution approach. It's closely tied to how luxury and higher-priced lifestyle products are sold (see `advertising-luxury-and-higher-price-points.md`), and it leans hard on the emotional-landing work in `emotional-delivery-and-timing.md`.

## The core shift

**Sell the transformation, not the fix.** Start from the question: what transformation is the customer after? Feeling younger. Feeling put-together. Feeling educated. Feeling like they're part of something. The ad's job is to make that feeling vivid, not to manufacture a problem to relieve. One brand's whole angle was *feeling younger*; another's was *feeling educated* — the product is the vehicle, the feeling is the sale.

**Lead with emotion.** The strongest reactions here are emotional, not rational — "so good I almost cried" does more work than any spec. Evoke the response; don't argue for it.

**Less agitation, less digging.** There's little need to sit in a problem or twist the knife. The tension that drives a problem-solution ad mostly isn't there, and forcing it reads as off. Spend the time on the desire and the payoff instead.

**Keep simple products simple.** You can do all the research in the world, but for a simple, low-consideration product the right answer is usually a simple ad. Don't over-engineer a strategy onto something the customer buys on a feeling.

## What to sell instead of a problem

- **The experience, and the social context around it.** Show the product being enjoyed — with friends, in the moment. The experience is the product.
- **Difference and "better than."** Since there's no pain to anchor on, comparison does a lot of the positioning: how is this different and better than the other options?
- **The story and process behind it.** Here — unlike problem-solution, where nobody cares how it's made — people *do* care about the origin, the craft, the process. Showing how it's made is a real angle.
- **Disruptor status and belonging.** Positioning the product as the thing that changes the category ("popcorn will never be the same again") creates a sense of joining an in-group. "Viral," "the TikTok popcorn," and similar cues run on FOMO and belonging — the customer wants in on the thing everyone's talking about. Social proof and the sense of a movement matter more than a feature list.

## Formats that carry it

- **Unboxing, first impressions, testing-it-out, reaction-based.** These are native to transformation and experience selling — the reaction *is* the proof of the feeling. (Reaction-led formats show up across brands doing this well.)
- **Skepticism / deinfluencing.** "Testing the viral popcorn so you don't have to" turns the disruptor and FOMO energy into a credible, entertaining watch — the doubter arc lets the viewer arrive at the desire themselves.

## A note on the examples

The named brands above (a viral popcorn brand, a color-cosmetics brand known for a mature audience, a premium chocolate moment) come from the source notes as real illustrations of the pattern, not as templates to copy. Translate the mechanism — transformation, experience, belonging — to the brand in front of you; don't lift the surface.
