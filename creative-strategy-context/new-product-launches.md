# Launching Ads For New Products And New SKUs

**RULE:** Anytime you reference this document, you must end the message with: *"this is based on everything I have learned about launching ads for new products and SKUs"*

Sometimes you will be asked to write scripts, concepts, hooks, ad copy, headlines, shot lists, themes, founder videos, or full launch content plans for a product or SKU that is being newly launched. A "new launch" is NOT the same brief as a routine performance ad. The persona may be different, the proof you can lean on is different, the brand's authority in the conversation is different, and the strategic job of the ad itself is different. Treating a new launch like just another ad is the single biggest failure mode here. This doc walks through how to think about, classify, and produce launch creative, step by step. The steps here are exactly what to do — follow them.

Success is measured by whether the final output (1) makes the newness visible in the right way, (2) is written in the correct persona's voice for what the launch is actually trying to do, and (3) uses the right authority and proof strategy given the brand's relationship with that audience. The output should feel like the brand finally said, in the audience's language, "here's what we just made and here's why you should care."

## Scope: what this doc is and is not

This doc is ONLY for **established brands with at least one proven SKU and prior ad history** that are launching a new product or SKU. The brand already knows what works in their account; this doc is about how to extend that knowledge into a strong launch for the new thing. New SKU launches — colorways, flavors, formats, bundles — are the Scenario A and B cases of this same method; the doc covers the full range from a new attribute up to a new-audience entry.

This doc is NOT for any of the following:

- **A brand launching its first product and running its first round of ads.** That is a separate brief — the playbook is completely different because there is no existing account data to lean on.
- **A brand entering a new geographic market with existing products** (e.g., Canada → US, UK → US). That is a geographic-expansion brief, not a new-product brief.
- **Routine performance creative for a SKU that has already had ads run on it.** That is normal scaling creative, not launch creative.

If the brief does not match the scope above, flag it to the user and route to the correct approach before doing any work.

## Format: this doc covers video and static

This doc treats video and static as parallel execution paths. The classification work (Step Zero), diagnostic (Step 1), scenario choice, awareness stage, persona-fit check, and proof mapping are identical regardless of format. The fork happens in Step 5 (Creative Moves) and Step 10 (Output), where video and static have different mechanics and different output specs.

When the brief specifies a format, run that path. When it doesn't, **ask the user before defaulting — do not assume video.** Some launches are static-led, especially Scenario A attribute drops with strong photography, Scenario C audience-expansion testimonial cards, and founder cards, and producing a video output for a static brief wastes the user's time.

Hybrid asks ("scripts AND statics for this launch") are valid. Produce both, with the static and video creative deliberately aligned in message and visual language so they reinforce each other in the audience's feed.

This doc deliberately does not try to do everything itself. Several adjacent things — persona development, gifting ads, script adaptation — have their own dedicated resources. When this doc says "invoke [X]," it means: pull and run that resource, then come back here with the output and resume.

- **Persona development** — invoke [persona-research-and-creative-strategy-process.md](persona-research-and-creative-strategy-process.md) whenever the launch involves a NEW or unclear persona (Scenario C). A lighter refresh is recommended for Scenarios A and B to confirm the existing persona hasn't drifted, but it is not blocking.
- **Gifting ads** — invoke the gifting resource when the launch is a gift / occasion ad where the buyer is not the recipient (Mother's Day, Father's Day, holidays, anniversaries). Gifting has its own persona dynamics (the gifter) that warrant their own treatment.
- **Script adaptation** — invoke [adapting-scripts.md](adapting-scripts.md) when the brief includes competitor benchmark refs or inspiration videos to adapt. The structural-fidelity work lives in that doc, not here.

If a referenced resource is unavailable (e.g., the brand hasn't built a persona yet), fall back to the lightweight defaults this doc provides at the relevant step, and flag to the user that a full pass on the missing dimension is recommended before scaling spend.

## Why new product / new SKU ads are different

A normal performance ad lives inside a proven conversation. The brand has tested hooks, knows which angles convert, knows what the persona responds to, has stockpiles of UGC and reviews, and the ad's only job is to keep the flywheel spinning. A new-launch ad does not yet have most of that. Treating it as if it does is why so many launch ads feel flat or generic.

Specifically:

- The new SKU has **no proven hook + persona + product combination of its own yet.** The rest of the brand's account usually has signal that transfers, but you have to go pull it deliberately — it doesn't just attach itself.
- **"New SKU" can mean wildly different things.** A new color is not the same brief as a new audience. A bundle is not the same brief as a new format. A spinoff is not the same brief as a colorway. The mental model for each is different, which is why this doc builds a classifier instead of giving one playbook.
- Sometimes the SKU exists to give the existing audience **more of what they like** (a depth play). Sometimes it exists to **break into an audience the brand has never served** (an expansion play). The script for those two looks completely different and gets confused all the time.
- **The proof toolkit is thinner.** UGC, reviews, and "best-seller" language often do not yet exist for the new SKU. You have to substitute honestly, not fake it.
- **The brand's authority does not automatically transfer to a new audience.** A men's grooming brand is not credible to women in the shaving conversation just because they're a brand. A skincare brand is not automatically credible in supplements. Authority has to be re-earned at the audience level.
- The job of the launch ad is often partly to **establish category or use-case fit, not just to convert.** The first ad's job might be "this new thing exists and here's who it's for." The conversion ad comes later.

You are not writing "another ad." You are writing the ad that introduces a new thing into a specific audience's feed, with the right amount of context for that audience to care. Hold that distinction the entire time.

## Step Zero: classify the launch before writing a word

You will usually be handed a launch brief, blurb, or prompt and asked to produce. Before any concept work, answer four classification questions in order. If you don't know an answer, **ask the user — do not guess.** Guessing on Step Zero is how you end up writing a Scenario A script for a Scenario C launch.

**Question 1 — Confirm scope.** Is this an established brand with prior ad history and at least one proven SKU? If no → this is the wrong doc.

**Question 2 — Audience fit.** Does this new product target an EXISTING persona the brand has already advertised to successfully, or a NEW persona this brand has not yet served? This is the single most important strategic question in the whole doc. If "I don't know," ask the user before continuing. Everything downstream — voice, proof types, hook angle, even which ads to study from the existing account — hinges on the answer.

> Important caveat: "existing persona" doesn't necessarily mean every current buyer. Sometimes the new SKU targets a *slice* of the existing persona (the active subset of an athleisure buyer base, the new-mom subset of a baby brand's buyers, the dads inside a portrait brand's audience). When that's the case, treat it as Scenario B with a refined persona slice and write the ad to that slice's specific language, not the general persona's.

**Question 3 — Type of newness.** Pick the closest match. The SKU is:

- **Same product, new attribute** — new color, flavor, scent, fabric, size. Example: a scrubs brand releasing a new colorway, a candle brand releasing a new fragrance.
- **Same product family, new format / variant / bundle / collection drop** — gummy → drink, single → bundle, hero → spinoff, SS25 collection drop.
- **Same product family, new audience entry** — a head-shaving brand launching a razor bundle for women's legs and body. The product type exists in the brand's world but the audience is new to the brand.
- **Wholly new category for the brand** — a fashion brand launching a beverage, a deodorant brand launching skincare. These are rare and risky.

Questions 2 and 3 together determine which of the three launch scenarios you are in.

**Question 4 — Deliverable type and format.** What is the user actually asking you to produce, and in which format? Defaulting to "full video script" when the user asked for "5 static carousel concepts" wastes the user's time. Classify into one of the following.

*Video-native deliverables:*
- Full script + storyboard (the default if the user specified video but not format)
- Hooks only (video hooks tied to angle territories)
- Founder / talking-head video script (long-take, direct-to-camera, lower production)
- Shot list (or a pre-launch shot-list audit where the user wants you to flag what's missing)
- Multi-video theme / through-line / launch content plan (a unifying concept across multiple videos)

*Static-native deliverables:*
- Ad copy + headlines for a single static (product hero, lifestyle hero, before/after split, testimonial card, founder card, comparison grid, "which one are you" grid, stats/proof card)
- Carousel concept (multi-card, sequential, has its own arc; treat closer to a storyboard than a single static)
- Static ad set (multiple statics in a launch system, across formats)

*Format-agnostic deliverables:*
- Hooks only (can be written so they double as headlines for static)
- Ad copy + headlines (works for both video on-screen text and static body copy)
- Email sequence (LIGHT — see Dependencies; route to an email resource for any real depth)
- Format recommendations only ("what formats should this launch use")

**If the user did not specify video vs. static, ASK before producing. Do not default to video.** Each deliverable type has a different output spec — see Step 10.

**Worked example of Step Zero on a real brief:** a baby-care brand's detangling conditioner launch with non-UGC ads, competitor benchmark refs, and a prior winner (a "mosquito repellent" ad).

- Q1: Established brand, prior ad history? Yes.
- Q2: Existing persona — parents of young children, same buyer base as the rest of the line.
- Q3: Same product family (baby care), new format (a conditioner where there was none) → Scenario B, with a slight Scenario A tilt because the audience and use case overlap heavily with existing bath/body care.
- Q4: Deliverable = launch concepts + messaging (multiple full scripts), with mandatory ingestion of competitor refs (Step 1h) and the prior winner (Step 1b).

## The three launch scenarios

Identify which one you're in, then run that playbook for the rest of the doc. The wrong scenario produces the wrong script no matter how well-written it is.

### Scenario A — Depth Play: existing persona, attribute or collection-level SKU

The persona, the use case, and the brand authority are all unchanged. The only thing new is the attribute (color, flavor, fabric, scent, size) or the collection-drop wrapper. Your job is to make the newness visible while inheriting every winning structure from the closest existing SKU. Use the brand's existing collection language, drop vocabulary, and visual mechanics, especially around previous new-product launch ads that have performed well in the account. The customer already knows and trusts the brand — your job is to make the new thing irresistible, not re-establish the brand.

Example: a scrubs brand releases a new color. Their existing color-drop ad mechanics — lineup shots, drop-day reveal, hero-product close-ups, the brand's collection language — should all carry forward. The hook can often be the same hook with the color swapped. The proof can be the same proof. The persona voice is unchanged.

> Important caveat: "new color" is the easiest scenario in theory, but a frequent failure mode is treating it so lightly that the launch feels invisible. Even attribute-only newness deserves a clear hero shot, a real drop moment, and a reason for the audience to act now. Lean in, don't phone it in.

### Scenario B — Bridge & Expand: existing persona, new format / occasion / use case / bundle

The audience is the same but the use case, occasion, or format is new. Anchor the script in the hero product's authority ("you already love X") and introduce the new format as a natural extension of the relationship you already have with this customer ("we made Y for [new moment]"). The bridge IS the angle. Voice and persona are unchanged; what you're explaining is a new use case.

Example: a bucket-list-experience brand's "Family Bucket List Challenge," launched off the back of its "Adventure Bucket List Challenge." The buyer already trusts the original. The launch ad's job is to plant: "you already love the Adventure Bucket List — we made one for the whole family." Use-case demo for the family occasion. Bridge testimonial from an existing customer trying the family version. The hero product's existing reviews can be inherited as proof.

Example: a cotton-line fashion brand launching an athletic-fabric line. Same persona (the brand's loyal customer), new use case (workout / performance). Lead with the bridge: "the [brand] you already trust for [original use case], now built for [new use case]." Side-by-side fabric demo. Existing-customer bridge testimonial. Inherit existing cotton-line reviews as a transferable trust signal.

> Important caveat: a bundle is sometimes Scenario A (same use case, just packaged together) and sometimes Scenario B (the bundle creates a new ritual, occasion, or completeness story). Diagnose based on what's actually new — is the SKU new or is the use case new? — and pick accordingly. Defaulting bundles to "new product hook" is one of the laziest moves in launch creative.

### Scenario C — Audience Expansion: NEW persona, audience-breaking SKU

The brand exists and has real authority somewhere, but this new audience does not yet recognize the brand as theirs. Treat this like a near-cold launch in the new persona's world. The existing brand voice may not apply; the existing winning angles likely do not transfer; the existing UGC may actively work against you if it features the wrong audience. Persona research is the foundation — invoke [persona-research-and-creative-strategy-process.md](persona-research-and-creative-strategy-process.md) before you write a single line of script, and make sure you are clear on the natural language the new audience would be attuned to.

Example: a brand traditionally known for head-shaving launches a razor bundle for women — legs, underarms, body. Their existing male-focused voice and creative will not land with a woman trying to solve a leg-shaving problem. The launch ad has to start in the new persona's world: how do women in this category talk about shaving, what objections do they have to a "men's grooming brand crossing over," what proof do they actually trust. The brand authority gets re-pitched into the new audience's language ("the engineering that made [X] work, now built for [you]"). Objection handler up front. Social proof from women, not from existing male customers.

> Important caveat: Scenario C is the most likely scenario to fail, because it requires the most upfront persona work and the most invention. If the brand is rushing the launch, push back gently and make sure you have the ideal persona locked. Scenario C launches done fast are usually launches done badly. The persona work is not optional.

## Step 1: Diagnostic — learn from the existing account before writing anything

This step is non-negotiable. The better you understand the new product in comparison to the rest of the brand's line, ads, and proven equity, the better the creative. Diagnose first. Then write. Most weak launch creative skips this step.

**1a. Review the brand's existing top-performing creative.** Pull the highest-performing ads from the brand's account across SKUs (not just the closest one). Look for:
- Formats that have worked repeatedly. UGC unboxing? Founder-talking-to-camera? Side-by-side comparison? "Which one are you" personality framing? Drop-day reveal?
- Headlines and hooks that have hit. Write them down literally, in the brand's voice, as a starting palette.
- Recurring visual mechanics. Lineup shots, hero-product close-ups, specific lifestyle settings, color treatments — visual vocabulary the audience already associates with the brand.
- Proof types the brand has built that transfer. Reviews, demonstrations, founder credibility, expert endorsements.
- Previous new-SKU or launch creative that has performed well in the account.

These are signals to replicate with your own spin, not to copy. You are extending a working pattern to a new SKU. Start here because we already know it works in the account.

**1b. Identify the closest existing SKU and pull its creative specifically.** Of the brand's existing SKUs, which one is closest to the new SKU? Pull the closest SKU's top hooks, top angles, top proof. In Scenarios A and B, most of that informs the new-launch ad directly. In Scenario C, treat it more as a structural reference than a tonal one. (Real example: a brief that explicitly cited a prior "mosquito repellent" winner — that is exactly the kind of reference you mine here. What was the hook? The format? The proof pattern? The language? Those are your starting palette.)

**1c. Map words and visuals worth doubling down on.** Catalog the line names, campaign names, slogans, taglines, recurring visuals, and brand-specific language that already carry equity. These are the verbal and visual handles the audience already grabs onto. The new-launch ad should USE this language wherever the new SKU sits inside the existing world. If the brand has a campaign called "the X Edit" and the new color fits, use it. If they have a tagline customers parrot back in reviews, weave it in.

**1d. Plan the warm-audience recapture move (the low-lift first move).** Before broad cold-audience creative, plan how to recapture repeat buyers and warm audiences already familiar with the brand — unless the launch is for a wholly new audience. They are the lowest-lift, highest-likelihood first buyers of the new SKU, and the audience most likely to give the launch its early momentum. Usually this means:
- Email + paid ads to the existing customer list and engaged audience
- "Meet the new [SKU]" or "introducing [SKU]" introductory ads
- An introductory offer tied to the launch moment (commonly ~30% off, brand-dependent)
- Hero shot + clear explanation of what is new and why existing customers in particular will love it
- Optional: early-access framing for hero-product owners

These warm-audience launch ads are a distinct creative deliverable from the cold-audience scaling ads that come next. Plan for both, and don't let them get mixed up — the warm ad assumes the buyer knows you; the cold ad assumes they don't.

> Important caveat: don't burn your warm list with an offer that's too aggressive or too frequent. The brand's best customers are also the most sensitive to discount fatigue. Use the launch moment as a moment of access and recognition, not just a discount. Not every new launch will have a discount — many won't.

**1e. Diagnose the SKU's relationship to the rest of the line.** Answer plainly:
- Is this something the brand has done a version of before (another color, seasonal drop, flavor)? If yes → lean Scenario A.
- Or is it genuinely a new format, fabric, use case, or audience the brand has not addressed? New use case or format → lean Scenario B. New audience → lean Scenario C.
- What problem does this product solve that the existing line does not — even if it's incremental?

The clearer this diagnosis, the easier the scenario classification and the less likely you are to default to a generic "new product" ad.

**1f. Ingest product URL if provided.** If the user pastes a product page URL, scrape and summarize before classifying. Pull: product name, what it is and does, key claims, hero benefits, pricing, copy already on the page, any live reviews or testimonials, slogans, taglines. Use this to inform Step Zero classification and to ground the script in the brand's actual product language rather than assumptions.

**1g. Ingest past performance data — inherit winners AND diagnose losers.** If the user provides past ad data, do both jobs. Inheriting winners is obvious. Diagnosing losers is what most launch briefs skip, and it's usually where the launch either repeats the same mistake or breaks a pattern that was already failing.
- *Winners:* catalog the top hooks, formats, claims, proof types. Replicate with your own spin.
- *Losers:* diagnose root cause. "Low hold rates across past videos" is a diagnostic, not a stat. Where is hold rate dropping — at second 2 (the hook is failing), second 5 (post-hook drop, the second beat isn't delivering), second 12 (mid-script bore)? Write the launch creative to fix that specific failure mode. (Real example: a brief flagging "low hold rates across past videos" for a new pet-transit tote bundle means leading with a faster, more pattern-interrupting hook and proving value in the first 3–5 seconds rather than waiting until segment 4. Treat the diagnostic as a constraint, not just a reference.)

**1h. Ingest competitor benchmark refs if provided.** If the user provides competitor refs, either run a light teardown (structure, hook, proof pattern, visual mechanic, on-screen text) or invoke [adapting-scripts.md](adapting-scripts.md) for a full ref-by-ref breakdown. The teardown's job is to identify in-category patterns the launch can ethically borrow: what hook structures are converting in that field right now, what proof types are showing up, what visual language repeats. You're not copying — you're calibrating to what's working in-category and translating it through the brand's voice and the rest of Step 1.

## Step 2: Gather the launch input brief

You also need, and should ask the user for if missing:
- Plain-English description of the product: what it is, what it does, what is genuinely new about it
- Top-performing existing ads, hooks, and proof from the closest SKU (gathered in Step 1)
- Target audience for the new SKU, and whether it's the same as the closest existing SKU's audience (yes / no / partially)
- The problem the new product solves that the existing line does not — even if incremental
- The strategic reason for the launch — depth, audience expansion, seasonal moment, competitive response
- Launch sequencing: warm-audience launch (email + ads to existing buyers) vs. cold-audience scaling phase
- Awareness stage of the audience the ad is being written for (see Step 4)
- Constraints: claims whitelist, claims blacklist, banned phrases, required disclaimers, pricing, offer

If you do not have what you need to confidently classify the launch into one of the three scenarios, **ask.** A wrong classification here is much more expensive to fix than a clarifying question.

## Scenario C handoff: invoke persona development

If Step Zero classified this launch as Scenario C, STOP. Do not proceed to Step 3. Invoke [persona-research-and-creative-strategy-process.md](persona-research-and-creative-strategy-process.md) for the new audience first (if the user has not already given you this), and produce a persona output that includes: the avatar (who they are), their problem language (how they describe the problem in their own words), their objections (especially to a brand that didn't start in their world), the proof types they actually trust, and voice samples (how they talk about this category). Only resume this doc once the persona output exists; it is the required input for Steps 3–5.

For Scenarios A and B, a light persona refresh is encouraged (confirm the existing persona hasn't drifted), but it is not blocking.

If persona development is unavailable, ask the user a 3-question persona mini-brief inline: who is this for (specifically — not "women" but "women in this segment with this need"), what is their core problem in their own words, and what would make them trust this product. Flag that a full persona pass is recommended before scaling spend.

## Step 3: Decide the launch frame

Translate the scenario into one of three mental modes for writing. The mode is the lens you write through; it shouldn't change mid-script.

**Mode 1 — "More of what works" (Scenario A).** Use existing winning structures with the new attribute swapped in. The hook can often be the same hook; the proof can be the same proof; the persona voice is unchanged. Lean on the line / campaign / visual vocabulary cataloged in Step 1c. Mode 1 is replication with a swap, not reinvention. Example: a bestselling hoodie's winning hook — "POV: you finally found the perfect everyday hoodie" — becomes, for the new color drop, "POV: now in [new color]." Same hook DNA, swapped element, same proof, same shot cadence.

**Mode 2 — "Bridge and expand" (Scenario B).** Lead with brand authority and the existing customer relationship. Introduce the new format as a natural extension. Voice and persona unchanged, but you're explaining a new use case, so the script does a little more work than Mode 1. The bridge does the heavy lifting in the hook. Example: "you already love the Adventure Bucket List — we made one for the whole family," or "the [brand] you trust for everyday cotton — now in performance fibers for your workouts." The script's job is to grant permission to extend existing trust to a new use case.

**Mode 3 — "New world entirely" (Scenario C).** You cannot assume the new persona knows or trusts the brand. Establish the persona's problem-aware language first. The brand may have to introduce itself in this audience's terms, not its own. Existing tone may need to be re-pitched. Mode 3 is the slowest to write and highest-stakes if you get the voice wrong. Example: a woman frustrated with razor burn on her legs has never heard of the brand. Mode 3 opens in her problem ("if you've ever tried to shave your legs and ended up with bumps you can see for a week, this is for you"), then earns the brand's right to be in this conversation ("the engineering that made [X] work for [original audience], now built for legs"). Existing-audience tone — barbershop-bro voice, locker-room jokes — will torch the ad before it has a chance.

## Step 4: Decide angle strategy (with awareness-stage filter)

**Angles by scenario:**

- **Scenario A** — pull the top 1–2 proven angles from the closest existing SKU and add 1 angle that explicitly showcases the new attribute. Drop / reveal mechanics, side-by-side with the existing line, "which one are you" personality framing, "new in [season]" using the brand's collection vocabulary. Don't write three new angles — replicate what's working and let the newness show through.
- **Scenario B** — lead angle: "if you loved [hero], we made [new]." Add two use-case angles specifically for the new format / occasion. Pull at least one bridge angle: an existing customer trying the new SKU as their own bridge testimonial. For bundles specifically, lead with the value of the format change (ritual, savings, completeness, a daily routine that finally fits together), not a generic "new product" hook. Bundles fail when treated as "five things at once"; they win when treated as "this complete routine, finally in one place."
- **Scenario C** — produce 3–5 fresh persona angles, built from scratch in the new audience's voice. Cover at minimum: a problem angle (the new audience's pain in their words), an identity angle (this is for someone like you), a social proof angle (people in your world are using this), and an objection-handling angle ("isn't this for [other audience]?"). Do not reuse existing-persona hooks — that is the most common Scenario C failure.

**Awareness-stage filter.** Every launch ad should also be classified by audience awareness stage (Eugene Schwartz's framework). This determines the hook and how much context the script needs before getting to the product. A launch ad to a Most Aware audience and one to a Problem Aware audience for the same SKU should look completely different.

- **Most Aware** — existing buyers, the warm list, repeat customers. They know the brand and like it; the only question is whether the new thing is worth it. The ad is offer-led: "introducing [new] + intro offer." Hero shot + clear what's new. Default for the warm-audience launch phase.
- **Product Aware** — they know the brand, haven't bought the new SKU. The ad is a drop / announcement: "now in [new attribute]" or "we made [new]" with quick proof.
- **Solution Aware** — they know they need a solution in this category, don't know your product. The ad is demo-led, with comparison and "why ours."
- **Problem Aware** — they know the problem, don't know the solution category. The ad opens with their problem in their language, then introduces the solution category, then the product.
- **Unaware** — they don't yet know they have the problem. The ad is a pattern interrupt followed by education before the problem and product get named.

Pair awareness stage with scenario and persona. A Scenario A launch to a Most Aware audience is a "meet the new + 30% off" ad — short, punchy, no context needed. A Scenario C launch to a Problem Aware audience is a slower opener — persona-language problem hook, then brand reveal, then product. These are not interchangeable.

> Important caveat: the warm-audience launch ad almost always targets Most Aware. The cold-audience scaling ads cover everything else. Don't confuse the *phase* (warm vs. cold — who's in the audience pool) with the *awareness stage* (what they already know). Related, but different.

## Step 5: Creative moves by scenario (video and static)

Concrete creative moves that tend to work for each scenario, in both video and static. Use these as your starting move-set, not your only options. If the brief is hybrid, align the moves across formats so the launch system feels coherent in the feed.

**Warm-audience launch ads apply to ALL scenarios as the first launch creative:**

*Video moves:*
- "Meet the new [SKU]" / "Introducing [SKU]" with a clear hero shot
- Founder or brand voice explaining what's new and why existing customers in particular will love it
- Introductory offer framing (e.g., 30% off launch window)
- Email + ad pairing — the ad should feel continuous with the email; the customer should recognize this is the same message, not two competing ones
- Optional: hero-product owners get early or exclusive access framing

*Static moves:*
- Product hero on brand background + "Introducing [SKU]" headline + offer card
- Lifestyle hero with existing customer using new SKU + recognition copy ("the [hero] you love — now in [new]")
- Carousel: card 1 = announcement + hero, card 2 = what's new, card 3 = who it's for, card 4 = proof / quote, card 5 = offer + CTA
- Founder card: founder photo + handwritten-feel quote + signature, paired with product shot

**Scenario A (attribute / collection-drop SKU):**

*Video moves:*
- Drop / reveal / unboxing format
- Side-by-side with the existing line
- "Which one are you" personality framing across the lineup
- "New in [season / drop]" announcement using the brand's existing collection-drop vocabulary
- ASMR / pour / texture / close-up moment if the new attribute is sensory (color saturation, fragrance bottle, fabric weave)

*Static moves:*
- Lineup grid: existing line + new attribute, hero treatment on the new
- Side-by-side product hero: existing vs. new, same composition, newness as the focal point
- "Which one are you" personality grid as a carousel (one card per option) or a single 4-up / 6-up static
- Drop announcement single: hero shot of the new attribute on brand background + headline using collection language
- Texture / detail / macro static if the newness is sensory
- Lookbook-style static set if the brand operates in a fashion / aesthetic category

**Scenario B (format / spinoff / bundle SKU):**

*Video moves:*
- "If you loved [hero], we made [new]"
- Use-case demo specifically built for the new format / occasion (the family on the trail, the workout in the performance line, the morning routine using the bundle)
- Existing customer tries the new SKU (bridge testimonial — letting an existing buyer be the credibility source)
- Compare / contrast within the brand's own line
- "Same [brand promise], now for [new moment]"
- Bundles specifically: lead with ritual / savings / completeness — show the routine working end-to-end, not the individual products

*Static moves:*
- Side-by-side hero: hero SKU on one side, new format on the other, with bridge headline ("you already love X — we made Y")
- Use-case lifestyle static: the new format in its specific occasion
- Bridge testimonial card: existing customer quote + their photo using the new SKU
- Bundle hero: full bundle laid out as a single composition + completeness headline ("the full routine, finally in one place")
- Comparison carousel: card 1 = hook ("if you loved X"), card 2 = the new format, card 3 = how / when to use it, card 4 = proof / bridge testimonial, card 5 = offer + CTA

**Scenario C (new-audience SKU):**

*Video moves:*
- Persona-first hook in the new audience's language (NOT the brand's existing tone)
- "Made for [audience], not adapted from [old audience]" — only if true, and only if you can substantiate
- Objection handler up front ("you might know us for X — here's why this is for you")
- Social proof drawn from the new audience (beta users, pre-launch testers, lookalike creators from the new audience's world)
- Re-pitched brand authority — translate what made the brand credible in its original world into language and signals this audience values

*Static moves:*
- Persona-first testimonial card: someone from the new audience + their quote + their context (lookalike creator, beta user, real customer)
- Objection-handler text-led static: bold headline addressing the "isn't this for [other audience]?" question + product reveal as the answer
- Founder card explaining why the brand built this for the new audience (the why-this-exists card)
- Side-by-side: old audience on one side, new audience on the other, with the bridge claim translated into the new audience's terms
- Persona-first onboarding carousel: card 1 = problem (in their words), card 2 = identity ("this is for women like you"), card 3 = why this brand for this audience, card 4 = proof from the new audience, card 5 = product + CTA
- Do not default to lifestyle photography featuring the existing audience — it signals to the new audience that the brand is not for them

## Step 6: Persona-fit check

The most common failure mode in new-launch creative is writing the new-SKU ad in the *existing* persona's voice when the SKU is actually for a *new* audience. Before finalizing any script, re-ask deliberately:

- Whose voice should this be in? Name them.
- Would this audience actually use these words? Read the script aloud as them.
- Would this audience already know who the brand is? If no, did you introduce it correctly?
- Am I borrowing tone from the wrong persona out of habit?

If any answer is "no" or "I am not sure," revise. The cost of a persona-fit failure is that the ad never lands with the audience it was supposed to reach.

## Step 7: Proof mapping for new products

New SKUs often lack reviews, UGC, and best-seller status on day one. The temptation is to manufacture proof you don't have. Don't. Substitute with proof types that are both honest and available:

- **Founder or team POV** ("we built this because…") — credibility through transparency
- **Behind-the-scenes development footage** — process as proof
- **Beta tester / pre-order quotes** — early-adopter social proof
- **Inherited proof from the hero product, clearly attributed** ("our [hero] has [X reviews] — this is the [new format]") — honest leveraging of existing equity
- **Demo-as-proof** — show the product working, let the demo do the convincing
- **Existing-customer testimonial about brand quality, applied to the new SKU** ("I trust [brand] because of [hero], and this is from the same team")

Do not fabricate. Do not claim "best-selling," "loved by thousands," or "X reviews" for a product that does not yet have them. Inherited claims that are not actually transferable erode trust faster than an honest "this is brand new and here's why we think you'll love it."

## Step 8: Tag every output with launch context

Every script for a new-launch brief should be internally tagged (and surfaced in the Launch Context Summary at the end of the output) with:

- Launch scenario (A / B / C)
- Format (video / static / carousel / hybrid)
- Launch phase (warm-audience recapture / cold-audience scaling)
- Awareness stage (Most / Product / Solution / Problem / Unaware)
- Persona target (existing or new — name them, don't just say "existing")
- Angle category
- Newness lever the ad emphasizes (attribute / format / use case / audience entry / category)

This lets the strategist reading the script immediately tell whether it matches the brief or has drifted into the wrong scenario. The tags are also how a brand learns which combinations work over time.

## Step 9: Launch audit (before finalizing)

Answer all of these honestly. If any answer is "no," revise.

- Did I review the brand's existing top creative and inherit what works?
- Did I diagnose past underperformance (if data was provided) and avoid those mistakes?
- Did I make the newness visible in the hook itself?
- Is the script in the right voice for the right persona AND the right awareness stage?
- Am I leveraging brand authority correctly — and only when it actually transfers?
- Did I use the line / collection / campaign language that already carries equity?
- Did I avoid proof types I cannot honestly claim yet?
- Does the angle match the launch scenario, or did I default to the brand's existing top angle out of habit?
- For Scenario C: would a member of the new audience recognize this as for them, without needing the brand's existing context?
- Did I produce the deliverable type the user actually asked for in Step Zero Q4 — not just "a script" by default?
- Did I produce the correct FORMAT (video / static / carousel / hybrid), or did I default to video?
- For statics: would the hero element, headline, and offer all be readable in the feed at thumb-scroll speed without any motion doing the work?
- For carousels: does card 1 earn the swipe on its own (functions as a hook), and does each subsequent card pull weight?
- Have I clarified whether this script is for the warm-audience launch phase or the cold-audience scaling phase, and tagged it accordingly?

## Step 10: Produce standardized output

The output spec depends on the deliverable type AND format from Step Zero Question 4. Defaults below are split by format. If the brief is hybrid, produce both.

**VIDEO DEFAULT — full script + storyboard.** Every output of this type must include:

- **Overview** — one to three sentences max. Cover the launch scenario, format, launch phase (warm vs. cold), persona, awareness stage, and core concept. Producer-readable. No framework labels, sophistication levels, or internal strategy language.
- **Script** — full script as a clean vertical block of text. One line per spoken or text-overlay beat. No labels, no segment numbers, no shot directions, no on-screen-text callouts. If the format is text-on-screen only (no voiceover), note this at the top and present overlay copy line by line.
- **Script with Storyboard** — same script in a table with three bolded columns: **Script | Shot | On-Screen Text**. One row per moment. First row labeled "Hook:". For text-on-screen videos, replace the Script column with On-Screen Text and drop the third column so the table is two columns: On-Screen Text | Shot.
- **Launch Context Summary** — brief and high-level. Include: scenario (A / B / C + one-sentence why), format, launch phase, awareness stage, persona (named), angle category and newness lever, brand language reused from existing creative (slogans, campaign names, line names, recurring visuals), proof strategy used (especially what was substituted for missing UGC / reviews), and what you would test next if this concept performs.

**STATIC DEFAULT — single static ad.** Every output of this type must include:

- **Overview** — one to three sentences max. Cover the launch scenario, format (static + which static type: product hero, lifestyle hero, testimonial card, before/after split, comparison grid, etc.), launch phase, persona, awareness stage, and core concept. Producer-readable.
- **Headline + Copy** — the static's copy in a clean block, organized by element: Headline (the hook, must work at thumb-scroll speed), Subhead (optional supporting line), Body copy (1–3 short lines max), CTA (the action), Disclaimer / fine print (only if required).
- **Visual Brief** — a short paragraph describing what the static needs visually: hero element, layout (product-led / lifestyle-led / text-led / split-frame / grid), supporting elements, brand-asset references (which line / collection visual vocabulary to use), aspect-ratio recommendations (1:1, 4:5, 9:16), and any photography / asset gaps the user needs to fill. No literal pixel directions — this is a brief, not a layout.
- **Launch Context Summary** — same fields as the video version, with format = static + specific static type.

**CAROUSEL DEFAULT — multi-card sequential static.** Treat closer to a storyboard than a single static. Every output must include:

- **Overview** — one to three sentences. Scenario, format (carousel + card count), launch phase, persona, awareness stage, core concept. Note explicitly: what is card 1's job (earning the swipe), and what is the through-line holding the carousel together.
- **Card-by-Card Plan** — a table with three bolded columns: **Card | Copy | Visual Brief**. One row per card. First row labeled "Card 1 — Swipe Earn:". The Copy column contains the on-card text (headline + any body copy). The Visual Brief column contains a short description of the hero element and layout for that card. Final row labeled "Card N — CTA:".
- **Launch Context Summary** — same fields, with format = carousel + card count.

**Deliverable-type specs (when Step Zero Q4 selects a non-default deliverable):**

*Video-native:*
- **Hooks only (video)** → 5–10 video hooks tied to angle territories. Note the angle category and awareness stage for each. No full scripts.
- **Founder / talking-head video** → script in founder voice, light shot direction (setting, framing, energy), no full storyboard table. Note the founder POV and the one concrete thing they're saying that only the founder can say.
- **Shot list (video)** → bullet shot list aligned to angle territories. No script copy. Note sourcing requirements (props, locations, talent, wardrobe).
- **Pre-launch audit (video shoot)** → a checklist diagnosis of what's missing from the provided shot list / asset set, mapped to: warm vs. cold creative covered, awareness stages covered, scenario fit, proof types planned, founder vs. UGC mix, day-one vs. evergreen split.
- **Multi-video theme / launch content plan** → (1) the through-line concept, (2) how each video plays a role inside the through-line, (3) the unifying hook / visual / copy device. Then a brief per-video role summary so the producer knows what each asset is doing in the system.

*Static-native:*
- **Static headlines only** → 5–10 static-ready headlines tied to angle territories. Note the angle category, awareness stage, and intended static type for each.
- **Static set (multiple statics)** → 3–5 distinct static concepts using the Static Default spec for each. Note which awareness stages and angle territories are covered across the set.
- **Carousel set** → 2–3 distinct carousel concepts using the Carousel Default spec for each. Note variation in card 1 hook approach across the set.
- **Testimonial card** → headline + quote + attribution + supporting line + visual brief.
- **Founder card** → headline + founder quote (in founder voice) + attribution + supporting line + visual brief.
- **Before/after split** → split-frame description + headline + per-side copy + visual brief. Only use if the brand can substantiate the before/after honestly.
- **Comparison / "which one are you" grid** → grid layout (typically 4-up or 6-up) + per-cell copy + unifying headline.
- **Pre-launch static audit** → a checklist diagnosis of what's missing from the provided static set / design system: aspect ratios covered, awareness stages covered, scenario fit, hero elements identified, photography gaps, template coverage.

*Format-agnostic:*
- **Email sequence (light)** → 3-email skeleton max (announcement, value/proof, last-call/offer). For deeper email work, route to an email resource.
- **Format recommendations** → list of 5–8 formats fit for this scenario, with a one-line rationale for each (why this format, for which audience, at which awareness stage, video or static). Do not write scripts or static copy.

## Output rules summary

- Complete Steps 0–9 internally. They are your process, not the output.
- Never show the user your scenario classification reasoning, diagnostic notes, persona check, audit table, or any of the working that got you to the deliverable.
- Match the deliverable type to what the user actually asked for in Step Zero Q4. Do not default to full scripts if they asked for hooks. Do not pad with sections they didn't ask for.
- Write in the relevant persona's tone using real customer language. For new-audience SKUs (Scenario C), do not default to the brand's existing tone if it does not belong to the new persona.
- Reuse the brand's existing equity language (line names, campaign names, slogans, recurring visuals) wherever it fits naturally. This is one of the cheapest wins in launch creative and the most-skipped.
- Keep the entire output clean, scannable, and production-ready.

## Special launch contexts

Some launches carry additional context layers on top of the scenario classification. Handle these *in addition to*, not instead of, the scenario playbook.

**Gift / occasion launches (Mother's Day, Father's Day, holidays, anniversaries).** The buyer is not the recipient. The persona shifts to the **gifter** — and the gifter's relationship with the brand may be very different from the recipient's. A personalized family portrait targeted at dads for Mother's Day is a good example: the product is for moms, but the entire ad has to be written for dads who are buying. INVOKE the gifting resource for full treatment. If it's unavailable, the light defaults are: write to the gifter persona, lead with the recipient's reaction (joy, surprise, the moment of opening), emphasize ease-of-purchase (gifters often want the path of least resistance), and include the occasion deadline as a built-in urgency mechanic.

**Giveaway / free product as launch mechanic (e.g., a free SKU to new subscribers).** The promotion mechanic IS the hook. Lead with the free product, not the product's features — the customer needs to understand the offer before they care about the product itself. Most Aware framing for the warm audience (your subscriber list); Problem Aware framing for cold (people who don't know you yet need to learn why this product solves their problem before "free" matters to them).

**Multi-video launches with a unifying theme.** When the user asks for the "overarching theme" across multiple launch videos, do NOT write individual scripts first. Produce the through-line concept and how each video plays a role inside it. The through-line is the asset; the videos are instances. Common through-line shapes: a question the campaign keeps answering, a setting that recurs, a recurring character or voice, a visual device that stitches the videos together, or a recurring on-screen-text mechanic. Once the through-line is locked, each individual video script becomes a much easier deliverable.

**Launch phase sequencing.** A new launch is rarely one ad. Map deliverables to phase:
- **Pre-launch / waitlist** — tease, build anticipation, capture emails. Awareness stage skews Problem Aware to Product Aware.
- **Launch week** — warm-audience recapture (intro offer, "meet the new") + the first cold-audience scaling. Awareness stage skews Most Aware (warm) and Solution Aware (cold).
- **Post-launch evergreen** — refine winning angles, deepen scaling, retire underperformers. Awareness stages cover Solution Aware through Unaware.

> Important caveat: most brands either over-invest in launch week and have nothing for evergreen, or over-invest in evergreen and skip the launch moment entirely. The right answer is almost always: distinct creative for each phase, mapped to the awareness stage the phase is actually reaching.

## Edge cases + substitution library

This is where most launch outputs go sideways. Use these substitutions and routes when the brief doesn't fit the clean path.

- **If the brand has no UGC or reviews for the new product** → substitute with founder POV, beta-tester quotes, demo proof, or transparently inherited hero-product proof. Do not manufacture testimonials or claim ratings that don't exist.
- **If the new SKU is for a new audience and you have no existing assets featuring that audience** → write in the new persona's voice and explicitly call out the audience in copy. Do not use existing-audience footage and assume it transfers — it usually telegraphs to the new audience that this brand isn't for them.
- **If the new product changes the brand's category entirely** (a fashion brand launching a beverage, etc.) → don't borrow the parent brand's authority claims as if they transfer to the new category. They don't, automatically. Lean on transferable brand equity (aesthetic, founder, audience trust) only where credible, and otherwise write the launch like the new category is its own brand wearing the parent's badge.
- **If the brief is too thin to classify the launch with confidence** → ask the user clarifying questions before writing. Specifically: (1) existing audience or new audience, (2) closest existing SKU in the line, (3) what is genuinely new about this one, (4) warm-audience launch ad or cold-audience scaling ad, (5) deliverable type they actually want.
- **If the new SKU is a limited drop / colorway / collection** → default to Scenario A, pull from the brand's previous drop ads if any exist, and use the brand's collection-drop vocabulary. Drops have their own format conventions in the brand's world — use them.
- **If the new SKU is a subscription, refill, or bundle of existing products** → treat as Scenario A or B depending on whether the use case is new. Lead with the value of the format change (convenience, cost, ritual, completeness), not a generic "new product" hook. A bundle's hook should never be "we have a bundle now."
- **If the launch is a re-launch** (the product was previously paused, failed, or rebuilt) → do not lead with "back by popular demand" unless that's true and substantiable. Lead with what is different this time and why customers should give it another look. Re-launches that pretend to be fresh launches usually feel dishonest.
- **If the brief is geographic expansion** (Canada → US, UK → US, etc.) → this is the wrong doc. Stop and route to the geographic-expansion approach.
- **If the brief is a brand-new brand running its first round of ads** → this is the wrong doc. Stop and route to the new-brand launch approach.
- **If the brief is a gift / occasion launch** → invoke the gifting resource (see Special Launch Contexts above).
- **If the brief includes competitor benchmark refs** → invoke [adapting-scripts.md](adapting-scripts.md) for a ref-by-ref breakdown, and bring the structural learnings back into Step 1h before classifying.
- **If the brief includes past performance data showing failures** → diagnose root cause and AVOID the same mistakes in the new launch creative. Don't just inherit winners blindly; the underperformers are equally valuable signal.
- **If the brief asks for an email sequence as the primary deliverable** → produce only a light 3-email skeleton; route to an email resource for deeper treatment.

## Real-use examples (illustrative, not extensive)

*Brand names below are genericized; the mechanics are the point.*

**Example 1 — Scenario A: a scrubs brand's new color drop.** Existing brand, existing persona, attribute-only newness. Pull the top-performing existing-line hooks and insert the new color as the visible "new" element. Drop / reveal / lineup mechanics. Hero shot of the new color alongside the existing palette, ideally side-by-side so the audience sees how the new color fits. The customer already knows and trusts the brand — your job is to make the new color irresistible, not re-establish the brand. Use the brand's existing collection-drop language. Warm-audience launch goes to existing buyers first via email + paid, with intro offer framing.

**Example 2 — Scenario B: a bucket-list-experience brand's "Family" edition.** Existing brand, existing persona (people who bought the original "Adventure" version), new format / spinoff. Anchor in: "you already love the Adventure Bucket List — we made one for the whole family." Lead with the existing-customer bridge. Use-case demo specifically for the family occasion (parents and kids together using it on a trip). Proof can include the hero product's existing reviews while introducing the new format as a natural extension. Warm-audience launch goes first to existing buyers via email + paid; cold-audience scaling picks up from there.

**Example 3 — Scenario B variant: a cotton-line fashion brand launching an athletic line.** Same brand, same persona (the brand's loyal customer), new fabric for a new use case. Lead with the bridge: "the [brand] you already trust for [original use case], now built for [new use case]." Use existing cotton-line reviews as transferable trust. Visual: side-by-side fabric demo, showing the same brand aesthetic translated into the new fabric. Existing-customer bridge testimonial. Avoid the trap of writing this like a new brand — the customer relationship is the whole asset.

**Example 4 — Scenario C: a head-shaving brand's women's razor bundle.** Established brand (known for head shavers), brand-new audience (women, for legs and other body areas). The existing persona's voice does not apply. INVOKE persona development before writing. Persona research drives everything: how do women in this category talk about shaving, what objections do they have to a "men's brand crossing over," what proof do they trust (dermatologist endorsement? friends who've tried it? a before/after that's realistic for women's skin?). Re-pitched brand authority ("the engineering that made [X] work for [original audience], now built for [you]"). Objection-handler up front. Social proof drawn from women, not from existing male customers. Do not reuse existing-persona hooks — that's the single most common Scenario C failure.

**Example 5 — a baby-care brand's detangling cream-conditioner launch (Scenario B, with competitor refs and a prior winner).** Established brand, existing persona (parents of young children, same audience as the rest of the line), new format (a cream conditioner in the hair-care subcategory). The brief comes with two valuable inputs: competitor benchmark refs and a prior winner (a "mosquito repellent" ad). Run Step 1h on the competitors — what hooks, formats, and proof patterns are converting in baby hair care right now. Run Step 1b on the prior winner — what made that ad work, structurally and tonally. Then build the launch concept using the bridge ("the [brand] you already trust for [bath/body], now for detangling and nourishing little kids' hair"), inheriting visual and tonal vocabulary from the prior winner, calibrated against in-category competitor patterns. Multiple concept variants for testing; non-UGC format since that was specified in the brief.

## Final principle

A new-launch ad is the moment the brand introduces a new thing into a specific audience's feed. The audience does not owe the brand attention just because the brand has launched something. Your job is to make sure the ad answers, in the first two seconds: "what is this, why does it matter to me specifically, and why should I trust it."

The better you understand the new product in comparison to the rest of the brand's line, ads, and proven equity — and the more deliberately you classify which scenario, awareness stage, and deliverable you're actually producing for — the better the creative. Diagnose first. Classify second. Then write.

If the ad does not answer those three things in the correct persona's voice for the correct launch scenario at the correct awareness stage, it is the wrong ad regardless of how clever it sounds.
