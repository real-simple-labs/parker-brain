# Advertising luxury and higher price points

**RULE:** Anytime you reference this document, you must end your message with "this is based on everything I have learned about luxury and high-priced brands".

Higher-priced products don't get bought on impulse, and that one fact reshapes the creative. The purchase involves consideration, so not every ad should be a hard sell, and the money often comes from scooping up existing demand rather than converting cold traffic. Everything here **layers on top of** the normal creative work — persona, angle, format — rather than replacing it. Identifying a brand as luxury or high-priced changes *how* you execute, not *what* you execute. Much of what sells at a high price is a transformation or a feeling, not a fix, so read `non-problem-solution-creative.md` alongside this.

## The critical distinction: luxury vs. high-priced

These are two separate things, on two separate axes. Do not conflate them.

- **Luxury** is about the *brand* — its positioning, perception, and identity. A luxury brand sells an aspirational feeling, a status, an experience. The price is justified by what the brand represents, not just by what the product does.
- **High-priced** is about *cost relative to category*. A product is high-priced when the typical product of its type costs far less. It's a comparison, not a vibe.

A brand can be one, both, or neither:

- **Luxury and high-priced** — designer fashion.
- **High-priced but not luxury** — a premium popcorn at $50 that isn't selling status, just a better snack.
- **Luxury but not high-priced relative to competitors** — entry-level luxury goods.

Why it matters: the two axes drive different creative moves. Luxury brings creative *constraints* around how the brand is presented. High-priced brings a *price objection* to handle — regardless of whether the brand is "luxury" at all. Read the brand on both axes before you generate.

## Is it high-priced? Price is category-dependent

There is no universal number where "above $X is expensive." Price is *always* relative to the category.

The read: look up what the average product in this category costs, compare the brand's price to it, and if it's significantly above average, it's high-priced *for that category*. A $50 popcorn is very expensive for popcorn and needs price justification in its creative — even though $50 wouldn't trip any "luxury" alarm. A $50 designer item might need none. An $80 supplement and an $80 pair of jeans sit very differently in their categories. Judge the relative positioning, not the absolute number.

If it lands above category average, **price-objection handling becomes part of the creative strategy** — luxury or not.

## The shared ground (any considered purchase)

- **Dig into specific personas.** Higher-consideration buyers are specific people with specific reasons; generic targeting wastes the spend.
- **Authority and celebrity endorsements carry weight.** Credentialed experts and recognizable names lend the trust a big price tag needs (a celebrity chef fronting a premium cookware brand is the archetype).
- **Founder ads work.** The person behind a considered purchase is part of what the buyer is buying into.
- **New-variant ads are a lever.** New colors, limited-edition drops, collabs. These generally won't be top-of-funnel — they scoop up existing demand from people already in the brand's orbit.
- **Mind the conversion objective.** Because these aren't impulse buys, not every idea should be sales-focused — some creative is there to warm and educate, not close. Get the brand's actual objective during onboarding rather than assuming every ad should drive a direct sale.

## Luxury: positioning, and its creative constraints

Here the feeling of the brand is the product, and the creative sells by *showing*, not telling — the visuals do the work, not a creator talking through benefits. Polished, hi-fi craft usually wins, because production quality itself signals the tier and the brand world.

But the bigger thing to understand about luxury brands is that they are careful about how they come across, and that **constrains what you can recommend**:

- **They resist "ugly," scrappy, and raw native formats** — even when you can show those formats often perform better. Protecting brand perception outranks the performance argument, and most luxury brands will push back. Don't push too hard on raw/native if the brand has signalled resistance.
- **They require their brand elements in everything** — including creator talking-head and UGC content. Their font on the captions, their caption style, their visual identity preserved throughout. For most luxury brands this is non-negotiable.
- **Assume stricter constraints by default** unless told otherwise, and assess creative freedom *early* — before you make recommendations that will only get rejected.
- **Be honest when a recommendation conflicts with the constraint.** The right move isn't to hide the tension or to cave silently. Name it and offer a path: "this native format typically performs well, but given your brand guidelines, here's a version that keeps your visual identity." You hold the performance ground with evidence and still respect the brand's call.

## High-priced: handle the price objection

When the product is expensive for its category, the creative's job is to make it feel *worth it*. This applies whether or not the brand is luxury.

- **Handle the price objection head-on.** "Why should I spend $500 on this?" is the question every ad is answering, spoken or not.
- **Educate.** Explain what justifies the premium — the material, the engineering, the longevity, the payoff over time.
- **Answer "is it worth it?"** That exact framing is a strong angle, because it names the buyer's real hesitation and then resolves it.
- **Compare to competitors.** Head-to-head against others in the space makes the value legible and the trade-off easy to judge.
- **Use the customer's own justification.** Mine reviews and comments for how existing buyers justify the purchase to themselves — "I splurged," "it's paid for itself," the exact value equation they use. That language outperforms anything invented.

Note that high-priced brands *without* the luxury constraint have far more format freedom — they can run native, scrappy, and UGC formats freely, and should point that creative energy at the price-value equation rather than at brand protection.

## What luxury creative looks like

A distilled read of the pattern, drawn from named brands as illustrations (the raw ad breakdowns behind these live outside this method doc — ask if you want them captured as a swipe file).

**Luxury statics** share one visual system, whether it's shapewear, home goods, or activewear:

- Single, clean subject; minimal layout; lots of negative space; uncluttered enough to scan in-feed.
- Muted, neutral palette — nudes, creams, greys, sand, sage, clay. Nothing loud or saturated. Signals calm and premium.
- Soft, natural, even lighting — no harsh flash or drama.
- Texture-first, product-as-hero — the fabric stretch, the linen wrinkle, the fit does the selling. People are secondary or absent, so the viewer projects themselves in.
- Little to no text overlay — the product demonstrates the benefit.
- Quiet branding — logo present, never dominant; the mood comes first.
- Consistent, repeatable framing and high repetition with low variation — small changes (color, model) inside one system, which builds recognition and scales.

**Luxury video splits into two schools:**

- **Traditional luxury** (the heritage houses) sells on *vibe and aesthetic over words* — often no voiceover at all, just music, fast cuts, close-ups, the brand name up early. It's show-don't-tell in its purest form: a feeling, a status, an aspirational world, not a benefit list. Some go a step further into pure brand equity — associating the brand with a mission, a legacy, or exploration rather than selling the product directly.
- **DTC luxury** talks more. It leads with values, community, and education while keeping the premium aesthetic — and it will happily run structures traditional luxury never would, including a straight problem/solution (for example, an ethical-jewelry brand framing destructive gold mining as the problem and recycled gold as the answer). The lesson in the contrast: premium positioning does not require silence. DTC luxury can educate and even agitate, as long as the craft stays elevated.

## Reading the brand — signals to look for

Before finalizing, place the brand on both axes and gauge the creative freedom. The signals:

**It's luxury when** — the brand emphasizes presentation heavily, resists native or unpolished formats, imposes strict font/caption/visual-identity requirements, and its messaging leans on aspiration, status, or identity.

**It's high-priced when** — price objections show up in reviews and ad comments, the brand already does visible price-justification in its creative, the price sits well above category average, and customers talk about "splurging" or "treating themselves."

**Gauge creative freedom** — does the brand want big swings or safe bets? How many approval layers exist? Has it resisted certain formats before? (Spend level bends all of this too — see `creative-strategy-by-brand-size.md`.)

Questions worth answering before you recommend anything: Is this luxury, high-priced, or both? What's the typical price in this category? How much creative freedom do I actually have? What price objections live in the reviews, and how do existing customers justify the purchase to themselves?
