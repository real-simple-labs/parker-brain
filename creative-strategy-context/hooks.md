# Hooks Context Document

This is the **reference taxonomy** for hooks — the proven formats and real examples. It is deliberately not a how-to. The *why* behind hooks (the cognitive mechanisms) lives in `hook-psychology.md`; the *how* — analyze the brand, pick the format, write to a specific ICP and emotion, and the compliance and output rules — is the **hooks skill** at `skills/hooks/`. Read this to know what the formats are and to see them done well; generate through the skill.

Examples below include real stats, quotes, and results from real ads. They are here to show structure and strategy — not numbers to reuse, and not lines to paste to a user. Never fabricate a stat to match one; that discipline, and how to source real ones, lives in the hooks skill.

## Hook Philosophy & Foundation

> The deep cognitive science of *why* hooks work — salience and the orienting response, habituation, the information gap and open loops, emotional encoding, negativity bias, self-reference, processing fluency, social proof, narrative transportation, working-memory load — lives in `hook-psychology.md`. This doc carries the tactical taxonomy; reason about mechanism from that one.

### Core Principles

The hook is arguably the most important part of any direct response ad. If your hook fails to get relevant attention, the rest of your ad doesn't matter. You should spend 80% of your time on the first 3 seconds of any video.

**Key Tenets:**

- Prioritize relevance over raw attention
- Avoid generic scroll-stopping tactics that attract the wrong audience
- Hook ≠ product intro. Hooks should set up conflict/problem, not pitch solution immediately
- There's no fixed formula for hook length — focus on delivering an immediately relevant message
- Hooks can be 3 seconds OR 10-40 seconds if they deepen the story

**The True Hook Structure:** Every hook is actually FOUR elements happening in the first 3 seconds:

1. **Text Overlay Hook** — The written words on screen
2. **Sound Hook** — The audio/verbal element
3. **Visual Hook** — What you're showing (often more impactful than text)
4. **Vibe Hook** — The overall feeling created by lighting, font choices, color grading, and aesthetic

BUT, not every hook will need all of those.

**Critical Insight:** The best hooks SHOW, they don't tell. Changing the visual hook often has bigger impact than changing the text overlay.

## The First Second — Won By Visual And Sound

The first second decides it, not the first three — and the brain triages ad-vs-content and emotion before any spoken line lands (you have room for ~5–6 words). So **the first second is won by the visual and sound hook; the spoken/text line does its work at the 1–3s re-hook.** This sharpens where the 80/20 energy goes; it doesn't replace it.

Four mechanics the brain stops on — the executable layer under "SHOW, don't tell." These build the visual/sound hook; they slot into whichever format is chosen:

- **Candid face mid-emotion.** Brains are wired to find faces. A face caught mid-expression, looking off-screen, about to speak, opens a *who/what* loop. Candid reads as a moment; posed-to-camera reads as an ad.
- **Motion against the scroll.** Motion cutting against the upward feed (something moving down, closing the gap) breaks the uniform scroll and forces a stop, with no words.
- **Format-break visual.** An object obviously in the wrong place, or an ambiguous partial reveal that turns into something else ("is it cake"). The brain stops on what doesn't belong or can't resolve.
- **Precognitive sound.** Familiar audio with half missing — one side of a phone call, audible pauses, no reply. The absent half opens loops the brain stays to close. Silence counts as a sound hook too.

### Two-second grabs that stack (and where to steal them)

One refinement to the heading above: **a few words of open-loop on-screen text is also a first-second carrier** — it's the *spoken* line that can't land that fast, not the text. An unfinished sentence the brain stays to complete ("If your baby bell was a square…", "Thank god I started recording on my phone" over a calm shot, seconds before an off-screen crash) does first-second work on its own. So the first second is won by visual, sound, **and** a short open-loop text overlay — never by the spoken hook.

These are two-second *grabs* that stack, not whole hooks: a known face, a fast angle-cut a half-second later, and a sound under it each buy a fraction of a second and carry the viewer to the spoken hook at ~3s. Grabs to stack, on top of the four mechanics above:

- **A short open-loop text overlay** — a few words, an unfinished sentence, sometimes revealed in staged beats so each beat opens a fresh loop.
- **A fast camera-angle change inside the first ~2s** (full face → split-screen reaction). Cut faster than feels natural; speed is the grab. Exception: brands whose product *is* calm. Older audiences are not an exception — they read fast and want depth, not slowness.
- **A pointing finger, an arrow, or a hand holding the product** — directs the eye and conveys real size. Attributed to attention/CTR research; treat as a reliable primitive.
- **A story cold-open under rising sound design** — an underdog/high-stakes first line over music or an SFX that ticks up and breaks into the scene.

**Match visual complexity to the message, and balance visual load against text load.** Mechanical/informational message → simple, high-contrast visual (clean field, few colors, red pulls focus) so there's room to read the text. Emotional/story message → a busy visual is fine; the viewer is feeling, not parsing. And the more arresting the visual, the *less* text it can carry — a grabbing visual eats the text, so keep first-second words to a few and stage the rest.

**It extends to statics.** An object *about to fall off the edge* of the frame (the catch reflex won't let the eye scroll past, and it drives comments), a product in an obviously wrong place, or obvious fantasy/AI — which works *only when it's clearly not real*; AI forced to pass as real backfires. Maximizing the visible product-vs-competitor difference (size, color, sound) is its own scroll-stopper.

**Steal from organic, not ads.** An ad is organic content with money behind it, and organic creators are sharper at the two-second grab because the feed selects hard for it. Workflow: scroll organic ~30 min/day and save anything that stops *you* (whether or not you're the customer — you're studying *why* it stopped a brain); then for each clip, transcribe it and name the **visual tension** in the first 2–3s — the open loop and the disruptors doing the work — and rebuild *that tension* for the brand. Translate the mechanism, not the surface; vet that it maps to the product, human in the loop.

## Hook Structure Framework

### The 80/20 Rule for Ads

**First 3 Seconds:**

- Will this hook the audience?
- What promise am I giving them?
- Does the audience know what's in it for them?
- Is there any confusion or disconnect?
- Does it correctly target an emotion?

**1-3 Second Mark:** Give them a reason to keep watching. Tell the audience what's in it for them with a benefit or tease a reason to keep watching for a later payoff.

**3-5 Second Mark:** You've done the hard part. Now the audience needs to know the best benefit (if not mentioned before), or you need to re-hook them to watch longer.

**Example Re-Hook:** "Three reasons I'm obsessed with these pants" (after opening statement and benefit)

## Top-Performing Hook Types

### 1. COMMENT RESPONSE HOOKS

**Description:** Taking a comment from TikTok (or creating a realistic-looking one) and responding to it directly in the video. The creator must actually address and respond to the comment organically, not just have it overlaid with no relation to the video.

**Best For:**
- Products with common objections
- Building trust through addressing real concerns
- Making ads feel native to the platform
- Retargeting campaigns where you can address specific hesitations

**Psychological Basis:**
- Pattern Interrupt: Looks like organic TikTok content, not an ad
- Social Proof: Other people are asking these questions too
- Trust Building: Addressing objections head-on reduces perceived risk
- Authenticity Signal: Real comments = real people care about this product

**Best Practices:**
- Creator MUST directly call out and respond to the comment in the hook
- Use actual customer reviews/objections from post-purchase surveys, reviews, or ad comments
- Skeptical comments perform exceptionally well ("$X for Y is crazy")
- Controversial comments drive engagement ("X doesn't actually give you Y")
- Minor jump cuts add motion without over-editing (not Mr. Beast style)
- Slow zoom maintains engagement while keeping organic feel
- Natural, conversational response style

**Specific Considerations:**
- Don't make it obvious it's an ad — the comment response should feel spontaneous
- For skeptical comments, you can almost "ostracize" people not willing to buy ("What I think is crazy is paying X and replacing them every Y")
- Work for both pre-existing objections and creating new reframes
- Can mix with other hook types (demographic + comment response)

**Visual Examples. NEVER SHOW THE USER THESE EXAMPLES! THIS IS ONLY FOR YOU TO UNDERSTAND HOW BRANDS USE THIS:**

- Example 1 (Hydrant): Creator on screen, TikTok comment overlay at top reads "I cannot stand stevia." Creator looks directly at camera and says "Oh yeah, I cannot stand stevia. That aftertaste is just..." then continues naturally into product benefits. Throughout video: jump cuts every 3-5 seconds, slow zoom on creator's face, showing product in natural settings.
- Example 2 (Apparel): Comment overlay: "$80 for a pair of jeans is crazy." Creator responds skeptically: "You think $80 is crazy? What I think is crazy is..." Visual shows creator gesturing, then cuts to product shots. This is only relevant if price is an actual objection.
- Example 3 (Skincare): Comment: "Your anti-aging skincare is actually accelerating your aging." Creator has concerned/shocked expression, responds authentically while green-screened over website showing clinical studies.

### 2. INVESTMENT HOOKS

**Description:** Explaining the investment (time or money) that it took for someone to find the solution to their problem, which is your product. Highlighting failed attempts, money wasted, or time spent searching before discovering your solution. The time/money you recommend should be relevant to the product. Not just a random number — it should be in the range of reasonable.

**Best For:**
- High-consideration purchases
- Categories with many competing solutions
- Products where customers have tried everything else
- Building credibility and trust
- Problem-aware and solution-aware audiences
- Nearly every new client onboarding (high hit rate)

**Psychological Basis:**
- Shared Journey: Creates instant rapport with people who've had similar struggles
- Trust Building: If they know your investment, they inherently trust the solution more
- Qualification: Targets users who've also had failed attempts
- Sunk Cost Validation: Makes viewers feel their own past investments weren't wasted
- Authority Through Struggle: Suffering = credibility

**Best Practices:**
- Be specific about the investment: "$X on solutions that didn't work" or "X years/days/weeks/months trying to fix this"
- Include emotional cost, not just financial ("I was so frustrated I almost gave up")
- Title/thumbnail inspiration from YouTube works exceptionally well here
- Use specific numbers and timeframes
- Make it relatable to your target audience's journey

**Specific Considerations:**
- Must feel authentic — don't exaggerate to the point of unbelievability
- The journey should mirror common customer experiences
- Works incredibly well for 40+ audiences and women-skewed demographics
- Can be combined with founder storytelling
- Almost always test this in first round of creative

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: "I tested over X ads. Here are the hooks ACTUALLY making MONEY" — Text overlay appears immediately, creator speaking directly to camera with authentic, slightly tired expression, implying the work behind the discovery.
- Example 2: Creator walking, casual setting: "I spent $X on Y before I found THIS." Visual cuts between creator talking and quick flashes of failed products (pill bottles, creams, treatments), then reveals the hero product.
- Example 3: "It took me X [timeframe] to learn this but I'll teach it to you in less than 1 minute"

### 3. SCAM HOOKS

**Description:** Using the word "scam" or similar visceral trigger words to stop the scroll by creating immediate tension, then resolving it by explaining why it's NOT a scam or how to avoid scams.

**Best For:**
- Categories with trust issues or perceived scams
- High-ticket items where skepticism is high
- New-to-market products that seem "too good to be true"
- Industries with lots of competitors making false promises
- Retargeting skeptical audiences

**Psychological Basis:**
- Loss Aversion: Triggers fear of being cheated/taken advantage of
- Curiosity Gap: "I thought this was a scam" creates unresolved tension
- Negative Bias: Our brains prioritize potential threats
- Pattern Interrupt: Unexpected admission/acknowledgment

**Best Practices:**
- Use one of the best visceral trigger words in advertising today
- Frame as: "I thought this was a scam" or "This looks like a scam but..."
- Quick resolution is key — don't leave tension unresolved too long
- Pair with proof elements (testimonials, results, credentials)
- Can iterate on existing top performers by simply changing the hook
- Works as text overlay + verbal hook combination

**Specific Considerations:**
- Must resolve the tension positively — don't just call out scams without solution
- Be careful with platform compliance — don't make it seem like YOU'RE scamming
- Works exceptionally well in competitive/saturated markets
- Can backfire if not executed with authenticity
- Test immediately — extremely easy to implement

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "I thought this was a SCAM." Creator has skeptical expression, arms crossed. Visual cuts to them using product, face shifting from skeptical to surprised/pleased. Shows results, testimonials appearing as text overlays.
- Example 2: Creator holding product: "This LOOKS like a scam, right?" Camera zoom on product with suspenseful music. "But here's why it's not..." Transitions to demonstration, clinical studies, authority figures using it.
- Example 3: Split screen: Left side shows "scam" products crossed out, right side shows your product with check marks. Creator voiceover: "I thought ALL of these were scams until..."

### 4. GIVE ME TIME HOOKS

**Description:** Asking for an upfront time commitment to either entertain or educate someone. Explicitly stating how much time the viewer should invest and what they'll get in return.

**Best For:**
- Educational content
- Complex products requiring explanation
- Building authority and expertise
- Longer-form ads (VSLs, mini-VSLs)
- Top-of-funnel awareness content

**Psychological Basis:**
- Commitment Bias: Once someone commits time upfront, they're more likely to follow through
- Value Exchange: Clear promise of what they'll receive
- Higher Engagement: Users inherently commit more time to these videos
- FOMO: Not wanting to miss the promised information

**Best Practices:**
- Be specific about time: "Give me X seconds" or "The next X minutes will change..."
- Promise specific value: "I'll teach you X" or "You'll learn Y"
- Inspiration from YouTube titles/thumbnails
- Results in higher hook and hold rates
- Pair with educational or transformation content
- Can work for both short and long-form content

**Specific Considerations:**
- Must deliver on the promise — don't waste their time
- Time commitment should match complexity of message
- Works better for solution-aware audiences
- Can be combined with investment hooks
- Test different time frames (30 sec, 60 sec, 2 min)

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "Give me 47 seconds" with countdown timer visible. Creator speaking directly to camera in casual setting. Timer counts down throughout the video, creating urgency.
- Example 2: "The next 2 minutes will save you $X" — Creator holding up fingers showing "2", then transitions to rapid-fire tips with on-screen graphics showing money saved.
- Example 3: "It took me 10 years to learn this but I'll teach it to you in less than 1 minute" — Split screen showing "10 years" on left with various clips, "60 seconds" on right with creator explaining.

### 5. POV (POINT OF VIEW) HOOKS

**Description:** Using "POV" (Point of View) format to put the viewer directly in a specific scenario or mindset, often paired with strong emotional triggers like "hate" or "love."

**Best For:**
- Highly relatable situations
- Emotional resonance
- Tribal identity targeting
- Lifestyle and identity-based products
- Gen Z and Millennial audiences
- Partnership ads with creators

**Psychological Basis:**
- Empathy Activation: Puts viewer directly in the scenario
- Tribal Identity: Creates in-group feeling
- Emotional Resonance: Strong trigger words amplify effect
- Native Format: Feels like organic social content

**Best Practices:**
- 10-15% of top-performing creative contains "POV"
- Pair with strong emotional words: "POV: you HATE X"
- Can also use simpler versions: "I hate XYZ"
- Make the scenario highly specific and relatable
- Use exaggerated reactions or situations
- Test in nearly every initial creative round
- Works exceptionally well with creator content

**Specific Considerations:**
- Must be genuinely relatable to target audience
- Exaggeration is okay and often preferred
- Works across multiple industries
- Can combine with demographic targeting
- Simple "I hate X" also performs well

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "POV: you hate when your skincare pills up under makeup." Creator dramatically rubbing face, showing frustration, makeup pilling. Quick cut to using new product, smooth application, satisfied expression.
- Example 2: "POV: you found an easy convenient way to buy lottery tickets." Creator in car, excitedly showing phone, exaggerated happy dance, green screen showing app interface behind them.
- Example 3: "I hate that 3pm energy crash." Creator at desk, head in hands, clearly exhausted. Clock shows 3pm. Quick transition to drinking product, energized expression, productivity montage.

### 6. DEMOGRAPHIC HOOKS

**Description:** Calling out a specific demographic, age range, job title, lifestyle group, or identity in the opening hook to immediately qualify the audience and signal "this is for you."

**Best For:**
- Broad targeting on Meta (Advantage+ or open targeting)
- Top-of-funnel content
- Scalability (targeting by identity vs. problem)
- Clear audience segmentation
- Products with distinct customer personas
- Nearly universal application

**Psychological Basis:**
- Self-Selection: People immediately identify if ad is for them
- Targeting Signal: Helps Meta deliver ads to right people via creative metadata
- Tribal Identity: Creates belonging to a group
- Relevance Filter: Qualifies ideal customers, filters out rest
- Attention Capture: Hearing your identity = instant relevance

**Best Practices:**
- Think about how users categorize themselves (age, job, lifestyle, health, affiliation)
- Use specific callouts: "If you're between 25-35" or "For working moms who..."
- Meta reads text overlays for targeting signals
- More top-of-funnel than problem-based targeting = better scale
- Targets bigger audience without requiring problem awareness
- Test multiple demographic angles
- Can combine with other hook types (demographic + controversy)

**Specific Considerations:**
- THE top-performing hook strategy for 2026
- Leaving money on table if not testing these
- Works because Meta doesn't rely on interest/lookalike targeting anymore
- Creative metadata (imagery + messaging) drives targeting
- Increases scalability significantly
- Must be respectful and authentic to the demographic

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Bold text overlay: "If you're between 25-35 and want to buy a home." Creator in this age range, speaking directly to camera in relatable setting (apartment, coffee shop), nodding along with the demographic description.
- Example 2: "Attention remote workers." Creator at home desk setup, Zoom screen visible in background, wearing casual clothes, immediately creating recognition for target audience.
- Example 3: "For moms who are tired of bedtime battles." Creator clearly a mom, kids' toys visible in background, exhausted but authentic expression, creates instant connection.

### 7. MULTIPLE HOOK STRATEGY

**Description:** Stacking 2-3 hooks at the beginning of your ad instead of relying on just one hook. Creates a "hook reel" that prevents drop-off and appeals to multiple psychological triggers simultaneously. YOU WOULD ALWAYS STACK DIFFERENT FORMATS OF HOOKS FROM THIS LIST!

**Best For:**
- Preventing early drop-off
- Complex value propositions
- Audiences with varied motivations
- Improving existing high-performers
- Testing efficiency (one video, multiple entry points)

**Psychological Basis:**
- Sustained Engagement: Prevents drop-off in first few seconds
- Multi-Trigger: Appeals to different psychological motivators
- Pattern Variation: Keeps brain engaged with changes
- Reinforcement: Multiple chances to hook the viewer

**Best Practices:**
- Test on existing high-performing content first
- Stack complementary hooks (demographic + controversy, demographic + anti-traditional, etc.)
- Keep each mini-hook punchy and quick
- Use explosion clips or visual hooks after first hook for added engagement
- Can test 4-5 hook variants on same core video
- Use visual elements (drip content, squeezes, abstract art, fruit explosions) between hooks

**Specific Considerations:**
- Easiest way to improve ad performance without making net new content
- Works with hook stacking tests on Meta
- Each hook should transition naturally
- Don't make it feel disjointed or confusing
- Maximum 3 hooks before it becomes too much
- Particularly effective for 40+ and women-skewed audiences

**Visual Examples:** Stacking a comment response hook, with then a 3 reasons why! But the messaging has to make sense and be congruent the whole time.

### 8. HOW DO I KNOW IF HOOKS

**Description:** Starting the ad by articulating the exact question viewers are already asking themselves: "How do I know if [product/service] is right for me?" Directly addresses uncertainty and frames the ad from the viewer's perspective.

**Funnel-stage siblings:** the opener verb maps to where the viewer is. *"How do I know if…"* = consideration/problem-aware (uncertainty). *"How do I pick / choose…"* = decision/solution-aware (comparing options). *"How to start…"* = activation/most-aware, and reads well for service businesses. Same format, three stages — match the verb to the audience's awareness.

**Best For:**
- Consideration stage content
- Products with multiple use cases
- Addressing objections preemptively
- Building trust and authenticity
- Retargeting campaigns
- Complex purchase decisions

**Psychological Basis:**
- Mind Reading: Mirrors exact thoughts viewer is having
- Empathy Signal: Shows you understand their uncertainty
- Genuine Conversation: Feels less like ad, more like help
- Question-Based Hook: Our brains seek to answer questions
- Trust Building: Acknowledging uncertainty builds credibility

**Best Practices:**
- Start literally with: "How do I know if [X] is right for me?"
- Follow with common objections or decision criteria
- Frame from viewer's perspective, not brand's
- Use conversational, helpful tone
- Provide clear decision framework
- Address real customer concerns from reviews/surveys

**Specific Considerations:**
- Feels more genuine and authentic than traditional ads
- Works exceptionally well when brand/product seems "too good to be true"
- Can reduce ad fatigue by changing how you approach messaging
- Great for retargeting people who've seen product but not purchased
- Must actually answer the question posed

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "How do I know if this is right for me?" Creator in thoughtful pose, finger on chin. Transitions to listing criteria with on-screen graphics. "If you [X], [Y], and [Z], then yes."
- Example 2: Creator speaking directly: "You're probably wondering if this will actually work for you..." Green screen over comparison chart or decision tree showing different scenarios.
- Example 3: Split screen: Questions appearing on one side ("Will it work for my skin type?" "Is it worth the price?"), creator answering each on the other side with genuine, thoughtful responses.

### 9. SPEED & TRANSFORMATION HOOKS

**Description:** Combining time-based urgency with visible before-and-after transformation proof. Shows fast results and tangible change to create instant gratification while building trust through proof.

**Best For:**
- Beauty and cosmetics (natural fit for quick visual results)
- Fitness and wellness
- Skincare
- Any product promising visible change
- Products that solve urgent problems
- Before/after transformation content

**Psychological Basis:**
- Instant Gratification: People want fast, visible results
- Proof Through Transformation: Seeing is believing
- Urgency Creation: Time elements drive immediate interest
- Fear of Missing Out: Quick results = don't want to delay
- Trust Building: Visual proof validates claims

**Best Practices:**
- Use time-based phrases: "in 7 days," "overnight," "within minutes"
- MUST include before/after visuals
- Speed gets attention, transformation builds trust — use both
- Be realistic with timeframes (overpromising backfires)
- Show the transformation clearly and undeniably
- Can use timer overlays or time-lapse effects
- Combine with demonstration footage

**Specific Considerations:**
- Why beauty/cosmetics crush on Meta — easy to show quick results
- Must be compliant with platform rules on before/after (especially health/weight)
- Works across industries beyond beauty if transformation is clear
- Time specificity matters ("3 days" better than "quickly")
- Visual proof is critical — not just talking about it

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "7 days to clear skin." Split screen showing day 1 (acne visible) transitioning through days 2, 3, 5, 7 with progressive improvement. Timer counting up, creator's face shown throughout.
- Example 2: "Watch this 60-second transformation." Time-lapse of makeup application, hair styling, or fitness movement. Before state clearly shown, transformation happens rapidly, after state held for emphasis.
- Example 3: "I tried this for 48 hours." Creator documenting journey, time stamps shown on screen. Visual comparison at beginning vs. end, genuine reaction to results.

### 10. CONVERSATIONAL HOOKS

**Description:** Hooks that don't even feel like hooks. Using casual, mid-conversation phrases like "No because...," "Okay these...," or "Wait why..." to make viewers feel like they're jumping into a conversation already happening.

**Best For:**
- Making ads feel native and organic
- All creator content (B2B to Beauty to Apparel)
- Building authenticity
- Reducing ad fatigue
- Gen Z and Millennial audiences
- Both paid and organic content

**Psychological Basis:**
- Pattern Interrupt: Doesn't signal "ad" to the brain
- Conversational Priming: Feels like talking to a friend
- Curiosity Gap: Jumping mid-conversation makes you want context
- Natural Attention: How we actually talk in real life
- Reduced Resistance: Lower psychological barriers than traditional ads

**Best Practices:**
- Use phrases like: "No because...," "Okay these...," "Wait why is this...," "Okay so..."
- Don't announce it's an ad or product immediately
- Maintain casual, authentic tone throughout
- Works with virtually any product or service
- Test on your own organic content first to validate
- Very easy to implement — just tweak opening of existing videos

**Specific Considerations:**
- Tested and validated by major creators (Julia Bloom methodology)
- Works for both paid and organic
- One of the most versatile hook types
- Can be combined with almost any other hook strategy
- Requires authentic delivery — can't sound scripted
- Perfect for ugc creators with acting experience

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Creator mid-gesture: "No because these are actually insane." Natural hand movements, speaking as if to friend off-camera. No formal introduction, jumps right into enthusiasm about product.
- Example 2: "Okay these..." Creator holds up product, trails off, shakes head in disbelief. "I wasn't expecting this at all." Casual setting, no formal framing.
- Example 3: "Wait why is this so good though?" Creator examining product, genuine confused/pleased expression. Looks like organic TikTok video documenting real discovery.

### 11. VIRAL PRODUCT HOOKS

**Description:** Tapping into existing virality or creating the perception of viral popularity. References trending status, millions of users, social media fame, or cultural moment to leverage social proof and FOMO.

**Best For:**
- Products with any level of existing buzz
- Beauty, wellness, fashion, skincare
- Categories where social proof drives demand
- Retargeting (building on awareness)
- Creating bandwagon effect
- Impulse purchase categories

**Psychological Basis:**
- Social Proof: If millions use it, it must be good
- FOMO: Don't want to miss out on what's popular
- Bandwagon Effect: Want to be part of the trend
- Risk Reduction: Popularity = less risk in trying
- Status/Belonging: Using viral products = being in-the-know

**Best Practices:**
- Use specific numbers: "2.5 million people," "went viral with 10M views"
- Reference platforms: "TikTok made me buy it," "went viral on Instagram"
- Frame as discovery: "I finally tried the viral [X]"
- Leverage reaction clips and before/after content
- If not viral yet, frame around viral movement or category
- Can reference celebrity use or expert endorsement

**Specific Considerations:**
- One of few hooks that works consistently year after year
- Must be honest — don't claim virality that doesn't exist
- Can frame around category virality if product isn't viral
- Works especially well during cultural moments (BFCM, gifting seasons)
- Combine with UGC reaction footage
- Easy to test with simple text overlay change

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "I finally tried the VIRAL sleep patches." Creator unboxing while scrolling phone showing multiple videos about product. Quick cuts of other people using/reviewing it.
- Example 2: "[number of reviews] people can't be wrong." Montage of different creators using product, news headlines, social media screenshots, building to creator's own testimonial.
- Example 3: "Why is this all over my FYP?" Creator looking at phone, scrolling gesture, surprised expression. Transitions to trying product themselves, documenting genuine reaction.

### 12. REACTION HOOKS

**Description:** Leading with a strong, authentic emotional reaction (shock, tears, anger, joy) to create an emotional cliffhanger that hooks viewers who want to understand what caused the reaction.

**Best For:**
- Products with dramatic/impressive results
- Emotional connection categories (gifts, personal care, life improvement)
- Beauty and skincare
- Trending during gifting seasons
- Creating empathy and connection
- Partnership ads with authentic creators

**Psychological Basis:**
- Emotional Contagion: Strong reactions trigger reactions in others
- Curiosity: We must know what caused this emotion
- Empathy Activation: We naturally care why someone is upset/excited
- Authenticity Signal: Real emotion = real product impact
- Mirror Neurons: We subconsciously mirror emotions we see

**Best Practices:**
- Brief creators to show natural reactions while testing product
- Don't fake it — must be genuine emotional response
- Combine with other hook types for stronger ad
- Frame as: "Why did I start crying when..." or "People are mad at me because..."
- Hold on the reaction moment before explaining
- Works across Instagram and Meta generally
- Test with unboxing, first use, or result reveal moments

**Specific Considerations:**
- Huge trend from organic social translating to paid ads
- Particularly strong in beauty industry currently
- Expanding to other categories
- Can't be manufactured — needs authentic moment
- Works best when emotion matches product benefit
- Brief creators to react naturally, not perform

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Creator with tears welling up, hand over mouth. Hold on emotion for 2-3 seconds. Text: "Why did I just cry trying this?" Transitions to explanation and product reveal.
- Example 2: "People are mad at me for sharing this." Creator looking defensive/excited, quick cuts of angry comment overlays, then reveals why it's actually good news/helpful.
- Example 3: Unboxing scene: Creator opens package, genuine shock/delight on face, covers mouth, looks at camera. "Wait..." Product slowly revealed while maintaining authentic reaction.

### 13. CONTROVERSY/ANTI-TRADITIONAL HOOKS

**Description:** Going against the grain, breaking traditions, or introducing debate to force viewers to see what the controversy is about. Identifying a common enemy, hot take, or challenging widely-held beliefs.

**Best For:**
- Products that challenge category norms
- Crowded/saturated markets
- Differentiation from competitors
- Driving engagement and comments
- Products with unique mechanisms
- Categories with established "wrong" solutions

**Psychological Basis:**
- Polarization Power: Debate forces engagement
- Pattern Disruption: Challenges expectations
- Tribal Alignment: Creates us vs. them
- Curiosity: Must hear the argument
- Engagement Boost: Agreement/disagreement both drive comments
- American Values: Going against the grain is culturally relevant

**Best Practices:**
- Call out the tradition directly in hook
- Use phrases like: "STOP using [X]," "If you're doing [X] you're f*cked," "NEVER do [X]"
- Frame as exposing truth or industry secret
- Use words like "controversy" explicitly
- Great for products challenging a norm
- Drives comments and shares (boosts algorithmic distribution)
- Perfect for before/after visuals showing old way vs. new way

**Specific Considerations:**
- Very similar to myth-busting hooks
- Works exceptionally well in US market
- Must have legitimate argument — can't just be controversial for attention
- Risk: some people will disagree (but that's okay — engagement is engagement)
- Great for sophisticated markets where people have tried many solutions
- Introduce a common enemy or wrong approach

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "STOP doing sit-ups for abs. Here's why." Creator demonstrating sit-ups, red X appears over them. Transitions to alternative method with check mark.
- Example 2: "Big Pharma doesn't want you to know this." Creator in serious tone, leaning in. Green screen over documents or research. Reveals alternative solution with urgency.
- Example 3: "Everything you know about [X] is wrong." Split screen showing traditional method being crossed out, new method being highlighted. Creator explaining with authority.

### 14. TRIGGER WORD HOOKS

**Description:** Using specific words that immediately trigger emotional or psychological responses — words that tap into deeply rooted human emotions like desire, fear, curiosity, or anxiety.

**Best For:**
- Breaking through attention barriers
- Emotional product categories
- Creating urgency
- Universal application (works across industries)
- Enhancing any existing hook
- Meta ad performance

**Psychological Basis:**
- Amygdala Activation: Certain words trigger immediate emotional response
- Attention Capture: Brain prioritizes emotional stimuli
- Memory Formation: Emotional content is more memorable
- Decision Influence: Emotion drives purchasing decisions
- Pattern Matching: We're wired to respond to these triggers

**Best Practices — Categories of triggers:**

- Curiosity/Intrigue: Secret, hidden, never, reveal, discover, truth
- Fear/Anxiety: Warning, danger, mistake, wrong, avoid, risk
- Shock/Controversy: Shocking, scam, scandal, exposed, banned
- Desire: Transform, breakthrough, ultimate, perfect, proven
- Emotion: Crying, devastated, obsessed, hate, love
- Anger/Resentment: Tired, sick, done, enough, betrayed

**Specific Considerations:**
- Analysis shows trigger words appear in 10-15% of top performers
- "Scam" is one of the most powerful trigger words currently
- Don't overuse — becomes spammy
- Must match emotional tone with product/offer
- Combine with other hook strategies
- Cultural sensitivity matters — some triggers work better in certain markets

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: "The SHOCKING truth about [category]." Bold, attention-grabbing text, dramatic music, creator with serious expression, quick cuts.
- Example 2: "I'm OBSESSED and here's why." Creator showing genuine enthusiasm, product featured prominently, energetic pacing, explaining obsession points.
- Example 3: "The SECRET nobody tells you about [problem]." Conspiratorial tone, creator looking around as if sharing insider info, whisper-to-normal voice dynamic.

### 15. MYTH BUSTING/CONTRARIAN HOOKS

**Description:** Directly challenging conventional wisdom, common beliefs, or widely-accepted practices in your industry. Positions your product as the enlightened alternative.

**Best For:**
- Sophisticated markets (where audience has tried multiple solutions)
- Products with unique mechanisms
- Categories with established "wrong" approaches
- Educational content
- Building authority and expertise
- High-ticket or complex purchases

**Psychological Basis:**
- Cognitive Dissonance: Creates tension between what they believe and what you're saying
- Curiosity Gap: Must know why their belief is wrong
- Authority Signal: Only experts can confidently contradict norms
- Value Elevation: If they're doing it wrong, there's a better way
- Validation Seeking: Want to know if they've been misled

**Best Practices:**
- State the myth clearly and early
- Provide compelling reason why it's wrong
- Offer your solution as the truth
- Use format: "If you're [doing X] just once per [timeframe], you're f*cked"
- Or: "STOP using [X] for [result]. Here's why."
- Can present as list: "3 things I would NEVER do if I wanted [result]"
- Back up claims with science, data, or expert opinion

**Specific Considerations:**
- Works best in high-sophistication markets
- Don't agitate the problem they already know — focus on failed solutions
- Must have legitimate alternative to offer
- Can alienate some people (that's okay — you're qualifying)
- Particularly effective when revealing hidden mechanisms
- Great for launching innovative products

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "If you're using retinol just once a week, you're f*cked. Here's why." Creator with serious expression, then transitions to explaining correct usage with product demonstration.
- Example 2: "STOP drinking electrolytes with stevia." Bold text, creator pouring out drink. Red X over traditional electrolytes, green check over alternative shown.
- Example 3: "3 things I would NEVER do in my 20s if I wanted to retire by 30." Countdown format (3, 2, 1), each mistake shown and explained, alternative revealed.

### 16. EDUCATIONAL HOOKS

**Description:** Promising to teach, explain, or reveal valuable information in a specific timeframe. Positions the ad as educational content rather than a sales pitch, while building authority.

**Best For:**
- Complex products requiring explanation
- Low-sophistication markets (audience doesn't know solution exists)
- Building brand authority
- Top-of-funnel awareness
- Service-based businesses
- B2B offerings

**Psychological Basis:**
- Value Exchange: Teaching = giving before asking
- Authority Building: Teachers are trusted
- Curiosity Satisfaction: We want to learn and grow
- Commitment Escalation: Once learning, more likely to act
- Pattern Familiarity: Educational content feels safe, not sales-y

**Best Practices:**
- Specify what they'll learn: "I'll teach you [X]"
- Include timeframe: "in less than 1 minute," "in this video"
- Call out target audience: "If you're a [demographic] and want [result]"
- Frame as discovery: "I just found the biggest [industry] cheat code"
- Deliver actual value — don't bait and switch
- Can use numbered lists for structure

**Specific Considerations:**
- Essential for low-sophistication markets
- Don't waste time educating problem in high-sophistication markets
- Works well for unaware and problem-aware stages
- Creates "light bulb" moments
- Can reveal "hidden villain" causing their problem
- Position product as natural extension of education

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: "If you're a remote worker and want better posture, listen to this video." Creator at desk, clear target demographic visual, transitions to teaching proper setup.
- Example 2: "It took me 10 years to learn this but I'll teach it to you in less than 1 minute." Creator speaking earnestly, on-screen timer, rapid-fire teaching with graphics.
- Example 3: "I just found the biggest skincare cheat code." Creator excited, conspiratorial tone, green screen over educational content revealing industry secret.

### 17. COMPARISON HOOKS

**Description:** Setting up a direct comparison between two options, approaches, age groups, or products to help viewer make a decision or see which path to follow.

**Best For:**
- Solution-aware audiences
- Categories with clear alternatives
- Differentiation from competitors
- Decision-stage content
- Products with clear superiority
- Educational positioning

**Psychological Basis:**
- Decision Simplification: Binary choices are easier than multiple options
- Contrast Effect: Things are understood better in comparison
- Value Demonstration: Shows superiority without explicit claims
- Relatability: Everyone compares options when buying
- Curiosity: Which one wins?

**Best Practices:**
- Frame as: "Cheap vs. Expensive [X]," "Lifting vs. Cardio for abs"
- Age-based: "If you're between [age] and [age], do these # things"
- Method comparison: What works faster/better
- Show both options fairly, then reveal winner
- Use split-screen or side-by-side visuals
- Can use yourself vs. friend/past self format

**Specific Considerations:**
- Great for sophisticated markets
- Avoid overly disparaging competitors (unless that's your brand voice)
- Must back up claims if saying one is better
- Works well with before/after content
- Can be subtle or explicit in recommendation
- Pairs well with demonstration content

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Split screen: "Cheap mascara vs. Expensive mascara." Left side shows drugstore, right shows luxury. Apply both, dramatic close-ups of results side by side.
- Example 2: "Lifting vs. Cardio — What gets you abs faster?" Creator demonstrating both, time stamps showing results, clear winner revealed with reasoning.
- Example 3: "If you're between 25-35 and want to buy a house, you have to do these 3 things." Numbered list format, each comparison point shown visually.

### 18. STORYTELLING HOOKS

**Description:** Opening with the beginning of a story that creates intrigue and promises a journey with resolution. Uses narrative structure to hook attention and build emotional investment.

**Best For:**
- Unaware audiences (don't know they have a problem)
- Building emotional connection
- Complex customer journeys
- Founder stories
- Testimonials and case studies
- Longer-form content

**Psychological Basis:**
- Dopamine Release: Stories trigger dopamine rise; brain craves progression
- Narrative Transportation: We become absorbed in stories
- Emotional Investment: Characters make us care
- Memory Formation: Stories are more memorable than facts
- Completion Desire: Must know how it ends
- Relatability: We see ourselves in others' stories

**Best Practices:**
- Start in medias res: "5 years ago my [person] told me [quote]"
- Show transformation: "In 1 year I went from [before] to [after]"
- Create perspective shifts — character learns/discovers something
- Include unexpected moments for dopamine spikes
- Build on surprises instead of letting them fizzle
- Try 15-40 seconds of setup before solution intro
- Use curiosity loops — open incomplete thoughts, delay resolution

**Specific Considerations:**
- Best for unaware → problem aware audiences
- Can spend longer in problem/education before product
- Meta's algo favors creative diversity across funnel stages
- Don't always make product the only hero
- Place product in middle of journey for authenticity
- Use story arcs: Intro → Conflict → Rising Action → Climax → Resolution
- Organic creators use story arcs vs. rigid DR formulas

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: "5 years ago my doctor told me I'd never run again." Creator looking reflective, cut to hospital setting, then progressive shots showing recovery journey, ends with creator running.
- Example 2: "In 1 year I went from sleeping 3 hours a night to 8 hours consistently. Here's how." Before clips show exhaustion, dark circles, frustration. After shows energized, happy morning routine.
- Example 3: Opening shot of creator looking defeated, voiceover: "I'd tried everything..." Montage of failed attempts, building tension, then "Until this moment..." Product introduced naturally in context.

### 19. AUTHORITY HOOKS

**Description:** Leveraging expertise, credentials, or achievement status to establish credibility and trust from the first frame. Using experts, professionals, or accomplished individuals to deliver the message.

**Best For:**
- Trust-sensitive categories (health, finance, professional services)
- High-sophistication markets
- Premium/high-ticket products
- B2B services
- Categories with lots of misinformation
- Partnership ads with experts

**Psychological Basis:**
- Trust Transfer: Expert authority = product authority
- Cognitive Shortcuts: Credentials reduce need for personal verification
- Social Proof: Experts validate our choices
- Risk Reduction: Less risky to trust an expert
- Authenticity: Real expertise is hard to fake
- Insider Knowledge: Access to professional secrets

**Best Practices:**
- State credentials clearly: "As a [doctor/expert/professional]..."
- Frame as: "Here's how my client got [result] without [pain point]"
- Use format: "I became a [achievement] at [age] and if I could give you 3 pieces of advice..."
- Whitelist through expert's account (Meta pushing partnership ads)
- Let expert use insider language and terminology
- Show expertise through demonstration, not just claims
- Pair with client results or case studies

**Specific Considerations:**
- More expensive than regular UGC (but worth it for trust)
- Can't fake credentials — must be legitimate
- Industry-specific: doctor (health), aesthetician (beauty), food critic (food), etc.
- Experts speak more authentically than actors reading scripts
- Use insider phrases/terms only professionals would know
- Whitelisting performs exceptionally well
- Will become more important as AI content increases

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: "As a dermatologist with 15 years experience..." Creator in white coat or professional setting, credentials shown on screen. Speaking from professional perspective about skin science.
- Example 2: "Here's how my client got 6-pack abs in 8 weeks without giving up carbs." Fitness professional showing client transformation, professional setting (gym), demonstrates expertise through teaching.
- Example 3: Music producer in studio: "Before Suno... [makes simple loop]." Shows professional workflow, uses insider terms ("stems," "production," "sauce"), demonstrates mastery of craft.

### 20. QUESTION HOOKS

**Description:** Opening with a direct question that the viewer instinctively wants answered. Can be rhetorical, provocative, or genuinely seeking engagement.

**Best For:**
- Engaging curiosity
- Problem-aware audiences
- Creating urgency around solutions
- Educational content
- Interactive feeling ads
- Sparking debate

**Psychological Basis:**
- Open Loop: Brain seeks closure to unanswered questions
- Personal Relevance: Questions make us self-assess
- Engagement: We mentally (or actually) answer questions
- Curiosity Activation: Must know the answer
- Pattern Recognition: Questions signal a conversation, not a pitch

**Best Practices:**
- Make it relevant to target audience's pain or desire
- Frame as: "Is it possible to [desired action]?"
- Or: "Do you ever wonder why [problem exists]?"
- Can be provocative: "Is it possible to get a 6-pack in 6 weeks?"
- Answer the question in the ad (don't leave hanging)
- Pair with educational or demonstration content
- Use viewer's internal voice

**Specific Considerations:**
- Must be genuinely interesting question
- Don't ask questions with obvious answers
- Can be rhetorical if it sets up your point
- Works well for solution-aware stage
- Combine with other hook types (question + demographic)
- Pause after question before answering (tension)

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1: Text overlay: "Is it possible to get clear skin without giving up dairy?" Creator with thoughtful expression, pause, then transitions to explanation with product demo.
- Example 2: "Have you ever wondered why you wake up exhausted even after 8 hours of sleep?" Creator in bed, relatable tired expression, then educates on sleep quality vs. quantity.
- Example 3: "Can you really build muscle eating only plants?" Shows split screen with question mark, then demonstrates with plant-based meals and fitness results.

### 21. PERMISSION / ZONE 1 HOOKS

**Description:** A calm, high-valence opener that *releases* the viewer from an obligation instead of *activating* fear or urgency. Where most DR hooks push ("Are you exhausted from X?"), a permission hook lifts a weight: "You don't have to keep doing this." It opens in the Soft zone — positive valence, low intensity — and meets a scrolling viewer in the decompression mood she's actually in. (The why is in `hook-psychology.md` #11: valence × intensity, the headspace at exposure, release-vs-activate.)

**Best For:**
- Cold audiences (relief converts on cold traffic where pressure makes people scroll)
- Exhausted/overwhelmed or saturated categories where everyone else opens in "Panic"
- Women 35–55 (permission as a psychological lever) and 55–75 (permission as respect, after decades of "fix-it" marketing)
- Trust-sensitive categories and gentler-alternative products
- Brands whose entire pitch is "there's a kinder way"

**Psychological Basis:**
- Release, not activation: gives the brain relief — something it wasn't braced for — instead of demanding a new high-stakes feeling
- Borrowed authority: a calm "you're allowed to stop" echoes a teacher/parent/mentor granting permission to rest
- Headspace match: lands in the Soft zone she's already in rather than ambushing her
- No-agenda signal: a hook that isn't immediately selling reads as trustworthy

**Best Practices:**
- Open in Zone 1 (calm, positive, low intensity). Openers: "You don't have to…", "You're allowed to stop…", "Nobody told you, but there's a kinder way to…"
- Name an obligation she's *already* carrying — excavate it ("she's been white-knuckling ___ for too long"), then convert it to a release
- It's fine to dip to Zone 3 (mild shared frustration / a lived micro-moment that makes her feel *seen*, not accused) to validate the problem, then resolve back to Zone 1 before the ask
- Sound like a calm, trusted friend reading it out loud; if it sounds urgent or salesy, soften it
- The zone has to **hold the whole way through** — the body cannot turn into a high-pressure pitch

**Specific Considerations — what kills this format (avoid):**
- **Contrast language too early** ("most people do X, but you should do Y") — reads preachy before trust exists; save it for the middle
- **Victim framing** ("you've been lied to") — positions her as helpless before she's opted in; use only on warm audiences
- **Hedging** ("this might not be for everyone, but…") — uncertainty in the hook signals uncertainty in the product
- **The fakeout** — a Zone 1 open with a Zone 4 body; she clocks the dissonance even if she can't name it
- Pairs well with a two-voice structure (a peer names the feeling → a credentialed/kind expert grants the permission)

**Visual Examples:**
- Example 1 (clean hair color): Voiceover, warm and unhurried — "You're allowed to want your color and your health. You don't have to choose." Show the salon chair, the cape, the smell that hits before the bowl is even open; she just sits there and waits for it to be over. Voiceover bridges: "I didn't want to stop coloring. I just didn't want to keep doing it like this. What should I do?" Calm open → a micro-moment she knows by heart → an open loop that makes the next frame feel like relief, not a pitch.
- Example 2 (supplement, older audience): "You've done enough hard things this year. This one's easy." Creator calm at a kitchen table, no urgency, no countdown.
- Example 3 (two-voice): Female peer opens — "we all somehow decided this was just how it had to be… it's not" — then a kind expert steps in to grant the permission and explain the gentler way.

## Platform-Specific Considerations

### Meta (Facebook & Instagram) vs. TikTok

**Current State (2026):** The line between TikTok-specific and Meta-specific content is increasingly blurred. Reels has essentially become a mirror of TikTok, meaning most formats work across both platforms.

**Key Differences:**
- TikTok: More forgiving of organic, raw content; trends move faster; younger skew; sound is more important
- Meta: Broader demographics; longer ad life; better for direct response; more sophisticated targeting

**General Rule:** If it performs on TikTok organically, it will likely perform on Meta as paid. If it performs on Meta, test organically on TikTok.

### Age-Based Considerations

- **18-25:** Very fast cuts, 15-30 second ads, trending sounds, high energy, authentic/raw
- **25-35:** 20-35 second ads, balanced energy, some polish acceptable, story-driven
- **35-50:** 30-45 second ads, more educational, clearer structure, slightly more polish
- **50+:** 45-60+ second ads, slower pace, clear explanations, trust-building, more traditional structure

**General Rule:** Video length should roughly match age range (20-30 year olds = 20-30 second ads).

**Pacing by Age:**
- Under 30: Cut every 2-3 seconds minimum
- 30-50: Cut every 3-4 seconds
- Over 50: Cut every 3-5 seconds (older generation has longer attention span)

### Partnership Ads (Whitelisting)

**Why It Matters:** Meta explicitly stated they are pushing partnership ads in 2026 and beyond. Whitelisting through creator accounts performs exceptionally well.

**Best Applications:**
- Authority figures and experts
- Influencers and micro-influencers
- Founder content through founder's personal account
- User-generated content through actual user accounts

**Performance Benefits:**
- Higher trust (coming from person, not brand)
- Better feed placement
- Increased engagement
- Lower CPMs in many cases
- Authentic social proof

### Organic vs. Paid Content Styling

**The Shift:** 2022-2023 style ads are instantly recognizable as ads now. The feed has moved past:
- Exaggerated "ad voice"
- Over-edited Mr. Beast style cutting
- "This is an incredible product! I have so much energy now!"
- Heavy soundtracks and stock music
- Perfect lighting and obviously staged settings

**What Works Now (2026):**
- Conversational, natural tone
- Slight imperfections that signal authenticity
- Natural captions (not perfectly formatted)
- Combination of scripted and unscripted content
- No or minimal soundtrack (many top performers have no music)
- Varied text styles and summary overlays
- Lighting should be GOOD but natural
- Different elements that organic creators use

**The Question to Ask:** "If this piece of content were on the organic feed, what would it look like?"
- How would it be scripted?
- What type of creator?
- Where would it be shot?
- How would it be edited?

Keep asking this relentlessly at every stage to ensure native feel.

## Must-Haves For Every Hook

**Relevance First, Attention Second**
- Wrong audience attention = wasted spend
- Must qualify ideal customer immediately

**Clear Promise**
- What's in it for them?
- Stated or strongly implied within 3 seconds

**Zero Confusion**
- Simple language (5th grade reading level or below)
- One clear idea
- No clickbait without payoff

**Emotional Targeting**
- Know which emotion/desire you're targeting
- If you can't name it, scrap the concept
- Emotion drives purchase, logic justifies

**Native Format**
- Must look like it belongs on the platform
- Not obviously an ad
- Study organic top performers constantly
