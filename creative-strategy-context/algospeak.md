# Algospeak

Algospeak is the coded language people invent to get past a platform's automated moderation while still being understood by human viewers. "Unalive" for suicide. "Seggs" for sex. "Corn" for porn. "Camping" for abortion. When a topic is legitimate but the filters can't tell the difference between talking about it and promoting it, creators route around the filter with a word the machine hasn't learned yet.

For a brand, this matters the moment the product or the message touches a policed category — cannabis, adult wellness, mental health, harm reduction, crypto, body image, sex education. The account keeps getting content removed or quietly suppressed, and nobody can point to the rule that was broken. Algospeak is one lever for that problem. But it's a navigation tool, not a magic bypass, and the biggest risk is treating it like the whole answer. The reasoning below is more durable than any single word, because the words change constantly — the moment a term gets detected, the community mints a new one.

## Why it exists and how it works

Platforms moderate with AI that scans for a short list of danger categories: self-harm, sexual content, drug references, hate speech, violence. The AI is blunt. It can't read intent, so a sex-ed teacher, a harm-reduction nonprofit, and someone actually promoting harm all trip the same wire. The platforms never publish the full ban list, and enforcement shifts by region, by moment, and by context — so what creators have instead is folk knowledge, shared and rotated as terms burn out.

Algospeak solves the machine problem without solving the human problem: the coded word slips past the classifier, but a person still reads the meaning. That's the whole mechanism, and it's why it works better in some places than others. It leans on the gap between what an automated filter catches and what a human reviewer catches — so the more a decision rests on the automated layer, the more room algospeak has to work.

## How enforcement differs by platform

The single most useful read is which layer of moderation you're up against, because that decides how much algospeak buys you.

**TikTok** runs the most aggressive automated filters and the most opaque rules, and the For You algorithm actively rewards content that avoids flags — so the coded-language culture is strongest here. There's no published ban list, and innocent words ("weird," "bean," "melon") have triggered flags. The filter scans on-screen text and captions harder than it scans spoken audio, which is the key tactical seam. Regional variation is real: the same clip can be removed in one country and left up in another.

**Meta (Instagram and Facebook)** mixes AI with human reviewers, so enforcement is more visible and more inconsistent than TikTok's, and documented bias runs through it. Algospeak buys less here because a human reviewer sees through obvious codes — but obfuscation still helps against the automated first pass, and framing (alt text, captions, context signals) does real work. Facebook Groups filter less aggressively than public posts, which makes them useful for community.

Reddit and LinkedIn sit far lower on algospeak usage — the entertainment-first, hardest-enforced platforms breed the strongest coded language, and those two are neither.

The takeaway isn't "TikTok = use more codes." It's that on the automated-heavy platform, subtle wording plus a strong hook can carry a clip past the filter; on the human-in-the-loop platform, lean less on the code and more on legitimate framing, because a reviewer will read the code the same way your audience does.

## The core principle: navigate stigma, don't hide violations

Algospeak is for legitimate topics that are stigmatized or legally restricted — sex education, harm reduction, a compliant CBD product, a licensed therapist talking about suicide prevention. It is not a way to sneak an actual policy violation past the platform, and it doesn't make a violation acceptable. Using "grape" to trivialize assault, dodging a required health disclosure, or hiding a genuinely banned product is not navigation, it's a fast way to lose the account. Everything downstream assumes the underlying content is allowed and the problem is only that the filter can't tell.

So the pattern that actually survives is: **subtle coded language, plus strong engagement, plus full compliance, plus educational or wellness framing.** Drop any one of those and the content either gets caught or gets caught later.

## The dictionary

Documented, real-world usage, grouped by topic. This is a reference to *understand the landscape* — what these terms mean when you see them in the wild and which ones a brand can responsibly borrow. Terms rotate fast, so treat any specific word as a snapshot, not a fixed vocabulary. Some entries below exist only so you recognize them and steer clear (the assault and harassment codes are documentation, never a brand tool).

**Death and suicide** — `unalive` / `unalived` (die, kill oneself), `sewer slide` (suicide, meme register), `Kermit sewer slide` (same, darker meme).

**Sex and sexual content** — `seggs` / `shmex` / `segss` (sex), `corn` (porn / explicit content), `spicy eggplant` (vibrator / sex toy, with 🍆), `accounting` / `accountant` (sex worker). `S.A.` (sexual assault) and `grape` / 🍇 (rape) appear in survivor discussion — recognize them; a brand does not use them.

**LGBTQ+** — `leg booty` ("LGBT" said aloud), `le$bean` / `le-dollar-bean` (lesbian).

**Drugs and substances** — `ouid` (weed), `gardening` (smoking weed), `Penjamins` / `Benjamins` (weed vape), `drip` (drugs or self-harm substances, harm-reduction context).

**COVID / pandemic** — `panini` / `panorama`, `Backstreet Boys reunion tour`, `pon de replay` (all pandemic misdirection).

**Abortion and reproductive health** — `camp` / `going camping` (getting an abortion).

**Anatomy** — `ahh` ("ass," spelled to dodge the filter).

**Algorithm-amplified slang (not censorship-driven)** — `rizz` (charisma / flirting), `delulu` (delusional, esp. about romance), `brain rot` (mind-numbing content, often 💩), `menty b` / `mentyh` (mental breakdown), `fine shyt` ("fine shit," misspelled). These aren't dodging a filter — the algorithm just amplified them into slang. Worth knowing because they read as native and can date a piece of creative fast.

**Harassment codes** — `KYS` ("kill yourself"). Documentation only; harassment either way.

## Category playbooks

Each category is a mix of what the platform outright forbids, what it allows with approval, and where coded framing does honest work. Read the platform rule first — no amount of clever wording fixes a category that's flat-out banned for paid ads, and there the answer is an organic-content strategy, not a workaround.

**Cannabis and CBD.** TikTok forbids paid ads for ingestible or smokable cannabis; non-intoxicating hemp topicals are sometimes allowed with certification. Meta broadly forbids cannabis ads, and CBD topicals need brand certification and prior approval with no targeting of minors. Across platforms, overt consumption imagery reads as promotion and gets pulled. So the honest play is wellness- and industry-framed content — "garden," "herb," nature and abstract-green visuals — never buds or smoking, always age-gated (21+), always disclaimed and labeled `#ad`. Message the wellness angle and the category, not "get high."

**Sex toys and adult wellness.** TikTok Shop bans sex toys and erotic products outright, and TikTok content that suggests sexual activity risks removal; Meta enforces strict nudity and sex rules. The workable frame is wellness and relationship health — "personal massager," "self-care device," "intimacy," "couples wellness" — with abstract shapes and elegant packaging rather than anyone using a product. Third-person, clinical framing ("research suggests…") reads as informational. Age-filter hard, never target minors, and always disclose. This is the clearest case where the product may simply be un-advertisable as a paid unit on a given surface, and the strategy moves to organic and community.

**Harm reduction and mental health.** This is the most heavily policed and the highest-stakes category, because the failure mode isn't a removed ad, it's harm. TikTok prohibits content that normalizes self-harm or dangerous drug use. The responsible pattern pairs coded terms (`unalive`, `sewer slide` in a hashtag or voiceover) with the opposite of what a violation looks like: trigger warnings, an explicit "reach out," and real help resources on screen. Overdose-prevention creators code naloxone as `moonshot reverser` or `Lil rescue 💉` to get life-saving information past the filter. The coded word is a delivery vehicle for help, never a substitute for it — if the resources and the safety framing aren't there, don't run it.

**Crypto and financial advice.** TikTok allows crypto ads only through an approval process and only for established exchanges and wallets, with strict disclosures, and forbids misleading investment claims. The honest posture here is education over hype — "blockchain basics," "digital gold," "market insights" — with meme-context slang used lightly (`hodl`, `bag`, `moon mission`), a standing "not financial advice" disclaimer, and no profit promises. The coded framing (a skit about a "money worm" eating dollar signs to mean inflation) is a creative device, not a way to dodge the disclosure rules.

**Body image and sex education.** TikTok's 2021 update explicitly allows frank sex-ed language in an educational context, which changes the game — labeling content as instructional unlocks clinical terms ("vulva," "period," anatomy) that read as violations out of context. Instagram still bans female nipples and has a long history of over-removing bodies. So the play is educational framing plus body-positive language ("curvy," "plus-size," never a negative adjective), graphics or hands rather than exposed anatomy, and — on Instagram specifically — the alt-text field to describe the image so context survives even if a caption word trips the filter. Hashtags like `#SexEd` and `#BodyPositivity` signal informational intent to both the algorithm and the reviewer.

## Tactical implementation

A few mechanics recur across categories and are worth holding as principles rather than a checklist.

**Name the category up front, code in the middle.** Moderation scans headlines and first frames hardest, and the algorithm needs to classify the content to serve it. So state the category cleanly in the first three seconds (the algorithm gets it), use the coded language in the middle where a human understands but the filter may miss, and close with a compliant CTA and disclaimers. This is the opposite of hiding the topic — you're being legible to the algorithm and subtle only where the filter is blunt.

**Play the text-vs-audio seam (TikTok).** Captions and on-screen text get scanned harder than spoken audio, so a flagged term said in voiceover survives where the same word on screen gets caught. Light character obfuscation (one substituted letter, a Unicode variant) helps on the text layer.

**Use Instagram's alt text.** Writing clear, detailed alt text reinforces harmless intent to the automated pass and helps accessibility at the same time — a rare no-downside move. Descriptive captions ("intimate wellness," "sexual health") beat blunt phrasing.

**Let engagement do work.** High early engagement can counteract algorithmic suppression, and a strong hook can carry a clip past a single flagged word. Creative quality is part of the compliance strategy, not separate from it.

**Test, tweak, and rotate.** If a post gets ghost-banned, changing one word or adding an emoji and reuploading often clears it. Terms burn out as they spread, so monitor the hashtags and rotate before a word is fully detected.

## Compliance guardrails

What algospeak is **not**: a license to break platform rules, a tool for hiding real violations, or a way around required disclosures. It sits *inside* the rules.

Always required, regardless of clever wording:

- **Age gates** — cannabis 21+, adult products 18+, any sensitive category.
- **Disclosures** — `#ad` / `#PaidPartnership` for sponsored or affiliate content, "not financial advice," "consult a doctor," and the like where the category demands them.
- **Help resources** — any time the content touches self-harm, suicide, or addiction.
- **Accurate claims** — no misleading the audience about a product's benefits or a regulation.
- **Approval where mandated** — Meta CBD certification, TikTok crypto approval, established entities only.

When *not* to use it at all: hate speech, child exploitation, and violence promotion are always prohibited and no framing changes that. Content that actually breaks a rule stays a violation in code. And where clarity is legally required — a health warning, a financial disclosure — say it plainly; do not obscure it.

## The reality check

Platform moderation is four layers, not one: automated filters (what algospeak navigates), human reviewers (who see through obvious codes), engagement signals (which can overcome suppression), and policy enforcement (which removes real violations no matter how they're worded). Algospeak only touches the first layer. That's why the durable formula is coded language *and* strong engagement *and* full compliance *and* educational framing — and why, when in doubt, the safer move is to frame the content as educational, give it proper context, include the disclosures, and stay inside the rules. Getting a clip suppressed costs a day. Getting the brand's account restricted costs the channel.
