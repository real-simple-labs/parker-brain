# Ideation and brainstorming

How a senior creative strategist generates ideas — the method Parker runs when it ideates. This is the reasoning the brand idea bank operationalizes — when Parker captures an idea, this is the thinking that decides what an idea is, where it comes from, and what makes it worth keeping. Spec for the storage and schema lives in `prompts/ideas-and-briefs/brand-idea-bank.md`.

Two modes run underneath this doc: the always-on ideation posture that never turns off, and the concept-planning method for sizing and shaping a round. Every example throughout is an illustration of the method, never a template to reproduce.

**The core posture: the instinct never turns off.** The most important thing to carry from this method is not a source or a technique, it is a stance. A senior strategist is always, quietly, asking whether the thing in front of them could become an ad — watching TV, scrolling, overhearing a stranger, reading a review. Parker should hold that same stance through everything it does, every context doc, every audit, every research pass, with one question running in the back of its mind the whole time: is any of this worth keeping? The sources and disciplines below are how that instinct gets pointed and recorded, but the instinct itself is the thing to internalize first. It is not a step at the end of a task. It is on the entire time.

## Ideation is not concepting

These are two different jobs done in two different mindsets, and the most common mistake an e-commerce brand makes is lumping them together. Ideation and brainstorming is the playground: the doors are fully open, the ideas are half-baked, and the work is to notice and log. Concepting is serious business: you sit down, pick from what you have already logged, and build a specific idea out into a brief with variations. One is playing, the other is execution, and the switching cost between them is real, because once you are building a concept all of your brainpower is on that concept and none of it is left for noticing the next idea.

The practical consequence is that ideation runs continuously and concepting happens in dedicated sessions. A strategist who only starts looking for ideas when it is time to concept will spend the first three hours of every concepting session scouring for inspiration and spinning their wheels. The strategist who has been logging ideas all month walks into concepting with a bank to prioritize from.

For the best strategists ideation never turns off. It happens watching TV and analyzing the ad you got served, overhearing how a woman describes her lipstick in a bathroom, walking, showering. The instinct is always on, and the only discipline that matters is logging what you notice the moment you notice it.

## The ice box is always-on memory

The idea bank, the ice box, the idea list — every strategist has one and they all look a little different, but the function is the same. It is a living, breathing document of half-baked ideas you pull from when you concept. It is not a concept doc and it is not a brief. It holds the core piece of inspiration and a note on what you liked about it, so that when you return to concept you remember why the idea earned a place.

The capture bar is lower than a concept's bar on purpose. Ideas do not need to be complete to be logged. A hook, a format, a storyline, a single line of customer language, a visual — any of these is enough to capture if something about it resonated. The note on why you liked it is what makes the half-baked idea retrievable later.

But the bar is freshness, not familiarity. The ice box is for new directions worth targeting, a fresh angle, a different visual, a hook the brand is not already running. It is not for the brand's own known assets reflected back. An award the brand already advertises, a collaboration already in its ads, a channel it already runs, hands the strategist nothing to test, and logging it is the opposite of ideation. A strategist hunts the directions others are not going, which is the whole reason the gold mine is old ads and not the same feed everyone scrolls. When an idea comes out of a brand's own reputation or identity work, the question to ask before logging it is whether it is already part of the brand's current story, because if it is, it is already known and useless as inspiration.

## Capture is a transfer, not a summary

The two phases divide on one move. **Ideas is a transfer:** carry the raw inspo across intact — the full media analysis, the exact words, the actual hook as said — and do not generalize it. **Briefs is the judgment:** take the transferred pile plus the Phase-2 recommendations and decide which ideas earn production, weighed against the hypotheses the brand holds, the gaps in the market, what the brand has already run, and which lever each idea pulls (persona, product, messaging, or creator and talent).

Capture stays verbatim because the brief phase grades the real source, not a compression of it. The moment a TikTok is flattened into "relatable gut-health UGC," the hook wording, the visual beat, and the exact phrasing that made it worth keeping are gone, and the idea is no longer gradable. This holds hardest for the media analysis — paste it in whole. A long verbatim entry is correct; a tidy summary is the failure. The strategist's note rides on top of the source to say what to notice; it never stands in for it.

## The spark rides with the source

The moment of noticing is also the moment of combining, and the combination is the creative act. When a strategist stops on a vintage comparison line, the brand's version of that line arrives in the same breath. When a floating-topics yapper format lands, the picture of the brand's benefits floating around a creator's head arrives with it. A vintage ad of a woman matching gowns to refrigerators became a top-performing rug ad because the transfer happened in the strategist's head the second she saw it, years before any brief. That one-breath brand collision is the **spark**, and it does not keep — three weeks later, "liked the format" is a dead entry, while the spark is a live one.

So the spark is a first-class field on the idea-bank entry. It sits *on top of* the untouched verbatim source, one breath long: what this is, and how it could carry this brand. The articulation test is its gate — if the spark arrives naturally, in a second or two, record it; if the fit has to be forced or labored into words, leave the field empty and say so, because a forced spark is evidence against the idea, not for it. An entry with no spark is still a valid entry.

The spark is a direction, never a build. "This format, pointed at our cup-to-disc switcher" is a spark. A storyline written out for the brand, variations, a script, a brief — those are concepting, and they stay banned inside an entry. And the spark lives only in the idea bank, the strategist's own icebox. Competitor, inspiration, and affinity *source-capture docs* stay purely descriptive per the attribution rules — a profile of what a rival is doing never contains how the active brand should respond. The two surfaces are different on purpose: one is a portrait, the other is a notebook.

## The weekly hunt, structured

The always-on posture never turns off, but the dedicated weekly hunt has a shape, and the shape exists because each step protects something the ad-hoc scroll loses.

**First, the hunt brief — assembled before any feed opens, and emitted with the run.** A strategist never hunts cold. Before scrolling one video she is holding the diagnosis, the personas ranked by confidence, what is working in the account right now, and what has been tried and failed — so she never logs a stale direction and never misses the lowest-hanging fruit. The hunt brief is that read, made mandatory: the roadmap's ranked priorities and personas, a live what's-working pull, the tried-and-failed angles per active SKU, the last evaluation's read of what the bank is starving for, and the week's user signals — what the team asked Parker to make, what they saved, what they rejected, read from the conversation history. The brief sets the week's lanes. A hunt with no brief in its output didn't run this method.

**Second, the cold pass — the notepad, before the feed pollutes.** Silence, no inputs: generate the simplest-language hooks and storylines the personas and the customer's own verbatim produce on their own, and log the ones that pass the articulation test. This runs first for exactly the reason the notebook exercise runs first: once the mind is full of everyone else's inspiration, its own simplest lines stop coming. Cold-pass entries carry the persona and the customer language that seeded them as their provenance.

**Third, the lanes — hunters with genuinely different dispositions.** One brain hunting one pass finds one kind of idea; the strongest sessions are soaked in other strategists' eyes, because two brains on the same account produce completely different creative outcomes. So the weekly hunt splits into lenses, each with its own sources and its own taste: the customer-language purist (reviews, ad comments, Reddit, community threads), the format hunter (organic TikTok and Instagram, affinity paid ads), the historian (the old-ads corpus), and the far-transfer wildcard. The wildcard's lane is deliberate: two or three *unrelated* categories each week — rotated, and the rotation logged in the run output so it never repeats back-to-back — hunted for mechanisms rather than messages, because the richest transfers come from outside the category entirely (swimwear and cotton dresses feeding a period-underwear round; refrigerators feeding rugs). A **trend lane** rides with the format hunter: what moved in the brand's space *this week*, because a video posted yesterday is often the best find in the session, and each trend keeper carries two or three sparks for how it could work.

**Fourth, the merge.** Dedupe across lenses, run the articulation test, write the entries — verbatim source intact, winning elements named, spark where it landed. The merge dedupes; it never grades. Capture stays generous, and grading remains the evaluator's job.

## Where ideas come from

The sources rank roughly, and the ranking shifts depending on whether Parker is ideating broadly or concepting for a specific brand. The order below is the broad-ideation view; the concepting view is in the next section.

**Old print ads — the gold mine.** There is nothing new under the sun, so the richest place to find inspiration is the past, especially for statics. Old print ads carry strong headlines and strong visuals because a print shoot cost so much that brands spent hours getting a single concept right, which is exactly the craft and confidence most modern AI statics lack. The books matter: All-American Ads categorizes by industry so you can find the beauty page or the appliance page, and Life magazines are where the long-form advertorials lived. The method is to find a piece of copy or a visual and adapt its shape. One example adapted a vintage category-comparison line into a new product comparison with the USPs arrayed around it. Another took a grid layout, which works on Meta because humans love choices, and used it as the bones of a new ad. A vintage matching-game visual can become a creator matching products or looks across three or four swaps until she finds her favorite, no words spoken. Training Parker on a database of old ads is something no other model is doing, and it is the highest-value source for Parker to have. That database now has a home: the **old-ads corpus** at `old-ads/` in this doc's directory, organized by industry and by mechanic, grown continuously from public-domain and openly archived material — read its README for the capture method, and treat live web archives as the fallback when the corpus is thin on an industry.

**The natural world and your own brain.** Before the mind gets polluted by a feed of inspiration, sit in silence with a notebook and write the simplest-language hooks your own brain produces. Say goodbye to period prison is the kind of line that comes out of that exercise. It works solo and it works even better with a team, where a stack of sticky notes from very different minds produces hooks no single person would have reached. This method needs the personas locked first, because you cannot write to someone you have not defined. Beyond the notebook, the world is full of ideas — a print ad on the subway, a phrase a stranger uses, a friend's offhand reaction. Log them to a notes app the instant they land.

**Affinity brands over direct competitors, on the paid side.** Competitor paid ads are tricky. Some brands build their whole method on watching competitors, spotting the gap, and copying what looks like it is working, and that approach exists for a reason. But copying a competitor is rarely a novel way to scale, and the stronger move is to study affinity brands — brands close in nature to the product but not direct rivals — where there is more usable inspiration and less risk of simply running what the category already runs.

**Organic on TikTok and Instagram.** Roughly thirty percent of the time an organic video is a full concept worth adapting one to one; the rest of the time you are mining for the high-level idea, the messaging, the hook, or the format. Build an avatar account to see what the target customer actually sees. Sometimes the find is a hook that stops the scroll even when the rest of the video is irrelevant. Sometimes it is a format worth borrowing, like a yapper ad with floating topics around the speaker's head, where the format gets logged and not the content.

**Text sources.** Reddit and threads for concept ideation, and customer reviews for the exact language customers use.

## The qualifier question: what makes an idea worth keeping

There is no clean threshold that tells you an idea qualifies, so this is judgment, and the judgment is rooted in evidence that differs by source.

On the **paid side**, spend is the best signal that a concept is performing, because a brand will not keep paying to run an ad that is failing. Meta's recent ability to sort by impressions gets you closer to what is working in an affinity account. But impression count is not clean: an ad launched yesterday has low impressions because it has not had spend yet, not because it is a bad idea, so always weigh the launch date against the count. You are working from assumptions, and you have to know that.

On the **organic side**, views and engagement are the confidence signal. Higher views or stronger engagement push a concept up the priority list. A low-view piece is not disqualified, it just carries less confidence.

Across both, the deepest qualifier is the **goal and state of the account**, because what counts as a good idea is entirely relative to what the account is trying to do.

- A brand running paid for awareness to drive in-store foot traffic can run a story-led ad with no conversion focus, because the goal is recognition. A brand that needs online sales now cannot afford that risk and should lead with a problem and a simplified message.
- A brand whose account is a blank slate has a very low bar and a very big sea — almost any well-reasoned idea can make it in, because nothing has been tested. A mature account that has already tried every hook and angle forces a much more granular bar, where you need higher confidence to break into a new persona or to revive a tired product.
- This is why picking a lane helps. Knowing the goal, the SKU, or the persona you are ideating for narrows the playground enough to make the ideation productive. A new-scent launch, a single core product you are trying to make win first, a specific persona — each is a lane that changes what you are looking for.

And underneath all of it: assumptions are the number one killer of ads. Everything is still a test. Some of the best ads are the ones the strategist would not have bet on, so part of the craft is trust and a willingness to let a weak-looking idea run. The qualifier work is about moving closer to confidence, not about reaching certainty.

**The articulation test — how the decision actually gets made in the moment.** The deciding move when Parker scrolls is fast and tacit: something stops it, and within a second or two Parker can say *what* it liked and, roughly, *how* it would carry the brand — "the hook is the thing, never mind what she's talking about, it's the format"; "log the entry point, not just the line, the on-ramp is half the concept already built." When the read lands that quickly and the winning element has a name, it goes in the bank. The inverse is just as important a signal: when a source is interesting but forcing it onto the brand will not resolve, that strain is the tell to let it go. If the idea can't be formulated, that is the sign of trying too hard; the good ones come naturally. So the rule for Parker is not to manufacture a fit. If a source is genuinely reusable, the reason it is reusable and the shape it would take come quickly; if the only way to justify logging it is a labored explanation, that is evidence against it, not for it. Capture what lands and name why in a breath; skip what has to be forced. The tacit read cannot be fully explained, but forcing the why into every entry is exactly what turns it into a recorded one.

## The hierarchy when you are concepting for a specific brand

When the job shifts from broad ideation to concepting for a brand, the ranking changes, and the customer takes the cake. Anything that can be pulled from customer reviews and actual customer language comes first, because that is the messaging. Then you pair that message with a format — a vehicle to carry it to the customer — found in organic or in an affinity brand, because a message needs a vehicle. Then Reddit and threads for additional concept ideas. Then competitors, for the hooks and angles they are testing that you might adapt and run differently.

**Message first, format second — and how hard you hold that line is set by the account's maturity.** The default is message-first: decide what the ad has to say before you decide how it looks. But the *room* to work format-first — to pull a proven format from an affinity brand or an organic hit and attach a message to it later — is a luxury a mature account earns and a thin one cannot afford. A brand doing $60–100k/month with a proven hero SKU can flippantly test off formats and external inspiration, because it already knows what it sells and to whom. A brand with a message problem — nobody knows what the product does or why it is different — has to step back and establish the message first, because testing formats on an unproven message just muddies the read. On the concepting run above, that reasoning produced a roughly even split: about half the concepts were seeded by an inspiration video, and about half were pure message-and-persona plays with no inspo attached at concept time, the format chosen later at the brief. **A concept does not need an inspiration source to be valid.** The strongest concepts on a message-problem brand often come straight from a strategy gap — a persona or a message the account has never tested — and the strategist chooses the format at brief time to carry it. Do not treat a missing inspo link as a missing concept.

**Before concepting, take two live reads of the account, even after the audit is done.** The first is *what is already working* — re-check it in the moment, because doubling down on the one thing that already converts is a senior strategist's lowest-hanging fruit, and it is the thing that gets skimmed past in the rush for a new unlock. If one format, one collab, one angle is carrying the account, a share of the round should scale it, especially into a channel it has not been pushed through yet. The second is *what has already been tried, and what failed* — pull it per SKU, not just account-wide, because concepting the lead SKU means knowing every ad it has ever run, what worked, and what flopped. The point is twofold: do not re-run a concept the account already has, and steer away from the angles that have already been tested and failed. On that run the failed-angle read was explicit — authority hooks, clinical language, and studio-polish aesthetics had all been tried on the working collab and had not converted, so the round deliberately avoided them.

**Every concept enters through a doorway.** The doorway is the entry point the viewer takes into the product — the cognitive move the ad asks them to make. A *switch* asks a user of an old solution to change (tampon-to-disc, cup-to-disc). An *awakening* names a problem or a possibility the viewer has not consciously considered (that a lacy, leak-proof option even exists). A *discovery* introduces a product to someone already primed for the category. Naming the doorway before the concept is built keeps the message aimed at where the viewer actually is, and it is a first-class column on the concept map because two ads to the same persona can take completely different doorways.

## Idea, concept, brief

These are distinct stages and the idea bank holds only the first.

An **idea** is the half-baked first shot, and it can arrive as a hook, a format, a storyline, or a visual. The form tells you how close it is to a concept. A storyline is nearly a concept because it already implies messaging. A hook is the beginning of a concept. A format on its own is not a concept at all, because it carries no message.

A **concept** is an idea adapted for this specific brand and built out into variations. In the Meta 2026 landscape you do not give a concept one shot — you build the storyline into two or three variations that look very different from each other in hook, setting, or length, so that Meta gets the signals it needs to push the concept and choose where to spend. The teaching-grandma idea, a granddaughter showing her grandmother why we use period underwear now and the grandmother reacting, is a storyline close enough to concept quickly, but it is not a concept until it is adapted for the brand and broken into those variations.

A **brief** is the direction handed to the creator, either a full script or enough direction that the creator can put their own spin on the storyline you have defined. When the power of an idea is a creator's natural reaction, the brief has to stay open-ended, a reference video and talking points rather than a word-for-word script, because scripting kills the unscripted moment that made the source work in the first place.

## Planning the round — how many concepts, and how they split

Concepting a brand is not "generate a pile of ideas." Before a single brief is written, a senior strategist plans the round: how many concepts this sprint, how they split across the SKUs, how they allocate across the personas, and how many variations each carries. This is the layer that gets skipped, and it is where the eight hours of a concepting session actually go. Every number is backed into from a constraint, and the constraints are the account's real spend and testing capacity, not a preference for volume.

**Sizing the round.** The count is not a default and it is not "as many as possible." Volume is a heavily debated topic and the loudest advice — more ads is always better — is wrong for most accounts, because more ads without a method just spreads a thin budget thinner and muddies the read. The senior move is to back the number out of evidence:

- **Start from spend and current cadence.** Pull how much the account is spending (the trailing 30 days, and the year for context) and how many *net-new* concepts it is currently launching per week. Strip the sale and promotional creative out of that count first — a brand pumping out dozens of sale variations will read as high-volume when its real evergreen output is a fraction of that. On the grounding run this mattered enormously: ~27 unique creatives a week collapsed to ~5 net-new *evergreen* concepts a week once 259 evergreen ads over 52 weeks were separated from the promotional flood. That five, not the twenty-seven, is the account's true creative bandwidth.
- **Set the round as a deliberate multiple of that baseline, then gate it.** The instinct is to roughly double the current cadence — enough to give the team more to test than they are used to, without overwhelming an account that cannot support it. Doubling ~5 gave a round of ~10. The gate is the account's confidence and budget: a low-spend account with little proof and thin testing budget gets a tight round, because nothing is bursting through the spend and flooding it with untested concepts wastes money that will not get spent. A high-spend, high-confidence account can support a much larger round.
- **The hard rule underneath the number: every concept carries a specific hypothesis.** The round is a test, not a volume dump. If a concept cannot name what it is testing, it does not earn a slot. This is what keeps a doubled round from becoming noise — each added concept is a distinct, reasoned swing, not a variation for its own sake.
- **Other ways to size exist, and spend is still the anchor.** A client may simply tell you the deliverable (many agencies run flat rounds of 5, 10, or 20). A larger brand may not care about the per-concept read at all and runs volume to see where the cards fall. But absent a mandate, spend plus the account's proven historical output is the safest anchor, and the honest posture is that you can always scale the round up once the first sprint shows what is working — the goal is a confident shot on goal, then double down, not maximum assets on swing one.

**Splitting across SKUs and lanes.** Once the count is set, decide how it divides, and the division follows the diagnosis. The two forces are *scale the hero* — invest the majority into the one SKU the strategy has chosen to make win — and *double down on what already works* — protect and grow the thing currently carrying the account. On the grounding run a 10-concept round split 6 to the hero SKU being scaled and 4 to the already-working collab. Two guardrails govern the split. Do not give the working thing full rein: loading the whole round onto what already works would eat the account's spend and prevent a clean test of the thing you are actually trying to scale. And do not over-swing away from it: going all-in on the new SKU abandons the lowest-risk revenue on the table. The split is the balance between the two.

**Allocating across personas by confidence.** Inside a SKU, the concepts divide across the personas, and the divide is ranked by confidence — the persona the strategy is most sure will perform gets the most concepts, the emerging ones get fewer, and a persona can be deliberately dropped from the round entirely when the strategist does not want to spend a slot on it yet. On the grounding run the hero SKU's six concepts went two to the highest-confidence persona, two to the highest-emotional-leverage persona, one each to two more, and a fifth persona was consciously cut from this round. Allocation is a confidence statement, and dropping a persona is a legitimate, documented choice, not an oversight.

**Setting variation counts.** Each concept is not one asset. The Meta 2026 landscape needs divergent signals to find where to spend, so each concept ships as two to four variations that look genuinely different from each other in hook, setting, or length while carrying the same message. The count per concept is a testing-budget decision — more variations on the SKU you most want to prove, fewer on the hedge. One useful move: give each concept a *static* variation alongside the video, so the team can start testing the message on statics immediately while the video footage is still being shot, since footage can take weeks. Bigger accounts run six to eight variations per concept; a thin account runs three, sometimes fewer, to keep the test legible and the budget from spreading too thin.

**The concept map is the artifact.** The output of all this planning is a concept map — one row per concept, filled in before any brief exists — and in practice it lives as a creative tracker the whole team works from. Its planning columns are the strategist's decisions: the job number, the concept name, a short description, whether it is evergreen or sale, the visual fidelity (hi-fi, lo-fi, or mix), the content type (video, static, GIF, carousel), the persona, the doorway, the leading emotion, the asset type (net-new, iteration, variation, adaptive), the page (brand or whitelisted), and the number of variations. Its execution columns — brief link, review link, status, strategist, editor, creator handle, ad copy, copy approval, landing page, upload date, source assets — stay empty at plan time and fill in downstream as briefs get written and assets move through production. That seam, between the planning columns a strategist sets and the execution columns production fills, is exactly the seam between the sprint plan and the briefs that follow it. A worked example of such a tracker lives at `parker-system/fixtures/creative-tracker-example.csv`; treat its 23 columns as an illustration of the column semantics and the planning/execution seam, never as a schema to impose. **The tracker is different for every brand, and the brand's own is what governs.** Do not walk in assuming this shape is the way to do it — share it as one example, but every team already tracks creative somewhere in their own variation, whether an Airtable, a Google Sheet, or a Notion board. The better move is to prompt them to connect that surface as a tool the brain can see, so the concept map writes into the tracker the team already lives in and the brain can update statuses and links on the back end with full context, rather than handing back a parallel tracker they have to reconcile. The tracker also carries sibling structure the concept map references: a persona key (each persona with its doorways and emotional triggers), an ad-copy bank (the cycled copy variations the ads pull from), and the icebox, which is the idea bank itself.

**Talent is the make-or-break unlock, and it is a planning input, not an afterthought.** For most brands the single biggest lever on whether a concept works is who is on camera, and with Meta's Andromeda-era targeting rewarding creator-native content, casting the real avatar — a person who looks like the actual buyer and can carry the product credibly — decides the ad more than the script does. Aspirational-but-relatable is the bar for a lifestyle-led concept; lived credibility is the bar for a sensitive one. Plan the talent need into the concept, because a concept that depends on a creator the brand's internal team cannot supply is a different, slower production than one it can.

**Sensitive topics change the concept and the call to action.** When a concept speaks to a vulnerable experience — a chronic condition, postpartum, a body change — the script is emotionally tugging and far harder to write, and the ad has to do more than sell. Mirror the apprehension the persona actually carries and offer the brand as the answer to it, not just the product. Where the brand has a human support layer — a coach, a fit guarantee, a real person who helps — surface it in the call to action, because on a sensitive purchase the proof that the brand listens and will help is itself the conversion lever. The way to get this right is to talk to that support layer directly and carry its real language into the brief.

## Mechanics, the magic moment, and persona rounds

Reusable formats get banked the way ideas do, and paired with messaging later. A winning skit format is worth logging with no message attached yet, simply because the brand will want a skit eventually. The format is a vehicle; the message gets attached at concept time. The recurring mechanics worth reaching for — steps and stages that force a viewer to keep watching, the avatar callout that labels its audience inside the hook, old-versus-new skits, reacting to a stranger's organic complaint so the product becomes the hero of someone else's problem, the skepticism-to-surprise arc of attempting a product for the first time — are illustrations of the kind of mechanic worth logging, never a menu to reproduce.

One exercise is worth pulling out on its own. Every product has a magic moment, the visual instant that stops the scroll, and naming it is a reliable path to strong hooks, especially around a launch. Find the moment a product does something a viewer has to stop and look at, then find new ways to show it.

Two cautions govern borrowing from creators. Reacting to or stitching another creator's content is generally safe only with a small creator and only the opening seconds, because creators do not always welcome it. And ripping off a founder-led brand with a large personal following risks a public callout, so tread carefully and bring the team in before acting.

Finally, ideation is not one and done. Once the personas are locked, run another round of inspiration inside the lane of each persona, because ideating for a specific persona is a different mindset that surfaces different ideas. Log ideas for a persona the brand has not reached yet, but do not concept them until the brand arrives there.

## What this means for Parker

Parker should be the always-on logging layer that means a strategist never concepts from a blank page. The idea bank should accumulate continuously from every surface Parker touches, each entry carrying what was liked and why — and the spark where it landed — so that at concept time there is a ranked bank to pull from rather than three hours of scouring. The structured weekly hunt above is how the dedicated capture session runs; the trend lane inside it is how fresh movement in the brand's space reaches the bank each week, surfaced as should-I-log-this candidates with the sparks already attached.

What this method points Parker toward: the old-ads corpus, grown continuously, so Parker can adapt from the past instead of from the same feed everyone else mines; a per-SKU or per-lane view of the idea bank that opens to every cup idea or every disc idea in one place (the `hunt_lane` field is what makes that view possible); a planned round, where Parker sizes the sprint from the account's real spend and evergreen cadence, splits it across the SKUs and personas, sets the variation counts, and hands back a concept map before any brief is written, so the strategist walks into briefing with the round already shaped; and a path from a logged idea straight into a drafted brief, where pointing at a logged hook and asking to build it spins up the concept — a path the spark shortens, because the collision is already on the entry. The hard part is the tacit read — seeing a format and knowing it has something while the why resists explanation. That is the gap the idea-bank schema and this methodology exist to close: by forcing the why, the winning element, and the spark into every entry, the tacit read becomes a recorded one.
