# User-profile template

> Instantiate this per user at `users/[user-id]/user-profile.md`. It is one of the always-loaded core docs, so it rides in every message and Parker knows who it's talking to before it answers. Fill what's true for this person, delete this header block.
>
> **These are prompts, not a form.** This person is unlike the last one, so do not force every section or write to a shape. Capture what's real for them, skip what isn't, and add whatever these headers miss. One flexible spine, and the content flexes hard to who they are. A founder, a strategist, and a media buyer share almost none of the same answers. Write for the person in front of you and let the read breathe. No parentheticals, no example lists jammed inline, no filler to look complete.
>
> **How it grows:** this is not a one-time intake. It starts light and fills in from the real conversations the person has with Parker, and from their whole connected ecosystem — every MCP and connector they've linked (calendar, email, Slack, task manager, Notion, docs, call transcripts). Their real day lives across those tools, so the nightly dreaming surveys the whole ecosystem to know them, not just the chat. The reactive loop folds durable user-learnings in here; the nightly user-dreaming pass proposes updates the user confirms. The more of their ecosystem is connected, the truer this doc gets. Keep lines sourced and dated like the brand docs, and mark a soft read `[~]` until it's confirmed.
>
> **Keep the full moment that caused it, verbatim — this is a hard rule.** Every entry that came from something the person said or did carries the real scene next to the rule or read it produced, word for word: their exact words, the Parker output they were reacting to, and enough of the surrounding conversation to make the moment legible on its own. Not just their line in isolation — both sides. A correction only makes sense when you can see what Parker did that earned it. Never a paraphrase, never a cleaned-up summary; if more history is needed to understand why the rule exists, keep more history. The abstracted rule drifts and gets misread; the full moment does not, and it lets Parker reason from the actual case instead of a guess at what they meant. This is the user's own memory, not cross-brand product canon, so the verbatim belongs here and stays here.
>
> **What it also feeds:** a learning about the person rarely stays put. When it touches how work gets done or who does it, it also updates `sub-context-docs/operations-and-team.md`, the team profile and team notes, and the "how the team uses Parker, user by user" part of `running-notes/brand-notes-from-org.md`. The user layer rolls up into the team and org picture.
>
> **Date-stamp what evolves, and weigh recent over stale.** People change over a long relationship, so every rule and every "what they're working on" entry carries the date it was set. When two entries pull against each other, the more recent one wins, because it reflects who they are now. Don't silently delete an old rule that's been overtaken — mark it superseded with both dates so the evolution stays visible and the model can reason about the arc, not just the latest line. The dates are context to reason with, not a hard expiry.
>
> **The shape is a floor, not a ceiling — keep making it better.** These sections are a strong starting point, not the boundary of what this doc can be. As Parker gets to know the person, it should actively look for better ways to capture and organize what serves them: a new section for something this person's role makes central, a split when one section is carrying too much, a sharper cut that predicts their reactions better, a structure that just fits them more. The best version of this doc for a founder is not the best version for a media buyer — let it become what each person actually needs. Propose a structural change the same way a content update is proposed: surfaced, confirmed by the person, then adopted. This is the brand's "growing-the-brain" principle pointed at the user — the doc is meant to keep optimizing toward serving them better, never to sit frozen.

# User profile — {{USER_NAME}}

**Role:** {{their role}} · **Last updated:** {{date}}

This is who Parker is working with. It loads on every message so the read, the tone, and the work fit this person, not a generic user. Read the whole thing through their role.

## Who they are

The real person behind the title, at the level a sharp colleague would know them after a few months. Their role and what it actually means day to day, where they sit, what they own, what they're measured on. Enough that Parker reasons about their situation and not a job title.

## What they use Parker for

The real jobs they come to Parker to do, most frequent first. This is what decides what "helpful" means for this person, so get it honest.

## How they work

Their actual process — the literal steps their work moves through from start to finish. How a script, a campaign, a report, whatever they own, actually gets made in their world: where it starts, who touches it, what order things happen in, where Parker fits in that chain. The workflow itself, so Parker slots into it instead of cutting across it.

## Their craft

Their own version of the job, the thing that makes them them at it. Not a generic version of the title but the distinctive way this person practices it: the approach they trust, the instincts they've earned, the moves they're known for, the expertise that's theirs. What they're genuinely great at, how they think about the work, the angle only they bring. This is the person as a professional, and it is what lets Parker meet them at their level and build on what they already do best instead of talking past it.

## How they like Parker to work with them

The preference that shapes every reply. How blunt, how long, how much reasoning shown, whether and how they want pushback, how they like a decision put in front of them. When in doubt this section wins, because it governs the how of every response.

## Rules they've set

The running list of standing directives this person has given, honored every single time so nobody has to say it twice. The specific calls that came out of real feedback and must never get lost to one conversation: the metric they want used instead of another, the language they insist on, the thing to always lead with, the hard don'ts. This is the person's version of the brand's hard rules, and it is the memory of every "do it this way." When they correct Parker or set a rule, it lands here through the reactive loop and stays. Date-stamp each rule; when a newer rule overtakes an older one, the recent one wins and the old one is marked superseded with both dates, never silently dropped.

Every rule carries the full moment that caused it, verbatim, right beside it: the person's exact words, the Parker output they were correcting, and as much of the exchange as it takes to understand. The rule is "always use net-new CPA, not blended"; the moment is Parker showing blended CPA, them catching it, and what they said back, in their words. Keep the whole scene, because the rule alone drifts into something they never meant.

## What they're working on

Their current focus and what's coming next, looking out across the near, mid, and far horizon. So Parker tees up what they'll need instead of only answering what's in front of it. This is a live reflection of their actual task list — what they're really doing, pulled from their real work across the ecosystem (their task manager, calendar, the threads and projects in flight), and kept current as tasks start, move, and finish. The nightly dream reads their active work and updates this section to match: a finished task rolls into "what's landed," a new one shows up here, a shifting priority re-sorts the horizon. The 30/60/90 mirrors their real to-do list and roadmap, not a snapshot someone took once. Date-stamp each entry — this section moves constantly, and the dates are how Parker tells the live focus from where they were three months ago.

## What's landed

What has genuinely worked for this person, kept specific and dated, so Parker repeats what hits and doesn't relearn it every time.

## What they'd want Parker to do for them

Parker's own read, drawn from everything above, of the work this person would want taken off their plate — the tasks to just do, or offer to do, before being asked. Not their stated jobs but the inference from the whole picture: given their role, their process, what they keep coming back for, what they hand off, and what they're working on, what would genuinely help if Parker simply started doing it. This is the section that makes Parker proactive instead of only responsive, and it is what the nightly tee-up acts on. Mark it as the assumption it is and sharpen it as they react — an offer taken is a confirmed read, an offer declined teaches the boundary. Offer, don't silently act, until a pattern is confirmed.

## Open questions about them

The honest still-figuring-this-out: what Parker doesn't yet know about how they work, what they want, or what they'll value. The working list the nightly user-dreaming pass mines for what to watch and what to gently ask, so the profile keeps getting truer.
