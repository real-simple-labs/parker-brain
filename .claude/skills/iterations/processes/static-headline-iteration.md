# Process — Static Headline Iteration

Test a new headline structure on a static ad while holding layout, visual, and supporting copy mostly constant. Headlines are often the lowest-effort, highest-signal iteration on a static — the headline carries the weight of the entire argument in one line, and a structural change often unlocks meaningful performance lift without any production work.

This process applies when the static's bottleneck is the headline. If the visual is the bottleneck (impressions but no clicks), pick a visual iteration instead.

## Required inputs

- The winning static ad and its performance data — specifically CTR and CvR.
- The current headline copy verbatim.
- The current layout, visual, supporting copy, CTA, and any social proof elements.
- Brand type — problem/solution or lifestyle. The headline approach differs sharply.
- Brand ICP, voice of customer, compliance constraints.

## Determine the brand type first

Before writing or recommending any headline iteration, decide whether the static is for a problem/solution brand or a lifestyle brand.

- **Problem/solution brands.** The audience knows they have a problem and is looking for a fix. Headlines can be direct, agitate pain, name the problem, promise the solution. The job is making someone instantly recognize their problem and believe the product solves it.

- **Lifestyle brands.** The audience is not in pain — they are seeking elevation, connection, self-expression, or identity signaling. Headlines need to signal identity, create desire, and make the right person feel "this is for me."

Some products straddle. Use judgment per product, not per brand.

## Core principles for any headline iteration

- **Assume no one knows or cares about the brand.** Headlines must work for cold audiences. Cold-focused headlines work for both cold and warm; warm-focused headlines never scale.
- **Clear beats clever.** Simple language, no jargon. State the transformation, benefit, or identity signal directly.
- **Under ten words.** If it can be shorter, make it shorter.
- **Every headline has an ICP and an emotion.** Generic headlines fail. Someone should read the headline and know who it is for and what they should feel.
- **Target System 1 thinking.** Scrolling viewers are not analyzing. The headline must land in two-to-three seconds. Avoid complex layouts, abstract benefits, multiple ideas per headline.

## The headline-structure menu

The whole point of iterating a headline is testing a DIFFERENT structure from the current one. Not a slightly different version of the same structure. Pick a structure that the current ad is not running.

**Short rhythmic sentences.** Rhythm creates stickiness. Confident, clear, punchy.

**Alliteration.** Repeating consonant sounds for memorability. Use sparingly to avoid gimmick territory.

**Parallel elements.** Repetition of phrases creates rhythm. "No X. No Y. Just Z." structure.

**Questions.** Stop the scroll and force an internal answer. For problem/solution brands, the question names the problem. For lifestyle brands, the question targets identity or experience.

**Rhyme and wordplay.** Aids recall when used carefully.

**Rejection and contrast.** Especially powerful for lifestyle brands. Defining what the brand is NOT is as important as what it IS. Patterns: "Not your [mainstream]," "Forget [what everyone does]," "F*ck [the old way]," "Stop [inferior behavior]," "Replace [bad] with [our]," "Ditch [inferior]."

**Identity and belonging signals.** Especially powerful for lifestyle brands. "Built for [identity]," "Finally, [product] for [specific group]," audience callouts ("PSA: [target group]").

**Badge value and status.** Lifestyle brands sell what ownership signals. Exclusivity statements, quality-over-convenience statements, expert validation, social proof as status.

**Problem setup.** Especially powerful for problem/solution brands. Question forms naming the problem, audience labels for specific issues, promise statements ("Last longer without pills," "No more [pain]").

**Metaphors and similes.** Make abstract benefits concrete by linking to something familiar. For lifestyle, capture the experience or vibe. For problem/solution, make benefits tangible.

**Viral and social proof signals.** Only when there is genuine social proof to back it up. "Trying the viral [product]," "[Number] people can't be wrong," "TikTok made me buy it." Never claim virality that does not exist.

**Capturing the experience.** Especially powerful for lifestyle brands. Sensory language ("Comfort in a cup"), reaction and discovery moments ("I wasn't expecting this," "Wait until you [try/see/taste]").

## Emotional depth — for problem/solution brands

Three levels of emotional depth. Headlines that hit Level 2 or Level 3 outperform Level 1.

- **Level 1 — Surface.** The obvious physical problem.
- **Level 2 — Behavioral.** What the audience cannot do because of the problem.
- **Level 3 — Identity.** How the problem makes them feel about themselves.

If the current headline is stuck at Level 1, push the iteration to Level 2 or Level 3.

## Customer sophistication — for problem/solution brands

- **High sophistication** — has tried three or more solutions. Skeptical. Headlines need to acknowledge past failures, show understanding of the journey. Structures: investment framing, myth-busting, bold contrarian claims, specificity about mechanism.
- **Low sophistication** — has tried zero to two things or is just discovering the problem. Needs education and clarity. Structures: simple problem naming, questions, metaphors, clear benefit statements.

## Customer-led copywriting

Headlines must sound like someone in the target audience would actually say it.

- Source language from reviews, surveys, comments, Reddit, forums, organic social.
- Avoid dictionary words. Use casual, real language.
- Use insider language only the target audience would understand. Signals "we are one of you," qualifies the right people, filters out the rest. Hard to find but gold when authentic.
- Speak in the audience's generational register. Boomers respond to clarity, trust, nostalgia, social proof. Gen X responds to autonomy, simplicity, control. Millennials respond to emotional validation, community, humor, skepticism. Gen Z responds to identity, authenticity, humor, community-led vibes.

## Pre-check

- Is the headline the actual bottleneck? Confirm via the diagnostic (CTR is the headline-plus-visual diagnostic on a static; CvR is the offer-and-supporting-copy diagnostic).
- Is the proposed new structure genuinely different from the current one, or is it just a reworded version of the same structure?
- Does the new structure match the brand type?
- Does the new structure match the audience's sophistication level?

## Execution

1. **State the current structure and the target structure.** Be explicit — "current headline is using problem-setup as a question; new headline iterates to rejection-and-contrast structure."

2. **Write the new headline.** Under ten words. Customer language, not marketing voice. Specific ICP, specific emotion.

3. **State what stays constant.** Layout, visual, supporting copy, CTA, social proof elements.

4. **State the ICP and emotion the new headline is built around.**

5. **Run the headline through the quality checklist.**

## Headline iteration quality checklist

Every iteration must pass all of these.

- Is it immediately clear what the product is or what problem it solves? A cold viewer should get it instantly.
- Does it have a specific ICP and a specific emotion?
- Is it under ten words?
- Does it use customer language, not marketing voice? Read it out loud — would the audience actually say this to a friend?
- Does it use a clear identifiable structural pattern? If there is no identifiable structure, rewrite.
- Is the structure different from the current ad's structure? If it is the same structure with different words, it is not an iteration.
- For lifestyle brands: does it signal identity or tribe? Would someone feel "this is for me" or feel excluded?
- For problem/solution brands: does it hit Level 2 or Level 3 emotional depth?
- Does it avoid the testimonial trap? "Best ever," "so good," "worth it," "love it," "can't believe how [adjective]," "whole family loves" — these are reviews, not headlines.
- Would a fifth grader understand it?

## Output content for this iteration

- A name that signals the structural move. Example pattern: "Headline Iteration — Rejection Structure on Existing Layout."
- The current headline verbatim and the structural diagnosis.
- The new headline verbatim and the structural identification.
- The ICP and emotion the new headline is built around.
- What stays constant — layout, visual, supporting copy, CTA, social proof.
- The proven-pattern note.

## What never to do

- Never propose a headline iteration that uses the same structure as the current ad. The whole point is testing a new approach.
- Never use testimonial-style language as a headline. Reviews are not headlines.
- Never propose a headline written in marketing voice instead of customer language.
- Never propose more than one structural change per iteration. If you also want a layout change or a visual change, those are separate iterations.
- Never invent stats or claims in the headline. Compliance and data integrity rules apply.
- Never include the proven-example headlines from `iterations.md` in user-facing output. Examples are for pattern recognition only.
