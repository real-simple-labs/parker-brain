# Process — Hook Iteration

Modify only the first three seconds of the ad while preserving the converting body. The hook is arguably the most important part of a direct-response ad — if the hook fails to earn relevant attention, the rest of the ad does not matter. The bet here is to spend disproportionate creative energy on the opener while leaving the proven body intact.

Hook iteration is the right call when hook rate or CTR is the bottleneck and the body is converting — high CvR or a hold rate that holds past the opener — or when the ad is fatiguing but the core message still works. Read the numbers through `ad-account-analysis.md`: a hook rate under 30% is the floor that flags a stop-scroll problem worth a hook iteration, and a strong hold rate (12% and above is the working range) with a weak hook tells you the body earns the watch once people stay, so the opener is the constraint.

## The source of truth for hooks

The hook craft — the opener taxonomy, the psychology behind each format, the brand-type and awareness-stage routing, the four-element hook anatomy, and the must-haves every hook has to pass — lives in `parker-system/creative-strategy-context/hooks.md`. That doc is canonical. Load it and execute it; do not work from a remembered version of it. This process file does not restate the taxonomy — it adds the layer that is specific to iterating a hook onto a *proven body*: which of the four hook elements to change, how to splice the new opener onto the existing body, and the tactical moves for refreshing an opener without touching what converts.

A hook iteration that never speaks the named concepts in `hooks.md` — the specific format it pulls from, the awareness stage it serves, the emotional driver it activates, the four hook elements — proves the doc was not opened. Use its vocabulary.

## Required inputs

- The winning ad and its performance data — specifically CTR, hook rate, hold rate, CvR.
- The current hook itself — timestamps, the four hook elements from `hooks.md` (text overlay, sound, visual, vibe), what is said, shown, and overlaid.
- The brand's ICP, personas, customer language, and stage of awareness.
- Brand type — problem/solution or lifestyle.
- Iteration history if available. If the ad has already been through five hook swaps, the sixth is unlikely to be the right move — confirm with the user before proceeding.

## Pre-check: identify ICP and emotion before writing any hook iteration

`hooks.md` requires this before any opener is written, and it applies here too. Before recommending or writing any hook iteration, identify:

- **ICP / Audience.** The specific person — their situation, what they have tried, what they believe. Not demographics.
- **Angle / Emotion.** What the viewer should feel. What pain point. What future state. Why THIS person stops for THIS ad.

Every hook iteration must be built around the specific ICP plus the specific angle. If a hook iteration could apply to any brand in any category, it is too generic. Someone reading the new hook should be able to say "this is clearly for this specific person feeling this specific emotion."

## Choosing the format and the element to change — run it through hooks.md

The opener taxonomy and the routing that picks a format are canonical in `hooks.md`. Do the work there, in its vocabulary, before drafting:

- **Pick which of the four hook elements is changing.** `hooks.md` names them — the text-overlay hook, the sound hook, the visual hook, and the vibe hook. A hook iteration is sharper when it is explicit about which one moves: "swap the hook visual, keep the voiceover" is a different test from "swap the text overlay, keep the visual." The doc's own insight that the best hooks SHOW rather than tell means the visual hook is often the highest-leverage element to change, even on an ad whose copy is fine.
- **Route to a format through the brand read.** `hooks.md` selects an opener by brand type (problem-solution vs lifestyle), awareness stage (unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, most-aware), and emotional driver (fear, aspiration, exhaustion, tribal identity). Run the proven ad through that routing and name the specific format from the doc's twenty — comment response, investment, scam, demographic callout, myth-busting, and so on — that the diagnosis points to and the brand has not yet exhausted. Look at the existing opener first: which format it already runs, why it works, and which formats are untested for this brand. The untested format that matches the audience psychology is the opportunity.
- **Build the new opener from the principle, not the example.** The examples in `hooks.md` teach the psychological mechanism; reproducing them with product names swapped in produces the generic "$80 for jeans is crazy" failure the doc warns against. Write the new opener in the customer's actual language, sourced from reviews, comments, and surveys.

## Tactical hook playbook

The formats in `hooks.md` are the psychological frameworks. The playbook below is the iteration-specific layer — the tactical menu of how to apply a format to an *already-proven body* without disturbing what converts. When recommending a hook iteration, name the tactic and the `hooks.md` format together: "open-loop format, delivered as a text-overlay change" is a recommendation; "new hook" is not.

- **Hook headlines** — add an attention-grabbing text overlay to the first three seconds. Best when CTR is low but CvR is strong. Very low effort, medium-to-high reward.
- **Hook visual swap** — keep the voiceover or script identical, test new opening visuals. Best when the first three seconds are weak but the voiceover is strong. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Comment bubble hook** — add an organic-feeling comment overlay to open. Best when engagement is low and FAQs or objections need addressing upfront. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Hook stacking** — stack two or three hooks back-to-back before the existing body. Best for VSL-style ads where the winning hook is uncertain. Low effort, high reward.
- **Hook format swap** — keep the hook message identical but change the delivery mechanism. Same message delivered as talking head, text-on-screen, product shot, UGC reaction. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Pattern interrupt hook** — open with something visually or conceptually unexpected. Best when the ad blends into the feed. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Question hook** — pose a direct question to create instant engagement. Best when audience is problem-aware. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Native hook** — mimic organic platform content in the first frame. Best when the ad reads as too polished. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Before/after hook** — lead with the transformation or end result immediately. Best when the product has a strong visual transformation. Low effort, high reward.
- **Social proof hook** — lead with a testimonial, review screenshot, or customer count. Best for cold audiences that need upfront credibility. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Fear/urgency hook** — open with a consequence, risk, or time constraint. Best when the audience is high-awareness but low-action. Low effort, medium reward.
- **"Wait for it" hook** — tease a payoff to create curiosity. Best when hold rate needs lifting and the content has a strong reveal. Low effort, medium reward.
- **POV hook** — first-person framing to put the viewer in the scenario. Best for relatable immersive content. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Reaction hook** — open with genuine or staged reaction to create curiosity. Best when the product has a surprise/delight moment. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Fourth wall hook** — creator acknowledges it is an ad, then reframes with genuine endorsement. Best when ad fatigue is high and the audience is ad-savvy. Low effort, high reward.
- **Stitch hook** — new creator stitches over the opening, adding reaction or commentary before the original body. Best when the ad has been heavily utilized. Medium effort, medium reward.
- **Text hook overlay** — add or change bold text overlay in the first frame. Best when the video hook is decent but CTR still lags. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Open loop hook** — open with an unresolved statement or tease that creates a curiosity gap. Best when hold rate needs lifting. Low effort, high reward.
- **Add offer** — add a headline banner with a promotional offer on evergreen winners during seasonal moments. Best for proven winners during promotional periods. Low effort, high reward.

## Pacing the opener

Hook delivery speed matches the target age range — the age-based pacing and length guidance is in `hooks.md` (the younger the audience, the faster the cuts and the shorter the ad). Hold the new opener to that pacing so it matches the body it splices into.

## Execution

1. **State which hook element is changing.** Text overlay, sound, visual, vibe, or some combination. Be explicit.

2. **State which hook format and which tactical move.** "Open loop hook delivered as a text overlay change" is a specific recommendation. "New hook" is not.

3. **Write the new hook copy, exactly.** Every word. If the hook is visual-only, describe the visual frame-by-frame for the duration of the hook.

4. **Specify the cut point.** The new hook ends at exactly this timestamp; the original body resumes at exactly this timestamp. The transition between them must read as natural.

5. **Cite the ICP and emotion the hook is built around.** A specific person feeling a specific emotion. If you cannot name them, the hook is not yet specific enough.

6. **Run it through the hooks.md must-haves.** Every opener has to pass the must-haves in `hooks.md` — relevance before raw attention, a clear promise inside three seconds, zero confusion, a named emotion, and a native-to-the-platform feel. If the proposed opener fails any of them, refine it or pick a different format.

## Output content for this iteration

- A name that signals the move. Example pattern: "Hook Swap — Open Loop Text Overlay on Existing Body."
- Which of the four hook elements from `hooks.md` is changing — text overlay, sound, visual, or vibe.
- Which `hooks.md` format and which tactical move from the playbook above.
- The exact new hook content — verbatim copy, frame-by-frame visual if applicable, on-screen text, audio cue.
- The cut point to the existing body, by timestamp.
- The ICP and emotion the new hook is built around.
- The proven-pattern note tied to the chosen format and the brand's audience psychology.

## What never to do

- Never recommend a hook iteration that does not pass the `hooks.md` must-haves.
- Never copy-paste a hook example with product names swapped. Generic hooks fail.
- Never propose a hook iteration without naming the ICP and the emotion.
- Never combine a hook change with a body change in the same iteration. If both need work, those are two iterations.
- Never propose a hook format the brand has already tested into exhaustion. Check the history first.
- Never include the source examples from this document or `iterations.md` in user-facing output. Examples are for internal pattern recognition only.
