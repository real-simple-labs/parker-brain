# Process — Script From Comment Thread

A comment-response script. A real comment — the dupe warning, the price objection, the skeptic's jab — is read on camera and answered. The comment IS the hook.

The objection is already public and already shared by the audience. Answering it on camera turns a doubt into the ad's opening.

## Required inputs

- A real comment from the brand's ad or organic surfaces. Source: the ad-comments audit — `parker-system/prompts/personas/ad-comments.md`, the `search_facebook_ad_comments_semantic` and `search_facebook_ad_comments_sql` MCP surfaces. The 32-Degrees dupe comment is a real documented example of this surface.
- Brand baseline — the brand's existing scripts.
- Brand profile, ICP, voice of customer, compliance constraints.
- The brand's defensible answer to the objection.

## Philosophy

A comment is a real objection the audience already holds, stated in their own words. Reading it on camera does two jobs at once: it is a pattern-interrupt hook because it looks like a real reply, and it pre-empts the exact doubt the viewer is carrying. The rebuttal that follows is not defensive — it is the most credible proof beat the brand has, because it answers a question someone actually asked.

Three common comment types. The dupe warning ("this is just a cheaper version of X"). The price objection ("no way it's worth that"). The skeptic ("there's no way this works"). Each becomes a hook the body then resolves.

## When to pick

- A real comment exists that names an objection the audience shares.
- The brand has a clean, defensible answer.
- Product-aware or solution-aware audiences sitting on a specific doubt.

## Execution

### Step 1 — Pull the real comment
Find the comment that names a widely-shared objection. Pull it verbatim. It must be real — never invent a comment.

### Step 2 — Stage the comment as the hook
Show it on screen or read it aloud as the opener. The framing is a genuine reply: the creator saw this comment and is answering it. That authenticity is the hook's power.

### Step 3 — Build the rebuttal as the body
Answer directly and honestly. The dupe warning is met with the real difference. The price objection is met with the value the customer gets. The skeptic is met with the demonstration or proof. No corporate defensiveness.

### Step 4 — Source the answer language from VoC
Pull the rebuttal's phrasing from how real customers describe the difference, the value, the result. The answer sounds like a person, not a brand.

### Step 5 — Brand baseline and compliance pass
Match the brand's pacing and CTA. Confirm the rebuttal makes only claims the brand can defend.

## What good looks like

- A real comment, verbatim, as the hook.
- The rebuttal answers the actual objection honestly and lands as proof.
- The whole script reads as a genuine reply, not a staged pitch.
- Customer language throughout.

## Common failures

- Inventing a comment. The comment must be real and traceable to the vault.
- A defensive, corporate rebuttal that confirms the skeptic instead of disarming them.
- Answering an objection the audience does not actually hold.
- A claim in the rebuttal the brand cannot defend.
