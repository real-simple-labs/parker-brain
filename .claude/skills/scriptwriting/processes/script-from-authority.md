# Process — Script From Authority

A credentialed expert carries the script — within the claims envelope. The expert's credential transfers trust to the product, but only for what the expert can actually say. The lived moment beats the lab coat.

Authority converts when it is real and bounded. The discipline is staying inside what the expert can credibly and compliantly claim.

## Required inputs

- A documented credentialed expert tied to the brand. Source: the expert-insights library at `global/knowledge/creative-strategy/expert-insights/INDEX.md` and the expert-signal DB at `prompts/global-databases/expert-signal-db.md`.
- The claims envelope — what the expert is permitted to say.
- Brand baseline — the brand's existing scripts.
- Brand profile, ICP, voice of customer, compliance constraints.

## Philosophy

A credentialed expert transfers trust. When a real dermatologist, doctor, or specialist stands behind the product, their credential vouches for it in a way a brand cannot vouch for itself. That trust-transfer is the engine of the script.

Two disciplines bound it. The claims envelope: an expert can say some things and not others, and the script stays inside what the credential and compliance permit — an expert recommending is different from an expert making a medical claim. And the lived moment beats the lab coat: the expert is most persuasive in a real, human moment — explaining the thing the way they would to a patient or a friend — not posed in a white coat reciting credentials. Casting for the lived moment outperforms casting for the costume.

## When to pick

- A real credentialed expert is documented for the brand.
- The category rewards authority — health, skincare, supplements, anything where credentials matter.
- Solution-aware or product-aware audiences who weigh expert endorsement.

## Execution

### Step 1 — Confirm the expert and the envelope
Pull the documented expert and what they are permitted to say. Confirm the credential is real and the claims envelope is defined. Never invent a credential.

### Step 2 — Anchor the trust-transfer
The hook establishes the credential naturally — who the expert is and why their word carries weight — without reciting a resume.

### Step 3 — Write the lived moment, not the lab coat
The body is the expert in a real human moment, explaining the product the way they would to a patient or a friend. Plain language, genuine register. The credential is established once; it does not have to be performed throughout.

### Step 4 — Stay inside the claims envelope
Every line the expert says sits inside what the credential and compliance permit. An expert recommendation is not a medical claim. Swap anything outside the envelope for the strongest compliant equivalent.

### Step 5 — Brand baseline and VoC pass
Match pacing and CTA. Even the expert's explanation borrows the audience's plain language where it helps. Confirm every claim is defensible.

## What good looks like

- The credential is real, documented, and established naturally.
- The expert is shown in a lived moment, not posed in a costume.
- Every claim sits inside the claims envelope.
- Pacing and CTA match the brand baseline.

## Common failures

- Inventing or inflating a credential. The expert and envelope must be documented.
- Casting the lab coat instead of the lived moment.
- An expert line that steps outside the claims envelope into an unpermitted claim.
- Reciting credentials throughout instead of establishing them once and getting human.
