# Process — Script From Competitor Ad

Adapt a captured rival script through target inversion. Keep the skeleton. Flip the buyer: the customer the rival shames becomes the customer we absolve, the claim they cannot make becomes the one we can.

The competitor already proved the structure with spend. The job is to run the same shape pointed at our buyer, not theirs.

## Required inputs

- A captured competitor script with full transcript and timing. Source: the rival's profile under `competitors/[rival]/` (its ad-account evaluation) and the single-ad teardowns under `audits/[YYYY-Q]/external/`, where rival scripts are already captured and tagged.
- Brand baseline — the brand's existing scripts.
- Brand profile, ICP, voice of customer, compliance constraints.
- The competitor's claim envelope versus ours — what they can say that we cannot, and what we can say that they cannot.

## Philosophy

A competitor's running ad is a validated skeleton paid for by someone else. Adapting it is the standard reference move from `parker-system/creative-strategy-context/adapting-scripts.md`, with one added discipline: inversion. A rival ad is built to position the rival. Lifting it 1:1 imports their angle. The stronger move is to keep the structure and invert the target — same beats, opposite buyer relationship. Where the rival shames the buyer into action, we absolve. Where the rival makes a claim they are cornered into defending, we make the claim that is true for us and not for them.

## When to pick

- A competitor ad has clear performance signal and a skeleton the brand's solution slots into.
- The brand can hold a defensible position the rival cannot.
- The user provides or points at a captured competitor script.

## Execution

### Step 1 — Deconstruct the rival per adapting-scripts.md
Run the full segment deconstruction. Segment count, hook type, proof pattern, emotional arc, CTA shape. The segment count must match the rival exactly.

### Step 2 — Find the inversion point
Read what the rival does to its buyer. If it shames ("you are still doing X wrong"), our version absolves ("X was never your fault"). If it leans on a claim that is shaky for them, we plant the claim that is solid for us in the same beat.

### Step 3 — Rebuild the skeleton with the inverted target
Same beats, same pacing, same proof position. The buyer relationship flips. The reviewer the rival blames becomes the reviewer we reassure. Every word rewritten in our brand voice from the brand baseline.

### Step 4 — Source language from VoC, not the rival
Pull our buyer's actual words from reviews and comments. The rival's phrasing stays out — only the structure carries.

### Step 5 — Drift and compliance audit
Confirm segment count, hook type, proof position match the rival. Confirm every claim is one the brand can defend. Swap any rival claim the brand cannot make for the nearest compliant equivalent in the same beat role.

## What good looks like

- Segment-for-segment structural match to the rival.
- A clean target inversion — the buyer relationship is genuinely flipped, not just reworded.
- Our defensible claim sits where the rival's weak claim sat.
- Every line is our brand voice and our customer's language.

## Common failures

- Lifting the rival 1:1 and importing their angle. Without inversion this just runs the category's existing ad.
- Carrying a rival claim the brand cannot defend. Compliance is a wall.
- Drifting off the segment count. Structural sameness is the priority.
- Letting the rival's marketing voice leak into the script instead of our customer's voice.
