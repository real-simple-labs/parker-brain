# How real ads talk (and cut) — the studied-corpus doctrine

Built from a dissection of winning ads pulled live from the ad library: top-ranked UGC (greens-gummy unboxing winners at library rank #5/#11, a 778-day #1 apparel ad), a $216k-lifetime-spend account UGC winner, a 2.7M-view TikTok Spark ad, a 150-day authority ad, a 2.5M-view green-screen expert review, celebrity UGC at rank #3, and errand-vlog account winners — every quote below is a verbatim line a winning ad actually shipped. The core finding: **a script can hit every structural metric (density, breath length, pace) and still read AI, because the tells live in PERSON, not rhythm.** Real creator speech narrates *a person encountering a product*; written copy describes *a product* — and viewers can tell in one line.

This doc governs every spoken-word format. Draft dialogue FROM these laws, then gate against the checklist at the bottom. The registers differ per format — using the right law in the wrong register is itself a tell.

## The seven speech laws (creator registers)

### 1. First-person density — the #1 discriminator
Winning UGC runs roughly **one I/me/my per 6–10 words**. The creator is the subject of almost every sentence; the product is the object.
- "**I'm** actually in shock. **I** got the sugar-free ones." · "**I'm** really curious how you make greens taste good in a gummy form." · "**I** actually love these. This could satisfy **my** gummy craving."
- "**I'm** just gonna be honest, **I've** tried to incorporate [product] into my diet for the longest time ever, but **I** couldn't find a way to make it taste nice."
- "**I'm** heading to Target but this time it's for one specific thing." · "**I** love that they have a few options too."

**The narrator test:** if a line could be spoken by someone who isn't in the room holding the thing, it is not UGC speech. ("You're not stacking up eight tubs on your counter" fails; "I'm not stacking up eight tubs" still fails weaker; "okay so this one bag just refills the jar? that's it?" passes — she's discovering it.)
**Synthetic-presenter zone fit:** first-contact formats keep this legal — reactions ("I'm actually in shock"), choices ("I got the…"), curiosity ("I'm really curious…"), present feelings ("I feel so good about this"), live discoveries. Usage-history first person ("I take this every day") stays banned for synthetic presenters; it's the one first-person class the zone forbids.

### 2. The authenticity lexicon — intensifiers, not filler sounds
The emphasis carriers of real speech: **actually, literally, honestly, just, really, so, "I promise", "to be honest," "lowkey."** One winning 59s ad uses "actually" three times. Meanwhile the same ad has **zero scripted ums** — casual ≠ stammering (a real celebrity's single "uh" ships because it's hers; writing one in is performing). Write the intensifiers where a person would spontaneously emphasize; never script um/uh/like-stammer.

### 3. Expectation arcs — every reaction argues with a stated prior
The spine of every winning demo/taste/try beat: **voice the expectation → test → react against it.**
- "I'm really curious how you make greens taste good in a gummy form." → "**No,** this is actually pretty good."  ("No," at the start = answering her own doubt. That's the realism engine.)
- "…I couldn't find a way to make it taste nice." → "…and they **actually** taste amazing."
A reaction with no prior expectation on record is an assertion, not a reaction. Reaction openers observed: "No," · "OK wait" · "Oh, this is really nice" · "…it's actually".

### 4. Facts arrive through objects (the pack is the authority)
Real creators don't recite specs — they **read them off something on camera**: "OK, so these support gut health, energy, immunity, recovery…" (reading the packet back), reviews read aloud off a phone ("4.86 out of five stars — I'll take that"), a label shown while naming counts. Numbers stay exact and spoken ("60 ingredients", "6 grams of prebiotic fiber", "the fourteen count, seven count"). The move: point the camera or the eyes AT the source, then say the fact — hesitation or a small self-correction while reading is native.

### 5. Lived-life anchors
1–2 per 30s: "**my gummy craving**" · "your **normal Target run**" · "it comes with me on **my High and Mighty tour**" · "my **better half** looks at my butt more often now" · "if you're **just getting started**." A concrete piece of the speaker's world does more realism work than any disfluency.

### 6. Vague-topic gestures are native
"Nobody really wants to talk about **this kind of stuff**, but I promise a lot of people are **dealing with this**." Real speech under-specifies where copy over-specifies. Gesturing at the problem ("this stuff", "all of it", "the whole thing") reads human; precisely naming it every time reads brief-driven.

### 7. Benign sloppiness survives; crafted lines don't
Shipped lines from winners: "Really easy **cause that was delicious**" (broken causality) · "This is by far been my most requested video" (broken grammar) · a #1 ad that numbers its selling points **1, 2, 4, then 3** — on purpose or not, it ships and wins. Don't repair the logic of a natural line. The inverse stays law: a phrase that would look good ON a static (a crafted image, a setup-payoff joke shape, a tricolon) does not belong in a mouth.

## Register map — what changes per format

| Format | Speech register | On-screen text |
|---|---|---|
| **Creator selfie-talk / unboxing** | All seven laws, full strength. No connector scaffolding ("So that's why…" is a tell) — speech jumps between observations. | Caption pills swap with speech; checkmark stacks for read-off lists |
| **Errand vlog ("come with me")** | One continuous run-on VO; second-person invitations ("If you've been curious about…"); location narration; run-ons are native ("…but this time it's for one specific thing if you've been curious…") | Captions persist ACROSS cuts (lower-third), plus a persistent premise header |
| **Expert / authority** | Scaffolding IS native here: "Well first, understand this…" · "Now what does that really mean? Well, number one…" · "So what's the takeaway?" Self-Q&A, hedged verdicts ("I do like… I wouldn't… Is it astronomical? No, but…"), credential ritual ("If you're new here, my name is…"), comment-reply frame. | Pro/Con signposts; comment sticker as premise |
| **Celebrity / founder** | Personality first: taboo-frankness, hyperbole ("They're saving my life"), body-as-agent jokes ("my gut will not let me skip a day"). Keep their real disfluency if the voice has one; never add one. | Minimal; name-card |
| **Skit / comedy review** | Rhetorical-question opener, numbered points (order optional!), self-deprecating + spouse jokes, reviews read aloud, soft anti-CTA ("Looking costs nothing.") | Point numbers, review screenshots |
| **VO + b-roll demo** | Produced register — polish allowed. "You"-narration of a day; contrast frames ("Everyone thinks you're wearing normal pants — but everyone else is sweating, not you"); scenario lists ("On the train, in the car, on a plane"). | Feature checkmarks, split-screen labels |
| **Statics** | (Copy, not speech.) Winning frames observed: social-proof switch headline ("Why over 1 million people switched 👇"), checkmark benefit stacks, question hook + emoji. Copy laws live in the statics track; don't import speech laws into headlines or vice versa. | — |

## Edit grammar — the motivated-cut law (the glitch killer)

Frame-level dissection of winners settled what separates a native cut from a glitch:

1. **Every cut carries a visible delta** — prop state changed (bag rotated, packet now open, bite taken), framing widened/punched, angle shifted, location moved, or the on-screen text changed WITH it. The delta is what tells the viewer "time passed on purpose." **A cut with no visible delta reads as a stutter/glitch — this is exactly how an "invisible" mid-shot dedup excision fails.** Any forced excision gets dressed: 3–6% punch + caption swap at the cut, or relocated to a natural state change.
2. **Mid-phrase cuts are native when motivated.** The winning unboxing cuts INSIDE "I'm actually in shock" — with the bag rotating across the cut. Composability with the no-dead-air law: trim gaps by cutting mid-flow freely, as long as each cut carries its delta.
3. **Cut cadence is register-specific — inherit the reference's, not a universal band:** selfie-talk/unboxing ≈ 1.5–2.5 jump cuts/10s with 8–12s uncut stretches tolerated mid-list; errand-vlog ≈ 5–8 b-roll cuts/10s (0.7–2s shots) under continuous VO; expert/green-screen ≈ zero body cuts (the background swaps instead). The parity spec measures the chosen reference; these are the sanity ranges per register.
4. **Caption behavior follows register:** selfie register swaps pills with speech; vlog register holds lower-thirds across cuts. Never let a caption change fire without either a speech phrase or a cut to motivate it.
5. **Ramps vs cuts:** speed ramps don't appear in the studied unboxing register at all. Prefer excising silence at a motivated cut over rubber-banding time inside a shot; keep ramps only for a referenced action that must survive in less time, under ~2x, and never across a rate boundary a viewer can hear.
6. **Take-level glitch scan:** before a take is locked, frame-step its full length for baked-in jumps (adjacent-frame delta spikes away from planned cuts) — generative sources can ship a glitch inside a "single take," and it reads as an edit error to the owner.

## The pre-flight nativeness checklist (gate every draft against this)

1. First-person count ≥ 1 per 10 words (zone-legal classes only for synthetic presenters).
2. Every reaction has its expectation on record earlier in the script.
3. Every fact/number enters through a visible source object.
4. ≥1 lived-life anchor per 30s.
5. Intensifiers present; zero scripted filler sounds.
6. No line survives the narrator test; no crafted images, tricolons, or setup-payoff joke shapes in mouths.
7. Register matches the reference format (scaffolding only where the register allows it).
8. Repeated-opener scan: the same first word doesn't open more than one segment ("Okay" ×3 is a pattern a human never produces by accident).
9. Edit plan: every planned cut lists its delta; no unmotivated excisions; cadence in the reference register's range.
