# Process — Investment / Journey Hooks

Open by explaining the investment — time, money, or both — that went into finding the solution. Highlights failed attempts, money wasted, time spent searching. The investment must be specific and reasonable for the category.

## Psychological framework

- **Shared journey** — creates rapport with people who have had similar struggles.
- **Trust building** — knowing someone's investment makes the solution feel more credible.
- **Qualification** — targets users who have also had failed attempts, filters tire-kickers.
- **Sunk cost validation** — makes viewers feel their own past spending was not stupid.
- **Authority through struggle** — suffering equals credibility in solution-aware categories.

## Required inputs

- Real journey data from customer reviews, founder story, or persona work. Common categories of failed attempts the audience has actually tried.
- A specific investment number that is believable for the category. "$2,000 on skincare" is reasonable; "$50,000 on skincare" is not.
- Brand voice and ICP.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** high-consideration purchases. Categories with many competing solutions. Solution-aware and problem-aware audiences. New client onboarding (consistently strong hit rate). Skews especially well for 40+ and women-heavy demographics.
- **Worst for:** unaware audiences who do not yet know they have a problem. Low-consideration impulse categories. Brands where the audience has not tried other things first.

## Execution

1. **Source the journey from real data.** What have these customers actually tried? Pull from reviews, comments, surveys. Common: "tried X for Y years," "spent $Z on competitor products," "saw N doctors."
2. **Pick a specific, believable number.** Specific dollar amounts and timeframes outperform vague descriptions. "$X on solutions that didn't work" works; "a lot of money" does not.
3. **Include emotional cost, not just financial.** "I was so frustrated I almost gave up" pairs with the dollar amount.
4. **Write the line.** "I spent $X on Y before I found this" is the structural shape. The investment must be relevant to the brand's specific category and audience.
5. **Lock the visual.** Creator walking, casual setting, talking-head delivery. Quick flashes of failed products or attempts. Reveal the hero product after the journey is established.

## Output content

- The exact spoken hook line.
- The investment number with rationale for why this number is believable for the audience.
- The visual sequence — talking head, failed-attempt cutaways, hero product reveal.
- The ICP and the journey the hook is tapping into.
- The proven-pattern note.

## What never to do

- Use a number that strains credibility for the category.
- Inflate the investment to dramatize.
- Pull a journey from a different category and swap it in. The journey has to be the brand's actual audience's journey.
- Use marketing voice. The journey must read like the customer telling it.
