# Process — Comparison Hooks

Set up a direct comparison between two options, methods, eras, or products in the opener so the viewer leans in to see which one wins.

The format and its psychology live in `parker-system/creative-strategy-context/hooks.md`; that doc is the source of truth if the two ever drift. This is the execution playbook — how to write it well, not what it is.

## Psychological framework

- **Decision simplification** — a binary choice is easier to process than many options.
- **Contrast effect** — things are understood better against each other than alone.
- **Value demonstration** — superiority shown through contrast, without an explicit claim.
- **Relatability** — everyone compares options before buying.
- **Curiosity** — which one wins?

## Required inputs

- Two genuinely comparable options the audience is actually weighing — cheap vs expensive, this method vs that method, then vs now, you vs your past self. The contrast has to be one the audience cares about.
- A real, defensible reason the recommended side wins. If the product is better, there must be backing — a demonstration, a result, a mechanism — for the claim.
- Brand voice — including how directly this brand is willing to name or knock a competing option.
- ICP and the decision they are stuck on.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** solution-aware audiences weighing alternatives. Categories with clear, named alternatives. Decision-stage content. Differentiation where the product has a real edge. Sophisticated markets where the audience already knows the options. Pairs naturally with demonstration or before-and-after content.
- **Worst for:** unaware audiences who don't yet know the options exist. Products without a defensible edge to reveal. Brands whose voice can't carry a competitive framing.

## Execution

1. **Lock the two sides.** Pick the comparison the audience is genuinely running — "cheap mascara vs expensive mascara," "lifting vs cardio for abs," "what I did at 25 vs what I'd do now." The two sides have to be a real fork the buyer faces.
2. **Show both sides fairly first.** Stack the comparison honestly before tipping it. A comparison that is obviously rigged from frame one loses the contrast effect that makes the format work.
3. **Reveal the winner with the reason.** Tip toward the recommended side and back it — the demonstration, the result, the mechanism. The contrast carries the claim; the reason makes it stick. Don't assert superiority without backing it.
4. **Calibrate how hard to knock the other side.** Match the brand voice. Some brands disparage the alternative directly; most are better served letting the contrast do the work rather than trashing a competitor.
5. **Lock the visual.** Side-by-side or split-screen is the native form — the two options literally next to each other. A you-vs-past-self or you-vs-friend staging works when there's no second product to show.

## Output content

- The two options being compared and why the audience is weighing them.
- The fair setup of both sides.
- The reveal of the winner and the real reason it wins.
- The split-screen or side-by-side visual approach.
- The ICP and the decision being simplified.
- The proven-pattern note tied to the comparison framework.

## What never to do

- Compare two options the audience doesn't actually weigh.
- Rig the setup so obviously that the contrast loses its force.
- Claim one side wins without backing the claim with a demonstration, result, or mechanism.
- Disparage a competitor harder than the brand voice supports.
- Pull from the format's source examples in the library — those are for pattern recognition, not for output.
