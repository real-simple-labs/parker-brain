# Process — Educational / Teach-in-Timeframe Hooks

Promise to teach, explain, or reveal valuable information in a specific timeframe. Positions the ad as educational content rather than a sales pitch while building authority.

## Psychological framework

- **Value exchange** — teaching equals giving before asking.
- **Authority building** — teachers are trusted.
- **Curiosity satisfaction** — we want to learn.
- **Commitment escalation** — once learning, more likely to act.
- **Pattern familiarity** — educational content feels safe, not sales-y.

## Required inputs

- A real teachable insight tied to the product or category. The ad must actually deliver value, not bait-and-switch.
- The timeframe promise — "in 60 seconds," "in this video," "in 47 seconds."
- The target audience — works best for unaware and problem-aware stages.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** low-sophistication markets where the audience does not know solutions exist. Complex products requiring explanation. Building brand authority. Top-of-funnel awareness. Service-based businesses. B2B offerings.
- **Worst for:** high-sophistication markets where the audience already knows the basics. Impulse categories where the audience does not want a lesson.

## Execution

1. **Lock the time commitment.** "Give me 47 seconds." "The next minute will change..." Specific numbers outperform vague language.
2. **State the value clearly.** What will the viewer learn? "I'll teach you the three reasons most diets fail" not "let me explain dieting."
3. **Deliver on the promise.** Educational hooks that bait-and-switch into pure pitch break trust. The teaching has to actually happen.
4. **Use lists or numbered structure if applicable.** "Three things I wish I knew at 25" gives the educational content a shape.
5. **Lock the visual.** Creator speaking directly to camera in casual setting. Counter or timer if the timeframe is visible. Teaching graphics if applicable.

## Output content

- The exact opening line with time commitment and value promise.
- The teaching that follows — what insight gets delivered.
- The transition from teaching into the product as the natural extension of the lesson.
- The ICP and the curiosity being filled.
- The proven-pattern note.

## What never to do

- Promise teaching and then deliver pure pitch.
- Vague time commitments. Specific seconds matter.
- Use this hook on high-sophistication audiences who already know the basics.
- Force the product into the lesson where it does not naturally extend from the teaching.
