# Process — How Do I Know If Hooks

Open by saying out loud the exact qualifying question the viewer is already asking themselves — "How do I know if this is right for me?" — then frame the rest of the ad as the honest answer from their side of the table.

The format and its psychology live in `parker-system/creative-strategy-context/hooks.md`; that doc is the source of truth if the two ever drift. This is the execution playbook — how to write it well, not what it is.

## Psychological framework

- **Mind reading** — mirrors the exact thought the viewer is having right now.
- **Empathy signal** — shows you understand their uncertainty instead of steamrolling it.
- **Genuine conversation** — reads as help, not a pitch.
- **Question-based hook** — the brain wants the question answered, so it stays.
- **Trust building** — admitting the purchase isn't for everyone is what makes the "yes" credible.

## Required inputs

- The real qualifying question this audience is stuck on — the actual "will this work for me" they ask before buying. Pulled from reviews, post-purchase surveys, ad comments, or the persona's stated hesitation, not invented.
- The honest decision criteria — the specific conditions under which the product is and is not a fit. The format breaks if every viewer "qualifies."
- Brand voice and ICP.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** consideration-stage content. Products with multiple use cases where fit is genuinely uncertain. Preempting a known objection. Retargeting people who have seen the product but not bought. Complex or higher-consideration purchases. Products that read "too good to be true" and need an honest framing to be believed.
- **Worst for:** impulse categories where the audience does not stop to ask whether it fits. Unaware audiences who do not yet have the question. Brands that cannot honestly name anyone the product is not for.

## Execution

1. **Lock the exact question.** Open literally with it — "How do I know if [product] is right for me?" The question has to be the one the audience is actually asking, in their words. A question they aren't asking lands as a non-sequitur.
2. **Answer from their side, not the brand's.** The voice is a knowledgeable friend talking the viewer through the decision, not a brand defending itself. Helpful and conversational.
3. **Give a real decision framework.** Name the specific conditions of fit — "if you [situation], [situation], and [situation], then yes." The honesty is the whole mechanism; if it reads like everyone qualifies, the trust evaporates.
4. **Be willing to disqualify.** Naming who this isn't for is what makes the "this is for you" believable. Use the real boundary, don't soften it into nothing.
5. **Lock the visual.** Creator in a thoughtful, talking-to-you register. Criteria can surface as on-screen points; a side-by-side of the questions and the answers works when there are several.

## Output content

- The exact opening question, in the audience's words.
- The decision criteria — the real conditions of fit, and who it isn't for.
- The transition from the framework into the product as the answer for the people it does fit.
- The ICP and the uncertainty being resolved.
- The proven-pattern note tied to the how-do-i-know-if framework.

## What never to do

- Pose a question the audience isn't actually asking.
- Frame the question from the brand's perspective instead of the viewer's.
- Build a framework where everyone qualifies — the honesty is the mechanism.
- Pose the question and never actually answer it.
- Pull from the format's source examples in the library — those are for pattern recognition, not for output.
