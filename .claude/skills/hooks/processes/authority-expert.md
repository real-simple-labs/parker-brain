# Process — Authority / Expert Hooks

Leverage credentials, expertise, or achievement to establish credibility from the first frame. Uses experts, professionals, or accomplished individuals to deliver the message.

## Psychological framework

- **Trust transfer** — expert authority equals product authority.
- **Cognitive shortcut** — credentials reduce need for personal verification.
- **Social proof** — experts validate viewer choices.
- **Risk reduction** — less risky to trust an expert.
- **Insider knowledge** — access to professional secrets.

## Required inputs

- A real credentialed person available to the brand. Doctor, dermatologist, dietitian, financial advisor, food critic, athlete, professor.
- The credentials must be legitimate — fabricating credentials is a compliance and credibility risk.
- Whitelisting permission if running through the expert's account (Meta is pushing partnership ads heavily in 2026).

## When to pick

- **Best for:** trust-sensitive categories — health, finance, professional services. High-sophistication markets. Premium and high-ticket products. B2B services. Categories with lots of misinformation.
- **Worst for:** brands without access to legitimate credentialed creators. Categories where the audience is anti-establishment or distrusts experts.

## Execution

1. **State the credentials clearly.** "As a dermatologist with 15 years experience..." or "I became a portfolio manager at 28..." Credentials front and center.
2. **Use insider language.** Experts speak with terminology only professionals would know. That language signals authenticity.
3. **Show, not just tell.** Demonstrate expertise through specific knowledge or technique, not just by stating the credential.
4. **Pair with client results or case studies if available.** "Here's how my client got X without Y" pulls double duty.
5. **Whitelist through the expert's account** when possible. Meta pushes partnership ads. Whitelisting performs exceptionally well.

## Output content

- The exact credential statement.
- The insider-language proof of expertise.
- The transition from credentials into the product or claim.
- The ICP and the trust the credentials are answering for.
- The whitelisting plan if relevant.
- The proven-pattern note.

## What never to do

- Fabricate credentials. The expert must be real.
- Use a credentialed expert outside their field of expertise. A dermatologist talking about supplements stretches credibility.
- Skip insider language. Experts who speak in generic terms lose the trust advantage.
- Use this hook on categories where the audience distrusts experts.
