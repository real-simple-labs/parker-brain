# Hooks — Strategy

This document picks which hook format(s) to write for a given brand and ad. The choice depends on brand type, audience awareness stage, dominant emotional driver, and what the brand has already tested.

## What to load before deciding

- Brand profile and ICP. Specifically the persona's identity, situation, what they have tried, what they believe.
- Voice of customer — the exact language customers use to describe their problem, their objections, their wins.
- Brand voice rules — what tone is on-brand, what is off-brand, what compliance rails apply.
- The brand's hook history — what hook formats they have tested, what has worked, what has failed, what is still untouched.
- The specific ad context if the user is writing hooks for a specific concept or body.
- `hook-psychology.md` — load it for any hook task. It lets you choose the format from the mechanism the situation needs (notice / qualify / intrigue / reassure / move / transport) and diagnose a weak hook by which job it misses, rather than reaching for the same formats by habit. `hooks.md` is the format/example reference; `hook-psychology.md` is the reasoning.

## The four-element hook structure

This is the four-element model from `hooks.md`, held here as the live decision lens for picking which element does the work. `hooks.md` is the source if the framing ever drifts. Every hook is actually four elements happening in the first three seconds:

- **Text overlay hook.** The written words on screen.
- **Sound hook.** The audio or verbal element.
- **Visual hook.** What is shown. Often more impactful than text.
- **Vibe hook.** The overall feeling created by lighting, font, color grading, aesthetic.

Not every hook needs all four. When recommending, be intentional about which of the four elements is doing the work. The best hooks SHOW rather than TELL — changing the visual hook often has bigger impact than changing the text overlay. The first second is won by the visual and sound hook (and a short open-loop text overlay) — not the spoken line, which can't land in ~5–6 words; see `hooks.md` ("The First Second") for the brain-stopping mechanics and the two-second grabs that stack. Match visual complexity to the message and keep first-second words few, since an arresting visual eats the text.

## Brand-type decision

The first question to answer.

- **Problem/solution brands.** Audience knows they have a problem and is looking for a fix. Hooks can be direct, agitate pain, name the problem. Investment hooks, comment-response hooks, scam hooks, myth-busting, educational hooks tend to work well.
- **Lifestyle brands.** Audience is buying identity, aesthetic, upgrade — not solving pain. Hooks need to create desire and aspiration. POV hooks, viral product hooks, speed-and-transformation hooks, conversational hooks tend to work well.

Some products straddle. Match the hook approach to the specific product and audience, not just the brand as a whole.

## Awareness stage decision

The audience's awareness stage shapes which formats fit.

- **Unaware.** Does not know the problem exists. Storytelling, educational, authority hooks.
- **Problem-aware.** Knows something is wrong but does not know solutions exist. Educational, myth-busting, investment, "how do I know if" hooks.
- **Solution-aware.** Has tried other solutions that failed. Comment-response, scam, investment, comparison, myth-busting hooks.
- **Product-aware / most-aware.** Knows the product, considering it. Authority, social proof, viral product, direct CTA hooks.

## Emotional driver decision

The dominant emotion driving the purchase shapes which formats hit hardest.

- **Fear-based** (health anxiety, missing out, making a mistake) → scam, controversy, question, myth-busting hooks.
- **Aspiration-based** (wanting to be better, transformation) → POV, viral product, speed-and-transformation, authority hooks.
- **Exhaustion or frustration-based** (tried everything, fed up) → investment, comment-response, myth-busting hooks.
- **Exhausted, overwhelmed, or wary on a cold audience** (especially women 45+) → permission / Zone 1 hooks — release rather than activate; calm converts where pressure makes her scroll. See the valence×intensity / headspace mechanism in `hook-psychology.md` #11.
- **Tribal identity-based** (defining who I am) → demographic, POV, conversational hooks.
- **Curiosity-based** (intrigue, wanting to know) → question, controversy, storytelling, "how do I know if" hooks.

Beyond matching the emotion, match the **zone and the headspace**: on cold traffic, default to a calm (Zone 1) or mild-shared-frustration (Zone 3) open and keep the zone coherent through the ad — opening cold in high-intensity fear (Zone 4) usually makes her scroll. `hook-psychology.md` #11 carries the why and the AI-defaults-to-drama caution.

## The format menu

The full hook taxonomy is `creative-strategy-context/hooks.md`. Strategy picks from that taxonomy; the processes below are the execution playbooks for the format families that have one. Picking a format means picking it from `hooks.md` — these entries are pointers to where the writing playbook lives, not a competing list of what formats exist.

Each process below holds the writing playbook for a family of hooks. Strategy picks one or more based on the decisions above plus the brand's hook history.

- **comment-response** — Address real objections via a TikTok-style comment. Best for products with common objections, retargeting, trust-building.
- **investment-journey** — Money or time spent before finding the solution. Best for high-consideration purchases, sophisticated markets, audiences that have tried everything.
- **scam-trust-flip** — "I thought this was a scam" tension and resolution. Best for categories with trust issues, "too good to be true" products, retargeting skeptics.
- **speed-transformation** — Time-based + before-and-after proof. Best for beauty, skincare, fitness, any product with visible quick change.
- **demographic-qualification** — Direct callout of a specific identity in the opener. Best for broad targeting, top-of-funnel, scalability, Meta Andromeda-era creative.
- **pov-emotional-relatability** — "POV: you hate..." or "I hate..." format. Best for highly relatable situations, lifestyle products, Gen Z and Millennial audiences.
- **authority-expert** — Credentials-led, expertise-led, professional voice. Best for trust-sensitive categories, premium products, high-sophistication markets.
- **viral-social-proof** — Trending status, "millions of people," bandwagon. Best for products with real social momentum.
- **controversy-anti-traditional** — Break a tradition or challenge widely-held belief. Best for crowded markets, products challenging norms, driving engagement.
- **question-curiosity-gap** — Direct question the viewer instinctively wants answered. Best for problem-aware audiences and educational content.
- **storytelling-narrative** — Open with a story beginning. Best for unaware audiences, founder content, longer-form content.
- **conversational-native-format** — Mid-conversation phrases ("No because...," "Okay these..."). Best for native organic feel, reducing ad fatigue.
- **educational-teach-in-timeframe** — Promise to teach or reveal in a specific timeframe. Best for low-sophistication markets, complex products, brand authority building.
- **how-do-i-know-if** — Open with the exact qualifying question the viewer is already asking themselves. Best for consideration-stage, multi-use-case products, retargeting skeptics.
- **reaction** — Lead with a held, authentic emotional reaction that demands explanation. Best for products with dramatic results, gifting seasons, beauty.
- **comparison** — Set up a direct side-by-side between two options, methods, or eras. Best for solution-aware audiences weighing alternatives.
- **multi-hook-stacking** — Stack two or three hooks back-to-back before the existing body. Best for VSL-style ads or improving existing high-performers.
- **permission / Zone 1** (`hooks.md` #21, no standalone process yet) — A calm, high-valence opener that releases rather than activates ("You don't have to…", "You're allowed to stop…"). Best for cold audiences, exhausted/saturated categories, and women 45+. Write it from `hooks.md` #21 and `hook-psychology.md` #11 directly.

Trigger Word (`hooks.md` #14) has no process and stays that way — it is an amplifier, not a standalone format. It is a layer of high-charge words ("scam," "secret," "obsessed," "never") applied inside whatever opener is already chosen, with no opening shape of its own. When the diagnosis calls for trigger-word intensity, reach for it from `hooks.md` directly inside the recommended format — never recommend it as a hook on its own.

## How to choose the recommended set

Apply these rules in order.

1. **Pass the ICP + emotion gate.** If brand context does not yield a specific ICP and a specific emotion to target, stop and ask. Skipping this produces generic hooks.
2. **Filter by brand type.** Problem/solution vs lifestyle eliminates roughly half the formats from consideration.
3. **Filter by awareness stage.** Different stages need different psychological levers.
4. **Filter by emotional driver.** Match the format to the dominant emotion the buyer is responding to.
5. **Check brand history.** Cross off formats the brand has tested into exhaustion. Lean toward formats they have not yet touched if the diagnosis fits.
6. **Pick 2-4 formats.** Never more. Quality over quantity. Each one gets deep justification.
7. **Tag each recommendation.** Safe bet, untapped opportunity, or iteration. The tag tells the brand the strategic posture of the recommendation.
8. **Mix safe bets and untapped opportunities.** At least one recommendation should be a format the brand has not yet tested if a strong match exists. Pure safe-bet sets produce diminishing returns.

## The must-haves for every hook iteration

These five criteria must pass on every hook. If a hook fails any of them, refine or pick a different approach.

- **Relevance first, attention second.** Wrong-audience attention is wasted spend. Qualify the ideal customer immediately.
- **Clear promise.** What is in it for the viewer? Stated or strongly implied within three seconds.
- **Zero confusion.** Fifth-grade reading level or below. One clear idea. No clickbait without payoff.
- **Emotional targeting.** Name the emotion. If you cannot name it, scrap the concept.
- **Native format.** Looks like it belongs on the platform. 2022 ad styling is dead.

## Common mistakes the strategy must avoid

- Recommending hook formats without identifying ICP + emotion first.
- Picking historically strong formats just because they are strong, without checking if they match the brand's diagnosis.
- Copy-pasting format examples with product names swapped in.
- Generic hooks that could apply to any brand.
- Recommending more than 4 hooks in a single output.
- Ignoring brand history and recommending formats the brand has already exhausted.
- Hooks written in marketing voice instead of customer voice.
