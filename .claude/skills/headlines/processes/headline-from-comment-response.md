# Process — Headline From a Comment Response (Answer the Objection in Their Words)

Take a real comment off the brand's ads and answer it in the headline. Name the objection in the customer's own language, then resolve it. The objection-named-and-answered move converts because it meets the exact doubt the scroller already holds.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** brands with active ad comment sections carrying repeated objections. Solution-aware and product-aware stages where the buyer has a specific doubt blocking the purchase. Categories where one objection dominates.
- **Worst for:** brands with thin or off-topic comments. Unaware audiences with no formed objection yet.

## Execution

1. **Pull the real comment.** Use the actual objection from the ad comments, not an invented one. The recurring doubts are the gold: "does this actually work," "I tried the competitor and it didn't," "isn't this just a dupe," "will it fit me."
2. **Keep the objection in the customer's words.** The phrasing is the hook. A scroller recognizes their own doubt and stops. If the comment language is "isn't this just a dupe," the headline should answer that wording directly rather than translating it into brand-safe abstraction.
3. **Answer it in the same breath.** The headline names the doubt and resolves it, or names it and promises the resolution the ad delivers. The resolution must be true and compliant.
4. **Do not strawman.** Answer the objection people actually raise, not a soft version that is easy to beat.
5. **Under 10 words.**

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The real comment it answers, quoted, with where it came from.
- The resolution and its proof.
- The ICP and emotion.

## What never to do

- Invent a comment. If no one raised it, this is not a comment response — it is a guess.
- Answer a soft strawman instead of the real objection.
- Promise a resolution the product cannot back or compliance forbids.
- Strip the customer's phrasing into marketing language. The recognized wording is the stop.
