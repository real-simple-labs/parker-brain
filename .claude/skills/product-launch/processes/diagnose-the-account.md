# Diagnose the Account

Runs on every launch, before any concept work, regardless of scenario. The better the new product is understood in comparison to the rest of the brand's line, ads, and proven equity, the better the creative. Diagnose first, then write. Most weak launch creative skips this step.

The output of this process is not a document the user sees. It is the working material every downstream step draws on — the palette of hooks, formats, proof types, and equity language the launch inherits from.

## a. Review the brand's existing top-performing creative

Pull the highest-performing ads from the account **across SKUs**, not just the closest one. Looking for:

- **Formats that have worked repeatedly.** UGC unboxing, founder-to-camera, side-by-side comparison, "which one are you" personality framing, drop-day reveal. Name them by the account's own AI tag taxonomy, not invented labels.
- **Headlines and hooks that have hit.** Write them down literally, in the brand's voice, as a starting palette.
- **Recurring visual mechanics.** Lineup shots, hero-product close-ups, specific lifestyle settings, color treatments. This is visual vocabulary the audience already associates with the brand.
- **Proof types the brand has built that transfer.** Reviews, demonstrations, founder credibility, expert endorsements.
- **Previous new-SKU or launch creative that performed well.** The most directly relevant prior art in the account, and the most commonly overlooked.

These are signals to replicate with a spin, not to copy. The job is extending a working pattern to a new SKU, not reinventing. Start here because it is already known to work in this account.

## b. Identify the closest existing SKU and pull its creative specifically

Of the brand's existing SKUs, which is closest to the new one? Pull that SKU's top hooks, top angles, and top proof.

How much of it transfers depends on the scenario. In A and B, most of it informs the new launch directly. In C, the closest SKU is a *structural* reference rather than a tonal one — the product may be structurally similar while living in a completely different audience world, which makes the creative inheritance partial.

When the brief names a specific prior winner, that ad is the highest-value object in this step. Take it apart: what was the hook, what format, what proof pattern, what language. Those elements become the starting palette for the new launch.

## c. Map the words and visuals worth doubling down on

Catalog the line names, campaign names, slogans, taglines, recurring visuals, and brand-specific language that already carry equity. These are the verbal and visual handles the audience already grabs onto.

The launch ad should **use** this language wherever the new SKU sits inside the existing world. If the brand has a campaign name and the new color fits inside it, use it. If there is a tagline customers parrot back in their own reviews, weave it in. This is the cheapest win available in launch creative and the one most often skipped.

## d. Plan the warm-audience recapture move

Unless the launch is aimed at an entirely new audience, plan how to recapture repeat buyers and warm audiences before broad cold creative. They are the lowest-lift, highest-likelihood first buyers, and the most likely source of early momentum. Usually:

- Email plus paid to the existing customer list and engaged audience
- "Meet the new [SKU]" or "introducing [SKU]" framing
- An introductory offer tied to the launch moment, where the brand uses one
- Hero shot plus a clear statement of what is new and why existing customers in particular will care
- Optionally, early-access framing for hero-product owners

Full treatment in `launch-phase-sequencing.md`. Two cautions worth carrying here: do not burn the warm list with an offer that is too aggressive or too frequent, and do not assume there is a discount at all — many launches have none, and access plus recognition often works better than a markdown.

## e. Diagnose the SKU's relationship to the rest of the line

Answer plainly, in plain English:

- Has the brand done a version of this before — another color, another seasonal drop, another flavor? If yes, lean Scenario A.
- Or is this genuinely a new format, fabric, use case, or audience? New use case or format leans B. New audience leans C.
- What problem does this product solve that the existing line does not, even if the difference is incremental?

The clearer this diagnosis, the cleaner the scenario classification and the less likely the output defaults to a generic new-product ad.

## f. Ingest the product URL if provided

If a product page URL is supplied — sometimes it arrives *as* the brief — pull and summarize before classifying: product name, what it is and does, key claims, hero benefits, pricing, the copy already live on the page, any reviews or testimonials already showing, slogans and taglines.

This grounds the script in the brand's actual product language rather than assumptions, and it frequently answers Question 3 on its own.

## g. Ingest past performance data — inherit winners *and* diagnose losers

Both jobs, not just the first. Inheriting winners is obvious. Diagnosing losers is what most launch briefs skip, and it is where the new creative either repeats the failure or breaks the pattern that was already failing.

- **Winners** — catalog the top hooks, formats, claims, and proof types. Replicate with a spin for the new SKU.
- **Losers** — diagnose root cause. A stated problem like "low hold rates across past videos" is a *diagnostic*, not a stat. Where is hold rate dropping? At second 2 means the hook is failing. At second 5 means the post-hook beat is not delivering. At second 12 means a mid-script bore. Then write the launch creative to fix that specific failure mode.

*Example: a dog-gear brand launching a transit tote bundle flags low hold rates across past videos. That means the launch creative needs a faster, more pattern-interrupting hook and has to prove the value in the first three to five seconds rather than waiting until the fourth segment.*

Treat a stated failure mode as a constraint on the new work, not just as background.

## h. Ingest competitor benchmark refs if provided

When the brief supplies competitor refs, either run a light teardown — structure, hook, proof pattern, visual mechanic, on-screen text — or hand off to `scriptwriting`'s `adapt-existing-script` process for a full ref-by-ref breakdown, per the method in `adapting-scripts.md`. The structural-fidelity work lives there, not here.

The teardown's job is identifying in-category patterns the launch can ethically borrow: which hook structures are converting in this field right now, which proof types keep showing up, what visual language repeats. This is calibration to what is working in-category, then translation through the brand's own voice and everything else in this diagnostic. It is not copying, and the mechanism is what transfers, never the surface.
