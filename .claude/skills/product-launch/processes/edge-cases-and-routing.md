# Edge Cases and Routing

Two checks that run on every launch before shipping, plus the substitution library for briefs that do not fit the clean path. This is where most launch outputs go sideways.

## The persona-fit check

**The most common failure mode in launch creative is writing the new-SKU ad in the existing persona's voice when the SKU is actually for a new audience.** It happens out of habit, not decision — the existing voice is proven, it is right there, and it belongs to someone else.

Before finalizing anything, ask these deliberately:

- **Whose voice should this be in?** Name them. Not "the customer" — the specific person.
- **Would this audience actually use these words?** Read the script aloud as them.
- **Would this audience already know who the brand is?** If no, was the brand introduced correctly?
- **Am I borrowing tone from the wrong persona out of habit?**

If any answer is "no" or "I am not sure," revise. The cost of a persona-fit failure is that the ad never lands with the audience it was built for, and the cost is invisible in the output — a Scenario C ad in the existing persona's voice reads perfectly fine to anyone who already knows the brand.

## The launch audit

Answer honestly. Any "no" means revise before shipping.

- Did I review the brand's existing top creative and inherit what works?
- Did I diagnose past underperformance, where data was provided, and avoid repeating it?
- Did I make the newness visible **in the hook itself**?
- Is the script in the right voice for the right persona **and** the right awareness stage?
- Am I leveraging brand authority correctly, and only where it actually transfers?
- Did I use the line, collection, and campaign language that already carries equity?
- Did I avoid proof types I cannot honestly claim yet?
- Does the angle match the launch scenario, or did I default to the brand's existing top angle out of habit?
- **For Scenario C:** would a member of the new audience recognize this as for them, without needing the brand's existing context?
- Did I produce the deliverable type actually asked for in Question 4, rather than a script by default?
- Have I clarified whether this is warm-phase or cold-phase creative, and tagged it?

## Substitution library

Routes for briefs that do not fit the clean path.

**No UGC or reviews for the new product** → substitute with founder POV, beta-tester quotes, demo proof, or transparently inherited hero-product proof. Do not manufacture testimonials or claim ratings that do not exist. See `proof-substitution.md`.

**New audience, and no existing assets feature that audience** → write in the new persona's voice and explicitly call out the audience in copy. Do not use existing-audience footage and assume it transfers; it usually telegraphs that the brand is not for them.

**The product changes the brand's category entirely** → do not borrow the parent brand's authority claims as if they carry. Lean on genuinely transferable equity — aesthetic, founder, audience trust — only where credible, and otherwise write the launch as though the new category is its own brand wearing the parent's badge.

**The brief is too thin to classify with confidence** → ask before writing. Specifically: (1) existing audience or new audience, (2) the closest existing SKU in the line, (3) what is genuinely new about this one, (4) warm-audience launch or cold-audience scaling, (5) which deliverable type they actually want.

**Limited drop, colorway, or collection** → default to Scenario A, pull from the brand's previous drop ads where they exist, and use the brand's collection-drop vocabulary. Drops have their own format conventions inside a brand's world; use them rather than generic launch language.

**Subscription, refill, or a bundle of existing products** → Scenario A or B depending on whether the *use case* is new. Lead with the value of the format change — convenience, cost, ritual, completeness. A bundle's hook should never be "we have a bundle now."

**A re-launch** — the product was previously paused, failed, or rebuilt → first confirm it is a launch at all. If the product genuinely changed, lead with **what is different this time** and why customers should give it another look. Do not lead with "back by popular demand" unless that is true and substantiable. Re-launches that pretend to be fresh launches read as dishonest to anyone who was there the first time. If only the marketing changed, this is not a launch — route to routine performance creative.

**A restock, back-in-stock, or "limited edition" wrapper on an unchanged SKU** → not a launch. Nothing is new. Route to `scriptwriting` or `iterations` for routine performance creative with an urgency mechanic, and say plainly why this skill is not the right one.

**Geographic expansion** with existing products → out of scope. No doc exists in the brain for this yet; say so rather than improvising a launch brief on top of it.

**A brand-new brand running its first round of ads** → out of scope. No doc exists in the brain for this yet either. The absence of account data is the whole reason it is a different playbook, so a launch brief built on nothing is worse than no brief.

**A gift or occasion launch** → layer the gifting treatment on top of the scenario playbook. See `special-launch-contexts.md`.

**Competitor benchmark refs in the brief** → run the teardown in the diagnostic, or hand off to `scriptwriting`'s `adapt-existing-script` process for a full ref-by-ref breakdown per `adapting-scripts.md`. Bring the structural learnings back before finalizing the angle set.

**Past performance data showing failures** → diagnose root cause and write the launch creative to avoid it. Do not inherit winners blindly; underperformers are equally valuable signal, and a stated failure mode is a constraint on the new work.

**An email sequence as the primary deliverable** → produce a light three-email skeleton only, and route to the email context doc for deeper treatment.
