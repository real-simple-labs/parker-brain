# Proof Substitution

New SKUs often lack reviews, UGC, and best-seller status on day one. The temptation is to manufacture proof the product does not have. The discipline is substituting honestly instead.

The doctrine is in `parker-system/creative-strategy-context/new-product-launch-creative.md`. This process is the working method: what to reach for, in what order, and where the lines are.

## The substitution set

Ordered roughly by how much weight each can carry.

**Inherited proof from the hero product, clearly attributed.** "Our [hero] has [X] reviews — this is the [new format]." The strongest available substitute when the scenario allows it, because the proof is real and the audience can verify it. **The attribution is what makes it honest.** Stripping the attribution and letting the review count read as the new SKU's is the exact failure this whole process exists to prevent.

**Founder or team POV.** "We built this because…" Credibility through transparency. Works in every scenario and is often the only proof available on day one for a genuinely new category. The founder can say things nobody else can say, which is also why it is a distinct deliverable type.

**Demo-as-proof.** Show the product working and let the demonstration convince. Audience-neutral, scenario-neutral, and unusually valuable in Scenario C where almost nothing else transfers.

**Beta tester or pre-order quotes.** Early-adopter social proof. Requires planning ahead of the launch, which is why it is worth naming during the pre-launch phase rather than discovering its absence on launch week. In Scenario C this is frequently the *only* audience-matched social proof available.

**Behind-the-scenes development footage.** Process as proof. Shows the work rather than asserting the outcome. Pairs well with founder POV.

**Existing-customer testimonial about brand quality, applied to the new SKU.** "I trust [brand] because of [hero], and this is from the same team." Transfers the relationship rather than the product claim. Strong in Scenario B, weak in Scenario C where the relationship is what is missing.

## What transfers, by scenario

| | Reviews & UGC | Brand-quality trust | Audience-matched social proof |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A** | Yes — same product, new attribute | Yes | Yes, already exists |
| **B** | Yes, attributed to the hero | Yes | Yes, and a bridge testimonial is the best version |
| **C** | Mostly no — and wrong-audience UGC can actively hurt | Partially, as brand character not category authority | No — must be built from beta users or new-audience creators |

The Scenario A caveat worth carrying: proof attaches to the **product**, not the attribute. Reviews for the hoodie support the hoodie in a new color. They do not support a claim *about* the new color. Any claim specific to the new attribute needs its own evidence.

The Scenario B equivalent: the hero's reviews support the brand and the product family. They do not support a performance claim about the new format. The new fabric's durability, the bundle's results as a routine — those need their own evidence or they do not get claimed.

## The lines that do not get crossed

**Do not claim "best-selling," "loved by thousands," or a review count for a product that does not have them.** Not with a hedge, not in a text overlay, not as set dressing in a mockup.

**Do not present inherited proof as the new SKU's own.** Inherited proof is honest when attributed and dishonest when laundered. The difference is a single clause.

**Do not use existing-audience footage for a new-audience SKU** and assume it transfers. It usually telegraphs to the new audience that the brand is not for them, which costs more than the missing proof would have.

**Do not fabricate testimonials.** A review-flavored line written to sound authentic is acceptable in a *draft* only when labeled illustrative in one plain line, so nobody runs a made-up quote as a real testimonial. Facts are different — numbers, sizes, specs, prices, and product claims must be real and must trace.

The reason this matters beyond ethics: inherited claims that are not actually transferable erode trust faster than an honest "this is brand new and here's why we think you'll love it." A launch ad that overclaims trains the audience to discount everything else the brand says, which is an expensive way to sell a first batch.

## Naming it in the output

The Launch Context Summary carries the proof strategy, **especially what was substituted for missing UGC or reviews.** A strategist reading the output should be able to see the substitution was a decision rather than an oversight, and should be able to tell at a glance whether any claim in the creative is standing on evidence the brand does not have yet.
