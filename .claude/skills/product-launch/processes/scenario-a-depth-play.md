# Scenario A — Depth Play

**Existing persona, new attribute or collection-level newness.** New color, flavor, scent, fabric, size, or the collection-drop wrapper around them.

The persona, the use case, and the brand authority are all unchanged. The only new thing is the attribute. The job is to make the newness visible while inheriting every winning structure from the closest existing SKU. Mode 1 thinking: replication with a swap, not reinvention.

## The posture

The customer already knows and trusts the brand. The ad does not need to re-establish anything — it needs to make the new thing irresistible. That means the existing collection language, drop vocabulary, and visual mechanics all carry forward, especially from previous launch ads that already performed in this account.

The hook can often be the same hook with the attribute swapped. The proof can be the same proof. The persona voice is unchanged.

*A bestselling hoodie's winning hook — "POV: you finally found the perfect everyday hoodie" — becomes "POV: now in [new color]," or "POV: the everyday hoodie just got a new uniform color." Same hook DNA, swapped element, same proof, same shot cadence.*

## The failure mode, named up front

**Treating it so lightly that the launch goes invisible.** A new color is the easiest scenario in theory, and that is exactly why it gets phoned in. Attribute-only newness still deserves a real hero shot, a real drop moment, and a genuine reason to act now.

The test: if the new attribute is not apparent in the first three seconds, the ad has not done its job. "Lean in, don't phone it in" is the whole discipline of this scenario.

## Angle set

Pull the top one or two proven angles from the closest existing SKU, then add **one** angle that explicitly showcases the new attribute. Three angles total is the target.

Do not write three new angles. The point of Scenario A is that the account already knows what works; replicate that and let the newness show through. Inventing fresh angles here discards the one advantage this scenario has.

Angles that fit:

- **Drop or reveal mechanics** — the launch moment itself as the angle.
- **Side-by-side with the existing line** — the new attribute in the context of what the customer already owns.
- **"Which one are you" personality framing** across the lineup.
- **"New in [season / drop]"** using the brand's own established collection vocabulary.

## Script moves

- **Drop, reveal, or unboxing format.** The most direct expression of the newness.
- **Side-by-side with the existing line.** Shows how the new attribute fits the palette or range the customer already knows.
- **"Which one are you" personality framing** across the full lineup, with the new attribute as one of the options.
- **"New in [season / drop]"** announcement using the existing collection-drop vocabulary rather than generic launch language.
- **A sensory close-up moment** when the new attribute is sensory — color saturation, the fragrance bottle, the fabric weave, the pour, the texture. If the newness can be *seen or heard*, the ad should let it be seen or heard rather than described.

## What to carry from the diagnostic

- The brand's previous drop or launch ads — the closest structural precedent in the account.
- The collection and campaign vocabulary from the equity mapping. Generic "new product" language is a downgrade from words the audience already recognizes.
- The existing hero shots and lineup mechanics. These are visual vocabulary; reusing them is what makes the new attribute read as part of the family rather than an orphan.

## Proof

Scenario A has the easiest proof situation of the three. The existing SKU's reviews, ratings, and UGC generally transfer, because the product is the same product — one attribute is different.

The one caution: proof attaches to the *product*, not the attribute. Reviews for the hoodie support the hoodie in a new color. They do not support a claim about the new color specifically. If the launch wants to claim something about the attribute itself, that claim needs its own evidence. See `proof-substitution.md`.

## Phase

The warm-audience launch usually goes first — existing buyers, email plus paid, intro offer framing where the brand uses one. Cold-audience scaling picks up from there. Both are distinct creative; see `launch-phase-sequencing.md`.

## Awareness stage

Most-aware for the warm phase, product-aware for the near-cold. A Scenario A launch to a most-aware audience is the shortest, punchiest ad in this whole skill — hero shot, what is new, offer, done. No context is needed because the audience already has all of it.
