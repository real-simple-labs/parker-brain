# Scenario B — Bridge and Expand

**Existing persona, new format, occasion, use case, or bundle.**

The audience is the same but the use case is new. Anchor the script in the hero product's authority — "you already love X" — and introduce the new format as a natural extension of a relationship that already exists. Mode 2 thinking: the bridge *is* the angle.

Voice and persona are unchanged. What the script is explaining is a new use case, which means it does more work than a Scenario A script but nothing like the invention a Scenario C script requires.

## The posture

The customer relationship is the whole asset. The script's job is to grant permission to extend an existing trust to a new use case — not to argue the brand is good, which the customer already believes.

*An outdoor brand whose hero product is an adventure challenge launches a family version. The buyer already trusts the original. The ad plants: you already love this, we made one for the whole family. Use-case demo for the family occasion. Bridge testimonial from an existing customer trying the new version. The hero product's existing reviews inherited as proof.*

*A brand known for a cotton line launches a performance-fabric line. Same persona, new use case. Lead with the bridge — the brand you already trust for one thing, now built for another. Side-by-side fabric demo, same brand aesthetic translated into the new material.*

The trap in both: writing it like a new brand. The existing relationship is the advantage; discarding it produces a colder, harder ad than the situation requires.

## The persona-slice case

"Existing persona" does not have to mean every current buyer. A Scenario B launch frequently targets a **slice** — the active subset of an athleisure buyer base, the new-mom subset of a baby brand's customers, the dads inside a broader audience.

When that is the case, write to the slice's specific language, not the general persona's. The bridge still works, but it bridges from what *that slice* already loves, and the use-case demo is built around their specific moment. Naming the slice in the Launch Context Summary matters — "existing persona" alone hides the actual targeting decision.

## Angle set

- **Lead angle: the bridge.** "If you loved [hero], we made [new]." This is the primary angle and usually the hook.
- **Two use-case angles** specific to the new format or occasion. Show the new moment the product now serves.
- **At least one bridge testimonial angle** — an existing customer trying the new SKU, acting as their own credibility source. This is the most transferable proof Scenario B has.

## Script moves

- **"If you loved [hero], we made [new]."** The core bridge line, in the brand's own voice.
- **Use-case demo built for the new format or occasion** — the family on the trail, the workout in the new fabric, the morning routine using the bundle. Specific moment, not a generic product beauty shot.
- **Existing customer tries the new SKU.** Bridge testimonial. Letting an existing buyer be the credibility source is stronger than the brand asserting the extension makes sense.
- **Compare and contrast within the brand's own line.** The new format against the hero, showing what each is for.
- **"Same [brand promise], now for [new moment]."** Explicit continuity of the promise across a discontinuity of use case.

## Bundles — diagnose before writing

A bundle is sometimes Scenario A (same use case, just packaged together) and sometimes Scenario B (the bundle creates a new ritual, occasion, or completeness story). Diagnose on what is actually new: the SKU, or the use case?

Once it is confirmed as B, the rule is: **lead with the value of the format change** — ritual, savings, completeness, a daily routine that finally fits together. Never a generic new-product hook.

Bundles fail when treated as five things at once. They win when treated as one complete routine, finally in one place. Show the routine working end to end rather than showcasing the individual products in sequence. "We have a bundle now" is not a hook, and defaulting to it is the laziest move available in this scenario.

## Proof

Scenario B has the strongest inherited-proof position of the three, because the buyer and the brand relationship are unchanged.

- The hero product's existing reviews transfer, **clearly attributed** — "our [hero] has [X] reviews; this is the [new format]." The attribution is what keeps it honest.
- An existing-customer bridge testimonial is the highest-value proof available and worth pursuing specifically.
- What does *not* transfer is any claim specific to the new format's performance. The new fabric's durability, the bundle's results as a routine — those need their own evidence. See `proof-substitution.md`.

## Phase

Warm-audience launch goes first, and in Scenario B it is unusually well-targeted: the existing buyers of the hero product are the single best audience for its extension. Segment to hero-product owners where the brand's list allows it. Cold-audience scaling picks up from there.

## Awareness stage

Most-aware for the warm phase. Cold scaling usually lands solution-aware — the new use case is the thing being sold, and a cold audience needs the use case established before the brand extension means anything to them.
