# Process — Static Ad Recreation

Recreate a competitor or inspo static for the user's brand. Preserve the original's story while rewriting every word and adapting every brand-specific visual. The structural skeleton stays — layout, hierarchy, copy mechanics, word count. The brand-specific content changes.

This is not redesign. This is faithful recreation of someone else's working ad in your brand's voice.

The recreation method is canonical at `parker-system/creative-strategy-context/static-ad-recreation.md` — that doc is the source of truth for the story-first discipline and the copy-mechanics rules. What follows is the execution playbook for running it; when the two diverge, the canonical doc wins, except that the recreation brief is written as prose here, never a table, per the no-tables output rule.

## Required inputs

- A visual overview of the original ad — layout, copy, colors, typography, composition. The user typically provides this directly or shares a screenshot.
- Brand context — voice, ICP, compliance, visual identity, customer language from reviews.
- Any compliance constraints that affect what claims the brand can make.

## Core philosophy

The original ad is telling one story through the combination of its visuals and copy working together. The job is to understand that story deeply, then tell the same story for this brand using this brand's language, product, and visual identity.

Every word gets rewritten. The structure stays — word count, copy mechanics, information hierarchy. Every word becomes the brand's word, rooted in the brand's customer language and voice.

Do not redesign the layout. Do not add elements that were not there. If the original has one headline, the recreation has one headline. If there is no CTA button, the recreation has none. The structural skeleton stays. The copy and brand-specific visuals change.

## Execution

### Step 1: Identify the story the ad is telling

Before writing any adapted copy, write out the full story this ad is telling in a complete paragraph. Not just the message — the story. How does the ad move a viewer from first glance to final impression? What does it want them to believe? What emotion or conviction is it building? How do the visuals and copy work together to build that conviction?

Go deep. The more thoroughly the story is understood, the better the adaptation. Every decision in the recreation must trace back to this paragraph.

### Step 2: Adapt the copy

Rewrite every piece of copy for the brand. Every headline, every subhead, every callout, every line. Nothing carries over verbatim.

Rules:

- **Preserve the purpose of each piece of copy.** If the original headline creates credibility through ingredient transparency, the recreated headline creates credibility through ingredient transparency. The persuasive function stays the same.
- **Match the length.** If the original headline is 4 words, the recreation is 4 words. If 8 words, then 8 words. Do not bloat.
- **Preserve copy mechanics.** If the original uses a number, the recreation uses a number. If it asks a question, the recreation asks a question. If it uses a customer quote, the recreation uses a customer quote.
- **Use the brand's customer language.** Pull from reviews, ad comments, voice-of-customer data. The copy should sound like it came from the brand's actual world.

### Step 3: Adapt the design

Identify what needs to change visually to make the ad feel like it belongs to this brand while keeping the same layout and visual structure intact.

- Swap product imagery for the brand's product.
- Adjust brand colors and fonts where needed to match the brand's identity.
- Adapt any visual elements that are specific to the original brand's product.

The composition, the layout, the information hierarchy, and the visual storytelling approach all stay the same. Only the brand-specific visual content changes.

### Step 4: Compliance check

Every adapted claim passes through the brand's compliance framework. If the original makes a claim the brand cannot make, substitute the closest compliant equivalent that serves the same persuasive function. For example, a before/after claim might substitute to "routine + small observable win"; a medical claim might substitute to sensory or experience language; a price-anchored claim might substitute to value framing.

## Output

### THE STORY THIS AD IS TELLING

A full paragraph. What the original ad wants the viewer to believe or feel, how the visuals and copy work together to build that conviction, why it works. Every adaptation decision below traces back to this paragraph.

### RECREATION BRIEF

For each section that applies, write it as prose, not a table: name the section, quote the original, then give the brand's rewrite in plain sentences. Drop any section that does not exist in the original.

- **Headline** — the primary copy, the main text that hits first.
- **Supporting Copy** — all other copy in the ad. Subheads, callouts, body text, CTAs, badges, disclaimers.
- **Design** — what changes visually to reflect this brand. Product imagery, colors, fonts, brand-specific visual swaps.

After the recreation brief, surface any compliance considerations — claims that need disclaimers, terms that need substituting, stats that need verification.

### Brand Context Applied

What brand context shaped the recreation, what was avoided, why this recreation fits the brand's current moment.

## What never to do

- Keep any of the original copy verbatim. Every word gets rewritten.
- Lose the story. If you cannot explain how the recreation tells the same story as the original, something went wrong. Trace every decision back to the story paragraph.
- Bloat the copy. The AI instinct is to add more. Resist.
- Swap copy mechanics. If the original is a question, the recreation is a question. If it uses a specific number, the recreation uses a specific number.
- Add elements that were not there. If the original has no CTA button, do not add one.
- Invent stats or claims. If no verified source exists for a number, flag it as `[STAT NEEDED — verify before publishing]` instead of making one up.
- Redesign the layout. Structural fidelity is the discipline of this process.
