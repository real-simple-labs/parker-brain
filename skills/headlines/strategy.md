# Headlines — Strategy

This document picks which headline structure to write for a given brand and request. The choice depends on brand type, awareness stage, customer sophistication, dominant emotional driver, and the customer language available.

## What to load before deciding

- **The running headline corpus.** This loads first, before anything else. Pull the ad_title, headline, and text_hook fields from the account's top spenders and top performers. What already wins here is the baseline. The strongest headline you can write is usually an adaptation of a structure this account has already proven, not a new structure. Run `build-headline-baseline.md` to group the corpus into families and read performance against it.
- **The rival headlines captured in the vault.** The claim a competitor cannot make is an angle. A competitor's own framing is something to counter with a spec they lack.
- Brand profile and ICP.
- Voice of customer — exact phrases from reviews, comments, Reddit, TikTok, forums.
- Brand voice rules — what tone is on-brand, what is off-brand, what compliance rails apply.
- The headline's destination — static ad, video text overlay, landing page hero, paid social.

## The voice audit

Run this off the baseline, before writing. The point is to make generated headlines indistinguishable from what the account runs.

- **Read the fingerprint.** Length distribution, casing and punctuation habits, how the brand uses numbers and specifics, which registers it runs. A brand that runs all-caps blunt callouts and a brand that runs sentence-case soft questions are not interchangeable.
- **Name the AI-tells by contrast.** The canonical AI-tells list in `spoken-script-voice.md` applies to headlines, not just scripts — read against it. Then look at what this account's winning corpus never does and forbid that too. Generated headlines reach for a colon-summary cadence, "Discover / Unlock / Elevate" verb openers, invented rule-of-three triads, hedge phrases like "say goodbye to," and the marketing abstraction in place of the named thing. The real corpus is blunter and more specific than that. If a candidate carries a tell, it dies.
- **Read aloud against the corpus.** Put each candidate next to three real winning headlines from the account. If the candidate sounds like a brand and the corpus sounds like a person, rewrite.

## Brand-type decision

This is the first and most important call. Make it deliberately per product, not per brand.

- **Problem/solution brands.** Customer knows they have a problem and is looking for a fix. Headlines can be direct, agitate pain, name the problem, promise the solution. The headline's job is to make someone instantly recognize their problem and believe the product solves it.
- **Lifestyle brands.** Customer is not in pain — they are seeking elevation, connection, self-expression, or identity signaling. The headline's job is to signal identity, create desire, make the right person feel "this is for me."

Some products straddle. A supplement brand might be problem/solution for one product and lifestyle for another. Match the headline approach to the specific product and audience, not just the brand as a whole.

## Awareness stage decision

- **Unaware.** Does not know the problem exists. Headlines need education and curiosity, not solution claims.
- **Problem-aware.** Knows the problem, does not know solutions exist. Headlines name the problem clearly and create curiosity about the fix.
- **Solution-aware.** Has tried other solutions that failed. Headlines acknowledge past failures and differentiate.
- **Most aware.** Knows the product, considering it. Headlines lean on social proof, urgency, direct CTA.

## Sophistication level decision (problem/solution)

- **High sophistication.** Has tried 3+ solutions. Skeptical, burned before. Headlines need to acknowledge past failures, show understanding of the journey, differentiate from what has not worked. Use investment framing, myth-busting, bold contrarian claims, specificity about mechanism.
- **Low sophistication.** Has tried 0-2 things or is just discovering the problem. Needs education and clarity. Use simple problem naming, questions, metaphors, clear benefit statements.

## Emotional depth (problem/solution)

Three levels. Headlines that hit Level 2 or Level 3 outperform Level 1.

- **Level 1 — Surface.** The obvious physical problem.
- **Level 2 — Behavioral.** What the audience cannot do because of the problem.
- **Level 3 — Identity.** How the problem makes them feel about themselves.

If the current headline is stuck at Level 1, push the iteration to Level 2 or 3.

## The idea paths — where the headline comes from

Pick the source before the structure. A headline grounded in something real in this account beats a clever structure built on air. These paths come first.

- **headline-from-winning-headline** — Adapt a structure this account has already proven to a new angle or SKU. The structure holds, the content swaps. This is the default first move whenever the account has a winner that fits the brief.
- **headline-from-review-verbatim** — Lift a customer line as written. The mining method flags lift-ready golden nuggets — phrasing a strategist could not have invented. Use it almost untouched, governed by the brand's claims and voice rails.
- **headline-from-comment-response** — Answer a real comment in the headline. Name the objection in the customer's own words, then resolve it.
- **headline-from-competitor-gap** — Make the claim a rival cannot. Build a spec-specific, receipt-backed counter-headline from the competitor evidence in the vault.
- **headline-from-stat-receipt** — Make a verified number the engine. Review counts, study counts, the receipts the brand under-claims. The no-fabrication wall applies: real numbers only.

## The structural pattern menu

When the idea paths above do not produce the line, or the brief needs a fresh structure, reach for the structural patterns. Each process below holds the writing playbook for one structural pattern. Strategy picks one or more based on the decisions above.

### Patterns that work for both brand types

- **rejection-contrast** — "Forget X," "F*ck the old way," "Stop wasting on Y," "Ditch the cheap version." Especially powerful for lifestyle; works for problem/solution when there is a clear adversary to push against.
- **metaphor-experience** — "Like the gym but for sex," "Your GPS for money," "Comfort in a cup." Makes abstract benefits concrete.
- **emotion-power-word** — Intensify with strong verbs and emotive triggers. "KICKSTART ketosis and SUSTAIN it" over "Exogenous ketones help you ease into keto."

### Lifestyle-only patterns

- **identity-signal** — "Built for real men, not just models," "For people who [behavior]," "PSA: Hairy Girlies." Identity-led tribal signaling.
- **badge-value-status** — "King of the office," "Premium picks," exclusivity and status framing.
- **viral-fomo-appeal** — "Trying the viral X," "Everyone's obsessed," "TikTok made me buy it." Only when real social proof exists.
- **reaction-discovery** — "I almost cried," "Blew my mind," "Wait until you try this." Captures the wow moment.
- **convenience-simplification** — "Goodbye lunch prep," "5-minute makeup routine," "A week of lunches for under $20."

### Problem/solution-only patterns

- **problem-naming-direct** — "Dandruff battle," "Nerve pain killer," direct callout of the problem in the audience's language.
- **question-engagement** — "Burnt out from weekly meal prep?" "Struggling to get it up?" Question forms naming the problem.
- **before-after-contrast** — "12 years of foot pain gone in 5 minutes." Time and outcome contrast.
- **promise-outcome** — "No more burning or tingling," "Last longer without pills." Clear outcome promise.
- **duration-acknowledgment** — "5+ years trying," investment framing. For high-sophistication audiences who have tried everything.
- **authority-proof** — "Doctor-recommended," "#1 thyroid supplement," credentialed validation.
- **alliteration-rhythm** — "Bye Bye Bloating," "Chronically Cold?" Memorability and stickiness.
- **parallel-structure** — "No bloat. No crash. Just clean energy." Rhythm and contrast.

## How to choose the recommended set

Apply these rules in order.

0. **Study the running corpus and pick a baseline family.** Before anything else. If a winning headline in this account fits the brief, the strongest set adapts it. Only invent when the corpus has no fit.
1. **Lock the brand type.** Different patterns are eligible for different brand types.
2. **Filter by awareness stage.** Different stages need different structural approaches.
3. **Filter by sophistication if problem/solution.** High-sophistication audiences need different patterns than low.
4. **Check current emotional depth.** If the existing headline is at Level 1, push to Level 2 or 3.
5. **Check the customer language available.** If no verified customer quotes support a testimonial-style structure, drop that pattern.
6. **Pick 2-5 structurally different headlines.** Never recommend 5 versions of the same structure. The point of multiple recommendations is testing different approaches.
7. **At least one structure should be different from what the brand is currently running.** If iterating on an existing headline.

## The quality checklist

Every headline must pass these before output.

- Does it sound like it belongs in this account's running corpus? Read it next to three real winners. If it sounds like a brand and they sound like a person, it fails.
- Does it carry an AI-tell — colon-summary cadence, "Discover/Unlock/Elevate" opener, invented triad, or a hedge phrase the brand never uses?
- Is it immediately clear what the product is or what problem it solves?
- Does it have a specific ICP and a specific emotion?
- Is it under 10 words?
- Does it use customer language, not marketing voice?
- Does it use a clear identifiable structural pattern?
- Is the structure different from the current ad's structure (if iterating)?
- For lifestyle brands: does it signal identity or tribe?
- For problem/solution brands: does it hit Level 2 or Level 3 emotional depth?
- Does it avoid the testimonial trap?
- Would a fifth grader understand it?

If any fails, rewrite or drop.

## Common mistakes the strategy must avoid

- Writing before studying the running corpus. The baseline is mandatory, not optional.
- Inventing a structure when the account already has a proven one that fits.
- AI-tells: colon-summary cadence, "Discover/Unlock/Elevate" openers, invented rule-of-three triads, "say goodbye to" hedges.
- Recommending the same structural pattern multiple times. Variety is the point.
- Marketing voice instead of customer voice.
- Headlines that work only for warm audiences. Must work cold.
- Headlines stuck at Level 1 emotional depth when Level 2 or 3 is available.
- Testimonial-trap phrasing ("Best ever," "So good," "Worth it").
- Generic headlines that could apply to any brand.
- Fabricated stats or claims.
- Headlines over 10 words.
