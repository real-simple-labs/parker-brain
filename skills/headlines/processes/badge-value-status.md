# Process — Badge Value / Status Headlines (Lifestyle)

Sell what owning the brand says about the buyer. Premium positioning, exclusivity, quality-over-cheap signaling. Activates pride and aspiration.

## Structural patterns

The canonical structures and proven examples for this pattern live under "Badge Value & Status Signals" in `lifestyle-headline-generator.md`. That doc is the source of truth; pull the structures and examples from it. The shapes below orient the execution steps that follow.

- Exclusivity and status — "King of the office."
- Quality over convenience — "Stop wasting money on cheap readers."
- Expert validation — "The #1 supplement recommended by [authority]."
- Social proof as status — "25,000+ 5-Star Reviews."
- "[Adjective] picks" or "[Adjective] for the [type] who [behavior]" — Premium picks for the man who has standards.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** lifestyle brands with premium positioning. Categories where the buyer is signaling taste or status. Audiences for whom price is not the lead constraint. Brands competing on quality.
- **Worst for:** value-positioned brands. Problem/solution brands where pain relief is the lever.

## Execution

1. **Identify what status the buyer wants to signal.** Insider knowledge, refined taste, success, sophistication, rebellion against mediocrity.
2. **Pick the badge structure.** Exclusivity vs quality-rejection vs expert validation vs social proof.
3. **Use precise language that signals status without bragging.** Premium-feeling brands do not yell about being premium; they assume it.
4. **Under 10 words.**

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The status being signaled.
- The aspiration the buyer responds to.
- Why this fits the brand's price point and positioning.

## What never to do

- Status headlines on value-positioned brands. The signal mismatches.
- Self-congratulatory language. Status is implied, not declared.
- Fabricated "#1" claims or invented ratings. Compliance and credibility risk.
