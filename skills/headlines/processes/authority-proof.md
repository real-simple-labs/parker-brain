# Process — Authority / Proof Headlines (Problem/Solution)

Lean on credentialed validation, expert endorsement, or specific rating claims. Trust transfer from a recognized authority to the product.

## Structural patterns

The canonical structures and proven examples for this pattern live in `problem-solution-headline-writer.md` (the supplement, makeup, and social-proof good examples) and under "Badge Value & Status Signals" in `lifestyle-headline-generator.md`. Those docs are the source of truth; pull the structures and examples from the one matching the brand's positioning. The shapes below orient the execution steps that follow.

- "[Number] [Authority]-recommended [product]" — The #1 Thyroid Supplement recommended by natural health practitioners.
- "Doctor-recommended" / "Clinically proven" — Clinically proven sweat-proof foundation.
- "[Authority Body]'s pick for [category]."
- "[Number]+ [Verified credential]" — 25,000+ 5-Star Reviews.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** trust-sensitive categories — health, finance, beauty, supplements. High-sophistication markets. Premium products. Categories with many competing claims where the audience needs a tiebreaker.
- **Worst for:** brands without real credentials or earned validation. Audiences who distrust experts or institutions. Categories where the buyer wants authenticity over authority.

## Execution

1. **Verify the credential.** "#1 X" claims, "clinically proven" claims, "doctor-recommended" claims all have legal and compliance constraints. Source must be real.
2. **Pick the proof structure.** Number-led, credential-led, ratings-led, or institutional-led.
3. **Be specific.** "Clinically proven" lands harder than "expert-approved." "25,000+ five-star reviews" lands harder than "thousands of reviews."
4. **Under 10 words.**

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The verified credential or rating with source.
- Compliance flags.
- The ICP and the trust gap being closed.

## What never to do

- Fabricate credentials, ratings, or institutional endorsements.
- Use "#1" without a defensible source. Compliance risk.
- Vague authority claims like "experts agree." Specificity is the lever.
- Authority headlines on brands or audiences that distrust experts.
