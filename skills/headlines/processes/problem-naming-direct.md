# Process — Problem-Naming / Direct Headlines (Problem/Solution)

Direct callout of the problem in the customer's actual language. Names the specific pain so the right buyer instantly recognizes themselves.

## Structural patterns

The canonical structures and proven examples for this pattern live under "Set Up The Problem" in `problem-solution-headline-writer.md`. That doc is the source of truth; pull the structures and examples from it. The shapes below orient the execution steps that follow.

- Direct problem name — "Nerve Pain Killer," "End Dandruff Forever."
- Audience label — "PSA: Hairy Girlies," "Chronically Cold?"
- Diagnostic callout — "Seborrheic dermatitis? We got you."

## When to pick

- **Best for:** problem/solution brands with audiences who already know they have the problem. Solution-aware and problem-aware stages. Categories where the specific problem has clear language attached.
- **Worst for:** unaware audiences who do not yet know they have the problem. Lifestyle brands. Categories where the problem language feels clinical or off-brand.

## Execution

1. **Pull the problem language from real reviews.** What words do customers actually use to describe the problem? Their words, not marketing language.
2. **Pick the structural shape.** Direct name vs audience label vs diagnostic callout.
3. **Hit Level 2 or Level 3 emotional depth.** Surface-level problem naming ("dandruff") is okay; behavioral or identity-level lands harder ("dandruff battle," "old man jeans").
4. **Under 10 words.**

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The customer language source.
- The emotional depth level reached.
- The ICP and the specific pain.

## What never to do

- Use clinical or marketing language for the problem.
- Stay at Level 1 emotional depth when the VoC supports going deeper.
- Direct problem naming on lifestyle brands where pain is not the lever.
- Headlines that the audience would not actually use to describe their own situation.
