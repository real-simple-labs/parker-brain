# Process — Question / Engagement Headlines (Problem/Solution)

Question form that names the problem and forces the audience to internally answer. The open loop demands closure.

## Structural patterns

The canonical structures and proven examples for this pattern live under "Set Up The Problem" (the ask-a-question move) in `problem-solution-headline-writer.md`. That doc is the source of truth; pull the structures and examples from it. The shapes below orient the execution steps that follow.

- "Struggling with [X]?"
- "Burnt out from [chore]?"
- "Do you have [problem]?"
- "[Specific symptom]?" — Tight socks cutting off circulation?
- "Why does [thing] always [bad outcome]?" — Why do my jeans always sag at the butt?

## When to pick

- **Best for:** problem-aware audiences. Categories where the problem can be named in a question form that the audience instinctively answers yes to. Both static and video text overlay placements.
- **Worst for:** unaware audiences (the question goes unanswered because the problem is not on their radar). Lifestyle brands where pain is not the lever.

## Execution

1. **Pull the specific symptom or pain from VoC.** The question must name something the audience actively experiences.
2. **Phrase as the audience would internally describe it.** Customer language, not clinical.
3. **The headline forces an internal yes.** If the answer is ambiguous, the question is too broad.
4. **Under 10 words.**

## Output content

- The exact headline question.
- The pain or symptom being named.
- The customer language source.
- The ICP who would internally answer yes.

## What never to do

- Vague questions. "Do you want healthier skin?" wastes the format.
- Yes/no questions where the answer is obvious in either direction.
- Questions the audience would not understand because the language is clinical.
- Multiple questions in one headline. One per headline.
