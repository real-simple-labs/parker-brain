# Process — Reaction / Discovery Headlines (Lifestyle)

Capture the "wow" or surprise moment. Headlines that read as the audience's genuine reaction create curiosity (what triggered this?) and social proof (if they reacted that strongly, it must be good).

## Structural patterns

The canonical structures and proven examples for this pattern live under "Reaction & Discovery Moments" in `lifestyle-headline-generator.md`. That doc is the source of truth; pull the structures and examples from it. The shapes below orient the execution steps that follow.

- "I wasn't expecting [this]."
- "Wait until you [try/see/taste] this."
- "This is [reaction word]" — insane, incredible, wild, unreal.
- "[X] blew my mind."
- "I can't believe [surprising element]."
- "They taste so good I almost cried."

## When to pick

- **Best for:** food products with exceptional taste. Beauty products with dramatic results. Products that exceed expectations. Anything with sensory impact. Lifestyle brands. Categories where the discovery moment is the asset.
- **Worst for:** problem/solution brands where pain is the lever. Premium brands where over-the-top reactions read as off-brand. Categories where the audience expects restraint.

## Execution

1. **Source the reaction from real reviews.** Pull verbatim or lightly cleaned phrases customers have used.
2. **Pick the structural shape.** Reaction word vs surprise reveal vs sensory description.
3. **Pair with visible reaction in the creative, grounded.** Reaction headlines land hardest when the visual matches. Source the paired visual from the brand's in-play shots in `visual-vocabulary.md` per `visual-vocabulary-method.md`; flag a reaction shot the brand has not actually filmed as out-of-play rather than directing invented stock.
4. **Under 10 words.**

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The source review or comment.
- The sensory or emotional reaction being captured.
- Why this fits the brand's positioning.

## What never to do

- Fabricated reactions. Source from real reviews.
- Reaction headlines on premium or restrained brands.
- Over-the-top reaction language that strains credibility.
