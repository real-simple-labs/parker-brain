# Process — Alliteration / Rhythm Headlines

Repeating consonant sounds or rhythmic phrasing for memorability and stickiness. The brain holds onto rhythmic phrases longer than flat ones.

## Structural patterns

The canonical structures and proven examples for this pattern live under "Proven Word Structures, Formats And Rhythms" in both `lifestyle-headline-generator.md` and `problem-solution-headline-writer.md`. Those docs are the source of truth; pull the structures and examples from the one matching the brand's positioning. The shapes below orient the execution steps that follow.

- Alliteration — Bye Bye Bloating. Chronically Cold? Crushed Nuts.
- Rhythmic short sentences — This is a meal. Not a protein shake.
- Repetition of stressed syllables — Soft, soft, so soft.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** memorability moments. Brand-building headlines that need to stick. Both brand types when the brand voice supports playful or rhythmic phrasing.
- **Worst for:** premium or restrained brands where rhythmic language reads as gimmicky. Categories where clarity matters more than memorability.

## Execution

1. **Identify the sound pattern.** Two or three words sharing a consonant or rhythm.
2. **Use sparingly within the headline.** One alliteration anchor per headline. More becomes a tongue-twister.
3. **Test the read-aloud.** If saying it out loud feels good, it will land. If it feels forced, drop it.
4. **Under 10 words.**

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The sound pattern doing the work.
- Why this fits the brand voice.

## What never to do

- Forced alliteration that strains the meaning.
- More than one rhythmic device per headline.
- Alliteration on brands whose voice is restrained.
