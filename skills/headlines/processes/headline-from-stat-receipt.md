# Process — Headline From a Stat Receipt (Verified Numbers as the Engine)

Make a verified number the headline's engine. Review counts, study counts, time-to-result, the receipts the brand has earned and tends to under-claim. A specific number stops a scroller and de-risks the purchase in one move. Some brands ride "30k+ 5-Star Reviews" across nearly every top spender — the social-proof receipt is the account's most-funded title. The no-fabrication wall governs this entire process: real numbers only, no exceptions.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** brands with real, strong receipts — high review counts, clinical studies, professional endorsements, time-bound results. Most-aware and social-proof stages where a number reduces risk. Brands that bury their best numbers in body copy instead of leading with them.
- **Worst for:** brands without earned receipts, or where the strongest number is weak. A small or soft number undercuts more than it helps.

## Find the under-claimed receipt

Brands routinely under-claim. Look for the strongest verified number the account is not leading with: a review count buried in the footer, a "7.5k health and hair care professionals" trust figure tucked in body copy, a 3-to-6-month result window stated as fine print. Pull it to the front and let it carry the headline.

## Execution

1. **Pull the real number.** From the brand context, customer reviews, verified studies, or data the user has provided. Never from a sense of what "sounds right."
2. **Lead with the specific figure.** "35k+", "7.5k professionals", "3-6 months." Specific beats round; an exact number reads as real and a suspiciously round one reads as marketing.
3. **Attach the number to the outcome.** A number alone is trivia. "35k+ 5-Star Reviews" works because the count implies a verdict. Tie the figure to what it proves.
4. **Keep it under 10 words and let the number do the work.** No power-word padding around a strong stat.
5. **Mark any gap.** If the headline needs a number the verified sources do not contain, write `[STAT NEEDED — verify before publishing]` rather than inventing one.

## The no-fabrication wall

Every figure traces to a verified source: brand context, real reviews, real studies, or user-provided data. You are never allowed to invent a statistic, round one up, or estimate a plausible figure, even if it would make the headline stronger. A fake number in a published ad is a legal and credibility risk. Leave the gap flagged rather than fill it with fiction.

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The number and its verified source.
- The outcome the number proves.
- The ICP and emotion.

## What never to do

- Invent, round up, or estimate a figure. The wall is absolute.
- Lead with a weak number that undercuts the claim.
- State a number with no outcome attached.
- Pad a strong stat with power words it does not need.
