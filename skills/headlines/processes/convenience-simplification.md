# Process — Convenience / Simplification Headlines (Lifestyle)

Make life easier, save time, simplify a task. Activates the convenience and simplification emotional driver. Often the strongest lever for busy audiences.

## Structural patterns

The canonical structures and proven examples for this pattern live under "Convenience & Simplification" in `lifestyle-headline-generator.md`. That doc is the source of truth; pull the structures and examples from it. The shapes below orient the execution steps that follow.

- "Goodbye [chore]" — Goodbye lunch prep.
- "The [time] [task]" — The 5-minute makeup routine.
- "[Outcome] for [time/effort]" — A week of lunches for under $20.
- "Don't change your life — your [routine] is enough."
- "[Outcome] in [unexpected timeframe]" — Video content done in minutes.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** busy audiences. Categories where the buyer wants to remove friction or save time. Products that simplify a process. Lifestyle brands targeting working parents, professionals, time-strapped buyers.
- **Worst for:** categories where convenience is the default and the buyer wants premium or transformative claims. Problem/solution brands where convenience does not address the pain.

## Execution

1. **Identify the friction or chore being removed.** What is the buyer tired of doing?
2. **Quantify the convenience.** Specific timeframes outperform vague claims. "5 minutes" lands; "fast" does not.
3. **Pick the structural pattern.** Goodbye-chore vs time-task vs outcome-in-timeframe.
4. **Match brand voice.** Some brands are warm and conversational ("Goodbye lunch prep"); others are precise and quantified ("A week of meals for under $20").

## Output content

- The exact headline.
- The friction being removed.
- The time or effort savings being claimed.
- Why this fits the audience's daily reality.

## What never to do

- Vague convenience claims. Quantify.
- Convenience headlines on premium brands where the buyer wants quality, not speed.
- Promise time savings the product cannot deliver.
