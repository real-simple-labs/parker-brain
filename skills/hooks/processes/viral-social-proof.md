# Process — Viral / Social Proof Hooks

Tap into existing virality or the perception of viral popularity. Reference trending status, millions of users, social media fame, or cultural moment to leverage social proof and FOMO.

## Psychological framework

- **Social proof** — if millions use it, it must be good.
- **FOMO** — do not want to miss what is popular.
- **Bandwagon effect** — want to be part of the trend.
- **Risk reduction** — popularity equals less risk in trying.
- **Status and belonging** — using viral products signals being in the know.

## Required inputs

- Genuine social proof to back the claim. Real review volume, real virality, real category momentum. Never claim virality that does not exist.
- Specific numbers if available — "2.5 million people," "10M views," "25,000+ five-star reviews."
- Platform reference if applicable — "TikTok made me buy it," "went viral on Instagram."

## When to pick

- **Best for:** products with any level of real buzz. Beauty, wellness, fashion, skincare. Categories where social proof drives demand. Retargeting (builds on existing awareness). Creating bandwagon effect. Impulse purchase categories.
- **Worst for:** brands without real social proof to point to. Premium brands where mass adoption would undermine exclusivity positioning. Categories where virality reads as gimmicky.

## Execution

1. **Source real social proof.** Review volume, follower counts, view counts, platform momentum. Specific verified numbers.
2. **Pick the framing.** "I finally tried the viral X" (discovery), "X people can't be wrong" (mass validation), "TikTok made me buy it" (cultural relevance), "Everyone's talking about Y" (popularity).
3. **Pair with reaction or unboxing content.** Viral product hooks land harder when paired with UGC-style reaction footage.
4. **If not viral yet, frame around category virality.** "I tried the trending [category]" works when the specific product is not yet viral but the category is hot.

## Output content

- The exact viral framing line.
- The real social proof being cited.
- The visual approach — unboxing, reaction, before-after.
- The ICP and the FOMO/curiosity being activated.
- The proven-pattern note.
- Compliance flag if any claim needs verification.

## What never to do

- Claim virality that does not exist. Easy to spot, kills trust.
- Use round numbers that read as invented. "Over 2.5 million" reads real; "millions and millions" reads invented.
- Skip the real social proof. The hook needs proof to land.
- Use this hook on premium brands where exclusivity is the positioning.
