# Process — Scam / Trust-Flip Hooks

Open with a visceral trigger word — typically "scam" — to create immediate tension, then resolve it by explaining why it is not a scam or by flipping skepticism into endorsement.

## Psychological framework

- **Loss aversion** — triggers fear of being cheated or taken advantage of.
- **Curiosity gap** — "I thought this was a scam" creates unresolved tension that demands resolution.
- **Negative bias** — brains prioritize potential threats over neutral information.
- **Pattern interrupt** — an unexpected admission stops the scroll.

## Required inputs

- Brand voice — does the brand have permission to use the word "scam" or similar visceral language? Some premium brands do not.
- Compliance check — platform compliance rules around the word "scam" and adjacent terms.
- A specific reason the audience would have been skeptical — pulled from reviews, comments, or persona work.

## When to pick

- **Best for:** categories with trust issues or perceived scams. High-ticket items where skepticism is high. New-to-market products that seem "too good to be true." Industries with many competitors making false promises. Retargeting skeptical audiences.
- **Worst for:** premium brands where the word "scam" would read as off-brand. Categories without genuine skepticism — using "scam" framing where it does not match the audience feels manufactured.

## Execution

1. **Frame the tension.** "I thought this was a scam" or "This looks like a scam but..." in voiceover or as text overlay. The word "scam" is one of the most powerful trigger words currently available.
2. **Resolve quickly.** The tension cannot sit too long. Within 3-5 seconds, the resolution starts — proof, testimonial, demonstration, credential, result.
3. **Pair with proof.** The resolution lands harder when paired with visible proof — before-and-after, testimonial overlay, result reveal, expert credential.
4. **Lock the visual.** Creator with skeptical expression initially. Visual cuts to product use or result. Face shifts from skeptical to surprised or pleased. Trust-building overlays appear during the resolution.

## Output content

- The exact opening hook line with the trigger word.
- The resolution path — what proves it is not a scam.
- The visual sequence — initial skepticism, resolution, proof.
- The ICP and the specific skepticism this hook is targeting.
- The proven-pattern note.
- Compliance flag if any.

## What never to do

- Use "scam" on a brand where it would read as off-brand.
- Leave the tension unresolved. The skepticism must flip.
- Use the framing without genuine proof to resolve it.
- Imply that the brand itself is scamming. Platform compliance can flag this.
- Use this hook on a category where the audience is enthusiastic rather than skeptical — it manufactures a problem the audience does not have.
