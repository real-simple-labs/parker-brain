# Process — Write Style-Based Script

Script written in a specific named style. Pick when the user names a style or when the brand's voice strongly favors a single mode.

## Style menu

Each style has its own tone, structure, and emotional signature.

### Humorous
Self-aware, playful, satirical. Pokes at the category, the customer's own behavior, or the absurdities of the problem. Works for lifestyle brands with permissive voice and audiences that respond to humor (Millennial-skewed especially).

### Testimonial
Customer voice front and center. The script reads as if the customer is recommending the product to a friend. Works for trust-sensitive categories and any brand with rich VoC.

### Demonstration
Show, do not tell. Product in use, results visible, mechanism demonstrated on camera. Works for products with strong visual proof — beauty, food, fitness, household.

### Educational
Teach a concept, reveal an insight, explain a mechanism. The product extends naturally from the teaching. Works for complex products, low-sophistication markets, brand authority building.

### FUD (Fear-Uncertainty-Doubt)
Lean into fear of consequences. What the audience risks by not acting. Works for safety, health, and security categories where consequences are real. Compliance-sensitive — never manufacture fear without a real risk.

### Comparison
Side-by-side against competitors or the generic category. Works for crowded markets and products with measurable advantages.

### Storytelling
Narrative arc with character and journey. Covered in its own process — write-storytelling-script.

### Lifestyle
Aspirational scenes showing the product in the customer's ideal life. Works for lifestyle brands where the buyer is purchasing identity or experience.

## Execution

1. **Confirm the style fits the brand voice.** Humor on a restrained brand fails. FUD on a snack brand is nonsensical.
2. **Use the style consistently throughout the script.** A humorous opener that gives way to a flat pitch breaks the style.
3. **Match the style to the audience.** Gen Z responds to humor and FUD differently than Boomers do.
4. **Follow the style's structural conventions.**
   - Humorous → setup-and-punchline beat structure.
   - Testimonial → customer voice throughout, minimal narrator.
   - Demonstration → visual-first, voiceover supports.
   - Educational → teach-then-introduce-product.
   - FUD → consequence-led opening, resolution close.
   - Comparison → split-screen or sequential side-by-side.
   - Lifestyle → scene-driven, aspirational visuals dominant.

## Output content

- The full script with timing.
- Visual cues per beat.
- The style chosen with rationale.
- How the style's structural conventions are reflected in the script.

## What never to do

- Mix styles within a single script. Pick one and commit.
- Use a style the brand voice does not support.
- Use FUD without a real risk to back it up.
- Demonstration script for products without strong visual proof.
