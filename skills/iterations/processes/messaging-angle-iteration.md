# Process — Messaging / Angle Iteration

Keep visuals and structure mostly intact; rewrite the script around a different angle, awareness level, or framing. Messaging is the quietest lever on the signal-change ladder, so this iteration is appropriate when the visuals work but the message is the bottleneck, when a new awareness stage needs addressing, or when a different framing of the same product unlocks a different audience.

If the message is already converting, do not propose a messaging iteration as the first move. Protect what works.

## Required inputs

- The winning ad and its performance data.
- The current script verbatim with beats and timing.
- The current angle, awareness level, and emotional driver — diagnose all three before iterating.
- Brand context: ICP, personas, voice of customer, compliance, brand voice rules.

## Decision: which messaging move

The messaging menu, with when to pick each:

- **New messaging angle.** Shift the emotional or motivational lens — for example from convenience to status, or from fear to savings. Use when the current angle has fatigued or has not been tested against the audience's other dominant motivations. Medium effort, high reward.

- **Awareness level shift.** Rework the ad for a different funnel stage. Add education for top-of-funnel, urgency for bottom-of-funnel. Use when the current ad is only converting one stage and the brand needs broader reach. Medium effort, medium reward.

- **Problem agitation.** Deepen emotional resonance by intensifying or reframing the pain point. Use only for brands with genuine emotional pain points — nerve pain, anxiety, sleep loss, financial stress. Never agitate problems on brands without real emotional weight. Medium effort, high reward.

- **Problem swap.** Target a completely different problem the product also solves. Same product, new entry point. Use when the product has multiple legitimate use cases the brand has not yet pulled forward. Medium effort, high reward.

- **Simplification pass.** Strip the script to its most essential, shortest version. Cut every sentence that does not move toward the sale. Use when the current ad has filler or hedging. Low effort, medium reward.

- **Story arc.** Restructure as a mini narrative — problem, discovery, transformation. Use when the brand benefits from emotional investment and the audience is unaware or problem-aware. Medium effort, high reward.

- **Listicle structure.** Reformat as "three reasons" or "five things I wish I knew." Use when the brand voice supports it and the message has multiple discrete points. Low effort, medium reward.

- **Comparison angle.** Position against a competitor, an alternative, or "the old way." Use when there is a clear adversary or category norm to push against. Medium effort, medium reward.

- **Identity messaging.** Shift to "this is for people who" framing to create tribal belonging. Use when the brand is identity-led or the audience is tribal. Medium effort, high reward.

- **Negative vs positive framing.** Test loss-aversion framing against gain framing for the same benefit. Same fact, different psychological trigger. Low effort, medium reward.

## Pre-check

- Is the message actually the bottleneck? Confirm via data (low CvR with strong CTR is a message problem; low CTR is a hook problem, not a message problem).
- Has the brand tested the proposed angle before? If yes, what did the prior result say?
- Does the brand context support the angle? Some angles are compliance no-gos.
- Is the new angle being targeted at the same audience or a deliberately different one? Be explicit.

## Execution

1. **State the current diagnosis.** What angle is the current ad running? What awareness level is it serving? What emotional driver is doing the work? Be specific.

2. **State the target.** Which messaging move from the menu. Which target angle, target awareness level, or target framing.

3. **Hold visuals constant where possible.** The point of the iteration is to isolate the message variable. If a small visual change is required to support the new message (a single b-roll swap, a different setting), call it out as scope.

4. **Rewrite the script in full.** Every beat, every line. The original script's structure can be preserved if it supports the new message, but the words change.

5. **Source the language from voice of customer.** If the brand has VoC data, the new message must use the actual language of the target audience. Do not write in marketing voice.

6. **Specify the CTA.** Match the CTA to the new framing. A status-led ad and a relief-led ad call for different CTAs even if the product is the same.

## Output content for this iteration

- A name that signals the move. Example pattern: "Angle Swap — From Convenience to Status Framing."
- The current angle and awareness level diagnosed from the source ad.
- The target angle and awareness level.
- The new script in full, beat by beat with timing.
- The visual constants and any minimal visual adjustments.
- The new CTA.
- The proven-pattern note tied to the framing.

## What never to do

- Never propose a messaging iteration when the message is converting.
- Never agitate problems on brands without real emotional pain. Problem agitation on a snack brand is nonsensical.
- Never change angle and structure and CTA all at once. Isolate the variable.
- Never use marketing voice. Customer language only, sourced from VoC.
