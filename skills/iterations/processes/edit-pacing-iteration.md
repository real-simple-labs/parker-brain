# Process — Edit and Pacing Iteration

Low-risk edit-level refreshes on a winning ad. This process covers the quieter signal-change levers — pacing, b-roll, captions, music, sound effects, length, headline overlays, press mentions. These iterations are appropriate when the brand needs a quick refresh on a long-running winner, when a mid-ad drop-off appears to be a pacing problem, or as a low-effort complement to bigger swings in the same recommendation set.

These iterations rarely break a real plateau on their own. Use them to refresh, complement, or fine-tune — not to fix a fundamental performance problem.

## Required inputs

- The winning ad and its performance data — especially hold rate, drop-off curves, and frequency.
- The current edit details — pacing, cut frequency, captions style, music, sound effects, length, on-screen text.
- Brand context including the brand's edit aesthetic and any non-negotiable visual standards.

## Decision: which edit move

The edit and pacing menu, with when to pick each:

### Pacing

- **Cold open.** Cut the intro entirely; drop the viewer into the most compelling moment. Use when the intro loses viewers and the strong content starts too late. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Pacing acceleration.** Tighten cuts, speed up delivery, remove pauses. Use for younger audiences or when the ad feels slow and mid-ad drop-off is heavy. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Pacing deceleration.** Slow the ad down, add beats, let key moments breathe. Use for older audiences or when key messages are going by too fast to land. Low effort, medium reward.

### Length

- **Length trim.** Cut the ad to a shorter version to improve hold rate or fit new placements. Use when the hook is strong but hold rate drops off midway. Test multiple lengths — 60s, 30s, 15s, 6s. Medium effort, medium reward.
- **Length extension.** Expand a short winner with more proof, story, or detail. Use when a short ad is winning and the brand wants deeper engagement and has additional proof to add. High effort, medium reward.
- **Product introduction — earlier.** Move the product introduction closer to the hook. Use when there is filler or low-value content in the first ten to fifteen seconds.
- **Problem agitation length adjustment.** Test different lengths of time spent on the pain point before the product enters. Extend when the audience needs to feel the pain more deeply. Cut when the pain section drags. Only for brands with genuine emotional pain points.
- **Stack winning ads.** Remove the CTA from one winning ad and stack it onto the end of another. Creates longer-form content from proven elements. Only when multiple winners exist. Medium effort, high reward.

### B-roll and visuals

- **B-roll refresh.** Replace supporting footage while keeping script and structure intact. Match the b-roll to the target archetype. Medium effort, medium reward.
- **Split screen.** Show before/after, comparison, or simultaneous perspectives. Works well for podcast-clip-style ads (podcast on one side, b-roll on the other). Low effort, medium reward.

### Captions and text

- **Caption style variation.** Change subtitle font, size, color, position, or animation. Test bold-centered vs bottom bar, or test removing captions entirely for an organic feel. Low effort, medium reward.
- **No captions vs full captions.** Test the ad with and without subtitles entirely. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Burned-in headline.** Add a persistent text bar at the top of frame with the core claim. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Text hook overlay.** Add or change bold text overlay in the first frame. Low effort, medium reward.

### Audio

- **Music or SFX swap.** Change the background track or sound effects. Low effort, low reward. Mainly a refresh lever for a long-running ad.
- **Music removal.** Strip all background music for a raw, intimate feel. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Sound effect layering.** Add satisfying sounds at key moments — clicks, pops, whooshes, dings. Low effort, medium reward.
- **ASMR treatment.** Add close-mic, tactile audio to product interaction shots. Low effort, medium reward.
- **Voiceover pacing.** Speed up or slow down the voiceover delivery. Low effort, low reward.

### Credibility

- **Press mention.** Add media coverage logos or references as credibility markers. Low effort, medium reward. Only use real, earned mentions — never fabricate.

## Pre-check

- Is the edit-level lever actually addressing the diagnosis, or is it being added because it is easy? Easy is fine as a complement to a bigger swing; easy is not fine as the only iteration when the brand needs a meaningful test.
- Has the brand already tested the proposed edit move? If yes, skip.
- Does the proposed move fit the brand's aesthetic? An ASMR treatment on a luxury brand can read as off-brand; a pacing acceleration on a meditative wellness brand can read as wrong.

## Execution

1. **State the specific edit move from the menu.** "Edit iteration" alone is not a recommendation. Name the specific move.

2. **Specify what is changing, exactly.** For pacing, name the new cut frequency. For length, name the new total length and which sections are being cut or added. For captions, name the new font, size, color, position. For audio, name the new track, the new sound effects, or the removal.

3. **Specify what is constant.** Everything else.

4. **Note the diagnosis the iteration addresses.** Mid-ad drop-off, fatigue refresh, older audience pacing, complement to a bigger swing. Be explicit.

## Output content for this iteration

- A name that signals the move. Example pattern: "Pacing Iteration — Accelerate Cuts to Three-Second Average."
- The specific edit move.
- The exact changes — new pacing, new length, new caption spec, new audio.
- The unchanged elements.
- The diagnosis this iteration addresses.
- The proven-pattern note tied to the chosen move.

## What never to do

- Never propose an edit iteration as the only iteration when the diagnosis calls for a bigger swing.
- Never use fabricated press mentions or credibility markers.
- Never combine multiple edit moves in a single iteration recommendation. Each move is its own variable. If a recommendation needs both a pacing change and a music change, those are two iterations.
- Never let an edit iteration drift the message or structure. This process is about polish and pacing, not message change.
