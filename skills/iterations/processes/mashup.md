# Process — Multi-Ad Mashup

Combine the best moments from multiple winning ads into a single compilation. Stack them back-to-back, strip the individual CTAs so the cuts flow, and let the pace changes and stacked social proof do the work. This iteration is only available when multiple winners exist — it is not a way to rescue a single struggling ad.

## Required inputs

- Two or more winning ads from the same brand with performance data.
- The exact best moments from each — by timestamp, with what is said and shown noted verbatim.
- The brand's tone and voice consistency rules. The mashup must read as cohesive even though the source moments come from different shoots.

## Pre-check

- Do at least two genuine winners exist? If only one ad is winning, this process is not viable.
- Do the moments flow energetically? Stacking a quiet emotional moment immediately into a high-energy reaction can work or jar — diagnose which.
- Does the brand context support a fast-paced compilation aesthetic? Some brands are too premium for the cut-heavy mashup energy.
- Is there a CTA strategy for the end? The mashup needs one strong closing CTA, not multiple competing ones.

## Decision: VO mashup or visual mashup

- **Visual mashup** — Cut between the best visual moments across the source ads, keeping each clip's native audio. Strongest when the visuals are dramatically different or when the visual moments carry their own social-proof energy.
- **VO mashup** — Remix voiceover lines from multiple winners over fresh or recycled visuals. Strongest when the spoken claims are the working element and the visuals are interchangeable.

Default to visual mashup unless the VO is clearly the strongest carried-over element.

## Execution

1. **Select the moments.** For each source ad, pick one or two beats — typically two-to-five seconds each — that capture what makes that ad work. Be forensically specific. Timestamp, dialogue, visual, on-screen text.

2. **Order them for narrative or energetic flow.** Either build energy across the cuts (start grounded, escalate to peak reaction) or alternate beats for variety. Do not just stack winners randomly — the order matters.

3. **Specify the transitions.** Hard cuts work for high energy. Brief fades or whip pans can soften jarring tonal shifts. Whatever the transition style, specify it per cut.

4. **Strip individual CTAs.** Each source ad has its own CTA. In the mashup, those CTAs are removed so the cuts flow without breaking momentum.

5. **Specify the new unified CTA at the end.** One CTA, placed after the final mashup beat, that fits all the source ads' offers. If the source ads have meaningfully different CTAs, pick the strongest or write a synthesizing one.

6. **Audio strategy.** Decide whether each clip keeps its native audio, whether a unifying music bed runs underneath, or whether a single voiceover ties the clips together. Be explicit.

7. **Total length.** Mashups can run longer than the source ads because the pace changes hold attention. Specify the target length and any version cuts (a 60-second version and a 30-second version, for example).

## Output content for this iteration

- A name that signals the mashup composition. Example pattern: "Mashup — [Ad A Moment] + [Ad B Moment] + [Ad C Moment]."
- Each source moment described exactly — source ad, timestamp, dialogue, visual, on-screen text.
- The order, with the rationale for the sequence.
- The transition style per cut.
- The unified CTA at the end.
- The audio strategy.
- The target length and any version cuts.
- The proven-pattern note: every component is pre-validated by its source ad's performance; the mashup combines proven elements with added social proof and pace variety.

## What never to do

- Never run a mashup with only one winner. Need at least two.
- Never include filler moments from non-winners just to extend the length.
- Never stack moments whose energies clash without bridging them. The flow has to read as intentional.
- Never leave multiple CTAs in. One closing CTA only.
- Never let the mashup drift from the brand's tone. Compilation energy can read as low-effort or low-quality if the brand voice does not support it.
