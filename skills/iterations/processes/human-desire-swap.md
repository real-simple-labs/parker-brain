# Process — Human Desire Swap

Identify the human desire the winning ad is currently activating and rebuild the same product, format, and structure around a different desire. This is the only high-confidence iteration that targets the emotional engine underneath the ad rather than a structural or visual element, which makes it a strong complement to format and creator iterations.

Steven Reiss identified 16 fundamental desires that drive human behavior, and `iterations.md` names this as the engine the swap targets. In advertising, the ones that hit hardest are beauty, romance, power, family, status, belonging, curiosity, and FOMO — though any of the sixteen can be the working engine of an ad. The point is not the taxonomy; it is recognizing that every working ad is activating one dominant desire, and that swapping which desire is being activated almost always extends performance and expands reach.

The desire the ad activates is the same thing `killer-performance-ads.md` frames as the emotion or mindstate the ad targets — its Life Force 8 and the Mindstates list are two named vocabularies for the same engine. When you diagnose the current desire below, cross-check it against whichever of those frameworks the brand foundation already speaks, so the swap connects to the canonical emotional read rather than a parallel one.

## Required inputs

- The winning ad and its performance data.
- The brand's ICP, personas, and voice of customer — what the audience actually wants and the language they use to express it.
- Brand context, especially the brand's positioning, what desires it has historically activated, and what it has not yet tested.

## Pre-check

- Can you clearly identify the dominant desire the current ad is activating? If you cannot name it, that is itself an iteration opportunity — but treat that as a different problem than a swap. Pick a desire to lean into deliberately on the next iteration rather than swapping a desire you have not actually identified.
- Has the brand built its entire positioning around the current desire? If yes, a swap risks brand drift. Flag it.
- Does the brand have audience data showing which desires resonate beyond the current one? If not, the swap is more exploratory and should be framed as such.

## Identifying the current desire

Look at the ad as a viewer. Ask which of these the ad is primarily activating:

- **Beauty** — the desire to be attractive, polished, admired for appearance.
- **Romance** — the desire to be desired, to attract a partner, to feel sexual confidence.
- **Power** — the desire to be in control, to dominate, to outperform.
- **Family** — the desire to nurture, protect, provide for loved ones.
- **Status** — the desire to be admired, respected, perceived as elite or successful.
- **Belonging** — the desire to be part of a group, to be accepted, to fit in.
- **Curiosity** — the desire to know, to learn, to satisfy a knowledge gap.
- **FOMO and viral appeal** — the desire to not be left behind, to be part of what is happening.
- **Security** — the desire for safety, control, predictability.
- **Achievement** — the desire to accomplish, to grow, to win.
- **Relief** — the desire to remove a pain, a frustration, a problem.
- **Convenience** — the desire to save time, simplify, reduce effort.
- **Pride** — the desire to feel good about oneself, one's choices, one's taste.
- **Joy and surprise** — the desire for delight, novelty, fun.
- **Nostalgia** — the desire for connection to the past, family, tradition.
- **Independence** — the desire for autonomy, self-direction.

The dominant desire is the one that, if you removed it, the ad would no longer work. If two desires are co-dominant, the ad is either unusually strong because of the dual engine or unclear because of the dilution. Diagnose which.

## Choosing the target desire

The target desire is the one you swap to. Choose deliberately:

- The target desire should be available to the brand — does not violate positioning, voice, or compliance.
- The target desire should be plausibly held by the same audience or by an adjacent audience the brand wants to reach.
- The target desire should not be one the brand has already tested unsuccessfully with this product or this format.
- Prefer a target desire that creates the largest emotional distance from the current one. Swapping convenience to status is a bigger move than swapping convenience to security. Big moves give clearer learnings.

## Execution

1. **Name both desires explicitly.** State the current dominant desire and the target desire so the swap is visible to the team.

2. **Hold the product, format, and structure constant.** This is the discipline of the iteration. The product is the same product. If the source ad is a 30-second video with a hook-body-CTA structure and a creator delivery, the new iteration has the same shape.

3. **Rewrite the language to express the target desire.** Every line of script or copy that was carrying the current desire gets rewritten to carry the new one. Source the new language from voice-of-customer data for the target audience holding the target desire.

4. **Adjust the visual cues just enough to support the new desire.** If the swap is convenience to status, the visual cues might shift from a busy-mom setting to a polished aspirational setting. But do not redo the format — only the cues. The structural beats stay the same.

5. **Keep the CTA structurally similar.** The CTA can match the new desire energetically but should not move to a fundamentally different ask.

## Output content for this iteration

- A name that names both desires. Example pattern: "Desire Swap — From [Current Desire] to [Target Desire]."
- A one-sentence diagnosis of what desire the current ad activates and what evidence supports that diagnosis.
- A statement of the target desire and why the brand should activate it now.
- The rewritten script or copy in full, with every shift from the original called out.
- The visual cue shifts, specifically, beat by beat where they change.
- The proven-pattern note: this is one of the most underrated messaging iterations because it leaves the product, format, and structure intact while fundamentally changing the emotional engine. Almost always extends performance and expands audience.

## What never to do

- Never propose a desire swap to a desire the brand cannot authentically carry. If the brand has no claim on status, do not propose a status swap.
- Never let the format, product, or structural beats drift in the swap. The whole point is isolating the desire as the variable.
- Never propose multiple desire swaps at once. One swap per iteration so the test produces a clean learning.
- Never propose a desire swap if you cannot identify the current dominant desire. Identify first, then swap.
