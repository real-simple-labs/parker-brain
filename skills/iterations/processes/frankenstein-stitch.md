# Process — Frankenstein Stitch

Lift a proven hook from Ad A and stitch it onto the body of Ad B. Every component is already validated individually, which is why this is one of the lowest-risk iterations Parker can recommend. The risk is the seam — if the energy, pacing, or visual style clashes at the cut point, the stitch feels forced and viewers drop off.

## Required inputs

- The two source ads — both winners, with metrics available.
- The proposed hook clip from Ad A. Identify the exact moment by timestamp and describe what is happening — what is said, what is shown, what the emotional energy is.
- The body of Ad B that the hook will attach to. Identify the exact moment at the proposed cut point, what comes immediately before and after, and the emotional energy at that moment.
- Brand context, including any compliance constraints that limit what either source ad can show or claim.

If any of these are missing, do not run this process. Ask the user.

## Pre-check: is the stitch viable

Before drafting the recommendation, answer all of these internally. If any answer is no, the stitch is not viable — recommend a different process.

- Are both source ads genuine winners with performance to back them up?
- Is the hook from Ad A self-contained — does it work as an opener without the rest of Ad A?
- Does the body of Ad B make sense as a continuation of the new hook? Could a viewer who just saw the new hook follow into the Ad B body without confusion?
- Does the visual style match well enough at the cut point? If Ad A is high-production studio and Ad B is raw UGC kitchen footage, the seam will be jarring.
- Does the emotional energy align? A shocked-reaction hook leading into a calm explainer body is a tonal clash.
- Does the audio transition work? Music, voiceover pace, ambient sound — all need to flow.

## Execution

1. **Lock the hook clip.** Pull the exact timestamps. Note what is said verbatim, what is shown frame-by-frame for the duration of the hook, and what the on-screen text is if any. Typical hook length is two to four seconds.

2. **Lock the cut point on the body.** Identify the timestamp on Ad B where the new hook will splice in. The cut point should be a natural moment — the start of a fresh thought, the entry into the main argument, or the first product mention.

3. **Describe the transition.** Spell out exactly how the hook from Ad A reads into the body of Ad B. Specifically: what does the viewer see at the moment of the cut, what is said immediately on either side, and why the transition reads as natural rather than spliced. If the natural transition requires a tiny bridge (a quick text overlay, a half-second of black, a music beat) call that out.

4. **Note what stays identical from Ad B.** The body, the structure, the CTA, the visual style of Ad B post-cut all stay the same. Be explicit about this so the team knows the iteration is bounded.

5. **Note any production work needed.** Pure stitches require only edit work. If color grading or audio leveling is needed to make the seam invisible, flag it.

## Output content for this iteration

When this process runs, the iteration recommendation must include:

- A name that signals what the stitch is. Example pattern: "Hook Stitch — Pull [Ad A Hook Description] onto [Ad B Body Description]."
- The exact hook clip from Ad A with timestamps, dialogue, visuals, on-screen text.
- The exact cut point on Ad B with timestamp and what is immediately on either side.
- The transition narrative — why it reads as natural at the seam.
- The unchanged Ad B body from the cut point onward, called out explicitly so the team knows the boundary of the change.
- The proven-pattern note: every component is pre-validated; the only risk surface is the seam.

## What never to do

- Never stitch from a losing ad. The hook must come from a proven winner.
- Never propose a stitch where the visual or emotional energy clearly clashes. If the seam will not work, pick a different process.
- Never blend more than two sources in a single stitch — that becomes a mashup, which is a different process.
- Never change the body of Ad B beyond the cut point. If the body needs adjusting too, this is no longer a stitch.
