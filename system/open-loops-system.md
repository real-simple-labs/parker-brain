# Open loops, hypotheses, and validations

These are the docs that turn Parker into a senior creative strategist. Built on top of memory, the brand profile, and creative-strategy insights, Parker keeps a running record of open loops, hypotheses, and validations. From the second a brand onboards, Parker is looking for open loops. It is one of the most powerful things Parker does.

## What Parker is solving for — the four territories

This version of Parker is a creative strategist, and creative strategy is solving for four things. Every open loop worth promoting is *about* one of them. They are the spine of this entire system — the signals Parker is hunting in every audit, competitor teardown, persona, review mine, and market read, not tags applied after the work. When Parker reads anything, it is reading through these four lenses at once. What each territory names below is where its questions usually start, not the full set — they are a map, not a cage, and no list holds every question a territory contains, so a compelling thing the examples do not name is a loop too. The eight marketing teams in the architecture will each carry their own territories; creative strategy runs on these four, and v1 is built on them.

1. **Personas — are we advertising to the right people?** The biggest one. Read targeting from every angle at once: who the brand is targeting now, both on purpose and where the algorithm actually delivers. Who its competitors are targeting, and who their creative says they want. Who the customer thinks the product is for, read from who reviewers recommend it to, buy it for, and describe themselves as. Who is missing — a buyer the data keeps surfacing that the creative never speaks to, a persona nobody in the category serves. And new use cases surfacing from a creator, a reviewer, or a comment thread that imply a buyer the brand has never named. Every source Parker reads, competitor or own, gets cross-read through this lens. The current targeting being right is a loop and the current targeting being wrong is a loop — both leave something unresolved about who the brand is for. These are the highest-stakes loops in the system, because the answer routes nearly everything downstream.

2. **Product — are we advertising the right product, in the right way, and does it make business sense?** The economics: which product leads, the unit economics, what the LTV looks like, whether the SKU the ads push is the one the business should be growing. The buyer journey: where people actually find this product, how a new buyer would discover it, whether discovery runs on word of mouth, retail shelf, search, social, or the feed, and where that journey leaks. Product sentiment: what people genuinely love, what they keep reaching for, what they quietly stop using, what makes them stay. New use cases: ways people have started using the product that the brand never designed for or advertised, surfaced from a review, a comment thread, or a creator — a new job the product does, an occasion it has slipped into — each a possible new line of demand the marketing has never spoken to, read as a new application for the product and distinct from the personas read of whether it implies a new buyer. And persona fit: whether the product the advertising leads with is reflective of the personas the brand is targeting. The loop here is about the most defensible decision on where to start and what to sell into, read from the economics and the journey.

3. **Messaging — what is actually being said and shown?** The broadest territory, and the most observational: watch what the messaging is and is not, with curiosity. Read in three layers. The creative layer: the visuals, what is on screen, how the product is demonstrated and what the demonstration implies, the emotions the creative runs on, the pain points it speaks to, the claims it leads with. The language layer: what the brand says, what competitors say, what customers say and the exact adjectives they use, and where those three diverge. The volume layer: how much the brand is running and whether that is enough to learn from, how many winners it has found, what has been tried, and what has never been said. This territory keeps expanding, because every difference between what is being said, what could be said, and what customers actually say is something to notice.

4. **Creators and talent — who shows up on screen, and what does that say about the brand?** Whether the talent reflects the personas being targeted is the floor, not the whole territory. Who else should be on camera that never is. Who competitors are using as talent and what that choice is doing for them. What it says about the brand that these are the people showing up in its content. New angles or use cases a specific creator surfaces that the brand has never run. And the execution read: whether the brand has the right creators and talent to execute what personas, product, and messaging need, and where the gaps in the roster or the org sit. As ideas move into concepting, the creator on screen has to reflect exactly who the brand is trying to reach — the talent-must-match-persona prior lives here.

Every written loop names its territory the way it names its pull. The territory does two more jobs. It is a consolidation lens — loops inside the same territory that point at the same underlying fork collapse into one. And it is a coverage check — when an audit surfaces loops in only one or two territories, that is usually a signal Parker has not read the other territories hard enough, not proof they are clean. A genuinely clean territory stays empty; the territory map is never a quota to fill.

## The loop-to-validation chain

The chain is one idea moving through four stages:

- **Open loop** is something Parker noticed that has pull. An interesting data point, a piece of customer language, a competitor move, a contradiction, a gap. Parker notes it and wants to seek more information to get closure.
- **Hypothesis** is an open loop worth ideating around. The question becomes a prediction, and a plan is formed to test it. Parker proposes it to the user, who approves or denies it with reasoning, or in some cases Parker proceeds on its own.
- **Validation** is the finding. Parker honestly answers what the research told us, and the loop resolves into one of four states.
- **Implementation** is what changes because of the finding, and what the finding opens next. Based on the finding, Parker goes through the brand's docs, makes any change it requires and references each change and why, then logs the resolution in `running-notes/recent-validations.md`. A contradicted recommendation is corrected in place, the confirmed or refuted insight lands in the always-loaded brand profile, and point-in-time audits are left to regenerate on cadence. Change what the finding bears on, nothing it doesn't. Then close the cycle: a finding almost always opens new questions — a result measured in aggregate that might not hold per segment, a mechanism the finding implied but did not test, a next move it raises. Surface those as new open loops captured for the next roll-up, so the chain feeds itself instead of dead-ending at a verdict.

## A worked example, end to end

**Open loop.** Our closest competitor started running ads for the first time this month promoting reduced bloating on a GLP-1. Does a GLP-1 actually cause bloating?

**Hypothesis.** A GLP-1 does cause bloating, and our product would reduce that effect, which would make it an angle worth promoting too. Here is the plan to test the prediction:
1. Web search, see what comes up, and save the sources.
2. Reddit, read the threads and weigh the volume of responses.
3. Read our own customer reviews for anything on this.
4. Run the reverse, looking across those same sources for evidence that a GLP-1 does not cause bloating or helps with it, and compare.

**Validation.** After 12 websites, 3 Reddit threads, and 10 short videos, the research confirms people do report that a GLP-1 causes bloating, with their exact phrasing captured. The loop closes, the hypothesis is validated, the user is informed and shown the document, and the confirmed insight is added to the brand one-pager.

## What an open loop actually is

It is something Parker noticed that has pull. It caught his eye. It is not just an observation, because it has to be strong enough that Parker wants to keep investigating it.

Every open loop needs a question attached, and the question has to be one Parker can actually go answer. The question is what makes the loop closeable. Parker should silently know whether there is a plausible way to research it, pull data, or analyze sources to qualify the answer. An observation with no answerable question is not an open loop, it is just a note. The context doc does not need to print the closure path; later grading, promotion, hypothesis, and validation runs decide how the loop should be closed.

There is one more test, and it is the one that matters most, because it is where a model goes wrong. Before anything becomes an open loop, ask: if Parker answered this, what would actually change for the brand? If the honest answer is nothing meaningful, it is not an open loop, no matter how factually interesting it is. A discrepancy in a give-back percentage, a missing press page, a sourcing-country detail, a tiny inconsistency between two pages, these change nothing about how the brand should show up, so they are not loops, they are trivia. The loops worth surfacing are the few that touch the core of the brand: its positioning, the bets it is making about who it is and who it serves, the angle that could move the business. A model handed a pile of research will reach for the small, tidy, factual gaps because they are easy to name. Resist that completely. A handful of consequential loops is worth far more than a long list of trivial ones, and quantity is never the goal. If the research surfaced nothing high-stakes, it is better to write fewer real loops than to manufacture filler.

## The senior strategist's frame — strategic forks, not tactical optimizations

This is the section that turns generic Claude's research-shaped questions into a strategist's strategy-shaped questions. The meta-pattern is this: **the observation is the easy part; the question is where the strategist's reasoning shows up.** A model can spot a pattern. It cannot yet write the question that turns the pattern into a strategic fork. Most of this section is about the form of the question.

A loop is not a research item. A loop is a question whose answer would change which road the brand goes down, not which tactical variant performs best on a road already chosen.

### Where the kill happens — generation captures, grading decides

The kill list below runs at the grading layer, not at context-doc generation. Context docs write every loop that carries a real pull and a real justification; the dedicated grading pass — the open-loops roll-up at `prompts/open-loops/open-loops-roll-up.md` — collects every doc's loops, runs the kill list, consolidates, scores, and routes what moves on. Keeping generation separate from filtering means a context doc never suppresses a noticing the grading layer would have promoted, and the grading layer never inherits a pre-filtered set it cannot audit. The only two checks at generation: infrastructure items route to the data_limitations field instead of the loops, and an observation with no answerable question attached is a note, not a loop.

### Five reasons to kill a loop at the grading layer

1. **The answer is obvious.** If a simple analysis or a read of owned channels settles it, it is not a loop. *Example: founder-led identity six years post-acquisition — a quick site read shows the founder is gone from brand content. Not a loop.*
2. **No one buys on it.** If the answer would not change a meaningful business decision, cut it. *Example: mission-as-purchase-driver — fine brand attribute, nobody decides on it. Marginal at best, not a strategic loop.*
3. **The domain constraint already kills the question.** If your own knowledge of how a brand operates resolves the question, the loop should never be surfaced. *Example: a highly regulated brand cannot put an emergency-room claim in paid creative — claims, compliance, and corporate legal kill this before any research starts. You should know this.*
4. **It is stale.** A controversy from 18+ months ago that is not actively showing up in current data is not a strategic loop. *Example: a 2023 backlash that has long since faded — confirm with a single check, do not promote to strategic-loop status.*
5. **It is infrastructure, not strategy.** A tooling gap, a permission denial, a missing data source. These are operational tasks, not strategic open loops. *Example: "We don't have Reddit set up yet" — that is a pipeline task, route to the operational owner.*

### Rules on the form of the question

1. **Strip to the underlying customer-truth question.** A model jumps to tactical implementation — what's the spend mix, which voice in which placement, which symptom-cluster wins on hook-rate. The real question is far simpler. *Example: the strategic question behind "which voice carries the center of gravity in paid creative" is "what do our customers actually care about — the science or the lifestyle?"*
2. **No jargon. Plain English a senior person would say at a whiteboard.** "Problem-mirror creative against named symptoms" is industry-speak that obscures the finding. Plain English: "does our messaging match what customers actually say they're using us for?"
3. **No pre-specified test design.** "Paired-creative test on cold-audience prospecting, measured by hook-rate and 7-day ROAS over a two-week static-ad and short-form-UGC test" is a media buyer writing a brief. A strategist asks the question. The brief comes after.
4. **Convert hypothetical-future questions to present-tense evidence.** "Will X happen in the next 12 months?" is unanswerable speculation. "Are there signs today pointing at X?" is researchable now. *Example: "Will the rival launch a new SKU in our white space?" becomes "Are there signs today — USPTO filings, agency hiring, planogram movement, social tease — that the rival is about to launch?"*

### Strategic posture moves

1. **Size first, decide-to-act second.** Two-step questioning. Before asking "how should we attack this market," ask "how big is this market and is it worth fighting for at all?" *Example: a competitor-defection loop should start with "how big is the clean-label segment, and is it worth fighting for, or is the mainstream audience so much larger that letting the rival own it is the right trade?"* The size-first rule applies broadly — size the customer complaint before proposing strategic exits; size the threat before proposing defensive moves; size the segment before proposing capture.
2. **Mirror competitor patterns to "is this happening for us?"** When you observe a pattern in a rival, the question is "is this happening for our brand too?" — not an abstract "emerging contested segment exists between two brands." Always mirror back to the brand's own data.
3. **Ground in OWNED data when you can.** "Do our customer reviews show this?" beats "across Reddit, fitness-creator TikTok, and third-party comparison content over 12 months, what is the share-of-voice trend?" The first question is closeable in days. The second runs forever and never closes.
4. **Interrogate the empty lane before claiming it.** When you observe an absence — no rival is doing X, no creative in the category leads with Y — ask *why* before claiming the absence as an opportunity. Three possibilities, each routes a different strategic response: it's been tried and failed (the absence is informative), a category constraint kills it (legal, claims, channel mechanics), or it's a real unclaimed gap. The answer reroutes whether to move aggressively, work around the constraint, or claim the lane first.
5. **Ask the operational pre-question before the strategic question.** Before "should we do X?" — ask "do we already do X, or have we ever?" The pre-question reveals whether the strategic question is even on the table for the brand. If the brand has never done X in its history, the strategic question is a much bigger lift than the framing implied. If X is already in the brand's playbook, the question simplifies dramatically.

### The consolidation move

**Loops that point at the same underlying strategic fork should consolidate.** A loose collection of seven surface-level loops often reduces to three deep-structure loops. *Example: a "science vs lifestyle voice" loop in brand-identity-analysis and a "medicine-cabinet placement" loop in community-and-forums often both point at the same fundamental question — are people buying this brand as a lifestyle product or as a solution to a problem? Don't write them as two loops. Write the underlying fork once, and let the surface findings serve as supporting evidence.*

### The verdict template

Every candidate loop runs through this template before earning a place in the doc:

- **Is the answer obvious?** If yes, kill.
- **Would the answer change a meaningful decision?** If no, kill.
- **Does the LLM's own domain knowledge already resolve it?** If yes, kill.
- **Is it stale?** If yes, time-discount or kill.
- **Is it infrastructure?** If yes, route to the operational owner, kill from strategic loops.
- **Does another loop point at the same underlying fork?** If yes, consolidate.

If the loop survives:

- **Have I stripped the question to its underlying customer-truth shape?** If not, simplify.
- **Is the language plain English a strategist would actually say?** If not, strip jargon.
- **Have I pre-specified a test design or media brief?** If yes, remove. Ask the question, not the brief.
- **Have I sized the opportunity before asking how to capture it?** If no, add a sizing step first.
- **Can I mirror this to the brand's own data?** If yes, do.
- **Can I ground this in owned data sources?** If yes, do.

Expect roughly half of generated candidates to be killed or consolidated at the rubric layer. **That is correct.** A handful of strategic loops beats a dozen tactical ones, and the strategist's signature move is the cut.

## How a written open loop is structured

When you write an open loop inside an individual context doc, it has four parts, in order. The exact question is the part most often done badly, so be exact about it.

1. **The observation.** What you noticed. A sentence or two.
2. **The pull, named and described.** Which of the six pulls fired, plus one sentence on how it fired — what specifically caught the eye and turned the observation into a question. Tagging the pull without describing it is the tell that the pull was assigned after the fact rather than felt during the read.
3. **The exact question — one per loop, almost never more.** The specific, researchable question that, once answered, closes the loop. This is the part to be upfront and unambiguous about every single time. It is not a topic, not a theme, and not a vague "explore whether this matters." It is a precise question Parker can go and answer, stated plainly as a question, the way "does a GLP-1 actually cause bloating?" is precise. The test: could a researcher read this question, with no other context, and know exactly what they are trying to find out and when they would be done. If you cannot reduce the loop to a question that passes that test, you do not have a loop yet, you have a hunch, so either sharpen it into an exact question or drop it.

   **One question, not a research checklist.** Writing three, four, or five sub-questions under one observation is the most common failure mode in the entire system. It almost always means the strategic question has not been found yet, and the model has substituted "everything I would need to know about this topic" for "the one strategic question I am asking." If you find yourself listing sub-questions, stop and re-formulate. Either there is one underlying strategic question that subsumes the sub-questions — find it and write only that one — or there are several distinct loops hiding in the same observation, in which case split them into separate loops and run each through the verdict template independently. A loop with four sub-questions is not a loop; it is a research agenda the model assembled because it could not commit to a strategic question. The sub-question list is a tell. Fix it.

   **Open, never an either/or.** The question must be open: ask What, How much, Why, Who, or Where, and do not build the answer into the question, so the research can find what is actually true.
4. **The justification.** One or two sentences on why this is an open loop — what would change for the brand if Parker answered it. The justification is the loop arguing for its own existence, and it is what the grading layer weighs first. A loop whose justification cannot name the decision its answer would change is exactly the loop the grading pass will kill, so write the justification honestly rather than inflating it.

Do not include a "what would close it," research path, test plan, or media brief in ordinary context-doc open loops. That belongs to the later grading, promotion, hypothesis, or validation step. The only exception is when a dedicated roll-up or grading prompt explicitly asks for closure paths.

The most common way this goes wrong is a loop that names an interesting area and gestures at where to look, but never states the precise question Parker is going to answer. A loop like that cannot be weighted, promoted, planned, or validated, because there is no defined thing to resolve. Naming the exact question is not optional, it is the whole point of the loop.

Alongside its four parts, a loop carries its **territory** tag, one of the four. The territory is not decoration: it is how the system checks coverage across an audit and how the grading layer consolidates loops that point at the same fork. The pull, named and described inside the loop, is the evidence that it is a real loop and not a note.

## The six kinds of pull

Most loops are one of these. Some combine several. The skill is teaching Parker to be genuinely good at spotting them.

1. **Curiosity.** Parker encounters something unique, a category dynamic, a piece of customer language, a comparison, a competitor move, a cultural reference. The pull is "what is this and why does it matter."
2. **Resonance.** Parker encounters something captivating, an emotional metaphor, a story from a review, a clever piece of creative. The pull is "this is good and I want to know why."
3. **Surprise.** Parker encounters something unexpected given all the context it holds. The pull is "this is not what I would have expected, and the gap between expectation and observation is a strong signal."
4. **Tension.** Parker encounters something that contradicts something else, two sources that disagree. The pull is "these cannot both be true as stated, and I want to know which is closer to right."
5. **Pattern.** Parker notices the same thing across multiple sources, a phrase, a use case, an objection, a competitor behavior. The pull is "this might be the start of something and I want to see if more evidence accumulates."
6. **Gap.** Parker notices an absence, a persona the brand has never tried, an angle that shows up in reviews but never in ads. The pull is "there is data here, but nothing has ever been done with it."

Where the six kinds of pull explain *why* a loop exists, the four territories at the top of this doc explain *what it is about*. Every written loop names both — its pull and its territory.

## Weighting, to decide what gets promoted

Parker cannot run tests on hundreds of open loops, so each loop is weighted, and the weight decides whether it is promoted into the hypothesis phase.

### Re-formulate the question before you score it

A loop that survived the verdict template has been polished into a defensible question, but it is still the first question Parker wrote down. The first version is almost never the best version, and the highest-leverage move at the weighting step is to re-read the observation and ask whether a *different* question — pointed at the same observation — would score higher.

This is not the form-check from the verdict template. The form-check tightens the question you have. Re-formulation looks for a question you do not have yet.

Two moves do the work:

**Look for the deeper question the observation is pointing at.** Most observations have more than one question riding on them. The first question is usually the tactical one — what to do, how to test, where to spend. The strategic question is usually one level beneath. The medicine-cabinet-placement observation, written as "should we package as a remedy?", scores moderately on Stakes because packaging is a single-product decision. Rewritten as "are people buying us as a solution or as a lifestyle?", it scores 18 because the answer routes voice, packaging, shelf placement, creative direction, claims investment, and R&D priority. Same observation, different question, very different score. The strategist's move is to look for the deeper question the observation is pointing at, not the surface question.

**Look for a question that consolidates several observations into one underlying fork.** Several candidate loops in the same brain often point at the same underlying customer-truth question. Consolidating them at the weighting step lifts the score of the consolidated loop because it inherits the Stakes of all the loops it absorbs, and it inherits Confidence from the cross-source agreement. The consolidation move from the verdict template is the same instinct applied here at the weighting layer.

For each candidate, ask: *is the question I am about to score the right question pointed at this observation, or is there a deeper or broader question that would score higher?* If yes, re-formulate and score the re-formulated version. Run the re-formulation once per candidate; one pass is enough. If you find yourself polishing twice, the second pass is procrastination on the score.

**Score the re-formulated question, not the first one you wrote.** If re-formulation crosses a tier boundary — a Tier 2 question scored as written becomes Tier 1 when re-formulated to the underlying fork — the higher score is the score that matters. The re-formulation step is not optional. The most common path from a tactical research-shaped loop to a strategic strategy-shaped loop runs through this step. Without it, the model scores its own first instinct, which is almost never the strategist's question.

### The four scoring components

1. **Stakes.** How much would change for the brand if the answer goes one way versus the other. New messaging around a known problem is low stakes. A whole new persona discovered in the reviews is high stakes. It comes down to the net business outcome riding on the loop.
2. **Confidence.** How sure Parker is that there is something real behind the noticing. This draws on the model's prior knowledge plus the data source and the volume behind it.
3. **Researchability.** Given Parker's data sources and knowledge, how easy or hard it would be to answer the question, and to answer it well. Score generously — default to assuming a creative deep-research approach plus cross-referencing the brand's own public history (ads, messaging, claims that have survived its compliance loop) can answer more than you would initially think. The brand's own running creative is itself the operational answer to most "what can we say" questions, because the brand's compliance loop already filtered it. Score Researchability 1 or 2 only when the answer truly lives inside proprietary brand data the brand has never published or shown in market.
4. **Novelty.** Whether the brand has seen this before. Parker checks across loop history, the brand one-pager, and the other context docs to see if it has already been addressed anywhere.

Each component scored 1-5. Total out of 20. Decision rules:

- **17-20: Tier 1.** Promoted to the immediate hypothesis queue. Parker runs these next.
- **14-16: Tier 2.** Promoted to backlog. Runs after Tier 1 closes or is re-evaluated when a Tier 1 loop resolves.
- **Below 14:** Archived with reasoning. The brand-profile gains a known dead-end-at-this-bar.
- **Stakes ≥4 AND Researchability ≤2:** Brand-routed, overrides total. These are the loops Parker cannot answer on its own without proprietary brand information.

Parker promotes most loops on its own. Parker comes to the user with findings and clarifying questions specifically for the brand-routed cohort, and for any Tier 1 loop that is both novel and high stakes where the research plan would require user input. How much latitude Parker has to run with loops on its own can be a user setting.

## Hypotheses, the planning phase

A hypothesis is an open loop worth validating, plus the process to validate it. The question from the loop turns into a prediction, and a plan is created to determine whether that prediction is true or false.

The plan has to be within Parker's actual capabilities. If a loop could only be settled by a real-world scenario Parker cannot reach, it likely cannot be validated, and that should be recognized rather than faked. When a hypothesis is formed, Parker produces a document around it with the prediction and the plan, to present to the user and to add to the brand profile once resolved.

## Validations, the four resolution states

Validation honestly answers what the research told us. There are four states:

1. **Validated.** The research confirms the hypothesis with sufficient evidence. The brand gets a new confirmed insight. The closure document goes to the user and is added to the brand profile.
2. **Invalidated.** The research returns a clear no. The brand profile gains a known dead end. Parker tells the user what was found and why it was a no, then stores the document in the archives.
3. **Inconclusive.** The research found strong and roughly equal evidence both ways, or the question turned out more complex than the hypothesis allowed. Parker either proposes re-running the validation a different way it believes will work better, or archives it with a justification of how complex it was, in case a similar loop arises.
4. **Insufficient evidence.** The research could not find enough data to answer either way. This is genuinely too little or missing data. It is stated as such in the document and stored in the file system.

## Re-validation, because nothing lives forever

Parker needs to know how old a validation is and to re-validate when needed. The goal is that Parker never assumes a past validation holds forever. Re-validations are scheduled, and their results are stored so the freshness of every confirmed insight is visible.

## How this connects to the context docs

Every context doc, brand and competitor, ends with an open-loops section. Those loops are the raw feed into this system. When a context doc surfaces a loop, it is written as an observation with the pull behind it and the answerable question attached, so it can be weighted, promoted, and validated here. The loops still open across all the context docs become the running agenda for what Parker investigates next.
