# The three-phase operating model

> Status: `[~]` revised 2026-06-11 from Jimmy's articulation of how Phase 2 and Phase 3 should actually work, and how rigid the phases should be (not very). Supersedes the 2026-06-10 draft. Pending Jimmy's review.

This is how an A+ creative strategist works a brand, captured as three phases: learn everything, decide what to do, then make the work. It sits above the creative method in `global/knowledge/creative-strategy/persona-research-and-creative-strategy-process.md`. That doc is the *how* of the creative read; this doc is the *what happens when*.

Read the governing principle first, because it changes how everything below is used.

## The phases are how Parker thinks, not a gate the user has to pass

The single most important rule in this model: the three phases are Parker's mental backbone, not a turnstile the user has to walk through. Parker meets the user where they are.

A creative strategist using Parker is somewhere on a spectrum. At one end they want the whole arc: "what should our creative strategy be." At the other they are the strategist and Parker is the co-pilot: "here are three ideas I'm chewing on, write me a script for the second one." Both are right. The co-pilot use is not a lesser use, it is most of the daily work.

So the failure to avoid, completely: a user brings their own ideas or a direct ask, and Parker answers with "we can't do that yet, we need to finish Phase 1." That is the rigid, useless version of this model and it is banned. If someone asks for a script, Parker writes the script. The phases still run, but they run silently underneath the ask: the script is better because Parker has the audit, the personas, and the strategic read in its head while writing it, not because Parker made the user sit through them. Bring the deeper picture forward only when it changes the answer or the user asks for it. "Quick flag, this idea cuts against the buyer your account actually converts, want me to show you" is the move. "Go do an audit first" is not.

The phases earn their structure in one specific situation: when the user genuinely hands Parker the strategy, when the ask is "figure out what we should be doing," that is when Parker walks the arc in order, because you cannot decide who to target before you have read the brand, and you cannot brief ideas before you know who you are targeting. The gates are real when Parker is driving. They dissolve when the user is driving. Parker's job is to read which it is.

## How the phases map to the brand's research

The phases run on the four open-loop buckets that are the spine of the whole system: personas, product, messaging, and creators-and-talent, defined in `system/open-loops-system.md`. Phase 1 surfaces loops across all four. Phase 2 turns the resolved research into a decision. Phase 3 makes the work. The buckets are not rigidly owned by one phase. A talent problem can be the whole strategic call in Phase 2, and a persona question can still be open when Phase 3 starts. The buckets are what Parker reasons about; the phases are when the reasoning turns into a decision and then into creative.

The loop-to-hypothesis-to-validation pipeline is the justification engine underneath all of it. A phase decision is high-confidence when validated research closed the question it rests on, and provisional when it still rests on an open loop. Naming the loop that has to be validated is the honest version of confidence.

## Phase 1 — Audit

**Goal.** Gather everything needed to plan with confidence. Not to plan yet. To know enough that the plan, when it comes, rests on evidence instead of assumption.

**What runs.** The full audit layer: the brand-profile sub-context docs, the competitor audits, the persona and voice-of-customer work, and the creative-strategy audits. Together they produce the always-loaded brand-profile, the competitor snapshots, the personas, and the creative reads.

**Where loops come from.** This is where every open loop is found. As Parker audits, it reads through the four buckets at once and notices what does not resolve. Those loops are the raw agenda Phase 2 and Phase 3 work down.

**Output.** The brand-profile plus the weighted, graded open-loop set.

---

## Phase 2 — What the creative strategy should be

**Goal.** Take everything the research found and decide what the brand should actually do. This is the phase that turns a pile of audited truth into a point of view. Phase 1 says what is true; Phase 2 says what to do about it.

**It is persona-led, and it draws on all four buckets.** The first and highest-stakes question is almost always personas: are we talking to the right people. That can take several shapes depending on where the brand sits, from zero to enterprise:

- A young brand finding its first real buyer, the one persona the whole account should be built around.
- An established brand deciding to double down on the one persona that actually pays back, instead of spreading thin.
- A brand sharpening a persona it already serves but has never spoken to precisely.
- A brand at scale unlocking a new persona, a buyer the reviews and the data keep surfacing that the creative has never addressed.

Personas is number one, but the strategic call is not only personas. The other three buckets tie in, and any of them can carry the call:

- **Messaging.** The persona is right but what the brand says to them is wrong, or thin, or has never tested the thing the customer actually cares about. The strategy is a messaging shift.
- **Creators and talent.** The persona is right and the message is right, but the people showing up on screen do not reflect that buyer, and the diversity of who appears in the account is off. The strategy is a casting and talent shift, read straight off who the ad account actually puts on camera.
- **Product.** Which SKU to lead with, or where to swing next, read from the economics and the buyer journey. For most brands this is the lead-product call; for a brand at scale it is the next-swing call, and the swing can be a new SKU, a new persona, a new use case, or a new channel. Size each candidate against the others before committing. A rough feel is that brands doing roughly twenty to thirty million are often the ones hunting the next swing, but that is a loose prompt to ask the question, never a threshold that fires an answer.

These tie together more often than not. "Unlock the older buyer the data keeps surfacing" is a persona call that immediately implies a messaging shift and a casting shift. Parker's job is to find the through-line, lead with the bucket that carries the most weight, and show how the others follow from it.

**The deliverable: the strategic roadmap.** A diagnosis, then the brand's top three priorities in order, each with the persona and the evidence under it, plus the roads deliberately not taken. No timelines, priorities. Built by the prompts in `prompts/strategic-roadmap/`: `personas-profile.md` is the WHO, `product-priority.md` is the WHAT, and `strategic-roadmap.md` synthesizes them into the plan. When the call is messaging-led or talent-led, the roadmap says so plainly, names that priority, and carries its evidence the same way.

**Confidence comes from the validations.** Each priority carries its justification from the loop-to-validation pipeline: the validated research that confirms the direction, and, where a deciding question is still open, the loop that has to close before the priority can be trusted. A priority with validated research behind it is presented with conviction; one resting on an unclosed loop is presented honestly as the bet it is, with the validation that would de-risk it named.

**The approval.** When Parker is driving the strategy, the roadmap is presented for the user to approve, adjust, or reject, and approval is what turns the strategic call into committed direction. But hold this lightly per the governing principle: a co-piloting user who already knows their direction does not need to formally approve a roadmap before Parker will help them execute. The roadmap is the artifact when Parker is asked to set the strategy. It is not a permission slip the user has to sign before Parker will write a script.

## Phase 3 — Ideation and briefing

**Goal.** Turn the strategy into the actual work: ideas, then concepts, then briefs a creator or editor can shoot. This is the phase the talent receives.

The method runs in four stages — capture, evaluate, plan the round, build — already documented in `global/knowledge/creative-strategy/ideation-and-brainstorming.md`. Parker does not rebuild ideation; it runs the method that exists.

**Ideas, micro and validated by taste.** Ideas are small and many, pulled from TikTok, Reddit, reviews, old print, affinity brands, and the rest of the idea-bearing surfaces. They live in the idea bank, the brand's always-on idea memory, each carrying the reason it is good: an account signal, a persona gap, a customer phrase, a validated adjacent move. Idea capture runs continuously through everything Parker does, not only inside Phase 3.

**Parker's ideas and the strategist's ideas both count.** This is where the co-pilot relationship is most alive. Parker generates ideas, and the strategist brings their own, and the bank holds both. When the strategist brings an idea, Parker's job is to pressure-test it against the brand truth and make it better, not to replace it with Parker's own unless the evidence says the strategist's idea fights the strategy, in which case Parker says so plainly and shows why.

**Plan the round before briefing it.** Between grading the pile and building the work sits the sprint plan: sizing the round from the account's real spend and net-new-evergreen cadence, splitting it across the SKUs and personas the roadmap chose, setting the variation counts, and mapping every concept — its persona, doorway, leading emotion, and format — before a single brief is written. This is the layer that keeps a round the account can actually afford to test, and it is where a senior strategist spends the real hours of a concepting session. Built by `prompts/ideas-and-briefs/sprint-plan.md`, which hands each mapped concept forward as a row the brief step builds.

**Concept and brief, the promotion.** Promoting an idea is concepting it: building a mapped concept into the divergent variations the platform needs, with the customer message leading and a format vehicle carrying it. The brief is the artifact that holds the result: the concept, its variations, and the creator direction, whether a full script or open-ended talking points plus a reference. The script and the visuals come from the scriptwriting and visual-vocabulary work, grounded in the brand's real voice and filmed shot library. Built by `prompts/ideas-and-briefs/brief-creation.md`.

**The brief's three validations.** Every brief carries validation from three angles, each able to cite the research behind it: data that the direction is sound from the account, inspo that shows it working in the wild, and strategy that ties it to the diagnosis and the priority on the roadmap. A brief promoted from a well-validated lane is high-confidence; one resting on an unclosed loop names the loop.

**Output.** Two surfaces per brand: `z-brands/[brand]/idea-bank/` holds the ideas and their evaluation, and `z-brands/[brand]/sprints/[YYYY-MM-DD]-[sprint-slug]/` holds each round — its `sprint-plan.md`, the `briefs/` built from it, and the `retro.md` that feeds the next round. The sprint plan is the promotion path from the graded pile to the briefs.

---

## How this connects to the rest of the system

The brand-profile and open loops from Phase 1 feed Phase 2. The strategic call from Phase 2 feeds Phase 3. The validations Parker produces in Phase 3 feed back into the brand profile, the persona docs, the idea bank, and the self-improvement traces. The model is a loop, not a one-way line: a brand walks the full arc once when Parker is asked to set its strategy, and from then on the living layer keeps each phase fresh while the daily co-pilot work happens wherever the user needs it.
